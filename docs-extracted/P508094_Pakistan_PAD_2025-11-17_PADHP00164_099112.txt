I.   STRATEGIC CONTEXT

1. This Program Appraisal Document (PAD) covers Phase 2 of the Pakistan Urban Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH)
Services Program, approved by the World Bank’s Board of Executive Directors on December 12, 2024. Phase 1 was the
Second Karachi Water and Sewerage Services Improvement Project (KWSSIP-2) (P171422; Report No PAD3707). Phase 2
contributes to the following outcomes of the Pakistan Country Partnership Framework (CPF) FY 2026–2035: (i) Reduced
child stunting, by improving access to safe water, sanitation, and solid waste management services for 6.5 million people
(CPF target 1.3: 60 million) and (ii) Increased resilience to climate change, by improving improved flood and drought
resilience for 4.5 million people (CPF target 3.2: 75 million). It also supports the CPF outcome for more public resources
for inclusive development by increasing municipal revenues. Phase 2 is aligned with provincial priorities to improve
sanitation, water, and drainage under the Chief Minister’s Punjab Development Program (PDP), and to enhance solid
waste collection under the Suthra (clean) Punjab Program (SPP).

 A. Country Context

 2. Amid an economic crisis at the start of fiscal year (FY) 2024, Pakistan undertook decisive measures to prevent a
 sovereign default, including sharp fiscal consolidation, exchange rate adjustment, and monetary tightening. An
 International Monetary Fund (IMF) Stand-By Arrangement helped to bridge financing gaps and restore confidence.
 Adjustment has been successful, with inflation now falling, the exchange rate stabilizing, and foreign exchange reserves
 at more comfortable levels, allowing for the easing of import controls. However, fiscal consolidation measures and tight
 monetary conditions continued to weigh on economic activity, with the economy growing by only 3 percent in FY25.
 The outlook has been further complicated by recent flooding, including the critical crop-growing areas of Punjab, with
 expected negative impacts on the fiscal balance and exports. Pakistan must now carefully navigate flood impacts while
 sustaining stabilization efforts to accelerate growth and reduce poverty from current high levels (estimated at around
 22 percent using the national poverty line). This will require reforms to make the tax system more effective and
 equitable, enhance the efficiency of public spending, reduce the large role of the state in the economy, lower trade
 barriers, improve the business environment, and improve basic services to address human capital deficits. Reform
 efforts are underway in critical areas but remain at early stages of implementation. The outlook is predicated on the
 IMF Extended Fund Facility program remaining on track, continued fiscal restraint, and continued external financing
 inflows. Significant downside risks arise from heavy banking sector exposure to sovereign debt, geopolitical factors, and
 further natural disasters and climate change impacts.

 B. Sectoral (or Multi-Sectoral) and Institutional Context

3. The 2022 Country Climate and Development Report (CCDR) highlights Pakistan’s severe climate vulnerability,
ranking it among the top 10 most affected countries worldwide. Frequent droughts, floods, heat waves, and risks to
coastal cities such as Karachi from sea level rise and erosion increase development challenges and could reduce gross
domestic product (GDP) by 18–20 percent annually by 2050. Punjab province in particular faces multiple overlapping
climate and environmental threats: it has the largest settlement area exposed to fluvial flooding (163 km2) and pluvial
flooding (129 km2) in Pakistan, while in the central and eastern regions over 700,000 residents are exposed to dangerous
heatwaves with routinely high temperatures. In 2025 alone, over 4.7 million people were affected by extreme flood events
in Punjab.

4. Pakistan faces an unfinished agenda on human development, which compounds its structural macro fragility and
creates a drag on the economy. The country currently ranks 141 out of 174 on the Human Capital Index, scoring 41 out
of 100. The stunting rate of children under five—at 40 percent—remains among the highest in the world. Evidence
suggests that the lack of safely managed WASH services is partly responsible for this staggering loss of human potential,
including via the impacts of unsafe water and sanitation on child health and stunting. 1

5. Punjab is Pakistan’s most densely populated province. Its urban population is large and rapidly growing, increasing
from 23 million in 1998 (31 percent of total population) to 52 million in 2023 (41 percent). 2 In addition to Lahore, its largest
city, Punjab has 15 cities with over 500,000 residents, and 40 cities with over 200,000 residents. While these secondary
cities play a key role in economic growth and provide housing, jobs, and services, they are smaller than predicted by the
rank-size rule, 3 suggesting that their growth may be constrained by the lack of infrastructure or by socioeconomic factors.

6. While Punjab is the most economically developed province in the country, there are considerable variations in
household welfare and deprivation. A recent World Bank analysis finds that wealthier districts in Punjab often have larger
absolute numbers of poor people than poorer regions, despite lower poverty rates. 4 There are stark disparities within
districts and in cities; low-income and informal settlements suffer due to poor planning and limited access to services. This
results in sterile agglomerations, where growth fails to translate into economic gains or better living conditions. Women
and vulnerable groups are largely excluded in the planning, execution, and monitoring of municipal services, as
considerations of inclusion are the mandate of social welfare departments. A sample of 14 municipalities in Punjab finds
under 10 percent of women in low-mid tiers (grades 16 and below) mostly working in sanitation, and just 7.4 percent in
technical or decision-making roles at Basic Pay Scale 17 and above. This is due to a combination of factors, including the
absence of gender-sensitive workplace policies, limited outreach and training opportunities, and a lack of safe, supportive
infrastructure for women in local government offices. 5

7. Safely managed WASH services are central to public health and quality of life and are necessary to address
Pakistan’s high rates of stunting which affect even those in the highest income quintiles. 6 Over half of urban households
lack access to safely managed water and sanitation, as defined under the World Health Organization/United Nations
Children’s Fund (WHO/UNICEF) Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply, Sanitation and Hygiene. Districts with the
poorest water and sanitation services (WSS) register the highest child stunting rates, trapping families, especially mothers
and daughters, in a cycle of poor health. There are major gaps in the networked sewerage services, particularly in low-
income areas, and sewerage networks are poorly managed; open-air sewers are common. There has been little progress
on the safe treatment of wastewater, which has resulted in concentrations of untreated fecal waste near human
settlements and bacterial contamination of surface and groundwater. Major gaps in the water distribution network result
in heavy reliance on mechanized or hand pumps, which are often contaminated with fecal matter and other pollutants.
Limited access to safe water and sanitation undermines child health and development by increasing the risk of diarrheal
diseases and environmental enteric dysfunction, which contribute significantly to malnutrition and stunting. In Punjab’s
secondary cities, WASH services are the mandate of its poorly funded urban local governments (ULGs). The 2014 Punjab

WASH Sector Development Plan estimated annual spending needs of PKR 40 billion to provide two-thirds of the population
with safely managed WASH by 2024; however, annual spending has averaged only PKR 24 billion during 2014–20 and
focused on infrastructure development over operation and maintenance (O&M). Since 2024, the Government of Punjab
(GoPb) has embarked on a series of initiatives to improve service delivery in cities, including the PDP and a Saaf Pani (Clean
Water) initiative to provide communities with water dispensing facilities.

8. Low performance of solid waste management (SWM), specifically poor collection and absence of modern disposal
capacity, has substantial environmental and public health impacts, including stunting. Collection rates for municipal
solid waste in urban areas are only 39 percent, 7 significantly below the global average of 71 percent in lower middle-
income countries. 8 In the absence of modern disposal facilities, municipal solid waste, when collected, is disposed of in
uncontrolled dumpsites, burned within settlements or dumped into waterways, sewerage, and drainage networks,
directly contributing to flooding, surface water contamination, and air pollution. Emissions from dumpsites account for a
large portion of urban methane emissions, while dumpsite fires are a major cause of atmospheric contamination, with 11
percent of ambient particulate matter of 2.5 micrometers or less in diameter (PM2.5) concentrations in Punjab being
attributable to the waste sector. 9 The SPP was launched in 2024 in response to the sector’s most pressing concerns,
demonstrating high political commitment to improving waste management in the country’s largest province.

9. Much of the province is vulnerable to climate change events such as flooding and drought. Changes in precipitation
seasonality, regularity, and extremes create a higher risk of floods and drought with increased frequency and intensity.
These risks are compounded by high rates of multidimensional poverty and weak coping capacity. Cities will be particularly
vulnerable to climate-induced displacement and changes in water levels, particularly flooding, given the lack of adequate
drainage systems and insufficient maintenance of existing infrastructure that is often clogged by waste, further reducing
drainage capacity.

10. Revenue performance in Punjab has shown improvement; however, key public services remain inadequately
funded. Pakistan’s system of fiscal federalism aims to provide resources to sub-national governments to deliver services
through intergovernmental transfer arrangements. Key revenues related to land and real estate are assigned to provincial
governments and ULGs, such as the urban immovable property tax (UIPT), stamp duty, capital value tax, and the tax on
transfer of immovable properties, land use conversion fees, and fees from private real estate development. However,
curtailed provincial development transfers to ULGs since 2019 have left ULGs reliant on revenue from taxes to meet
operating deficits and maintain services. Revenues from land and property represented 65 percent of the total own-source
revenue (OSR) for local governments in Punjab in FY18–19. The GoPb aims to improve Punjab’s low UIPT to GDP ratio,
which was 0.04 percent in FY22, compared to the low-income country average of 0.3 percent. The GoPb has recently
changed the basis of UIPT valuation from annual rental value to capital value and has announced increased stamp duty
rates in urban areas from FY26, which has the potential to increase ULG OSRs. Tariffs have also not kept pace with surging
operating costs for municipal services (with increasing expenditures on payroll, pensions, and energy). The operating cost
ratio (OCR) for most secondary cities providing WSS services is under 5 percent, indicating a lack of financial sustainability.
Consultations show high demand for better WSS services and willingness to pay higher tariffs for reliable and quality

 C. Relationship to the CPF and Rationale for Use of Instrument

12. The Punjab Inclusive Cities Program (PICP) supports the World Bank’s mission to end extreme poverty and promote
shared prosperity on a livable planet through its alignment with the CPF for the Islamic Republic of Pakistan (Report no.
CPF0000037), discussed by the World Bank Board of Executive Directors on January 14, 2025. PICP will contribute to
the CPF outcome of reduced child stunting (Outcome 1) by improving access to safe water, sanitation, and SWM services
for urban populations at risk. PICP is also expected to contribute to increased resilience to climate change (Outcome 3)
through improved drainage and ﬂood management, and to more public resources for inclusive development (Outcome 5)
by supporting increased revenue through better tariﬀs and real estate revenues. The Operation is part of a World Bank
regional initiative to provide WASH services to 100 million people across South Asia by 2035. It also complements ongoing
Bank operations that seek to address the drivers of stunting through the provision of safely managed water and sanitation
(Punjab Rural Sustainable Water Supply and Sanitation Project (P169071)) and through improved maternal and child
health outcomes in the ﬁrst 1000 days (Punjab Family Planning Program (P178410)).

13. PICP is consistent with and supports Pakistan's climate change strategies, including the National Water Policy (2018),
the National Climate Change Policy (2021), the National Adaptation Plan (NAP, 2022), and the Nationally Determined
Contribution (NDC, 2021), and draws from the recommendations of the Pakistan CCDR (2022). The CCDR prioritizes
addressing child stunting through increased access to WASH facilities and highlights the need to promote climate-smart
municipal services and circularity by expanding water and wastewater treatment capacity and rehabilitation of water
supply infrastructure. The Program is also aligned with the World Bank’s Gender Strategy 2024-2030 as it supports better

 II. PROGRAM DESCRIPTION

 A. Government Program

15. Punjab has embarked on a series of flagship programs to support improved livability and service delivery outcomes
across the province. The PDP aims to increase access to water, sewerage and drainage, and to build sidewalks in Punjab’s
urban areas. The program, commencing in FY26, will support 189 cities in Punjab: (i) 92 cities with a population greater
than 100,000 in Phase 1; and (ii) the remaining cities with populations between 25,000 and 100,000 in Phase 2. A WSS
needs assessment in March 2025 examined existing sewerage networks, wastewater treatment facilities, water supply,
and stormwater drainage in small to medium sized cities. The assessment identified gaps in access to safely managed WSS
and proposed a series of infrastructure investments with the goal of providing improved services to 2035, keeping in mind
population growth estimates. Efforts are also underway to strengthen the institutions responsible for WASH. Water and
Sanitation Agencies (WASAs) are being established in district headquarters with populations over 500,000 and will be
regulated by a provincial Punjab WASA. In smaller secondary cities that lack financial resources, systems, and capacity,
the mandate to provide services remains with ULGs, through dedicated WSS staff.

16. The GoPb launched the flagship SPP in 2024. The first phase of the program is underway and aims to address critical
service gaps such as increasing the door-to-door collection rate from 35 percent to 90 percent. To achieve this target, the
program expanded the mandate of 10 urban waste management companies (WMCs) to collect household and commercial
waste from both urban and rural areas in their districts. The SPP considers infrastructure development by designating
transfer locations and disposal sites in all 140 tehsils (administrative sub-divisions) of Punjab. A cost recovery strategy was
devised based on progressive increases of user charges, in accordance with the approved tariff structure. The program
has developed operational key performance indicators at the district and provincial levels supported by a Waste
Information System. In its second phase, the program will target the remaining gaps by introducing waste diversion and
value recovery strategies, consolidating cost recovery, and launching large-scale communication and outreach campaigns.
This second phase is cognizant of the need for developing modern processing and disposal facilities to manage the rapidly
mounting volumes of waste, resulting in massive environmental and social (E&S) externalities if not properly managed.

17. PICP supports the GoPb’s key priorities by supporting the sustainable improvement of water, sanitation, and
drainage outcomes in Punjab. It builds on the achievements of earlier initiatives, including the PCP, which delivered
improved WASH services to 4.75 million people, operationalized new municipal e-governance systems, enhanced OSRs in
16 ULGs, and provided wastewater treatment in 11 cities. PICP will also draw from global experience in strengthening
urban governance, financing, institutional and service delivery systems, and E&S management.

18. The 10-year Urban WASH Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) has three overlapping phases with a total

around public-private partnership (PPP) transactions, and will in turn share experience on PPP transactions in secondary
cities through the WASH Platform. Counterparts from the two phases will benefit from targeted learning events hosted

 D. PforR Program Scope

22. Program boundaries and duration. PICP will support: (i) a geographic and thematic subset of the PDP for improved
WSS and drainage; and (ii) a geographic and thematic subset of the SPP. Program funds will be used for works for selected
municipal infrastructure and services under the government programs, including: (i) potable water production and
distribution networks; (ii) sewerage networks and wastewater treatment facilities and hygiene behavior change; (iii)
stormwater drainage networks, nature-based solutions for flood control, and storage facilities; and (iv) SWM
infrastructure for value recovery and sanitary disposal. PICP will be implemented over a period of five years between
2025 and 2030.

23. Punjab Development Program. PICP supports improved WSS outcomes, including better revenue performance,
institutional sustainability, and private sector participation in 16 cities. The cities have been selected using evidence-based
targeting criteria for cities with populations between 150,000 and 500,000 based on poverty (bottom 40 percentile) and
stunting prevalence. Cities receiving support from other international financiers or bilateral donors are excluded.

24. Suthra (clean) Punjab Program. The ongoing phase of the SPP is well-resourced. PICP will reward results that respond
to technical gaps identified during preparation, particularly with regards to systems performance and monitoring, and
environmental and financial sustainability. Program disbursements upon the achievement of these results will finance
investments in safely managed waste disposal in Jhang, including the construction of a sanitary landfill and the closure
and rehabilitation of the existing dump.

25. Financing. The cost of the overall government program is US$3.4 billion. The World Bank Program is proposed as
US$721 million. The World Bank financing is US$375 million (52 percent), and Government of Punjab’s financing is US$290
million (40 percent) of which US$75 million contributes to civil works contracts in WSS in participating ULGs and SWM
infrastructure in Jhang. Private sector financing is expected to finance approximately 7 percent of the Program. The
Operation also includes an IPF component of $25 million.

    a. RA1: Safely managed water, sanitation, drainage and basic hygiene services. Access to safely managed WSS has
       considerable public health and environmental benefits by decreasing the incidence of water-related diseases,
       thereby contributing to reduced stunting incidence in selected areas. Interventions under this RA will encourage
       participating ULGs to prepare and provide safely managed WSS, drainage, and SWM services, by supporting:

          i. Safely managed WSS connections provided in eligible ULGs (IBRD US$160 million). This Disbursement Linked
             Indicator (DLI) will encourage the establishment of new or improvement/rehabilitation of existing WSS
             connections in eligible ULGs, (i.e., water supply schemes, sewerage lines and connection points, drainage and
             wastewater treatment plants), that meet quality standards. Program interventions will rely on solarization,
             which will help cut emissions and support Pakistan’s NDC and NAP goals, and will also incorporate climate-
             resilient measures in infrastructure designs to mitigate the impact of urban floods as well as stormwater and
             potential wastewater reuse to adapt to shifting water availability and reduce groundwater reliance. The
             rehabilitation of distribution systems is expected to reduce non-revenue water (NRW). At present, the
             reduction cannot be quantified due to the absence of metering. However, meters will be installed during the
             project, allowing the improvements to be accurately measured.
         ii. Improved performance of solid waste management systems in Punjab (IBRD US$16.5 million). This DLI will

             minimization, and environmental sustainability through modern treatment and disposal solutions, including

 IV. ASSESSMENTS SUMMARY

 A. Technical

40. Program design is relevant and aligned with the GoPb’s development priorities. It is informed by detailed sector and
ULG needs assessments and reflects the lessons and experience of a longstanding urban engagement in the province. The
Program builds on the experience of PCP and its precursors in Punjab, which used results-based financing to incentivize
improved institutional capacity and infrastructure development by municipalities, as well as similar institutional and
financial flow arrangements. 11 Systems for procurement, revenue, asset and financial management, complaints resolution
and monitoring will be mainstreamed across Program ULGs and capacity developed in partner WATSAN units to
implement and maintain them. Funds received for implementing minimum conditions, performance measures, and
improved revenues and operating costs will be allocated for WSS and drainage investments, thereby supporting
sustainable service delivery. This results orientation with a focus on service delivery outcomes incorporates lessons from
implementation from PCP and the Competitive and Livable City of Karachi Project (CLICK; P161402). 12 The use of incentives
from performance improvements in SWM by the LG&CDD and FWMC to address critical infrastructure gaps is based on
experience from CLICK and the Sindh Solid Waste Emergency and Efficiency Project (SWEEP; P173021). SWM interventions
draw from recent experiences in waste management in Morocco, which support adherence to E&S policies requirements.
A TA component will complement these interventions and build capacity and strengthen systems in low capacity ULGs
(particularly in southern Punjab) to build and maintain systems for better service delivery.

41. The Program is grounded in technically sound WSS and drainage assessments that inform targeted, cost-effective
investments in secondary cities. These assessments highlight infrastructure gaps and outline cost estimates to meet
current and future needs, factoring in population growth and climate trends, as well as opportunities for climate mitigation
and adaptation. On the mitigation side, solarization of infrastructure for water extraction is planned to reduce energy
consumption and GHG emissions, contributing to Pakistan’s NDC and NAP commitments. On the adaptation side, the
planned infrastructure is climate resilient—designed to withstand floods—and includes stormwater collection (and reuse)
and potential wastewater reuse, making it more adaptable to changing water availability. These measures also help reduce
reliance on increasingly unreliable groundwater by diversifying water sources. Finally, the proposed infrastructure
solutions are technically sound, and cost estimates align with current market conditions. Based on these estimates, the
Program envelope can support investments in 16 of 20 prioritized secondary cities as described in the Technical
Assessment.

42.     The PICP incentivizes the use of PPPs to mobilize private sector financing in two cities. The recent Punjab Public
Private Partnership Act 2025 provides the enabling environment for PPPs, as it introduces a comprehensive PPP
framework to attract private sector investment in public infrastructure. Under the Act, a Punjab PPP Authority has been
established to support line ministries to prepare and implement PPP projects. It has also introduced financial instruments
such as VGF and a Project Development Facility.

43.     Kasur and Chakwal have been selected as pilot cities for a PPP in water supply, sanitation, and drainage due to
their sufficient scale, clear infrastructure gaps, and strong cost recovery potential identified through willingness-to-pay
surveys. The PPP contracts will support bulk water development, new water treatment plants, network rehabilitation,
wastewater collection improvements, drainage works, and the construction or upgrading of wastewater treatment plants
(WWTPs) in both cities. To enhance bankability, a VGF allocation of 25 percent (US$ 20 million) has been proposed to
reduce upfront private sector risk and keep tariffs affordable. The VGF allocation will be financed through disbursements
from DLI 6 and the Government of Punjab’s counterpart funding. The PPP structure will be designed for flexibility and
replicability, with clear service delivery models, risk-sharing mechanisms, tariff frameworks, and performance monitoring
indicators. IFC has submitted a two-phase transaction advisory proposal: Phase 1 will involve extensive technical, legal,
commercial/financial, and E&S due diligence; Phase 2, once the transaction structure is approved under the PPP Act, will
focus on preparing and issuing tender documents, including bidding procedures, contractual obligations, performance
standards, risk allocation, tariff structures, and monitoring procedures. Given the challenges of attracting foreign investors
for water infrastructure projects, a market sounding exercise is planned to solicit bids from consortiums comprising local
investors with prior experience in managing utility-scale projects and securing financing, alongside foreign operators with
the necessary expertise.

44. The establishment of the WATSAN unit is grounded in a sustainable service delivery model. Each unit can be seen
as a “proto-utility” composed of a municipal officer (MO), two sub-engineers, an information and technology (IT) assistant,
finance accountant, E&S officer, and a GIS expert, covering all aspects of WSS delivery. Given the current low OCR across
most Program cities, locating the WATSAN unit within ULGs allows access to broader municipal revenues and provincial
fiscal transfers in the short term. In the medium to longer term, PICP aims to progressively strengthen the financial
sustainability of WSS services. A key objective is to improve OCR over time, enabling the WATSAN units to become
financially self-sufficient. Importantly, revenues generated through water tariffs will be ring-fenced to exclusively cover
WSS O&M. This mechanism will enhance transparency, accountability, and long-term viability. This institutional approach
is aligned with the broader reform efforts of the GoPb, which is working to establish WASAs at the district level in cities

45. DLI 2 supports SWM system improvements in critical areas identified through a review of existing systems and
operations. The GoPb has taken focused measures in the first year of SPP implementation to strengthen SWM services,
including: (i) a significant increase in sector allocations; (ii) mandating specialized technical agencies (WMCs) to manage
SWM sector operations across all urban and rural areas of Punjab; (iii) establishing a fully private contractor-led model for
waste collection, transportation, and disposal; (iv) deploying a province-wide waste management information system
relying on modern systems for performance tracking and operational oversight; and (v) initiating cost recovery under a
user tariff structure adopted in 2024. At present, SWM contractors have been engaged in 138 out of 140 tehsils in Punjab
and PKR 180 billion (US$630.0 million) has been allocated for SPP implementation for FY25–26. The GoPb now intends to
widen and deepen the SPP to improve the impact and sustainability of interventions. This will entail a set of regulatory,
policy, institutional, and technical measures for outcomes consisting of improved sector planning and oversight, E&S
performance, circular economy, cost recovery, and citizen engagement. DLI 2 will incentivize the achievement of these
goals through tracking performance towards key milestones. Interventions are structured into a set of cohesive measures
based on a detailed review of the SPP, preparing for the rollout of its next phase. Technical due diligence and site visits
have identified readiness in terms of resources and technical capacity of officials at the LG&CDD and of the LG&CDD’s
officials and WMCs to operationalize the proposed activities. The design of DLI 2 is also informed by the World Bank-
supported interventions in Karachi over the last decade through the CLICK and SWEEP projects. In addition, the GoPb
intends to utilize disbursements against SWM results from the Program to develop waste recovery and sanitary disposal
capacity in Jhang. While there are E&S risks associated with the demonstration landfill site, specific criteria will be applied
to ensure the exclusion of high-risk E&S activities. The detailed criteria are discussed in Section IV.C.

46. Readiness for implementation. Several preparatory activities have been frontloaded for early implementation. Design
and supervision contracts for the WSS and drainage works are under procurement with a goal of initiating Planning
Commission Form 1s (PC-1s) in year 1 on the Program. Land parcels have been identified in most cities and will be allocated
to the Program by effectiveness. A transaction advisory is being explored with the IFC to support PPP modalities. The
LG&CDD has already notified the WATSAN units and initiated hiring for key positions.

48. Economic analysis. The Program is anticipated to generate several economic and social beneﬁts. Quantiﬁable beneﬁts
include savings in healthcare expenditures due to a reduction in waterborne diseases, indirect beneﬁts such as decreased
lost working or school days, and the coordinated purchases of WSS facilities. PICP is expected to reduce GHG emissions,
enhance property values through improved environmental conditions from better SWM, bolster ﬁscal sustainability via
increased OSR for local governments, and create multiplier eﬀects from IPF ﬁnancing. Non-quantiﬁed advantages include
reducing infrastructure loss from climate change, and compliance with international commitments such as the Paris
Agreement, the Sustainable Development Goals, the demonstration eﬀect on other cities, and from fostering equality of
opportunity (including on gender) and shared prosperity.

49. The quantiﬁed beneﬁts are organized into four categories, with beneﬁts projected over a 20-year horizon. These
projections utilize a discount rate of 5 percent, derived from the real GDP growth rate and real interest rate, and assume
purchasing power parity to convert future PKR values into US$. These include: (i) beneﬁts related to WSS, including savings
in households' out-of-pocket expenses for waterborne diseases, as well as savings in indirect costs such as lost working
days and school absenteeism, along with reductions in disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), valued through the Value of a
Statistical Life. The present value of these beneﬁts is estimated at approximately US$681.8 million, with an interval
estimate ranging from US$476.4 million to US$887.3 million; (ii) beneﬁts from SWM sector interventions, evaluated based
on reduced GHG emissions valued at carbon credits, the social value of carbon reduction, land value appreciation, and
decreased direct and indirect costs associated with disease and DALYs. The present value of these beneﬁts is estimated at
US$237.4 million, with a range from US$113.4 million to US$361.3 million; (iii) increases in ULG revenues of at least 80
percent by 2030 are estimated to generate beneﬁts with a present value US$194.4 million with an interval estimate ranging
from US$117.4 million to US$234.5 million; and (iv) through the IPF component using the multiplier eﬀect of spending
within the local economy as a proxy, with beneﬁts estimated at US$30.6 million. The cumulative economic beneﬁts from
these four categories amount to US$1,144.2 million, with a net present value (NPV) of US$785.5 million. Given the project
cost of US$400 million, with a discounted value of US$358.7 million, the beneﬁt-cost ratio is estimated at 3.19, with an
interval estimate of 2.04 to 4.24. The economic internal rate of return is calculated at 26.9 percent, indicating good value
for money.

50. Citizen Engagement. Community groups or committees will be formed to provide inputs during the design phase,
and to provide oversight during implementation. In addition, DLI 4 incentivizes the rollout of a behavior change
communication (BCC) campaign regarding hygiene and water conservation. Under DLI 3, a grievance redress mechanism
(GRM) developed under PCP will be adopted by ULGs and integrated with the LG&CDD dashboard for the entire Program.
PICP will also ensure consultations include women and marginalized groups; undertake extensive communications and
outreach; and adopt citizen’s right-to-information measures. Stakeholder engagement will also be ensured through the
implementation of Stakeholder Engagement Plan and during the implementation of other E&S management instruments.

51. Gender. Women remain signiﬁcantly underrepresented in Pakistan’s urban WASH sector, despite being primary users
of public services and essential workers. Their limited presence in municipal institutions reﬂects broader structural barriers
to inclusion and is compounded by the reluctance of many women to accept postings outside major cities due to unsafe
or unsupportive working environments. In large urban centers such as Lahore, female sanitation workers frequently
experience harassment and the absence of protection from harassment. These systemic challenges, combined with
entrenched patriarchal norms, limit women’s participation (particularly in technical and leadership roles) and prevent
municipal services from eﬀectively addressing the needs of half the population. To address these challenges, the Program
will incentivize institutional, infrastructure, and outreach measures to support improved working conditions for women
and to achieve the LG&CDD’s 15 percent quota for women. Key eﬀorts include: (i) investing in gender-sensitive workplace
infrastructure, such as separate bathrooms; (ii) adopting inclusive by-laws for greater female participation; and (iii)
prioritizing the hiring of women. ULGs will adopt the Harassment of Women at the Workplace Act into their by-laws to
ensure enforceable protections as part of DLI 4, and gender complaint desks will be established in each ULG, staﬀed by a
woman or transperson, with monthly reporting and follow-up. Strengthening the enforcement of harassment protections

                                                                                                                                    Page 18

will be particularly important for women sanitation workers, who face high levels of vulnerability and often lack recourse.
Paired with targeted capacity building, 13 these reforms can also help vulnerable female workers build new skills and access

promote inclusive public service use and shift restrictive social norms. The Program will measure progress by the increase

52.      Maximizing Finance for Development (MFD): PICP aligns with the World Bank’s MFD approach, with private
investment counted toward Private Capital Mobilization (PCM) and MFD-Enabling interventions. Result Area 1 (notably
DLI 2) expands private participation in SWM via outsourced collection contracts. Beyond service provision, the PICP will
catalyze private financing for value recovery (recycling and waste-to-energy), reducing fiscal dependence and creating
green jobs. Result Area 2 (DLIs 4 and 5) will strengthen municipal management capacity, improve municipal finances as
well as investments in WASH and SWM services. PICP has been tagged as MFD-e. Under DLI 6, the Program will develop a
plan for the construction of WSS investments in at least two of selected cities through a PPP model. The DLI will potentially
leverage IFC transaction advisory role to support the initiation and approval of two PPP contracts. In turn, the achievement
of DLI6, together with the Government of Punjab’s counterpart funding, will trigger the disbursement of approximately
25 percent of the total WSS construction investment to be used as VGF. The private entity will mobilize the remaining
financing. The total PCM is estimated at US$56 million, structured with a 70:30 debt–equity ratio. These PPPs represent a
pilot approach for Punjab’s secondary cities with potential for scale-up.

53. Climate Co-Benefits. Pakistan is highly vulnerable to climate hazards, as outlined in the initial climate and disaster risk
screening for the Program, due to the likelihood of flooding, drought, and rising heat episodes in the coming decades.
Program design is cognizant of these trends and ensures that investments are climate-screened and resilient. Drainage
and water storage infrastructure will ensure protection against flood and drought events. WSS infrastructure will take a
longer-term development horizon and utilize a modular approach to allow for future scale-ups. In addition, performance
measures under DLI 4 also support climate planning by municipalities. Improved waste collection in Jhang will also ensure
reduced drain and waterway obstruction. Annex 2 provides further details.

54. Program impacts on GHG and landfill gas emissions. The total yearly municipal waste generated in Jhang tehsil yearly
was estimated to be 255,000 metric tons. Approximately 95 percent of this volume is disposed of in uncontrolled
dumpsites and 5 percent burned through open burning. The existing dumpsite is an uncontrolled facility, lacking effluent
or landfill gas collection. Waste composition data was drawn from field work conducted under PCP showing a proportion
of organics, specifically food waste, ranging from 40 to 50.5 percent, which is responsible for the majority of GHG
emissions. PICP will support the development of a modern facility and the closure of existing dumpsites, which will
contribute to a reduction in GHG emissions both directly and indirectly through: (i) the capture and flaring of landfill gas;
(ii) regulatory measures to prevent the burning of waste within the Program area; and (iii) increased diversion rates
resulting from enhanced recovery activities. Based on the assumption that 50 percent of landfill gas can be effectively
recovered, preliminary calculations indicate a reduction of net emissions of 350,000 tons of carbon dioxide equivalent
from the baseline over a period of five years.

for O&M and asset management to respond to climate risks. Risks from climate hazards are accordingly reduced to an
acceptable level and are not likely to have material impact on the operation and its development objective.

accordance with the World Bank’s Procurement Regulations for Borrowers for Goods, Works, Non-Consulting and
Consulting Services dated July 1, 2016, and revised February 2025. The Program will be subject to the World Bank’s
Anticorruption Guidelines (ACG). The implementing entity will prepare a simplified Project Procurement Strategy for
Development (PPSD) that will inform the overall procurement and contract management approach for the IPF component.
The World Bank’s Systematic Tracking of Exchanges in Procurement (STEP) will be used for all procurements under the IPF
component. The Borrower will identify procurements under the TA component and propose a draft Procurement Plan for
the first 18 months.

62. Financial Management (FM). Program funds will be managed by selected ULGs and the FWMC. The FSA assesses their
fiduciary systems to manage funds, staff capacity and adequacy, planning and budgeting, flow of funds, and reporting and
auditing arrangements. Any fiduciary systems strengthening will be undertaken through the IPF window. The FSA draws
upon existing knowledge of government systems (including ULG systems) and the Public Expenditure and Financial
Accountability assessment, and assesses the Borrower’s regulatory framework and procedures, fiduciary management
capacity, implementation performance, and key fiduciary risks. At this stage, the fiduciary risk for this Operation is rated
as “Substantial.

63. FM for IPF Window: Separate designated accounts (DA) under the GoPb’s foreign currency revolving fund assignment
account procedures will be opened in PMDFC, FWMC and E&TNDC. Funds will be front loaded to the respective DA, based
on the cash forecasts for the following semi-annual basis provided in the Interim Financial Reports (IFRs). Implementing
entities will submit their respective semiannual IFRs within 45 days of the close of six months.

 C. Environmental and Social

64. The Program is expected to deliver substantial E&S benefits, including improved quality and coverage of WASH
services and waste management in selected cities. These improvements will help reduce pollution and enhance public
health. However, the Program also presents significant E&S risks and impact. Environmental risks include the potential
loss or conversion of natural habitats due to infrastructure development, typical construction-related impacts (e.g., dust,
noise, and waste generation), and operational risks such as mismanagement of sewerage systems, wastewater treatment
plants, and sanitary landfills, which could lead to soil and water contamination. Additionally, changes in land use for WASH
and landfill infrastructure may place further stress on local ecosystems. Social risks involve involuntary resettlement and
land acquisition. While state-owned land will be used for sanitary landfill sites and wastewater treatment plants, some
WASH sub-projects and last-mile connectivity like access roads and utility connections may require private land
acquisition. The Program may also impact vulnerable communities and pose risks and have impacts related to occupational
and community health and safety, labor influx, livelihoods of informal waste pickers currently operating at dump sites
selected for upgrading, and financial burdens on low-income households related to service connection fees. Social
tensions may also arise from these changes.

65. The Program design includes measures to manage E&S risks. Due to absence of sanitary municipal waste disposal
facilities in Punjab, PICP will support the greenfield development of one modern sanitary landfill, along with two years of

management. To address the E&S risks associated with the landfill, exclusion criteria have been developed 15 during
Program preparation for selection of the landfill site to ensure that high E&S risk activities are excluded. Key E&S
considerations for the pilot site include: (i) site situated away from densely populated urban centers; (ii) no need for new
private land acquisition except for last-mile connectivity; (iii) no forced evictions in the recent past; (iv) no history of legal
proceedings and demonstrations; (v) absence of sensitive ecological receptors; (vi) receipt of hazardous waste limited to
insignificant quantities (less than 5 percent of total waste); and (vii) a waste management company with a strong E&S
performance record. The landfill will be designed in line with Good International Industrial Practices, featuring appropriate
lining, leachate management, and landfill gas collection and flaring systems to ensure safe and sustainable operations.

66. E&S management under the Program will be based on the existing legal, regulatory, and institutional system in
Pakistan and the Punjab province along with the E&S Core Principles of the World Bank. The LG&CDD and PMDFC have
successfully implemented water supply, sewerage—including wastewater treatment—and drainage investments with
several intermediate city governments under PCP. Effective E&S risk management will be ensured for habitat protection,
pollution control, and sustainable land use. The sanitary landfill site and wastewater treatment plants will be implemented
only on the state land. State land will also be prioritized for WASH sub-projects; however, private land may be required in
some WASH sub-projects and for last-mile connectivity, including access roads and utility connections. Standard Operating
Procedures (SOPs) developed under PCP on land and resettlement will be updated and used for addressing such issues.
Inclusion of low-income, marginalized, and vulnerable groups will be ensured, strategies will be designed to mitigate any
impact on livelihood of informal waste pickers, assessment of financial impacts on disadvantaged and vulnerable groups
due to increased user fees and taxes will be done, and stakeholder consultations will be conducted throughout the life of
the Program. The Program will make efforts to enable access to jobs, livelihood opportunities, and public services to
mitigate potential adverse social impacts. Any locations which have been subjected to Anti-Encroachment Drives since
November 4, 2024 will be excluded from Program financing. 16

operation. An independent assessment of waste disposal infrastructure development and operations to ensure

Health and Safety Guidelines for waste management facilities is a mandatory requirement as part of PICP design.

  V.    RISK

72. The Overall Program risk rating is Substantial.

73. Political and Governance risk is Substantial. Political focus may shift to more populist interventions during the later
years of the Program, and this may impact resources available during the outer years of the Program and reduce
willingness to raise tariffs. Mitigation measures will include continued dialogue and close collaboration with the GoPb and
the PSC, and the use of legal covenants to ensure adequate financing for the Program.

74. Macroeconomic risk is Substantial. Although Pakistan has made significant progress in achieving macroeconomic
stabilization over the past 18 months, risks and vulnerabilities remain elevated. Any external shock could trigger
macroeconomic instability, diverting government attention from program implementation and limiting progress against
program outcomes. Significant risks include international price or interest rate shocks; natural disasters that may impact
domestic production, revenue collection, or expenditure needs; reversal of recent reforms to constrain expenditures or
to ensure exchange rate flexibility; and lack of progress in further structural reforms in the fiscal, state-owned enterprise,
and energy areas. The realization of such risks may lead to macroeconomic instability, with major impacts on economic
activities, prices, and household incomes, thereby impeding the achievement of Program results. These risks will be
partially mitigated by ongoing World Bank and other development partners’ support to structural policy reforms in the
fiscal, energy, and state-owned enterprise sectors. For PICP, the risk is mitigated through the GoPb’s continued focus on
WSS and SWM in the budget and through Program design that earmarks budget for municipal WASH investments.

75. Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability risk is Substantial. The ULGs supported by the Program
have low capacity for O&M, which poses high risks to the sustainability of WSS investments. These risks are mitigated by
Program design that leverages the longstanding experience of the PMDFC in building the capacity of ULGs, the addition of
dedicated units within participating ULGs, enhanced and ring-fenced revenues to cover operating deficits, and systems
for ensuring O&M allocations and effective asset management. ULGs lack experience in managing PPPs for service
delivery. This risk is mitigated by the involvement of IFC’s transaction advisory which will provide ULGs with extensive
implementation support.

76. Fiduciary risk is Substantial. ULGs will undertake a high volume of procurement activities amid constraints of low
capacity, weak complaints mechanisms, and limitations of compliance audit with respect to procurement performance.
Delays in the approval of PC-1s or inadequate or delayed releases of budgetary resources for Program activities contribute
to these risks. Several PAP actions and legal covenants addressing these constraints will help mitigate this risk, as well the
role of the PMDFC to build ULG capacity for improved FM and procurement.

77. Environment and Social risk is Substantial. Institutional capacity and systems for E&S are weak at the ULGs and
FWMC, which lack dedicated human and financial resources in these areas. These risks are mitigated by the
recommendations of the ESSA, which are incorporated in PICP’s design. The risks associated with the development of a
demonstration landfill site are mitigated through exclusion criteria used to select the land parcel for waste disposal and
the ability of the WMC to manage E&S considerations. DLI 2 and the PAP will also support the implementation of E&S
considerations.

                                       ANNEX 2. ADDITIONAL TECHNICAL ASSESSMENT

1.      PICP is Phase 2 of the Pakistan Urban Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH) Multi-Phase Programmatic Approach.
The technical design of PICP is based on extensive data collection and pre-feasibility studies across 20 cities. These cities
were selected based on population, poverty, stunting, and land availability for treatment plants. The Program adopts low-
cost, sustainable technologies (e.g., waste stabilization ponds for wastewater) and emphasizes climate resilience, with
investments screened for climate risks and designed to reduce GHG emissions.
2.       PICP’s DLIs are central to its PforR approach, directly linking disbursements to the achievement of measurable
results in urban WASH service delivery and institutional strengthening. The incentive structure used in PICP is based on
local and global experience, including the PCP and urban operations in various countries. DLIs 1, 3, 4, and 5 incentivize and
sustain institutional reform in the WSS sector, using disbursements to incentivize municipal committees to meet and
exceed performance benchmarks. MACs and performance measures (PMs) in particular follow sector experience, using
annual assessments to determine the allocation of funds, motivating ULGs to improve governance practices and service
delivery mechanisms. The incremental increase in available funds, coupled with strengthened systems for investment
planning, procurement, and expenditure management, establishes safeguards against inefficiencies, waste, and
mismanagement. Transparent monitoring and reporting mechanisms, accountability frameworks, and adherence to
procurement best practices ensure that funds are utilized effectively and deliver the intended outcomes.

2. Financial Management and Disbursement Arrangements:
    a)       Staffing arrangements. PMDFC is the lead technical agency for the implementation of WSS and drainage
             activities under the IPF. PMDFC has a dedicated Finance and Accounts Wing with qualified and experienced
             staff to manage FM responsibilities of similar size and complexity including the under the PCP.

    b)       Budgeting. PMDFC will prepare annual budgets based on its work plans and will submit these to the World
             Bank at least one month before the beginning of the fiscal year for review and ‘no objection’. The budgets will



                                                                                                                          Page 51
        The World Bank
        Punjab Inclusive Cities Program (P508094)


            follow applicable government/entity budgeting guidelines. During the fiscal year, the budgets will be
            monitored semiannually using interim unaudited financial reports which are to be submitted to the World Bank
            within 45 days after the end of the semester.

   c)       Funds flow and disbursement arrangements. PMDFC, FWMC and E&TNCD will open a DA at the National Bank
            of Pakistan and submit an online Withdrawal Application to the World Bank via Bank System Client Connection
            supported by a six-month cash forecast (embedded in the IFRs). Subsequent disbursements will be made based
            on semiannual IFRs, providing details of expenditures of the preceding semester and cash flow projections for
            the next two quarters.

   d)       Accounting and Financial Reporting. For the IPF component, PMDFC will follow IPSAS-Cash basis of accounting
            in preparing project financial statements as required under the country system. PMDFC uses Quick Books
            (accounting software) for accounting and financial reporting which meets the project’s accounting and financial
            reporting requirements.

   e)       Internal controls/Internal Audit. The FM manual for PMDFC establishes clear roles and responsibilities with
            segregation of duties. PMDFC has Internal Auditor that reports to the managing director and reviews all cases
            before payments are made. This function does not cover performance aspects in its work.

   f)       Fraud and corruption. PMDFC will report to the World Bank on compliance with ACG once a year. Each
            implementing agency will report to the PMDFC on ACG compliance twice a year.

   g)       External Audit. The external audit of the IPF component shall be carried out by the Auditor General of Pakistan
            (AGP) in accordance with international standards of supreme audit institutions. PMDFC will prepare project
            financial statements, and coordinate with the relevant field audit office of the AGP for external audit of the
            project. The Annual Audit Report—along with Management Letter shall be due to be received at World Bank
            by December 31 for the preceding financial year. Audit observations shall be followed up at the Special
            Departmental Accounts Committee and an audit log maintained for that purpose.

3. Procurement Arrangements: All procurements under the IPF component shall be carried out in accordance with the
World Bank’s Procurement Regulations for IPF Borrowers for Goods, Works, Non-Consulting and Consulting Services dated
(February 2025). Procurements to be made under the IPF component shall be identified, and a simplified PPSD and
Procurement Plan for the IPF component shall be prepared covering the first 18 months of project implementation. The
World Bank’s format and guidelines for the PPSD shall be followed in the drafting of this document. Subsequently, annual
procurement plans will be prepared as part of annual budgeting cycle, and the Word Bank’s No Objection obtained to the
procurement plans. The World Bank’s STEP will be used to initiate and monitor procurement performance.
