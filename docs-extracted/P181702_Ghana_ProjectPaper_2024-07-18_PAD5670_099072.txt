 I.      BACKGROUND AND RATIONALE FOR ADDITIONAL FINANCING

A.         Introduction

1.       This Project Paper seeks the approval of the Executive Directors to provide an IDA credit in the amount of US$30
million from the Scale Up Window Shorter Maturity Loan (SUW-SML2) to the Republic of Ghana as a second additional
financing (AF2) for the Greater Accra Metropolitan Area (GAMA) Sanitation and Water Project - P119063. The proposed
AF2 will be used to fill a financing gap to complete activities anticipated under the first Additional Financing (AF1) which
could not be implemented in full due to a budget shortfall driven by price escalations linked to the pandemic and the
global economy issues in recent years. The AF2 will be used to: (i) complete the rehabilitation and expansion of the existing
simplified gravity-operated Asafo Sewerage Network, and to connect households in low-income communities to the
network feeding the plant; (ii) complete the implementation of the Non-Revenue Water activities with Ghana Water
Limited (GWL) by supplying and installing 10,000 domestic meters; and (iii) complete the institutional strengthening and
technical assistance (TA), hygiene education and support for the preparation of a long-term engagement based on the
Ghana Water Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH) Sector Development Program on Water Security and Water Sanitation and
Hygiene (WASH). The proposed AF2 is aligned to the World Bank Global Challenge Program (GCP) on strengthening water
security and climate adaptation. The proposed AF2 will involve the following restructuring: (i) Revision of Project
Component Structure and Costs; (ii) Revision to Results Framework; and (iii) Extension of Project Closing Date.
Country Context
2.      Ghana has a population of 34.5 million3 and has stable democratic credentials over the last thirty-two years.
Ghana doubled its per capita Gross Domestic Product (GDP) between 2000 and 2021 from US$1,020 to $2,014, reducing
the proportion of its population living on less than US$2.15 a day from over a half to under a quarter.4, 5 Ghana entered a
severe macroeconomic crisis at the end of 2022, as preexisting structural weaknesses and fiscal imbalances were
exacerbated by exogenous shocks and policy slippages. In response, the Government of Ghana (GoG) launched a
comprehensive debt restructuring at the end of 2022 and secured support of about US$3 billion under a three-year
International Monetary Fund’s Extended Credit Facility (IMF’s ECF) program in May 2023. The country has since made
steady progress toward restoring economic stabilization with a firmer monetary policy stance by the Bank of Ghana (BoG)6
to combat inflation, reduce pressures on the exchange rate, and build international reserves. In addition, the government
has embarked on a frontloaded fiscal consolidation, which helped improve the primary balance by over 4 percentage
points of GDP in 2023 and is on track to achieving a primary surplus of 0.5 percent of GDP in 2024. Growth in 2023 was
more resilient than projected, reaching 2.9 percent, and inflation declined to 23.2 percent in December 2023 from a peak
of 54.1 percent in December 2022. However, GDP growth is projected to remain subdued in 2024 (3.1 percent), because
of the ongoing fiscal consolidation, macroeconomic uncertainty, elevated interest rates and inflation, and financial and
energy sector weaknesses. The country reached an agreement with the official creditor committee in June 2024, for a
comprehensive restructuring of US$5.4 billion of debt under the G20’s Common Framework; the completion of the first
review and associated second disbursement of the IMF program; and the approval and disbursement of the Ghana First

Sector Context
3.       Ghana met the Millenium Development Goal (MDG) targets for access to water supply in 2010, five years ahead
of the target year 2015. The country has embraced the sanitation challenge as a national priority. The Ministry of
Sanitation and Water Resources (MSWR) was created in 2017, and the GoG’s strategic plans to achieve the Sustainability
Development Goals (SDGs) are particularly focused on addressing the sanitation gap and reducing open defecation.
However, despite these efforts, access to basic sanitation services remains stubbornly low compared to other Human
Capital Indices in Ghana. According to the 2021 Population and Housing Census, only 25.3 percent of the population has
access to at least basic sanitation, with another 55.5 percent using shared facilities. Open defecation rate is 17.7 percent7.
4.       MSWR, with support from the World Bank, has developed the Ghana WASH Sector Development Program 2021-
2030. The Program Document provides the framework for inclusive sector planning, implementation, monitoring,
evaluation, and reporting for sustainable and universal access for water supply, sanitation, hygiene services and
sustainable water resources management. The program aims to achieve at least Universal Basic WASH Services by 2030,
and to progressively increase the proportion of the population who have access to Safely Managed WASH Services and to
provide guidance to all WASH stakeholders for coordinated development of the sector. The water sub-sector has relatively
clearly defined roles with (i) Ghana Water Limited (GWL) responsible for urban water supply; (ii) the Community Water
and Sanitation Agency responsible for facilitating rural water supply services; and (iii) the Water Resources Commission
with the responsibility for water resources management. The institutional framework for sanitation is more fragmented
with the MSWR providing overall policy and strategic direction, and the Metropolitan and Municipal Assemblies (MMAs)
responsible for sanitation services.
5.       In the last few years, the GOG and GWL, have been investing in the production and expansion of the water supply
networks for urban centers and have made efforts to improve the GWL’s operational efficiency. GWL’s low operational
efficiency perpetuates relatively high levels of Non-Revenue Water (NRW). Although there has been a drop from over 55
percent in 2014 to the current level of 46 percent at the end of 2023, this level is still high and substantially affects GWL’s
operational efficiency and financial standing. Thus, GWL requires a comprehensive performance diagnostic and action
plan to attain a positive trajectory towards credit worthiness. The World Bank is coordinating with USAID/Ghana and the
Conrad Hilton Foundation and other Development Partners to support the implementation of a financial turnaround plan
in GWL. While improving efficiency and cost recovery, NRW reduction is likely to lead to energy efficiency gains, with clear
climate co-benefits, reduction in operating costs and improvement in operational performance leading to increase in the
availability of water for customers, making the targeted communities more resilient to extreme weather events such as
droughts and extreme temperatures.

for climate vulnerability8. The impact of climate change on Ghana is projected to be severe. The more intense and less
predictable precipitation events and higher temperature will exacerbate problems related to the inadequate water and
sanitation services in the project area. The higher temperature and drought occurrence will further increase demand for

C.         Project Background and Status

7.       The GAMA Sanitation and Water Project (P119063) was approved on June 6, 2013, for US$150 million with the
Project Development Objective (PDO) to “increase access to improved sanitation and improved water supply in the
Greater Accra Metropolitan Area (GAMA), with emphasis on low-income communities and to strengthen management
of environmental sanitation in the GAMA.” Following the successful implementation of the parent project an additional
financing of US$125 million (AF1) was approved on September 22, 2020. AF1 was designed to scale up the parent project
result in GAMA and to extend the project’s geographical coverage to the Greater Kumasi Metropolitan Area (GKMA). The
development objective of the project was revised to include GKMA, “to increase access to improved sanitation and
improved water supply in the Greater Accra Metropolitan Area (GAMA) and Greater Kumasi Metropolitan Area (GKMA),
with emphasis on low-income communities and to strengthen management of environmental sanitation in the GAMA and
GKMA.”
8.       The proposed AF2 supports the World Bank’s vision of ending extreme poverty and boosting shared prosperity on
a livable planet and aligns closely with the World Bank’s Human Capital Project (HCP) and efforts to build climate resilience.
The proposed AF2 is also aligned with the focus Area 2 of the Ghana Country Partnership Framework 2022-202610 which
aims at increasing availability of basic services (including WASH) in underserved urban and rural areas. The AF2 will
consolidate WASH gains in the GAMA and GKMA. This is expected to contribute to improved health and productivity,
which underpins economic growth and prosperity.
9.      Parent Project Progress. The AF1 is an IPF of US$125.00 million. The PDO and Implementation Progress (IP) have
been rated satisfactory due to generally even progress across components. By end of June 2024, the AF1 had disbursed
US$104.15million out of the US$125.00 million with an undisbursed balance representing 16.68 percent of the AF1 and 8
percent of the entire project amount. Nearly all the undisbursed balance is committed with only recurring budget for
program management left uncommitted. All indicators will be achieved by the end of the AF1 with the exception of the
indicator on "capacity of sludge treatment plants constructed or rehabilitated under the project” which will be achieved
with the AF2 due to the cost overrun with respect to the Asafo Sewerage System. PDO indicators will be exceeded by the
close of the AF1. The number of people provided with access to improved sanitation facilities under the project was
533,176 above the target of 469,900 as of April 30, 2024. 472,885 school pupils have been provided with improved
sanitation facilities above the target of 430,000. 666,749 people have been provided with access to improved water
sources under the project out of the expected 668,000. The outstanding 1,300 people to meet the project target will be

  B. Technical

37.      Provision of Sewerage Services and Connection/Provision of Household Toilets: The project will improve access
to sewerage services in the low-income communities in selected communities in the GKMA. The provision of sewerage
services will enable houses in the high-density low-income urban communities to connect to sewerage services and to
have household toilets. These households without toilets mostly use public toilets which are poorly and unhygienically
managed leading to a plethora of water-related ailments. Additionally, the houses with toilets are mostly connected to
septic tanks which leak with age and pollute groundwater and the vacuum trucks used for tank emptying are relatively old
diesel-powered vehicles which produce high GHGs. Open defecation and the indiscriminate dumping of sewage in drains,
water bodies and open spaces will also be curtailed. The health benefits and the reduction in the disease burden in the
beneficiary communities is a major benefit of this intervention.
38.      Provision of Water Meters and House Connections: The project will improve water supply services to low-income
urban households by connecting the houses and also, providing water meters to replace old, manual and faulty meters.
This will help members of the households, especially women and children, reduce the time spent daily fetching water. The
water meters are part of the operational efficiency measures of the urban utility which will help in NRW management,
improve revenue management, reduce water losses leading to conservation of the water resources to serve more
customers and setting the utility on the path to financial sustainability.

  D. Procurement

45. The residual Procurement risk after implementation of the mitigation measures is Moderate.
Procurement under this Project will follow World Bank procurement regulations, guidelines, and standard documents.
Specifically, it will be undertaken in accordance with the World Bank’s “Procurement Regulations for IPF Borrowers”
(Procurement Regulations) fifth (5th) edition dated September 2023 as well as the revised “Guidelines on Preventing and
Combating Fraud and Corruption in Projects Financed by IBRD Loans and IDA Credits and Grants”, dated July 1, 2016; and
other provisions stipulated in the Financing Agreements. The procurement documents will be based on the World Bank
Standard Procurement Documents for international market approach, with modification for national market approach, as
well as the recently enhanced documents to address Environmental, Social, Health and Safety, Gender-Based Violence
(GBV), Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (SEA) and Sexual Harassment (SH) as appropriate issues. The Procurement Plan, has
been prepared and approved in the Systematic Tracking of Exchanges in Procurement (STEP), based on the approved
Project Procurement Strategy for Development (PPSD), and will be updated as and when required. STEP will be the primary
platform to be used to submit, review, and clear all procurement plans and prior review procurement activities, as well as
real-time repository for the post review procurement activities.
46.     As in the parent project, procurement implementation will be undertaken by the GAMA PCU (for sanitation related
components) and the GWCL PIU (for water related components). The procurement staff at both the GAMA PCU and GWCL
PIU have gained experience and proficiency in World Bank Procurement requirements and processes to date through the
parent project and AF1.
47.       Summary of the updated PPSD for the parent project indicates that the operational context for the procurement
is favorable as the political, sustainability and technological parameters are encouraging. The economic terrain is however
not favorable due to high inflation and depreciation of the Ghana Cedis which could affect market predictability and
pricing. It is expected that the micro and macro stability being pursued by the GoG especially through the IMF program
will aid in improving the procurement environment to make it more competitive, realistic, and facilitate the achievement
of value for money.
48.     The major risk to procurement that remains under the AF2 is mainly due to (i) high inflation and depreciation of
the Ghana Cedis; (ii) lack of STEP updates leading to persistent red flags; and (iii) STEP contract management module not
updated in real time to reflect actual contract status. To mitigate these risks, the following actions are recommended: (i)
use of price adjustment, on a case-by-case basis, for contract with more than six (6) months duration; (ii) ensure real time
updates of procurement process on STEP especially for post review procurements; and (iii) assigning staff with
responsibility of managing each contract and updating the STEP contract management module.
49.      The World Bank’s oversight of procurement will be done through increased implementation support, and annual
procurement post reviews based on a 20 percent sample initially and varied based on the dynamic procurement risk rating,
in addition to the World Bank’s prior review of specific procurements as appropriate and duly reflected in the Procurement
Plan.

  E. Social (including Safeguards)

50.      The proposed AF2 is expected to have a positive social impact as it will increase access to improved sanitation
and water supply services, with emphasis on low-income communities and will also strengthen the management of
environmental sanitation in project areas. The safeguards classification of A is maintained. The Institutional arrangements
for the project remain the same. Each of the implementing agencies has an experienced Social Safeguards Specialist and
an assistant who have performed satisfactorily. The ESMF and RPF cleared by the World Bank prior to the AF1 remain
relevant. The project has prepared a Resettlement Action Plan for the Asafo Sewerage Treatment facility site. This plan
will be updated to incorporate the full scope of work once it is identified. It is not expected that there will be relocation
of people, but rather disturbance of people who live along the routes of the sewerage network. The project has prepared
a Gender-Based Violence, Sexual Exploitation and Abuse, and Sexual Harassment Prevention and Response Action Plan.
The implementation will continue under the AF2.
51.       Citizen Engagement: The AF will build on the citizen engagement structure designed into the parent project so as
to improve transparency, accountability and participation. The AF will follow the parent project’s-specific Grievance
Redress and Feedback Mechanism for concerns or complaints when people feel unfairly treated or are negatively affected
by any of the sub-projects. Among other things, the mechanism will: (i) provide information about project implementation;
(ii) provide a forum for resolving grievances and disputes at the lowest level; (iii) resolve disputes relatively quickly before
they become unmanageable; (iv) facilitate communication between the project and the project affected people; (v) win
the trust and confidence of project beneficiaries and stakeholders to create productive relationships between parties; and
(vi) allow communities to express views, including any adverse views, on project activities. The mechanism is envisaged
to be at multiple levels (site, local government, ministerial, national) and will address complaints, including logging,
tracking, and resolving grievances promptly during and after project implementation.
  F. Environment (including Safeguards)

52.       The project will continue to use the existing Project Coordination Unit (PCU) for the sanitation component and
the Project Implementation Unit (PIU) for the water component led by the Ghana Water Limited (GWL). Each
implementing agency has a seasoned Environmental Specialist whose performance has consistently been satisfactory with
continued demonstration of commitment towards environmental risks and impacts management. For the PCU, the
Environmental Specialist is supported by an Environmental Officer, due to the magnitude of work involved in the sanitation
component. The Environmental Assessment (EA) category of ‘A’ will be maintained considering the nature of proposed
interventions and expected magnitude of environmental risks and impacts that may be associated with the rehabilitation
of Asafo Sewerage Treatment Facility. Potential environmental risks and impacts of AF2 include generation of solid and

injuries from entanglements, crushing, and impacts, etc. The project has drafted an ESIA inclusive of an Environment and
Social Management Plan for the rehabilitation of Asafo Sewerage Treatment facility covering the priority (essential) works.
This report has been reviewed and conditionally cleared by the Bank, following which the Borrower disclosed it on May
30, 2024. Following the approval of AF2, the Borrower will revise the ESIA to cover the full scope of rehabilitation works,
share the report with the World Bank for review and clearance, and disclose it before civil works begin. The Borrower has
already obtained an Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA’s) permit for the rehabilitation of Asafo Sewerage Treatment

            V. WORLD BANK GRIEVANCE REDRESS

Grievance Redress: Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by a project supported by the
World Bank may submit complaints to existing project-level grievance mechanisms or the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service
(GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed in order to address project-related concerns. Project
affected communities and individuals may submit their complaint to the Bank’s independent Accountability Mechanism (AM).
The AM houses the Inspection Panel, which determines whether harm occurred, or could occur, as a result of Bank non-
compliance with its policies and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution Service, which provides communities and borrowers
with the opportunity to address complaints through dispute resolution. Complaints may be submitted to the AM at any time
after concerns have been brought directly to the attention of Bank Management and after Management has been given an
opportunity to respond. For information on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS), visit
http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s Accountability Mechanism, visit
