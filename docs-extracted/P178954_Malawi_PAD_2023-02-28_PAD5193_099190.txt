I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

A. Country Context
1.     Poverty remains stubbornly high in Malawi. With over half of its population living below the national poverty
line, which has remained virtually unchanged in the last decade, Malawi remains one of the poorest countries in the
World. Between 2010 and 2019, the real gross domestic product (GDP) growth per capita remained largely flat and has
had a very limited impact on poverty reduction.1 Malawi ranked 142 out of 173 on the World Bank's Human Capital Index2
in 2020 and 169 out of 189 countries on the United Nations Human Development Index3 in 2021. Although Malawian
households have diversified to other sources, such as short-term labor (ganyu),4 agriculture has remained the main
income source over the last ten years, albeit with persistently low returns. The transition from agriculture to higher-
return activities has been slowed, partly due to a lack of access to financing, market constraints, skill gaps, and high
vulnerability to shocks.
2.     Malawi is highly vulnerable to exogenous shocks. According to the Malawi Poverty Assessment, for every three
Malawians that moved out of poverty between 2010 and 2019, four fell back due to the impact of weather shocks. The
high vulnerability is driven by an overreliance on natural resource-intense economic activities, limited infrastructure, high
population density, and catchment degradation. Climate change scenarios suggest that Malawi will see increasing
climatic variability, higher temperatures, longer dry periods, and more erratic and intense rainfall events.5 While the
country was still recovering from Cyclone Idai of 2019, tropical storms Ana and Gombe hit parts of the country in early
2022, directly affecting close to one million people, with US$326 million in damages and economic loss.6 Further, the
global COVID-19 pandemic dampened Malawi's medium-term growth prospects. Economic growth fell from 5.4 percent
in 2019 to 0.8 percent in 2020 before partially rebounding to 2.2 percent in 2021.7 Amid uncertainties following some
external shocks, economic growth is projected to increase modestly in the medium term.
3.      Women face disproportionate disadvantages in accessing services affecting their performance in different
economic activities. For instance, in the presence of low access to electricity and water, women also spend more time
than men collecting firewood and water.8 In 85 percent of Malawian households, women are responsible for collecting
water.9 Similarly, the lack of water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) facilities is particularly challenging for girls, who, in
many cases, are forced to stay at home during their menstrual period due to the absence of adequate sanitation facilities
in their schools. This limits school attendance and retention for girls, which in turn limits their future livelihood
opportunities. Among the key policy actions proposed by the Malawi Poverty Assessment to enhance economic growth
is the need to address determinants of unequal opportunity, especially low completion rates for secondary education,
by keeping girls in school much longer through improved water sources and sanitation facilities at schools.
4.   Urbanization has slowed down over the past decade, limiting the country's opportunities for economic growth.
Whereas cities are known as productive engines for growth where labor is matched with employment opportunities,
Malawi has experienced a deceleration of urbanization in contrast to its regional peers. The proportion of urban

inhabitants increased only by one percent between 2010 and 2019, which correlates to the country's poverty reduction
trends. A key driver for urbanization in Malawi relates to job opportunities. In the past decade, about 39 percent of the
internal migrants from rural to urban areas sought job or business opportunities.10 However, cities suffer from inadequate
access and reliability of most basic infrastructure services, which add to production costs and weaken the
competitiveness of Malawi's private sector. The availability of services, such as water supply and sewerage, in built-in
city footprints, can significantly contribute to agglomeration economies, thus reducing urban sprawling, a major
challenge affecting the productivity of Malawian cities.11 Addressing the urban infrastructure and service gaps is therefore
critical to boosting urban productivity and supporting structural transformation for Malawi.
5.      Unreliable services and lagging infrastructure development significantly hinder private sector growth. According
to the World Bank's global study12 on the economics of water scarcity, a single water interruption in an urban firm can
reduce its revenue by more than eight percent. If that firm is in the informal sector, sales decline by 35 percent, ruining
livelihoods and stagnating economic growth. As part of the 2014 World Bank Enterprise Survey13 in Malawi, firms
reported experiencing an average of 6.7 electricity outages lasting about 3.5 hours. Firms also reported 5.3 days of
insufficient water in a typical month, which is more than double the regional average for countries in Sub-Saharan Africa
(1.8 days in a typical month). Water outages were reported more prominently by enterprises in Blantyre compared to
the other parts of the country. Further, the water and electricity utilities are failing to cope with the population growth
and increased demand. Major donor-financed programs did not adequately address the infrastructure deficit, given the
focus on expansion and less on improving the operational efficiency of the systems.
6.     Blantyre City, the second-largest urban center in Malawi, needs urgent infrastructure interventions. With close
to 900,000 inhabitants, and an average growth rate of 2.8 percent per annum, the city has struggled to accommodate its
growing population in a planned manner, despite its vibrant urban economy and historical and cultural heritage. Nearly
70 percent of the city's population lives in unplanned, informal settlements characterized by high poverty rates,
overcrowding, poor road access, and limited access to basic services, such as electricity, water, and sanitation. Low and
unreliable energy and water supply access have also been among the key constraints to private sector development, in
addition to unstable macroeconomic conditions and limited access to financing, thus, limiting the city's capacity to further
contribute to the country's economic growth.14

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
7.      Water and sanitation service reliability is a major challenge in Malawi. With 70 percent access to basic water
supply and 27 percent access to sanitation,15 the country is significantly off-track to achieving the sustainable
development goal on clean water and sanitation for all.16 17 Despite appearing to be better off than some of its Southern
Africa region peers, the absolute number of unserved people has increased in the last ten years (by 0.9 million for water
supply and 1.4 million for sanitation), and official access figures often mask the poor quality of services. Globally, Malawi
ranked 5 out of the top 10 countries (with a population greater than 1 million) with the highest proportion of people at
risk of frequent water shortages and 3 for the highest proportion of people lacking sanitation. 18 Although there is an

8.      Climate change exacerbates the impacts of poor water supply and sanitation on public health.20 The Malawi
Demographic and Health Survey (2016) indicated that 22 percent of children under the age of five years had diarrhea
two weeks before the survey.21 According to the World Health Organization, about 58 percent of diarrheal deaths in low-
income countries are caused by poor WASH services.22 In Malawi, 37 percent of the children under age five are short for
their age due to the long-term effects of poor sanitation, consequent diarrhea, and malnutrition, and 20 percent are
severely stunted.23 Climate-related events such as floods and droughts have worsened Malawi's already poor access to
water and sanitation, with unpredictable water availability, contamination of water sources, and related water scarcity.24
The country is currently trying to contain a cholera outbreak due to poor access to water and sanitation and water
contamination after the intense rainfalls and flooding from Tropical Storm Ana. As of January 31, 2023, there were 34,918
reported cholera cases, with 1,133 deaths across the country since the outbreak, with Blantyre District registering the
highest number of cases.25 Emergency response support is being provided by multiple partners, including the World Bank,
following a request for support by the Government. The World Bank response includes US$100 million to provide
emergency support and enable the continued delivery of essential health services in Malawi under the Emergency Project
to Protect Essential Health Services (P180231), and US$17 million for essential medicines under the Malawi COVID-19
Emergency Response and Health Systems Preparedness Project (P173806) contingency emergency response component
(CERC). These interventions provide life-saving health services for cholera treatment. However, a longer-term approach
is needed to address the structural WASH service delivery gaps that led to cholera outbreaks, with investments in climate-
resilient WASH systems and solid waste management, particularly in high-risk areas such as Blantyre City.
9.     The recently established Ministry of Water and Sanitation (MoWS) oversees the water sector, including water
resources management, water supply, and sanitation. Urban and small-town water supply is under the responsibility of
the two city water boards (Lilongwe and Blantyre) and the three regional water boards (Northern, Central, and Southern).
The water boards report to MoWS on technical matters and to the Department of Statutory Corporations—under the
Office of President and Cabinet—on policy issues (such as financial, administrative, and managerial oversight). The Water
Works Act, the National Water Policy, and the Sanitation Policy are currently under review to enhance the sector policy
framework and accelerate investments to cover the access gap. However, further reforms are required to enhance the

10.     Addressing the WASH challenges in Malawi would require a strategic approach to the sector's structural
challenges preventing further improvements in access and quality of services. The GoM is committed to providing
adequate, reliable, and sustainable water and sanitation services to Malawi's urban, peri-urban, and rural populations.
The Water Sector Public Sector Expenditure Review (PER) of 2020 indicates that despite limited fiscal space, the GoM has
increased budget allocations to WASH from 0.39 percent of total government expenditure in 2014/15 to 0.49 percent in
2018/19. These improvements come as a result of the GoM’s recognition that investments in water supply and sanitation
have the potential to harness human capital and enhance productivity in the country. However, the sector faces two
major interrelated challenges – a lack of investment financing to match needs and the inefficiency of the water utilities
and service providers, which is exacerbated by their weak corporate governance. First, there is a need to shift from the
traditional water source/production expansion into prioritizing and fast-tracking investments that result in rapid
improvements in the reliability and resilience of water production and distribution services. Second, there is a need to
enhance the support to water service providers with incentives to reduce operational inefficiencies by linking public
financing for capital expenditure and tariff reviews to verifiable improvements in efficiency, governance, and innovation.
Third, the Government should play its part in strengthening the financial position of water utilities by minimizing the
accumulation of unpaid utility bills through the adoption of prepaid services for all Government agencies and
departments. Finally, the water service providers can adopt greener and more efficient processes to build resilience to
external shocks.
11.     The MoWS prepared a water sector development program concept26 to improve access to water and sanitation
services, enhance climate resilience, promote economic growth, and support poverty alleviation. The concept builds
upon the key country and sector planning instruments: (i) the Malawi Vision 2063 (MW2063), which aims to transform
Malawi into a wealthy and self-reliant industrialized 'upper-middle-income country' by the year 2063,27 (ii) the national
climate resilience financing strategy (2022-2032) which outlines the path to cover the financing gap to achieve universal
access for water and sanitation and ensure sustainability considering the climate change scenarios, (iii) water sector
investment plan (2012-2030) which targets full water supply coverage and at least 40 percent sanitation coverage by
2030, and (iv) the water utility reform agenda initiated in 2020 to enhance the operational efficiency and quality of service
of the water boards. The program design relies on key lessons from the National Water Development Projects (NWDP) I
(P001667) and II (P096336), and the Lilongwe Water and Sanitation Project (LWSP - P163794), where investments in
operational efficiency and targeted infrastructure have resulted in significant service quality improvement and expansion,
with the Lilongwe Water Board (LWB), fully covering its city boundaries and moving into the settlements in the
surrounding areas. As a sequence to the LWSP, the concept proposes Blantyre City as the next priority in the urbanization
strategy outlined in the MW2063, in parallel with performance-based financing for small towns and rural growth centers
water supply schemes in an accelerated investment path to: (i) gradually reduce the infrastructure deficit, (ii) improve
service delivery capacity, and (iii) close the gap to achieve universal access to water supply in urban areas by 2030. Key
proposed reforms include: (i) incentivizing the water utilities to improve operational and financial efficiency through
performance-based financing; (ii) instituting a tariff review framework that covers recurrent and replacement of short-
life assets; and (iii) investing in sector capacity building to enhance investment planning and implementation.

12.     The Blantyre City's water supply system is under strain and is expected to worsen with increasing climate
change impacts. Walkers’ Ferry (WF) on the Shire River is the city's main water source, accounting for 80 percent of the
water supplied to Blantyre. The other 20 percent comes from the Mudi Dam and Likhubula River. The average water
production (88,700 m3/d) only covers two-thirds of the current estimated demand. The city is already facing water
shortages, and these are expected to become severe over time with the increased intensity of climate-related events
such as floods and droughts. Blantyre water supply is highly vulnerable to water shocks, with frequent water supply
interruptions due to high sediment load, deteriorating water quality, and aging infrastructure. In February 2022, in the
aftermath of Cyclone Ana, Blantyre residents were left without water for more than three days, as both sources at Shire
and Mudi were loaded with sediments from the flooded waters, and part of the network was washed away with the
floods.
13.     The Blantyre Water Board (BWB),28 the city's water supply utility, is facing major operational and financial
challenges. With an existing reticulation system of about 1,407 km, the water supply system currently meets a total
average water consumption of 42,000 m3/day. The system services around 87 percent of the population through about
67,000 individual connections, 830 water kiosks (for communal supply), and 1,900 commercial and industrial
connections. The utility is experiencing financial and operational stress resulting from the technical complexities and high
energy costs relating to high head pumping, with long rising mains and pipe aging, which combined, are leading to
elevated operational costs and NRW. Energy costs currently account for 46 percent of the total operating costs, and NRW
is estimated at 53 percent. BWB's financial performance remains highly unstable, with working ratios above 1.4 over the
past five years, representing a continuous erosion of equity and reserves.
14.     With increasing climate variability, poor sanitation is becoming a major public health issue and significantly
affects the quality of life of Blantyre residents. During flooding events, which are becoming more frequent and intense,
uncollected solid waste ends up in sewerage and drainage systems, and water bodies, further exacerbating the
floods.29 Less than 50 percent of Blantyre's 475 tons of solid waste generated daily is collected and partially disposed of
in an open dumping space, while the remaining waste ends up in the open spaces in peri-urban areas, streams, roadsides,
and drains. The provision of sanitation services is currently under the responsibility of the Blantyre City Council (BCC), 30
which lacks the financial and technical muscle to maintain the services, update and enforce its by-laws. The sewerage
infrastructure covers about 16 percent of the population, while the majority rely on onsite sanitation systems. Most of
the sewage generated in the city ends up in the environment without treatment, either through leaks in the sewer lines
or discharges from the five wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) to several streams across the city. Recorded biological
oxygen demand (BOD) effluent values for Soche, Blantyre, and Limbe WWTPs indicate discharge values of 130mg/l,
382mg/l, and 86mg/l, respectively, against Malawi's Standard discharge value of 20mg/l. This situation is exacerbated by
poor solid waste management. The heavy pollution of rivers and streams has led to breeding disease vectors, loss of
aquatic life and recreation activities, and a poor general outlook of the city. The updated National Determined
Contribution highlights the rehabilitation and reconstruction of WWTPs in Blantyre as a mitigation priority with
adaptation benefits.31
15.    BWB and BCC are embarking on an investment program to enhance the city's water supply and sanitation
services. Both institutions have traditionally implemented small interventions to minimize critical operational and service
delivery challenges. However, these have resulted in limited progress so far, primarily due to limited financial resources.

II. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

19.      Building upon the experience and lessons from the ongoing water supply efficiency improvements in Lilongwe
and the recently developed concept by MoWS, a Series of Projects (SOP) is proposed to increase water supply and
sanitation access and to support the development of a national water program for the country. GoM's vision is to

Financing Strategy. The SoP signals the commitment of both parties to a programmatic set of investments in the water
sector, with sustained support contingent on overall implementation performance and achievement of results, and is
justified by the following reasons. First, the SoP is built on a strong foundation of tested approaches and tools to enhance
utility efficiency and access to water and sanitation services and provides an opportunity for evidence-based learning.

into profit-making entities, thus reducing the fiscal burden on the central government. Third, it allows for long-term
support for institutional strengthening, which is critical for the sustainability of water supply and sanitation services.
20.     The overarching objective of the program is to improve access to water and sanitation in Malawi. In the medium

services. The program will also support the sector regulation to strengthen governance, improve service delivery and
financial efficiency, and enhance resilience against external shocks such as climate, financial and health-related shocks.

C. Project Beneficiaries
38.     The project is expected to directly benefit a total of (i) 330,000 people with improved water services, (ii) 100,000
people with improved sanitation services from the rehabilitation and upgrade of the sewerage network and treatment

Water supply interventions under the project include treatment upgrade, transmission, enhanced storage, and
distribution network, which are expected to increase water availability, eliminate hydraulic bottlenecks, improve
operational efficiency, and reduce NRW from 53 percent to 35 percent with an average of 18 hours of supply. This would
result in improved service quality (hours of service and pressure) for people that currently receive intermittent services
and new access to those currently relying on unimproved sources. Most of the network expansion beneficiaries reside in
the poorer areas on the outskirts of the city, where there are currently no services, and there is a higher incidence of
cholera, Scabies, and Typhoid. The sewerage upgrades will reduce environmental pollution in the peri-urban areas where
the poorer reside and river streams that are an important water source for multiple uses in the city. Poor and vulnerable
households and peri-urban markets will be specifically targeted for solid waste management services. The sanitation
interventions are expected to result in reduced methane emissions and ultimately improve Blantyre's quality of life and
business environment. About 44,000 pupils will benefit from improved sanitation. In addition, local entrepreneurs and
waste pickers will benefit from business development opportunities and income-generating activities. BWB and BCC staff
will benefit from training and an improved working environment.

F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

42.    The project design draws on lessons from previous water, governance, and urban projects and analytics in Malawi,
as well as from global trends and experience in tackling utility performance improvements in developing countries. The
key lessons learned and reflected in the project design are summarized below:
43.      Investing in institutional capacity strengthening is critical for efficiently delivering project results and translating
infrastructure investments into improved access to water services. Under the LWSP, a comprehensive and structured
TA and institutional capacity strengthening was integrated into the core project design and resulted in extensive capacity
building for both the water utility and Lilongwe City Council (LCC). This approach helped ensure faster project
implementation, proactive decision-making, and early delivery of results. Further, capacity-building activities were
expanded into the operational and customer services functions of both LWB and LCC, which were upgraded to respond
to the new infrastructure and service needs. While these are early results, experience shows that such interventions are
more likely to have a lasting impact if they build on and support an existing change management program within the
utility. The project’s support for ICSAP has been designed with these lessons in mind.
44.    PBFM has proved to be effective in driving key reforms, including prioritization of resources, financial efficiency,
and improved service delivery. Under the Malawi Governance for Enabling Service Delivery Project (P164961), the World
Bank is financing a series of local government-led interventions on priority public services through a performance-based
grant that incentivizes fiscal and municipal governance improvements, as well as prioritization of resources for critical
public services. Similarly, under the Mozambique Urban Sanitation Project (P161777), performance-based grants are
being utilized to incentivize institutional reforms on urban sanitation in five cities. Early results from both operations
demonstrate that performance-based grants are an effective financing tool to enhance local government and utilities
governance systems, improve revenue collection and enable service delivery, particularly where there are extensive


                                                                                                                      Page 20 of 64
       The World Bank
       Water and Sanitation Project - I (P178954)



financial constraints. Given the current status of BWB and the financial stress, the project will introduce a PBFM to
incentivize improvements in key financial and operational areas.
45.     Addressing urban sanitation challenges, related public health risks, and environmental pollution requires an
integrated approach that tackles excreta management interventions and combines them with solid waste
management improvements and localized drainage investments where needed. The LWSP design includes only excreta
management interventions that greatly enhance access to sanitation and reduce environmental pollution in the city’s
rivers and streams. However, improving effluent quality discharging from the WWTPs and reducing sewer leaks will have
a limited impact on the overall waste load entering the environment, as most of the solid waste generated in the city
ends up in the streams and eventually into Lilongwe River. The project is thus designed to address the sanitation
challenge, looking at sewerage, drainage, and solid waste management investments, as well as the necessary capacity
building and institutional strengthening required to address these challenges.

C. Sustainability
52.    The project is well aligned with the MW2063, water, and sanitation sector strategies, and the GoM is fully
committed to the project and the sustainability of project outcomes. Investing in water and sanitation is one of the
GoM's key development priorities, as it is currently hindering the country’s growth prospects. The GoM is also committed
to supporting the transformation of water utilities into commercially viable entities that can provide operationally viable
and high-quality, financially sustainable services. In 2021, GoM approved a 4-year tariff increase plan for all water boards
to gradually improve the cost recovery ratio while ensuring improved quality and expansion of water supply services. An
ongoing dialogue is exploring alternatives to establish a water sector regulatory agency to ensure transparent, equitable,
and sustainable water and sanitation services. While the sanitation functions remain less developed, the GoM is
advancing with the review of the National Sanitation Policy to establish the key guiding principles for service expansion
and improvement, including the transfer of sewerage services to water boards, so that they can progressively
professionalize and establish a revenue stream to cover its operating costs for sanitation services.

53.     The project design includes several features to enhance sustainability. First, institutional capacity strengthening

IV. PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY

A. Economic and Financial Analysis

54.    A cost-benefit model was used to assess the ex-ante efficiency of the project. Annual cost and benefit flows are
estimated as the difference between ‘without project’ and ‘with project’ net benefits for direct beneficiaries. The project
is expected to result in increased access to improved water sources and sanitation for approximately 500,000 residents
of the Blantyre Metropolitan Area, with significant health and economic benefits to the city and surrounding towns. The
project is also expected to help reduce inequalities in service delivery between different population segments by
increasing access to water sources in the city's underserved areas. Higher-level benefits include improved public health,
business environment and access to jobs due to increased water availability and wastewater conveyance, improved
environmental conditions, and resilience. Moreover, BWB’s revenues are expected to increase due to NRW reduction.
55.     For water supply, the benefits include (i) health benefits and improved living standards due to increased
availability and reliability of water supply, and (ii) an increase in revenue from the reduction of NRW and substitution of
electricity by solar photovoltaic generation (SPV). Water supply improvements at the household level are expected to
yield improved living standards and reduced incidence of water-borne diseases, such as cholera and diarrheal diseases,
from the combined effect of water and sanitation interventions. NRW interventions will result in a reduction of
operational costs and in the prevalence of areas with standing water from leaks– the breeding ground for mosquitoes
(the main vector for malaria infection). In addition, the installation of SPVs will reduce the electricity costs of the BWB.
56.     Benefits from sanitation investment include reduced coping costs of handling wastewater and increased value of
housing with functioning sewer connections and solid waste management services. Investments in sanitation will reduce
the burden of waterborne disease morbidity and associated costs by at least 15 percent. Climate Change mitigation
benefits include reducing methane emissions associated with the reduction of discharges of untreated wastewater due
to increased wastewater collection and improved operation of WWTPs. The project plans methane capture at the two
largest WWTPs. Solid waste operations will also be economically sustainable and contribute to climate change mitigation
by implementing recycling for refused plastic, paper, and metal, and control of GHG emissions.

financial model. However, the project will finance a set of TAs to identify and structure viable revenue sources for

B. Technical

63.     The Project design builds upon three strategic areas: (i) increasing the access to and reliability of water supply
services; (ii) increasing access to sanitation facilities and reducing environmental pollution; and (iii) enhancing the
operational and financial efficiency and resilience of BWB. The project will prioritize citizen engagement and customer
relations, which have been identified as a critical challenge for both BWB and BCC, and ensure that regular citizens' and
stakeholders' feedback is sought through multiple communications platforms to be financed under component 3.
Further, the project will support the development and implementation of a consolidated gender strategy for both
institutions. Climate resilience will be a key design criterion for both infrastructure and services.
64.    The project's rationale, scope, and technical design are based on findings from multiple studies and analytical
work, including climate risk assessment. The technical soundness of the project’s infrastructure investments has been
established through engineering design assessments, including feasibility studies and detailed designs for both water
supply and sewerage interventions. The World Bank conducted a simplified urban drought (UD) assessment for Blantyre
City, which identified that although water availability is not an issue even in drought conditions, the city's water supply
systems are under extreme pressure due to population growth, aging infrastructure, high leakage, and unreliable energy
supply. The UD framework further identified the lack of drought monitoring and early warning systems, as well as

                                                                                                                   Page 25 of 64
       The World Bank
       Water and Sanitation Project - I (P178954)



contingency plans, and recommended a set of preventive and responsive measures, which will form an integral part of
the proposed business continuity plan. A detailed scoping study was conducted for the solid waste RPEL, which formed
the basis for the project's technical design. Detailed cost estimates have been prepared based on previous studies, using
a comparison of market rates from similar projects under implementation by BWB and other utilities and including
provisions for escalation and contingencies. The estimates have been reviewed by the World Bank.
65.    Priority water supply investments were determined using the innovative i-TOC system, which prioritizes
investments based on the efficiency-yielding interventions from the costs and benefits analyses. Although BWB is still
calibrating the hydraulic model, the i-TOC provides a preliminary assessment of how the existing network may be
reconfigured, optimized, and expanded to control water losses and improve the quality of services. Priority interventions
were identified to address current hydraulic bottlenecks and optimize and increase the flexibility of the existing water
production capacity. Works are expected to start within the first year of the project and be completed before additional
production capacity is put online. The benefits of the interventions (in terms of reduction in losses, increase in hours of
service, and pressure) have been simulated using the i-TOC model and will be further refined with the hydraulic model.
66.    Sanitation investments were estimated based on the pre-feasibility study and a detailed field assessment of the
existing sewerage network and WWTPs. Given the extensive pollution levels from sewer leaks and untreated
wastewater, the focus is to fix the leakage and upgrade the connections and WWTPs to comply with the national
standards and international best practices on wastewater effluent quality. Priority sewer sheds were determined based
on the volume of wastewater generated and the risk to the surrounding and downstream communities. A detailed
assessment of the proposed solid waste RPEL site was conducted and formed the basis to assess the proposed area's
technical, environmental, and social feasibility. Pre-feasibility level cost estimates were prepared and checked by the
World Bank. The feasibility studies and detailed design of the priority sanitation investments will be undertaken during
the project's first year and tendered out for implementation from the second year onwards.
67.     A priority package of works comprising 42.5 percent of the total financing is expected to be committed in the
first 12 months after effectiveness as outlined in the 18-months procurement plan. These include the cholera response
package, planned for the first three months of project implementation, priority water supply network upgrades, and
sewerage works. The project is pursuing advanced procurement for priority design consultancies and cholera response
activities. Further, BWB is working on preparatory activities to enable access to the PBFM pertaining to the institutional
prerequisites, including the tariff framework. These will be complemented with expected commitments and
disbursements for TA and equipment, for which the terms of references (ToRs) have been prepared and reviewed by the
World Bank. A US$5 million package of emergency cholera response activities, comprising water supply interventions
(Component 1), awareness raising, and public sanitation facilities (Components 2 and 3), will be implemented in the first
four months of the project.

77.    Projects on International Waterways (OP 7.50) is applicable to this project. The project will finance activities that

international waterway. The exception to the riparian notification requirement, according to paragraph 7(a) of the Policy
applies because activities are limited to minor expansions and small-scale rehabilitation and upgrade of existing schemes
which aim to restore the capacity to the original design capacity. The relevant investments will not adversely change the
quality or quantity of water flows to the other riparians. The exception to the riparian notification requirement under
paragraph 7(b) also applies to project-financed feasibility studies involving international waterways. The terms of
reference for these studies will include an examination of any potential riparian issues. The exception to the notification
requirement was approved by the RVP on February 1, 2023.

78.    The project's environmental and social risks are rated Substantial. The project will be implemented in the
Blantyre metropolitan area by BWB and BCC through a PIU housed at BWB. The risk rating is based on the nature and
scope of the proposed works, the preliminary screening of associated environmental and social (E&S) risks and the
capacity of BWB to manage risks and apply the new World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) and relevant

       Water and Sanitation Project - I (P178954)



standards. BWB and BCC have demonstrated experience managing World Bank-financed projects. However, both
institutions have limited experience with the E&S requirements of the new ESF. These risks will be mitigated by equipping
the implementing and beneficiary entities with qualified E&S specialists who are trained to address E&S risks and impacts
that may materialize during project implementation. Further, E&S risks will be managed through the Environmental and
Social Commitment Plan (ESCP), which includes capacity building for the PIU, beneficiary entities, and service providers.
The ESCP was disclosed February 17, 2023 on the World Bank’s website and on January 9, 2023, on the BWB’s website.

79.     Project activities screening for potential E&S risks indicate that the project will have positive environmental and
social outcomes for the residents of the Blantyre Metropolitan Area. The main benefits arise from the provision of
potable water, improved sanitation, and solid waste. These activities are anticipated to generate significant adverse E&S
risk to the environment and people, and the following Environmental and Social Standards (ESS) under the World Bank
ESF: ESS1, ESS2, ESS3, ESS4, ESS5, ESS6, ESS8, and ESS10 apply. Though their footprint is considered localized with site-
specific, reversible, and manageable impacts, the magnitude is Substantial with medium to long-term effects. The
anticipated environmental impacts are related to loss of natural habitat, air quality, land, and noise pollution during
construction of the RPEL for urban wastes (including industrial and medical wastes), public toilets, water supply, and
sewerage networks; and the exposure to wastes and hazardous gasses, pollution of water streams and other habitats.
There are occupational health and safety (OHS)-related risks during the rehabilitation of water and sewerage networks,
including exposure to infectious wastes, asbestos-containing material, risks associated with excavations, trenches, and
injury from operating machines. There are also potential risks during the operation and maintenance of project-financed
infrastructure. These include the release of odors, contamination of water bodies and soils, and related eruption of
infectious diseases within the communities and among the project workers due to inappropriate handling of waste or
failed design, an accident, or a weather event. Climate change events such as flooding, droughts and cyclones may
exacerbate the risks. Though the project is not financing the rehabilitation of the Mudi dam, the Blantyre water supply
network relies on the dam for its resilience. The dam does not qualify as an associated facility, yet there are potential
impacts related to safety. The risks include downstream impacts on people and infrastructure, reduced spillway capacity
due to the inoperability of the gates, a lack of a reliable draw-down facility, and unavailability of an emergency
preparedness plan (EPP) and O&M plan. To mitigate these risks, a comprehensive assessment of the Mudi Dam will be
conducted, and priority dam safety measures will be developed and implemented under the project, including repairs
and dam safety plans (EPP and O&M plan). The ESCP has captured these obligations.
80.     Social risks and impacts that have been identified include physical and economic displacement leading to loss
of land and other assets. Physical and economic displacement is anticipated where the upgrade and expansion of the
water supply and sewer network and the proposed RPEL are concerned. Some sections of the existing water supply and
sewer mains are considerably encroached, with houses, shops, and markets constructed on the mains. Although pipe re-
routing will be explored, there is a likelihood of physical and economic displacement due to the nature of the city. There
are significantly built-up areas and limited space for pipe construction, particularly in the informal and unplanned
settlements, where utility services compete for space along the road margins. There is a risk of exposure to infectious
and hazardous waste to waste pickers, potentially worsening their health. Other anticipated social risks relate to labor
management and influx within beneficiary communities and will likely impact their social systems with an anticipated
increase in SEA/SH-GBV cases. Further, community and workers' health and safety are likely to be impacted due to
trenching during construction, traffic disruptions due to road closures or construction along the roads, noise and air
pollution concerns, and possible chance finds during excavations. Extensive community engagement will be required to
ensure community buy-in and resolve grievances related to construction activities and resettlement.
81.    Potential risks exist with regard to forced labor with allegations of forced labor risks associated with the polysilicon
suppliers for the solar PVs. Through a rigorous procurement process, BWB will undertake market analysis to identify
possible contractors of solar PVs, ensure solar PV bidders provide forced labor performance declarations which covers

82.     Gender-based violence. The GBV risk rating for the project is assessed as moderate. The project will develop a

manage those risks. Further, all environment and social documents shall integrate SEA/SH-GBV issues.
83.     Gender and social inclusion. There are three potential pathways for the project to contribute to enhanced gender
diversity; (i) Access to sanitation and MHM for schoolgirls, (ii) Reduction of time spent on accessing water by women to
maximize productivity, and (iii) professional development for female employees. BWB and BCC's decision is to pursue
professional development for female employees to reduce the gender gap in decision-making roles, harness the value of
diversity in service delivery and comply with the country's policy requirements on gender. The underrepresentation of
women in water employment, especially in management, is one of the key gender gaps identified in BWB and BCC.
Currently, only 10 percent of decision-making positions, including managerial and supervisory, are held by women, which
aligns with the current gender profile for public sector agencies in Malawi, despite the 60:40 ratio recommended by
Law.34 Labor force assessments in the past decade show persistent gender inequalities in labor force participation, with
women falling significantly behind men in accessing employment, particularly waged jobs.35 On average, in Malawi, more
men (12 percent) than women (4 percent) are salaried workers. 36 A common reason given by public institutions for why
gender inequity persists within ministries is that there is a lack of qualified women available.
84.     The project will address the gender gaps identified in BWB and BCC in two ways. First, through Component 3, the
project will incentivize BWB and finance its plan to improve female representation in decision roles through the PBFM
(PBC-5). Second, the project will support MoWS and BCC to provide targeted career and skills training on engineering and
leadership topics and mentorship opportunities to increase women's capacity in the sector. Their participation and
representation in water and decision-making will be promoted through training opportunities and leadership mentoring
(both technical and non-technical supervisory or managerial roles) informed by the gender assessment and skills gap
analysis to be developed in the first year of project implementation. Third, the project will promote management trainees
for female graduates, particularly in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM). Service providers under
the project will also be incentivized to engage women and youths in project design during the construction phase and
provide them with training opportunities. The results framework includes an intermediate results indicator as part of the
PBCs to incentivize women empowerment efforts and monitor the success of these actions. The indicator refers to the
percentage of staff in decision-making roles (supervisory or managerial) who are female. In addition, the project will
actively support girls' empowerment with access to improved sanitation and MHM in schools.

87.     Grievance Mechanism. The project will establish a grievance mechanism to address any concerns, disputes,

of these GRCs will take into consideration gender, youth, disadvantaged/vulnerable groups, and the representation of
the project-affected parties at the community level. The provision of the grievance mechanism has been stipulated in the
Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP), while anticipated grievances related to resettlement and project workers have been
further detailed in the Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF) and respective Resettlement Action Plans (RAPs), and the
Labour Management Procedures (LMP) respectively.

stakeholders, including government agencies, beneficiaries, civil society, the private sector, and donor partners, will be
undertaken throughout project implementation and operation. A budgeted SEP was prepared, and resources were
allocated to its activities within Components 1 and 2 of the project. The SEP was disclosed on January 25, 2023, on the

disclosed not later than one month after the grant's effectiveness, as outlined in the ESCP.

a result of Bank non-compliance with its policies and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution Service, which provides
communities and borrowers with the opportunity to address complaints through dispute resolution. Complaints may
be submitted to the AM at any time after concerns have been brought directly to the attention of Bank Management
and after Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For information on how to submit complaints to
the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS), please visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to
submit complaints to the Bank’s Accountability Mechanism, please visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.

VI.     KEY RISKS

92.     The overall risk of the project is considered Substantial. A brief explanation of the key substantial risks and
proposed mitigation measures is provided in the following paragraphs. Macroeconomic, fiduciary, environmental and
social, and Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability residual risks are substantial. Political and
governance, Sector Strategies and Policies, Technical Design of the Project, residual risks are assessed as moderate.
Residual stakeholders’ risk is assessed as low.
93.    Macroeconomic risk is Substantial. The residual macroeconomic risk results from high vulnerability to exogenous
shocks, including weather and health-related shocks, rising inflation, price shocks, and a deteriorating fiscal situation with
exchange rate instability and continued currency depreciation. Generally, the country's macroeconomic situation and
global market response to economic and health shocks remain unpredictable. Mitigation measures include limiting
counterpart financing to the minimum required, monitoring market prices and including price contingencies in the project
cost estimates and maximizing the use of locally available labor and products.
94.    Fiduciary risk is substantial. The FM arrangements for the project have been assessed as Satisfactory, and the
residual risk is Moderate. The procurement risk of the project is rated as Substantial. The key identified risks that could
lead to delays in project implementation and/or non-compliance, include: (i) limited experience of BWB and BCC in
implementing projects using World Bank's Procurement Regulations; (ii) contract management complexity including
limited experience in the management of E&S risks in works contracts; (iii) Supply chain disruptions due to the war in
Ukraine including increasing inflation, price fluctuations at the global level, and scarcity of forex, (iv) delays caused by
multiple layers of vetting contracts through the government systems. To mitigate these risks, the following measures will
be undertaken: (i) hire and maintain a procurement specialist with qualifications and experience acceptable to the World
Bank; (ii) provide procurement and contract management training to BWB and BCC project staff; (iii) advance

    comprising of rules, procedures and responsibilities, timeline, actions and decisions, sample documents and evaluation
    reports.
    95.     The environmental and social risk rating for the project is Substantial. The Environmental and Social Review
    Summary (A- ESRS) has been prepared and disclosed. Anticipated environmental risks relate to OHS during the

    96.      Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability – Substantial. BWB and BCC have limited experience

                            ANNEX 1: Implementation Arrangements and Support Plan

Fiduciary Arrangements
Financial Management
1.     The implementing entity will be the BWB. The financial management arrangements of the project will be
centralized at the BWB. BCC will only manage incremental operating costs. A financial management assessment
of BWB and BCC was undertaken to determine if the entities will ensure that (a) the funds will be used for the
purposes intended in an efficient and economical manner and the entity is capable of correctly and completely
recording all transactions and balances related to the project; (b) the project's financial reports will be prepared
in an accurate, reliable and timely manner; (c) the assets acquired under the project will be safely guarded, and
(d) the project will be subjected to auditing arrangements acceptable to the World Bank. The assessment
complied with the World Bank Directive: Financial Management in World Bank-Financed Operations and other
Operational Matters (Catalogue number OPCS5.05-DIR.147) Issued September 7, 2021, and effective from
September 7, 2021.
2.    Budget arrangement: The project will prepare an AWPB and submit to the World Bank for approval, based
on the project components, and applying the government budget calendar, which is also applicable to statement
of expenditures (SOEs). BWB is expected to link project cost elements to the general ledger codes in Sage 300
ERP. The AWPB performance will be monitored by comparing the budget versus actual costs through the
quarterly IFRs. The project will include in the IFRs explanations and mitigating measures for any significant
variance between the budget and the actual. The AWPB will be reviewed by the World Bank and other
stakeholders for the identification of the risks and recommend appropriate mitigating measures.
3.     Accounting arrangement: The Public Finance Act of 2022, the Financial Regulations thereof, and other
circulars issued by MoF from time-to-time guide government financial management procedures. The financial
regulations and circulars are being revised to align them with the new Public Finance Management Act (PFMA).
The accounting policies and procedures for BWB are in compliance with the PFM and related regulations. The
project will use BWB systems for accounting and reporting. The assets acquired through the project funds will
be recorded in the accounting system including in the fixed assets register. The project will prepare a financial
procedure manual satisfactory to the World Bank. Adequate staffing arrangements are in place at BWB. Names
and qualifications of the individuals that will be assigned to the project FM will be shared with the World Bank.
4.     Internal controls: Adequate internal control system is in place. The internal controls allow for the
segregation of duties for the initiation, approval and authorization of project expenditures. The internal controls
system is adequate and can safeguard the project assets. The entities will prepare and document the internal
control system in the project's Financial Procedure Manual (FPM) in line with the public finance regulations of
the GoM, and acceptable to the World Bank. The FPM will be part of the PIM. The internal audit department of
BWB and BCC will be responsible for the internal audit activities of project. The internal auditors will develop
risk based internal audit annual work program and submit to the project management for approval. The work
program will be shared with the World Bank. The internal audit will be carried out on quarterly basis and biannual
internal audit reports submitted to the World Bank 45 days after the end of each semester. Also, the internal
auditors will submit the internal audit reports to the Audit Committee of the institutions.
5.   Financial reporting: Adequate financial reporting arrangements are in place for both institutions. For their
normal operations, monthly and quarterly financial management reports are prepared and discussed by

20. Contract strategy. Goods, services, and civil works are packaged in economical packages to attract local
and foreign bidders who are qualified and can offer good prices and complete contracts within the stipulated
time frame, resulting in value for money. Packaging for procurement is decided in such a way that encourages
adequate participation and is based on two principal forms of procurement packaging: (a) the grouping (or
bulking) of procurement activity within a procurement category for acquiring them under a single contract and
(b) the division of one activity into multiple lots, where bidders can submit bids for one, several, or all lots (as
would be stipulated in the procurement documents) and where a contract could be awarded for each lot or
various lots when the awarded meets the qualifications requirements.
21.     The following have been considered for procurement packaging:
        a. The likelihood of local suppliers being able to fulfill the requirements and if the packaging would limit
           their participation.
        b. If the requirements are needed (or can be received) simultaneously or there are different delivery
           dates between requirements. Unless the selected supplier can deliver at different intervals, receiving
           all the goods at one time could result in a potential logistical problem; therefore, under these
           circumstances, it may be preferable not to package the various requirements.
        c. The availability of several suppliers that can provide a combination of procurement categories as may
           be required in the case of supply, installation, commissioning, and training.
        d. The implementing agency's capacity to coordinate several suppliers. If the implementing agency’s
           capacity is limited, this may create a preference for packaging to reduce the number of suppliers that
           the PIU would have to coordinate with.

                                      ANNEX 2: Detailed Project Description

Component 1 - Water supply improvements

Introduction

1.     Under Component 1, the project will finance investments to improve water production, stabilize and
improve network operational efficiency, reduce water losses, improve water supply service quality, and expand
water access to unserved areas. Priority water supply interventions were determined with the aid of i-TOC, which
determines the main efficiency-yielding interventions based on a cost-benefit analysis and the optimum leakage
level under intermittent conditions. Key interventions include a combination of production, transmission, and
distribution upgrades to eliminate hydraulic bottlenecks, enhance pressure management and recover leakage.
The i-TOC preliminary assessment confirmed that, with a return period of fewer than six years, a substantial
investment to reduce leakage is economically worthwhile and is key to inverting the current negative financial
situation of BWB. The target economic leakage level for a continuous supply condition is 35 percent. This would
yield a predicted saving of 10 million m3/year or 27,000 m3/d.

2.      Priority transmission and distribution network upgrade. The main objective of these interventions is to
eliminate the hydraulic bottlenecks to ensure that the water produced reaches the service reservoirs, achieves
a more even distribution of the pressure throughout the network, and reaches the customer. A preliminary
assessment of the network interventions was done using the existing hydraulic model developed six years ago.
Despite not being fully calibrated and inappropriate allocation of the demand, the model allows for an initial
estimate of the interventions to stabilize the network. The network upgrades include (i) the replacement of
266km of distribution pipes in priority supply areas in the Soche and Kabula zones, (ii) the construction of five
new reservoirs at Chileka, Chiriri, Ndirande, Nyambadwe, and Mudi to ensure a minimum 18 hours of storage,
(iii) construction of 26km of new transmission mains, and (iv) upgrade of eight pumping stations.

3.     Permanent pressure and leakage control system. BWB has pre-established 131 DMAs, under 18 pressure
zones, as part of their efforts to control pressures. However, the system still faces significant operational
challenges due to inadequate configuration, pipe aging, and lack of pressure control instruments. Under the
project, a permanent pressure and leakage control system will be installed. A preliminary assessment, based on
the existing model and pressure zones, indicates an estimated 32 pressure-reducing valves (PRVs), 70 valves, 18
flow meters, and 6.6 km of pipe upgrade required to fully establish the pressure and leakage control system. A
detailed assessment will be conducted using a calibrated hydraulic model. With a fully operational pressure and
leakage control system, new bursts and leakages can be more easily identified and recovered. The total
estimated cost to operationalize the pressure zones, including contingency, is US$2.4 million.

4.    Treatment Works Upgrade. The project will finance the upgrade of Mudi WTP to increase the water
supply system's resilience and ensure supply continuity during Walker’s Ferry operational breakdown. Although
BWB claims a design capacity of 45,000m3/d, the JICA assessment indicates an estimated capacity of
approximately 17,000m3/d based on the surface area of the sedimentation tanks. Further, a yield analysis for
the Mudi Dam indicates a maximum yield of 13,000m3/d. The project interventions include the (i) review and
update of the treatment process based on raw water quality and supply requirements and the construction of

    The World Bank

an upgraded water treatment plan. The costs are estimated at US$12.5 million, which will be confirmed at the
detailed design stage.

5.     Network Expansion. This sub-component will finance the expansion of the distribution to increase
coverage of improved water services. Investments will include the construction of approximately 40 km of
distribution pipelines and connecting approximately 19,700 new household connections, constructing one new
reservoir and transmission to Chilimoni, and 60 communal water points. However, network expansion should
carefully consider water availability from improvements in the efficiency of existing treatment plants, savings
from physical water loss reduction activities, construction of a new treatment plant, or a combination of these
three. The proposed network expansion areas include Njuli, Chileka, Chikuli, Mpemba, and Matindi.

Component 2 - Priority Sanitation Investments

6.     This component will finance the most critical investments needed to reduce environmental pollution and
health risks as a result of the current dysfunctional state of the sewerage and solid waste systems. The estimated
cost of these interventions is US$32.18 million based on available pre-feasibility assessments and projections
considering other similar recent and ongoing projects in Malawi and the region. Given the limited financing, a
multi-criteria analysis was conducted to prioritize interventions based on coverage, load, environmental
pollution, and benefits and health risks. The upgrades are expected to bring the priority infrastructure back into
full operational capacity and to enable BCC and service delivery partners to operate and maintain the systems
and services sustainably. Network expansion will be limited to the treatment capacity in the existing plants.

7.    Priority sewerage network and treatment upgrade. The project will prioritize the rehabilitation and
upgrade of Blantyre and Soche WWTPs, and depending on the availability of resources, some additional
upgrades in the Chirimba plant. A field assessment indicates that two plants received a combined flow of
11,200m3/d against a design capacity of 18,400m3/d. Blantyre WWTP serves a catchment area of 228 km2
comprising the Blantyre city residential, commercial and civic, and industrial properties only to the north of the
Soche Catchment, while Soche WWTP serves a catchment area of 24 km2 comprising the southwest residential
areas of the city including the main central hospital. Both plants rely on biological filters. Unlike Blantyre WWTP,
which includes aerated lagoons for handling septage from vacuum tankers, Soche does not have septage
treatment capacity. However, the plant is still loaded with septage from vacuum tankers. Both plants have been
extensively dilapidated, and the majority of the mechanical and electrical equipment is damaged, vandalized, or
stolen.

8.     Priority works were identified during the project preparation based on the pre-feasibility analysis.
However, the exact scope of all treatment plants' rehabilitation/ expansion will be further determined after a
detailed feasibility study is carried out during the first year of project implementation. The project will prioritize
the rehabilitation of the plants to bring them into operation and to ensure the capacity will be around the
combined design capacity of 14,325 m3/day. The project will also finance a methane capture and reuse system
to reduce emissions from the WWTPs. The two sewage works targeted incorporate preliminary treatment
(screening and grit removal), primary sedimentation, biological filters, and bio-digesters for the treatment of
sludge from primary settling tanks and humus sludge from biological filters. Methane gas generated from the
bio-digesters will be captured through the installation of covers and an extraction system.

9.    The methane gas will either be flared through burning to convert it to carbon dioxide, which has a low
impact, or reused to fire gas generators which could potentially reduce the energy consumption at the sewage

improve the quality of the effluent that will be discharged into the environment. A detailed estimate of the
sewerage network upgrades is presented in the project files.

Priority Solid Waste Management Interventions

10. The project will finance TA, equipment, and tools to improve solid waste sorting and collection at source
and the construction of a new solid waste RPEL in Chigumula, aiming at maximizing waste reuse and minimizing
emissions from uncontrolled solid waste dumping. A detailed assessment of the site, including a geological
survey, was conducted in 2004. Preliminary field analysis indicates that the location is appropriate for a landfill.
However, a full environmental and social assessment will need to be undertaken to fully determine the site's
feasibility. Given the location of the proposed Chigumula RPEL, about 20km from Blantyre City Center, some
decentralized waste processing stations might be required to manage the loads and ensure efficient transport
of the waste.

11. The design of the landfill component of the RPEL uses aerobic composting to reduce methane gas
generation and emission. Aerobic composting will be achieved by installing an appropriate natural ventilation or
air supply system, which is expected to reduce methane production by over 70 percent. A gas extraction system
will be installed for the limited methane gas produced, particularly at night when aeration may be low. The
methane gas will be flared or burnt to convert it to carbon dioxide or used to incinerate part of the solid waste.
A leachate collection and treatment system will be installed to reduce groundwater pollution. Trees with high
uptake of heavy metals and other toxic elements will be planted around and on the RPEL after use.

Public sanitation interventions (US$2 million).

12. The project will finance the construction of 20 public sanitation facilities (ten for schools' sanitation, five
for health centers, and five for markets). The designs will include hand-washing facilities, accessibility features
for people with disabilities and MHM requirements for schoolgirls and female marketers. An assessment done
by the Basic Education Administrators in all the 62 primary schools within Blantyre revealed that ten schools had
serious sanitation challenges that required urgent attention. The toilet-pupil ratio is 1:300 in most schools,
against a national standard of 1:30 for boys and 1:25 for girls. The school sanitation interventions will be
supported by training on hygiene and MHM to enhance the retention of girls in schools. Similarly, a shortage of
sanitation facilities at markets and health centers results in the spread of fecal-matter-related diseases such as
cholera and environmental contamination. In order to improve sanitation services in public markets, the project
will finance the construction of five toilets at priority markets and five toilets in priority health centers. BCC will
engage small-scale private operators to operate and maintain the market facilities at a user fee.

13. The project will finance a sanitation marketing and hygiene promotion campaign to influence sanitation
behavior change and create demand for improved sanitation services (sewer connections, Fecal Sludge

Component 3 - Institutional Capacity Strengthening
14. This component will finance a set of institutional development activities aimed at enhancing the
institutional capacity of BWB, BCC, and MoWS. The institutional strengthening interventions will complement
the infrastructure investments to enhance service delivery capacity and sustainability of the project investments.
Financing for the BWB's institutional strengthening activities will be done through the PBFM, to incentivize
critical reforms needed to improve the operational and financial performance of the utility.

BWB institutional development
15. An assessment of BWB institutional capacity was conducted in early 2022 using the Water Utility Maturity
(WUM) model, covering five dimensions of utility operations, namely, behavior, structure, capability, tools, and
influence. BWB maturity was assessed as level 3 (proactive level of maturity) of the WUM model, meaning that
it has the basic behavioral orientations, structures, capabilities, and tools required for a utility to perform; and
is proactively engaging with its operating environment. However, several gaps have been identified, including (i)
high operational inefficiencies driven by aging infrastructure, hydraulic bottlenecks, and lack of operational
procedures, (ii) poor financial performance resulting from low tariffs, high administrative costs, and poor
governance, (iii) low production and distribution infrastructure capacity, (iv) inadequate systems and tools to
ensure efficient management of its administrative and operational needs, and (v) low staff productivity. In the
current scenario, a traditional approach of input-based financing for capacity building is likely to yield limited
results given the required structural changes to organizational culture across the Board. A PBFM scheme will
provide financing to enable BWB to deliver service improvements and increase its ability to gradually collect and
recover from the current downward performance trajectory.

16. The PBFM is built from the ICSAP developed by BWB through a company-wide consultative process to
comprehensively address the issues identified by the maturity assessment. The plan was also consulted upon
and endorsed by the workers union, who have committed to supporting BWB management in its
implementation and in meeting the targets. The plan is estimated at U$30 million, with US$27 million financed
through the project and the remainder by BWB and other partners (Waterworks and JICA) through targeted TA.
The project will incentivize priority actions under the ICSAP with a direct impact on the project results and
sustainability of the planned service improvements. The key performance indicators, targets, and financing
under the ICSAP are described in the project files. The PBC will be subject to one independent verification per

17. BCC Institutional Development Support (US$1.75 million). To pave the way to gradually address the
sanitation sub-sector challenges in Blantyre, the project will support (i) the development and update of priority
municipal policies and regulations for sanitation, (ii) training, and capacity building in sewerage and solid waste

18.     Sector Institutional Development Support (US$5.46 million). In line with its commitment to increase
access to water and sanitation, the GoM has recently established the MoWS, which oversees the water sector.

lack of a water resources management strategy with a clear roadmap of reforms and investments to increase
water availability, (ii) an outdated water and sanitation investment plan, lacking a comprehensive and inclusive
set of interventions to close the coverage gap, (iii) inadequate investment planning and implementation
capacity, (iv) limited sector financing, and (v) the lack of a regulatory body and accountability mechanisms that
enable citizen engagement. The project will finance policy and planning instruments, including the feasibility
assessments and engineering design of priority infrastructure investments for selected Boards, training, and
equipment to enhance the sector coordination and technical support to the water boards. The project will also
support the development of a water and sanitation sector regulatory function and the establishment of a ring-
fenced water tariff framework. The project will also support stakeholder engagements through WSTF to facilitate
the sewerage transfer dialogue and ensure the financial sustainability of sewerage services.
