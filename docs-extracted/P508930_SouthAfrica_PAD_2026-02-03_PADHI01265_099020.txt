 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT


 A. Country Context

1.      South Africa (SA) has ambitious development goals, but slow growth and weak job creation continue to hold
back progress toward shared prosperity. Economic growth averaged just 0.7 percent over the past decade.
Unemployment exceeds 30 percent, with 46 percent of 15–34-year-olds unemployed. After earlier gains, poverty has risen
since 2011 and now affects 68 percent of the population at the US$8.3 upper-middle-income poverty line. Investment has
averaged about 15 percent of gross domestic product (GDP) over the last decade—roughly half the upper-middle-income
country average—constraining growth and contributing to a widening infrastructure gap.
2.       Deteriorating infrastructure is a central barrier to unlocking growth and jobs. Two reinforcing dynamics—
declining performance and declining investment—have pushed the electricity, freight logistics, and water sectors into
significant strain. Power shortages, rail and port inefficiencies, and unreliable water services raise firm costs, reduce
productivity, and undermine competitiveness, with disproportionate impacts on small firms and low-income households
that lack alternatives (for example, rooftop solar, generators, reliable private transport). Climate change further pressures
already aging and overstretched systems.
3.       SA has deep financial markets, with institutional investors holding assets equivalent to roughly 160 percent of
GDP, yet they do not efficiently allocate long-term finance to infrastructure without significant government guarantees.
Private participation remains limited, with many projects requiring 100 percent government guarantees. Yet,
infrastructure needs are vast—estimated at US$254–329 billion between 2022 and 2030. Given constrained fiscal space,
the public sector cannot meet these needs alone. Infrastructure-related guarantees already amount to almost 9 percent
of GDP in 2024/25, underscoring the urgency of market-based de-risking that mobilizes private capital without further
increasing contingent liabilities.
4.       Weaknesses in the enabling and regulatory environment—especially in state-owned enterprise (SOE)-
dominated network sectors—significantly constrain investment. Limited competition, operational inefficiencies,
governance gaps, and financial fragility among SOEs and municipalities undermine performance in electricity, transport,
and water. These shortcomings are rooted in unclear sector policies, weak and fragmented regulatory frameworks, and
inefficient institutional processes. Poor creditworthiness and gaps in the sector-regulatory architecture further deter
private investment, even as the Government begins opening infrastructure sectors to greater private involvement.
5.      These constraints are compounded by a thin and underprepared pipeline of bankable projects. Many sponsors—
national departments, municipalities, and SOEs—lack the institutional capacity, technical skills, and familiarity with
appraisal frameworks needed to develop high-quality, investment-ready proposals. In combination with an imperfect
enabling environment and underperforming foundational infrastructure, this weak pipeline continues to constrain
investment and, ultimately, SA’s ability to generate higher growth and better jobs, despite ample liquidity in the financial
system.
6.      The infrastructure bottlenecks reflect structural and sectoral inefficiencies—most notably weak SOEs,
fragmented governance and regulatory processes, and suboptimal use of public funds for private capital mobilization
(PCM). SOEs dominate critical sectors, yet unclear policies and fragile regulatory settings constrain their ability to prepare
or procure viable projects. The resulting bottlenecks in energy, transport, and water impose economy-wide costs
estimated at 2–3 percent of GDP annually, while private capital remains on the sidelines despite strong underlying project
economics. This has forced the Government to finance most infrastructure on its own balance sheet and to backstop SOE
obligations.

7.        SA is committed to a just energy transition toward a low-carbon, climate-resilient economy. The Just Energy
Transition Investment Plan and Just Transition Framework identify investment needs of approximately US$100 billion for
2023–2027, aligned with the updated Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC). Given constrained fiscal and debt
conditions and elevated policy/regulatory risks, private finance has not filled the gap. SA’s Renewable Energy Independent
Power Producer Procurement Programme (REIPPPP) mobilized private investment at scale with National Treasury (NT)
guarantees to backstop Eskom offtake risk, but this has expanded contingent liabilities to ZAR 650 billion (about US$36
billion) in FY2024/25—roughly 90 percent in the electricity sector—creating fiscal sustainability concerns. Bridging the gap
will require coordinated public and private financing alongside reforms to reduce perceived risks.
8.       Substantial infrastructure investment is also critical for competitiveness. Infrastructure has forward linkages to
green industries and backward linkages to renewable energy equipment manufacturing and critical minerals—vital for a
just transition, industrial competitiveness, and jobs. The investment backlog raises industry costs, while potential trade
disruptions with the United States and the European Union’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism pose additional
challenges to the trade competitiveness of South African industries.

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

9.      SA’s infrastructure bottlenecks stem from structural and sectoral inefficiencies. This includes weak SOE
performance, fragmented governance and processes, and limited use of public resources to mobilize private capital. SOEs
dominate energy, transport, and water but face operational inefficiencies due to unclear sector policies, weak regulation,
poor governance, and financial stress. A thin pipeline reflects weak project readiness and sponsor capacity (departments,
municipalities, and SOEs) to prepare robust proposals and navigate appraisal. Poor creditworthiness of
SOEs/municipalities, inadequate regulatory architecture, and inefficient processes deter private participation. Despite a
sophisticated domestic financial sector capable of mobilizing capital and mitigating foreign exchange risk, infrastructure
exposure remains below potential due to sector-specific shortcomings and delivery bottlenecks.
10.     To address these constraints and build an investable pipeline, the Government is streamlining infrastructure
delivery and advancing sector reforms through Operation Vulindlela, focusing on electricity, water, and freight
transport for their growth impact. Sectoral reforms focus on improving governance and structural inefficiencies and
introducing markets and competition, aiming to enhance sector financial sustainability. Infrastructure delivery system
reforms include modernizing public-private partnership (PPP) regulations (national and provincial, with municipal reforms
under way); rationalizing institutions; and strengthening core functions: harmonized project prioritization, consolidated
preparation via a new Infrastructure Financing and Implementation Support Agency (IFISA), which will become operational
by April 1, 2026, and faster, centralized appraisal through a reformed Budget Facility for Infrastructure (BFI) to improve
project quality, bankability, and agility, and crowd in private capital.
11.     SA’s electricity sector is shifting toward greater competition and financial sustainability to support energy
security and decarbonization. The system’s reliance on aging coal plants constrains reliability and emissions reduction.
Key reforms include the Electricity Regulation Amendment Act (ERAA) (effective January 2025) and Eskom’s unbundling
with the National Transmission Company of South Africa (NTCSA) (July 2024). The National Energy Regulator of South
Africa (NERSA) has approved regulations to enable private participation, and a wholesale market is planned for 2026.
Private investment has already flowed into generation and storage: REIPPPP and Battery Energy Storage Independent
Power Producer Procurement Program (BESIPPPP) mobilized about US$16 billion through 2024, adding 8.3 GW of
renewables and 1.7 GW of storage under sovereign guarantees. The Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) 2025 projects 33 GW
of new capacity by 2030 (27 GW from renewables and storage). South Africa’s Climate Investment Fund (CIF) Accelerating
Coal Transition Investment Plan carries a total financing envelope of US$2.6 billion (US$500 million from CIF in
concessional/grant financing), leveraging multilateral development banks (MDBs) and private capital.

12.      Transmission is the principal bottleneck to scaling renewables, with congestion—especially north-south—
limiting connections needed ahead of coal decommissioning. The Transmission Development Plan (TDP) 2024 estimates
about US$25 billion in investment by 2034, to be financed via Eskom/NTCSA balance sheets and independent transmission
projects (ITPs). To bolster the bankability of ITPs, the framework draws on ERAA principles, the 2025 Transmission
Infrastructure Regulations of the Department of Electricity and Energy (DEE), NERSA’s forthcoming Cost Recovery Rules,
and standardized contracts. Given NTCSA’s nascent credit profile and the phase-out of sovereign guarantees, additional
credit enhancement is likely required to attract private investment.
13.      In addition, growing investment needs in generation, distribution, and storage infrastructure for the energy
transition require private sector participation. The IRP 2025 aims to add, for the next five years, 27 GW of renewable
energy and storage capacity, for which PCM will account for a substantial portion, but without proper mitigation of
NTCSA’s offtake risk, the market appetite may be constrained. South Africa is also taking forward a reform agenda in the
distribution sector, which is exposed to increasing distributed renewable energy and operational inefficiency, with
substantial investment needs. Risk mitigation measures for creditworthiness of distribution companies are critical to
mobilize private capital to fill the financing gap in the distribution sector.
14.      Similar to the electricity sector, the transport and logistics sector, dominated by the Transnet SOE, has also been
undergoing changes prompted by poor performance and legacy issues that have hurt the economy. South Africa’s freight
logistics system—centered on Transnet’s state-owned rail and port operations—has struggled with declining performance
that has shifted freight from rail to costlier, more polluting roads, undermining export competitiveness. Aggregate rail
volumes fell from 226 million tons in 2017/18 to 151 million tons in 2023/24, with export coal volumes at their lowest
since 1993 and iron ore at a decade low. Container terminals in Durban and Ngqura ranked among the worst globally,
while Transnet’s financial health deteriorated to a net loss of about US$500 million in 2021. The failures are attributed to
underinvestment, poor maintenance, theft and vandalism, and operational inefficiencies, costing the economy an
estimated 7.4 percent of GDP in 2022.
15.       In response, the Government adopted a Freight Logistics Reform Roadmap in December 2023, supported by the
World Bank, to unbundle Transnet and strengthen regulation. The Transport Economic Regulator Act (June 2024) and an
official rail network statement (December 2024) established a framework for competitive, non-discriminatory access, with
the transport economic regulator overseeing slot allocation and pricing, access and compliance, and dispute resolution.
By September 2025, 11 private operators were granted rail slots, and Requests for Information (RFIs) were issued for five
PPP concessions for main lines and the Durban and Cape Town ports. Transnet received ZAR 98 billion (about US$6 billion)
in rescue financing in 2024/25, conditional on improvements in balance sheet strength, operational efficiency, workforce
engagement, and governance.
16.      Looking ahead, restoring rail and port performance to 250 million tons per year will require US$10–12 billion—
funding needs that cannot be met by the public sector alone. Mobilizing private capital at scale is therefore essential to
clear maintenance backlogs, renew and maintain rolling stock, and protect assets against theft and vandalism, with priority
on security‑critical corridors. To crowd in private investment, the Government must strengthen delivery capacity;
streamline procurement to reduce delays and risk; and break the vicious cycle in which underinvestment reduces
availability, erodes revenues, and further constrains investment. Well-structured PPPs, transparent tariff and access
regimes, and bankable concession frameworks will be key to aligning risk‑return profiles and unlocking long‑term
financing.
17.     The water supply and sanitation sector is undergoing significant reforms to enable private sector participation
amid severe service gaps. Currently, 46 percent of households lack safely managed water supply and 49 percent lack
safely managed sanitation, with the most acute needs in informal settlements and rural areas. Non-revenue water loss
stands at 41 percent—both technical and financial—costing municipalities about ZAR 10 billion annually. These
vulnerabilities contributed to Cape Town’s ‘Day Zero’ crisis (2015–2018), when drought, underfunded and aging

infrastructure, high consumption, and climate change combined to reduce water levels, causing an estimated economic
impact of 3.4 percent of provincial GDP and 37,000 jobs lost.
18.      To address these challenges and mobilize private capital, the Government has advanced several critical reforms.
It established the National Water Infrastructure Agency by consolidating the Water Trading Agency and the Trans-Caledon
Tunnel Authority—South Africa’s main water financing body—to drive PPPs for large water infrastructure. The Water
Services Amendment Bill, brought on October 3, 2025, before Parliament, requires all water service providers to be
registered and licensed, including private operators. Additionally, the Department of Water and Sanitation created the
Water Partnerships Office in 2023 to promote PPPs across programs such as non-revenue water reduction, wastewater
reuse, and desalination. The Government of South Africa (GSA) has taken several steps to support a more conducive
environment and a systematic review of the infrastructure delivery landscape.
19.      SA’s financial sector is large and sophisticated—around 300 percent of GDP—with banks at 118 percent and
institutional investors at roughly 160 percent (pension funds approximately 92 percent and insurance approximately 68
percent), yet institutional investors allocate only about 5–7 percent of assets to domestic infrastructure, far below peer
exposures of roughly 35 percent, reflecting a scarcity of long-term, investable domestic assets and a preference for
government debt, listed equities, and foreign holdings. To unlock more local capital, the NT has advanced enabling
policies: Regulation 28 was amended to permit up to 45 percent of institutional portfolios in infrastructure, and tax
incentives are under consideration for vehicles such as infrastructure debt funds and infrastructure Real Estate Investment
Trusts (REITs).
20.      Sector reforms must be complemented by credible de-risking solutions until sector and SOE reforms build a
strong performance track record. Today, PCM at scale for infrastructure requires market instruments to mitigate and
price risk, to move away from existing practices of cost-free sovereign guarantees, which concentrate financing in
programs like REIPPPP and strain fiscal and contingent liabilities. The Government is exploring market-based de-risking—
potentially via specialized credit-guarantee vehicles drawing on global experience—to crowd in institutional investors, and
with an estimated US$573 billion in institutional assets, a 1 percentage point reallocation to infrastructure could mobilize
about US$5 billion.

    C. Relevance to Higher Level Objectives

21.     The GSA has targeted investment of 30 percent of GDP according to its National Development Plan and National
Infrastructure Plan (2022). However, total investment spending has fallen from nearly 30 percent of GDP in the early
1980s to approximately 15 percent since 2022 (of which 4.1 percent public sector investment and 10.8 percent is private
sector investment). The GSA, through Operation Vulindlela, has prioritized reforms in the electricity, water, and freight
transport sectors, because of their potentially high impact on economic growth (Operation Vulindlela 20201; State of
Nation 20222). The Minister of Finance prioritized the need for mobilizing private capital, including mobilizing institutional
investors for infrastructure finance (Budget Speech on March 12, 2025; Medium-Term Budget Policy Statement Speech
November 2025).
22.      The GSA has tightened its emission targets and expanded its adaptation goals in its updated NDC (2025). The
NDC 2025 is shaped by the Climate Change Act of 2024 and has sharpened its emission targets to be in a the range of 320
to 380 MtCO2eq in 2035, and net zero by 2050. Currently, carbon emissions in SA are 519 MtCO 2e. It also stated that
resilient infrastructure is an important national adaptation goal. The IRP 2025 plays an important role as it outlines the
transition to cleaner energy with less coal and more renewable energy.

    D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach

(i) Rationale for Using MPA
24.      The Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) delivers quick wins while laying the groundwork for deeper
reforms. An early priority is the establishment of a Credit Guarantee Vehicle (CGV) to de-risk transactions, reduce
perceived credit risks, and crowd in private capital—particularly for new models such as the independent transmission
program. Subsequent phases target enhancement of a viability gap fund (VGF) to bridge the affordability gap and co-
investment platform to mobilize domestic and global institutional investors. This will be done while advancing
complementary, medium-term reforms, including updates to the PPP law, clearer institutional mandates, a consolidated
infrastructure-financing agency, strengthened project preparation, an expanded project pipeline, and scaled risk
mitigation instruments. The sequencing is intentionally logical and adaptive, enabling early results that signal credibility
to markets.
25.     The MPA is designed to tackle SA’s systemic infrastructure-finance weaknesses through sequenced structural
reforms and targeted financial innovation. It enables near-term actions—building on Development Policy Loan (DPL)-
supported reforms—while supporting a phased transformation of the infrastructure ecosystem, avoiding an
unmanageable ‘big-bang’ approach. By allowing the GSA to borrow in phases, the MPA accommodates the long-term
nature of the infrastructure finance agenda without exerting undue pressure to rapidly utilize the facility; notably, it
mitigates the risk of paying commitment fees on unused balances after four years by aligning disbursements with
readiness and need.
26.      A stand-alone operation would be insufficient for challenges rooted in regulatory, institutional, and market
failures. The MPA’s phased design provides a structured yet flexible framework for iterative learning, adaptation, and
course correction. By structuring interventions in phases, the Program incorporates feedback, responds to unforeseen
challenges, and makes course corrections as needed—reducing implementation risk and the chance of large-scale failure.
Each phase builds on the successes and lessons of the previous one, ensuring reforms and investments are sequenced to
maximize impact, sustainability, and ultimately deliver more transformative outcomes.

(iv) Program Framework
29.      The MPA Program Framework consists of four phases implemented over a 10-year period, built on a sequenced,
multiphase approach that addresses South Africa’s infrastructure-finance challenges through targeted reforms and
financial innovation. The framework explicitly treats Private Capital Enabling (PCE) and PCM as two sides of the same
coin—advancing upstream PCE for both the infrastructure (that is, energy, transport, and so on) and domestic financial
sector (regulations to encourage long-term financing) through Development Policy Operations, advisory, institutional
strengthening, and market infrastructure while deploying PCM instruments such as guarantees, viability gap funding and
co-investment platforms in different modalities of risk sharing. These parallel tracks ensure quick wins, continuous
learning, and adaptation while laying the groundwork for deeper sectoral transformation. Through these coordinated and
overlapping activities, the Program creates a credible pathway for large-scale private and public investment in resilient
infrastructure, with measurable outcomes tracked through key indicators.

    II. PROJECT DESCRIPTION
    A. Project Development Objective (PDO)
       (i) PDO Statement
33.    The PDO of the first phase of the MPA is to establish a Credit Guarantee Vehicle to mobilize capital for
investment in resilient infrastructure. The first phase of the MPA will establish a CGV as the first instrument of the Blended
Finance Platform to mobilize capital including private capital, public capital, capital from development partners, and
concessional resources for investment in critical and resilient infrastructure such as electricity, transport and logistics, and
water and sanitation sectors, to create jobs, reduce carbon emissions, and deliver infrastructure services for citizens and
businesses.
          (ii) PDO Level Indicators
34.       The success of the first phase of the Program will be measured through three PDO-level indicators.
          (a)   The first PDO-level indicator is the amount of capital mobilized for resilient infrastructure finance. The
                baseline is zero and the end target is US$4.5 billion (after five years), of which US$3.5 private capital
                mobilized and US$1 billion capital mobilized from development partners.
          (b)   The second PDO-level indicator is the number of direct and indirect jobs created within the scope of the
                resilient infrastructure (related) projects. The baseline is zero and the end target is 218,890 (after five years).
          (c)   The third PDO-level indicator is the amount of GHG emissions reduced. The baseline is zero and the end
                target is 14.95 MtCO2e per year (after five years).

    B. Project Components

35.       The project will have three components.
36.      Component 1: Capitalization of the Credit Guarantee Vehicle (US$368.82 million in total, of which US$338.82
from IBRD and US$30 million financing gap). This component will fund the GSA equity participation via the NT in the CGV
in three tranches: (a) US$5 million in start-up capital, (b) US$95 million for initial capitalization, and (c) US$268.82 million
for scale-up capitalization7. The initial CGV’s capital structure is set at US$500 million and will be as follows: (a) a tier of
junior capital (first loss) for up to 40 percent of the total capital of the CGV (US$200 million), (b) a tier of senior capital for
up to 30 percent of the total (up to US$150 million), and (c) a tier of callable capital for up to 30 percent (up to US$150
million). The amount of US$100 million of the IBRD loan will be used for the GSA’s initial equity participation in the CGV
and will cover the junior capital tier (first loss). NT plans to seek US$ 30 million of additional concessional financing from
the Clean Technology Fund to close the financing gap for the junior capital tranche of the CGV. The remaining part of
US$300 million of the initial capital is expected to be subscribed by domestic, regional, and international DFIs, specialized
development funds, and philanthropic funds. The US$5 million start-up cost of the CGV allows for the operationalization
of the CGV and project preparation. As of January 2026, it is expected that a group of development partners will participate
in the equity for an indicative conditional amount of US$460 million which is more than expected. The operationalization
includes hiring and developing a professional management team, developing a business plan, establishing a governance
structure, and defining an operating model. Project pipeline development includes final and legal due diligence part of the
project preparation. The proposed capital structure may be revised based on commitments from development partners
and project pipeline readiness.

 C. Project Beneficiaries

43.     The direct project beneficiaries are project sponsors (equity providers) and commercial financiers through the
special purpose vehicles (SPVs) for approximately twelve infrastructure projects. The CGV will issue priced guarantees
that will de-risk critical project-finance risks—beginning with public off taker risk—that commercial lenders and bond
investors are unwilling to assume, and which currently constrain private capital mobilization.
44.      The ultimate beneficiaries from the project are the end users of infrastructure services. The CGV allows PCM
from project sponsors and commercial lenders, as they would otherwise be reluctant to finance infrastructure projects.
The delivery of infrastructure projects will allow citizens and businesses—the ultimate beneficiaries—to make use of
infrastructure services.

 D. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners

45.      The project benefits from the One WBG Approach. The integrated deployment of IBRD, IFC, and MIGA
instruments enables risk allocation across institutional balance sheets in ways that no single instrument could achieve
independently. IFC and MIGA involvement also gives confidence (due diligence, reputation) to crowd in other local and
international investors. The project benefits directly from IBRD’s financial and technical capabilities of different global
practices, IFC’s financial and technical support, including equity participation and the catalyzation of other investors, as
well as MIGA’s ability to share risks of the CGV and create further leverage, and provide technical underwriting capacity.
The technical design of the CGV has been developed by IBRD in close collaboration with IFC and MIGA through the Joint
Capital Markets Program/Sustainable Finance Facility (JCAP/SFF) funded by the State Secretariat for Economic Affairs
(SECO). It supported the development of the CGV’s institutional capacity, by conducting legal and financial due diligence,
and the development of a business plan, providing a clear framework for its operations. It is also supporting the
development of the project pipeline, including the bankability of ITPs. Further work is needed to ensure that the CGV is
legally sound and financially sustainable and that the ITPs are bankable.
46.     The project also benefits from strategic partnerships with development partners. A bespoke approach has been
adopted to mobilize development partners over 1.5 years (initial conversations with a range of development partners, the
formal invitation from the Minister of Finance, the issuance of an Information Memorandum, and deep dives with seven
development partners that indicated they were interested in participating in the first phase of the CGV). Based on
extensive consultation, it is highly likely that sufficient capital will be mobilized to reach US$500 million. In the event that
US$500 million is not collectively reached, there is room to increase the portion of the NT while capping voting rights and
adjusting the governance structure.

 E. Lessons Learned and Progress on Learning Agenda

48.      The CGV design draws on World Bank analytical work reviewing 19 international experiences and select
operations, including Nigeria, Indonesia, and India, adapted to South Africa.11 Nigeria’s InfraCredit (2017) provided
unconditional, irrevocable guarantees for local currency infrastructure debt, achieving 5x leverage in five years (US$178
million capital backing US$890 million guarantees), derisking exclusively private sector risk. The Indonesia Infrastructure
Guarantee Fund (IIGF) (2010) guaranteed payment and termination risks, issuing US$4.7 billion in guarantees and
mobilizing US$22.3 billion in investments (approximately 5:1 leverage) over a decade, focusing exclusively on SOE de-
risking. India’s ADB-backed partial credit guarantee (PCG) (US$128 million) supported first-loss guarantees on local
currency bonds but was terminated in 2017 due to central bank limits on guarantee fees, underscoring the need for legal
due diligence and enabling regulation. India’s ADB-backed PCG focus on derisking both private and public sector risk.
Overall, leverage for such vehicles in emerging markets ranges from 2:1 to 10:1. The CGV is being established as
multisector approach, though it is expected to start with de-risking projects in the transmission subsector. Lastly, it is
expected that the CGV, while it benefitted greatly from the technical assistance provided, will continue to learn until it
has demonstrated its efficacy as a de-risking instrument.
49.      To unlock capital for infrastructure finance, there is a need to create an enabling environment, develop a project
pipeline, and deploy more financial innovations besides the CGV. It is expected that lessons learned in the first phase
will inform subsequent phases of the MPA.

 C. Sustainability

59.      The design of the CGV is to ensure its credibility, independence, and long-term business and financial
sustainability. The CGV will be established as a new institution in the private sector, where the GSA is a significant minority
shareholder, and will not be added to an existing institution in the public sector. The CGV will be established as a non-life
insurer, licensed, regulated, and supervised by the SARB/PA and operating on a commercial basis while meeting prudential
requirements. It will also establish an ESMS to address E&S risks in accordance with IFC Performance Standards. It is
expected that the CGV will continue to operate after project closing.

 IV. PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY

 A. Technical, Economic, and Financial Analysis
 Technical Analysis
60.       The CGV is designed to mobilize private capital for infrastructure by offering market-priced credit guarantees
to Paris-aligned infrastructure projects that backstop non-payment risks of public entities (SOEs, municipalities,
agencies, and ministries). The public sector cannot finance this amount due to fiscal constraints, and the private sector is
reluctant to invest without a full guarantee from the NT, which is no longer feasible as the NT has reached its contingent
liability limits. The CGV will offer payment and termination guarantees to replace sovereign guarantees sustainably,
mobilizing private financiers without distorting the market and minimizing the impact on government contingent
liabilities.
61.       The CGV will be an SA-domiciled, privately run non-life insurance company. Operated on a commercial basis by
independent professional management under robust private sector governance and risk management, the CGV will
initially focus on local currency guarantees with an ambition for AAA local rating and prioritize de-risking instruments to
enable private participation in the electricity transmission subsector. Its first products will target private parties involved
in concession or implementation agreements with public offtakers, and over time the CGV will broaden its guarantees to
the water and transport sectors, both at the national and subnational levels, and tailor coverage to evolving market and
project risk needs.
Financial Analysis
62.      The CGV has been designed to attain financial sustainability with full cost recovery. The financial analysis of the
CGV has been undertaken to inform its business model and capital adequacy to keep the CGV financially self-sustaining.
The guarantee premium to recover essential costs, including operating expenses and cost of capital has been initially
estimated at 150 basis points (excluding the risk premium). The CGV will develop and adopt an underwriting policy to
ensure the minimum level of guarantee premiums, while final premiums reflect key underwriting factors, including the
risk level of transactions. In a case where the CGV with a capital base of US$2.5 billion covers a US$10 billion risk portfolio
(4:1 leverage ratio), the estimated average IRR to equity holders is about 11.62 percent, which is sufficient to meet the
projected Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) of 5.45 percent and offer a reasonable return for risks taken.
63.      The project benefits from the IBRD Framework for Financial Incentives (FFI). This includes a price incentive for
US$18.75 million (grant from IBRD Surplus-Funded LPF) along with a US$75 million volume incentive (from the Global
Solutions Accelerator Platform).12 The FFI price and volume incentives play a pivotal role in decreasing the cost of capital
of the junior tranche of the NT. It is expected that the IBRD target terms for the junior equity tranche uphold the ratio of
62.5 percent government and 37.5 percent FFI, and that the volume incentive can be drawn up to 50 percent of the final

Economic Analysis
65.       The economic case of the CGV is strong, as mobilizing private financiers for infrastructure investments would
be challenging without the CGV. Market sounding indicated that insufficient creditworthiness of public sector off takers
is the primary concern of the market in participating in infrastructure projects through PPP that rely on off takers’
payments over a long term under contractual arrangements. It is critical that the GSA is committed to reform and
participates in the junior equity tranche/first loss to demonstrate ‘skin in the game’. Without the CGV or a similar risk
mitigation mechanism, a number of sponsors and financiers indicated that PPP transactions are not bankable. It was also
clear that the financial market is not in a position to offer a similar guarantee program, as other commercial insurers find
it difficult to underwrite the risk of payment defaults of public sector off takers, including SOEs, municipalities, and
government agencies. The CGV is, therefore, critical to facilitate the delivery of strategic infrastructure projects that will
bring positive economic impacts to the country.
66.       The cost-benefit analysis indicated that the project is strongly welfare enhancing under both the baseline and
conservative emissions-pricing scenarios. The project yields a consolidated economic internal rate of return (EIRR) of 36.2
percent and an economic net present value (ENPV) of about US$30.8 billion, far exceeding the hurdle rate of 10 percent,
with the low value of shadow carbon price in accordance with the relevant World Bank guideline. With the high value of
shadow carbon price, the EIRR and ENPV will increase to 41.5 percent and US$40.9 billion, respectively. Returns are robust
across all major sectors: transmission and renewable generation drive the highest gains, followed by water and sanitation
and freight rail, which post solid returns well above the discount rate, while port investments also remain economically
justified. These results indicate that the CGV-backed pipeline generates large net economic benefits overall, with network
utilities and freight corridors accounting for the bulk of welfare gains.

Climate
76.       The project is Paris aligned, as it is consistent with SA objectives to meet the Paris Agreement. SA objectives to
support the country’s pathway toward low GHG emissions and climate-resilient development are articulated in the NDC,
Long-Term Strategy, and National Adaptation Plans. It will achieve this by making Paris alignment part of the CGV’s
screening criteria and only de-risk those projects that are Paris aligned. In addition, the CGV will ensure that it has
capacities, appropriate policies, and structures in place to undertake the screening. This approach will mitigate the climate
risks to low.
77.     The project will support climate mitigation. It will achieve this by de-risking only those infrastructure projects
that support – or do not undermine – South Africa's pathway toward low greenhouse gas emissions. The CGV pipeline is
structured in phases from 2026 to 2036. The first five-year phase, ending in 2031, has the potential to mitigate 14.9 Mt
CO₂e annually, with seven projects operational by then. During this phase, ITP 1 is expected to bring 1.95 GW online (4,274
GWh/year) which, if displacing coal-fired generation, would reduce emissions by 2.82 Mt CO₂e annually. Generation
projects that displace coal-fired electricity at Eskom Just Energy Transition sites could reduce emissions by 2.23 Mt CO₂e
annually. A moderate modal shift from road to rail freight across three corridors – responsible for less than 20% of Transnet
Freight Rail volumes – could reduce emissions by 9.85 Mt CO₂e annually. Water projects, through planned integration of
renewable energy for pumps, biogas capture and SCADA implementation to optimise operations, could reduce emissions
by 0.01 Mt CO₂e annually.
78.     The project will also support climate adaptation. It will achieve this by de-risking only those infrastructure
projects (as part of the eligibility criteria of the CGV) that are targeted to be climate resilient, as articulated in the second
NDC 2025. This is important, as South Africa is vulnerable to rising temperatures and variable precipitation that result in
droughts, floods, and heatwaves (WBG CCDR 202214).

Gender
81.      SA continues to face significant gender inequality, and women remain underrepresented in senior and top
management positions despite progressive legal frameworks. In the financial sector, women hold only about 30.8
percent of top management roles, with white males alone occupying nearly half of these positions. At the senior
management level, female representation is slightly higher with 42.4 percent but still lags behind men with 53.1 percent.
In infrastructure services—including construction, utilities, and transport—the disparity is even more pronounced: women
account for less than 25 percent of top management roles and are vastly outnumbered in technical and leadership
positions. Root causes include historical occupational segregation; social norms steering women away from science,
technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) fields; care burdens and limited childcare; workplace bias; weak
retention mechanisms; and slow implementation of equity policies. These factors create a ‘leaky pipeline’, where women
enter the workforce but are not promoted or retained at rates sufficient to reach leadership positions.
82.       To improve gender equality in the leadership, the project will increase female representation in the CGV’s
Executive Board and the CGV’s Senior Management, targeting 35.3 percent and at least 45.3 percent, respectively. This
is in line with the Government Employment Equity Act (1998) amended on April 25, 2025. Phase 1 of the MPA will support,
under Component 3 Project coordination and implementation, the CGV to develop a Gender Action Plan with clear policies
and guidelines, including (a) setting specific hiring and promotion targets for women in technical senior management and
board roles, integrated into workforce plans; (b) applying unbiased recruitment practices and inclusive policies, including
female technical professionals on hiring panels; (c) providing leadership training, assigning women to strategic technical
projects, and offering sponsorship and mentorship from senior leaders; (d) implementing flexible working hours, parental
leave, and childcare support to retain skilled women; (e) ensuring transparent promotion and pay processes and fostering
an inclusive workplace with anti-harassment and anti-discrimination policies and regular staff training; and (f) developing
a policy that provides incentives (lower guarantee premiums) for SPVs that meet at least five-year numerical targets for
the different infrastructure sectors in their infrastructure projects. In the infrastructure sector, the target ranges from 25
percent to 36 percent female representation.

             ANNEX 1: Infrastructure Sectoral Challenges and Upstream and Down Stream Reforms

(a)     Upstream Reforms
1.      Project readiness challenges are weaker in South Africa than in emerging markets and developing
economies (EMDEs). Less than 10 percent of government projects in EMDEs reach financial close, primarily due
to breakdowns in project readiness during the midstream phase. These breakdowns are driven by coordination
failures—where multiple fragmented roles complicate infrastructure project development—alongside capacity
and institutional gaps, such as weak technical skills and insufficient human capital that undermine the bankability
of feasibility studies and risk assessments. Additionally, underinvestment in project preparation, marked by
inadequate funding and fragmented support mechanisms, further exacerbates these issues. In South Africa, these
challenges are compounded by a decade of weak economic growth, rising fiscal pressures, and consolidation, all
contributing to underinvestment in infrastructure. The absence of a credible pipeline of investable projects, the
lack of clear and uniform procedures for project delivery, and institutional proliferation across government
spheres have resulted in operational complexity and inefficiencies throughout the infrastructure delivery value
chain. These constraints are particularly acute at the municipal level, where project preparation capacity is
weakest and where replicable financing models are needed to bring projects to bankability and scale.
Strengthening project preparation through dedicated windows (for example, project preparation and viability gap
funding), revolving mechanisms linked to downstream transaction cost recoveries (success fees), and
programmatic screening and prioritization can help address midstream readiness failures and improve the rate of
financial close.
2.        The GSA initiated reforms to address infrastructure delivery efficiencies. To tackle these endemic
challenges, the GSA has launched several reform initiatives. These include reforming the PPP regulatory regime at
the national and provincial levels—though municipal reforms are still pending; conducting a systematic review of
the infrastructure delivery institutional landscape; and prioritizing key institutional reforms. The reforms focus on
harmonizing project prioritization approaches, consolidating project preparation mechanisms and functions
through the creation of the IFISA as a center of excellence, and centralizing and streamlining project appraisal
processes. In addition, the system is being strengthened through the operationalization of viability gap funding as
a revolving facility, supported by transparent success-fee recovery from financially closed transactions. The BFI
window is being adapted into a comprehensive blended financing platform to enable more agile and responsive
support. Together, these measures aim to formalize and adequately fund project development/preparation
facilities, accelerate screening and preparation, and provide a clear pathway from prioritization to bankability—
especially for PPP-driven PCM and municipal projects requiring replication and scaling.
(b)     Downstream Reforms
Electricity Sector
3.       Energy security and just energy transition are among the top priorities of the GSA. The country has 56
GW of electricity generation capacity, of which 40 GW is coal power plants that are, on average, about 40 years
old. South Africa suffered from electricity supply shortage until recently. The worst-ever load shedding was
experienced in 2023, recording 6,838 hours of rolling blackouts across 335 days. While supply security has been
significantly improved since March 2024, it is dependent on aging coal stations, which pose risks to long-term
supply security. South Africa’s commitment to the transition away from coal dependence, which has been
demonstrated through the Just Energy Transition Framework and Investment Plan, has been affected by energy
security. Due to such concerns, Eskom has delayed the planned decommissioning of three coal stations (Camden,
Hendrina, and Grootvlei) from 2027 to 2030. Electricity tariff has increased more than fivefold since 2010, while




                                                                                                                Official Use Only
          The World Bank
          Blended Finance Platform for Resilient Infrastructure(P508930)


the consumer price index (CPI) has approximately doubled. Declining, inefficient, and costly energy supply
hampers economic growth and South Africa’s reindustrialization efforts.
4.      South Africa is committed to advancing sector reforms toward a competitive, transparent, and
financially sustainable energy sector with greater private sector participation. ERAA came into effect in January
2025, promoting competition and transparency in the electricity sector. Unbundling the vertically integrated utility
(Eskom) is progressing, with the establishment of NTCSA in July 2024. NERSA has approved several key regulations,
including the Wheeling Framework and the Curtailment Framework, that facilitate increasing private sector
participation and competition. A wholesale electricity market is planned to be launched in 2026. Private sector
participation in renewable energy generation has been promoted, for large-scale projects through REIPPPP since
2011 and for small-scale embedded generation up to 100 MW by exempting license requirements in 2021.
5.      While these reform measures are mobilizing private capital and delivering outputs, substantial
investment is required in electricity generation, transmission, distribution, and energy storage. REIPPPP has
successfully added 8.3 GW of generation capacity from private sector independent power producers and
mobilized US$16 billion of private capital. The aggregate generation capacity of distributed solar PV has grown
rapidly since 2021 and exceeded 6 GW. The success of REIPPPP, despite Eskom’s weak financial standing, was
partly due to the provision of sovereign guarantee from the NT in case of payment defaults. Going forward, to
achieve energy security, the IRP 2025 envisages adding about 33 GW of generation and storage capacity by 2030,
of which 27 GW comes from renewable energy and energy storage. TDP 2024 also sets a target to expand
transmission systems by about 14,500 km of new transmission lines and 210 new transformers by 2034 to
integrate 56 GW of new generation capacity.
6.       In particular, transmission is a major bottleneck to further scaling up renewable energy and advancing
just energy transition, with its substantial investment need that calls for both public financing and private
investment. While electricity demand and coal power generation capacity are concentrated in the North,
renewable energy resources are abundant in the South. NTCSA launched the latest TDP 2024, which requires ZAR
112 billion (US$6 billion) by 2029 and ZAR 450 billion (US$25 billion equivalent) by 2034 to expand transmission
systems and integrate new generation capacity. To timely implement TDP and address transmission congestions,
the GSA adopts a two-pronged approach: (a) mobilizing public financing from DFIs on NTCSA’s balance sheet and
(b) launching the ITP Procurement Program to crowd in private sector investment in transmission.
7.      The ITP Phase 1 is on track, expected to award bidders by end-2026, with a plan to make it a program
that runs regular market offerings. A ministerial determination for ITP procurement was announced in March
2025. The Electricity Transmission Infrastructure Regulations (so-called ITP Regulations) have been gazetted by
the DEE in October 2025. The RFQ for the ITP Phase 1 was issued to the market in July 2025, to be followed by the
RFP to short-listed bidders in Q3 CY2026. The ITP Phase 1, comprising seven projects, will develop 1,164 km of
400 kV transmission lines and associated substations through the build-own-operate-transfer model, with an
estimated total investment of around US$500 million.
8.       There are various measures to enhance the bankability of ITPs, but offtaker creditworthiness remains
a primary concern for the private sector. The GSA is setting up a legal and regulatory framework to ensure that
the NTCSA will receive cash flows to meet its payment obligations to ITPs. ERAA sets an overarching principle of
cost recovery and financial sustainability of the electricity sector. The DEE gazetted the Electricity Transmission
Infrastructure Regulations, which include a provision on ITP cost recovery. NERSA is also developing the Cost
Recovery Rules for ITPs, which clarify regulatory principles for the cost recovery of transmission capacity procured
from ITPs. The regulatory framework will be finalized before the RFP to give a clear signal to the market that
NTCSA’s payments to ITPs will be recovered through NERSA’s tariff determination mechanisms, which are
currently the Multi-Year Price Determination (MYPD). Contractual arrangements with ITPs, including but not

to fill the financing gap in the electricity distribution sector. The proposed CGV aims to provide risk mitigation to
this specific risk and attract private investment in key electricity infrastructure.
Transport Sector
10.      South Africa’s freight logistics crisis is driven by chronic underinvestment, aging and disrupted rolling
stock, escalating cable theft, and systemic operational inefficiencies at Transnet that severely undermine rail
performance. The transport and logistics sector’s challenges, as outlined in the Cabinet-endorsed Freight Logistics
Reform Roadmap, stem from entrenched issues, including historical underinvestment in rail infrastructure that
Transnet has acknowledged but struggled to resolve; severe rolling stock constraints marked by aging assets—
many over 40 years old— and ongoing disputes over procured electrified locomotives that have left many units
idle or undelivered; a sharp rise in cable theft since 2020 that disrupts operations, particularly on the Container
and Northern Corridors (with the North-East Corridor also hit during the October 2022 strike), causing significant
delays and cancellations, and necessitating stronger security measures and tighter management of scrap metal
exports; and operational inefficiencies within Transnet, where traffic volumes, speeds, and productivity lag
international benchmarks—apart from the high-performing iron ore line—evidenced by the surge in manual train
authorizations from 50,000 in 2018 to over 250,000 in 2024.
11.      SA’s freight logistics underperformance stems from three structural problems. First, Transnet has long
operated under vague, complex, and often unfunded mandates, including developmental objectives without
corresponding state subsidies. This created a contradiction between its commercial, profit-driven corporate
model and its role in the developmental state, with an oversized network expected to be self-sustaining despite
segments that are not commercially viable. Second, ports and rail infrastructure are essential facilities—natural
monopolies critical to national economic performance—yet Transnet’s vertical integration as both owner and
operator has dampened competitive pressures, constrained efficiency, and limited private investment,
highlighting the need for strong regulatory oversight or structural reforms to enable fair access and competition.
Third, governance failures, documented in the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into State Capture (Part V, Volumes
1 and 2), have crippled operations, notably through corrupt locomotive procurement that reduced fleet
availability. With major investments ahead in rail and ports, reforms must ensure that past governance failures
are not repeated and actively crowd in private capital.

                                  ANNEX 2: Financial Intermediary Financing Assessment

Introduction
1.       The objective of the assessment is to confirm that the proposed CGV can offer sustainable de-risking
products in agreement with World Bank FIF requirements and the 16 Principles. As the following assessment
finds, (a) the structure of the CGV, (b) the financial sector’s capacity, and (c) the macroeconomic and fiscal
framework are conducive to a credit guarantee model that complies with the FIF guidance.
Macroeconomic Framework and Fiscal Situation
2.       Macroeconomic conditions are in place for lending for infrastructure, especially if supported by a credit
guarantee. The annual growth rate of 0.8 percent is balanced by a low inflation rate of 3.4 percent (CPI, September
2025, SARB). Government debt to GDP reached 77 percent (with an increasing trend) and the national budget
deficit reached 5 percent of GDP in 2025. The policy interest rate was set at 7 percent, and the SARB reported the
prime interest rate at 10.5 percent in November 2025.
Financial Sector Policies and Performance
3.      The country is characterized by broad and deep capital markets, which are critical to the project. The
success of credit guarantees requires participation in the financing of a minimum number of sound financial
intermediaries. The five largest banks are highly concentrated, holding 90 percent of banks’ assets. However, their
financial ratios show a resilient and growth-oriented sector (for example, ROE of 19 percent on a combined basis)
and therefore nonperforming loans have declined from 5.47 percent in June 2024 to 5.18 percent by the end of
2024. S&P Global’s 17 assessment improved both the sovereign rating (to BB/B- for long- and short-term foreign
currency), and improved ratings from stable to positive for six financial institutions.
Implementation Arrangements
4.      The CGV will be operated with full institutional, operational, and financial autonomy from the GSA.
Autonomy will be ensured by the governance requirements established under the Companies Act, the Insurance
Act, and their accompanying Prudential Standards. The CGV will be incorporated as a private non-life insurance
company providing credit guarantees with a license from South Africa’s PA. Only licensed entities under the PAs
are able to offer credit guarantees for a premium in the South African market. The CGV would be incorporated by
the South African Companies Act 71 of 2008.
5.      The CGV will be licensed under the Insurance Act 18 of 2017. Supervision of the CGV will be conducted
by the PA of South Africa, whose powers comprise licensing financial institutions; issuing standards and directives,
conducting inspections and investigations; and, overall, enforcing regulations. The CGV ownership will be mixed
with the GSA holding a minority equity stake (25–30 percent), and the remaining shares invested by select
international development partners. Capital raising efforts are proceeding with prospective shareholders, in
addition to the IFC.
6.       The CGV will be financially self-sufficient, generating enough revenue to fund its own operations
without relying on regular budget allocations from shareholders. This autonomy comes from (a) sufficient early
capitalization, (b) investment returns, and (c) guarantee premiums as the business develops. Financial self-
sufficiency will ensure that the entity is insulated from political pressure and can make long-term strategic
decisions based on its objective to support strategic and bankable infrastructure projects. Findings from the
review of the CGV’s planned regulatory framework, governance, and operational and risk management approach

7.      The CGV will operate a risk management framework that preserves and protects its capital. This

indicators to anticipate potential threats and maintain sufficient capital buffers. Governance structures mentioned
in the paragraphs above—such as independent risk committees and internal audit functions—will enforce
accountability.
Financial Products
8.     The CGV will provide credit guarantees to de-risk some of the project-related risks that are difficult to
absorb by private investors in the development and improvement of South Africa’s sustainable infrastructure.
Guarantees will be offered to multiple infrastructure sectors where these types of credit risk support mechanisms
make commercial sense to increase PCM.
9.      The design of the first generation of the CGV’s guarantees will be based on those being offered by the
NT to the REIPPP. These are payment guarantees covering off taker payment default (up to 90percent, depending
on needs) in a power purchase agreement or availability payments with SOEs or other off takers such as
municipalities. A target sector in the first phase is the ITPs, provided project structures and payment flows meet
a minimum bankability threshold. Credit guarantees will be priced on a cost-recovery basis and as a function of
the underlying credit risk being covered and its percentage of coverage. Beneficiaries will, in principle, include
debt holders (that is, commercial lenders and bond holders), and in selected cases, when the determining event
of default is linked to the offtake payment by an SOE, also the equity holders (that is, project sponsors).
Monitoring and Evaluation
10.      A strong M&E framework will be established to track the additionality of infrastructure projects. In
addition to the oversight by the PA and corresponding financial reporting obligations under the Companies Act
and the Insurance Act, reasonable targets will need to be established on infrastructure projects facilitated, PCM
and the impact on job creation and economic growth. This would track the additionality of support to
infrastructure projects, including the number of projects able to secure finance that would not have otherwise
been possible. The PIU will be responsible for M&E and gathering relevant financial soundness indicators for
capital adequacy, profitability, portfolio quality, and other indicators as determined by the World Bank.
IFC and MIGA Coordination
11.     There has been close coordination with IFC and MIGA. Both entities having been contributing to all
phases of the conceptualization and design of the CGV, along with the IBRD team. IFC is well advanced in the
process of committing to an equity investment in the senior tranche. MIGA is considering two types of products
to support the CGV: a reinsurance product covering a share of the CGV’s guarantee portfolio and a Policy Risk
Insurance product to shareholders of the CGV that may require it.

Conclusion
12.      The CGV is expected to be a solid entity with a high probability of market acceptance and success.
Findings from the review of the CGV’s planned regulatory framework, governance, and operational and risk
management approach indicate a prospective solid entity with reasonable perspectives of success. Key aspects in
the proposed approach are strong guardrails against political interference, a strong and well-diversified group of
credible shareholders, and a solid supervisory and governance framework. All these elements should facilitate the
establishment of a strong technical team capable of achieving the CGV’s objectives of de-risking strategic
infrastructure in South Africa for PCM. A key uncertainty to monitor is the availability of a strong pipeline of
projects that would allow the CGV to scale up and diversify its guarantee portfolio as planned.
