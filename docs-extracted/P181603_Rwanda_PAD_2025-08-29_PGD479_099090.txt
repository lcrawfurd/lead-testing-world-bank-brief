                         IDA PROGRAM DOCUMENT FOR A PROPOSED CREDIT TO REPUBLIC OF RWANDA

    I. COUNTRY CONTEXT AND OPERATION SUMMARY

1. The proposed Disaster Risk Management (DRM) Development Policy Credit with Catastrophe Deferred Drawdown
Option (Cat DDO) is a stand-alone operation in the amount of US$141 million. The development objective of this Cat
DDO is to improve Rwanda’s preparedness and response to disaster and health emergencies, while strengthening
resilience of its built environment and natural resources. This proposed operation, fully aligned with the Government of
Rwanda’s (GoR) program, seeks to integrate disaster, climate and public health risk management as central elements of
public policies. The proposed Cat DDO is structured around three pillars with eight prior actions, which were all agreed
jointly with relevant GoR ministries, departments and agencies (MDAs). Likewise, the results indicators are consistent with
the associated policy reforms, bolstering government ownership of the program.

2. Rwanda is at risk of numerous natural hazards, including droughts, floods, earthquakes, landslides, storms
(windstorms, lightning, rainstorms, and thunderstorms), wildfire, diseases, and epidemics. Since the early 2000s, the
frequency and severity of disasters, particularly caused by floods, landslides, and droughts, have significantly increased,
with increasing human casualties as well as economic and environmental losses.1 Heavy rains during the night of May 2–
3, 2023 alone, caused floods and landslides, accounting for 131 fatalities, 104 injuries, and more than 18,000 left homeless.
Total physical damages and economic losses of these heavy rains were estimated at US$187 million, and total recovery
needs at US$ 547.29 million2. The transport sector was the worst affected, with damages estimated at US$115.3 million
(72 percent of total damages), followed by the housing, environment, and water resources management sectors (each
accounting for 12 percent of damages). The May 2023 disaster was estimated to reduce the country’s Gross Domestic
Product (GDP) growth rate by 0.4 percentage points.3 In Rwanda, approximately 40 percent of the population is exposed
to landslides due to residing in vulnerable areas in the highlands of the western, southern, and northern provinces.4 During
the past decade, Rwandans have been among the world’s most affected by landslides. Droughts are also of concern as
they have historically had the widest reach; the 1996 event affected 12 percent of the population.5 The eastern province
is considered the most vulnerable to climate impacts as dry spells are increasing in length, leading to food shortages. In
the west, rising temperatures are likely to force valuable tea and coffee production (which account for more than 20
percent of export earnings) into higher, less productive lands6. With only about 6% of arable land irrigated, the economy
of Rwanda depends mainly on rainfed agriculture, which makes the country highly vulnerable to climate change due to
the expected increasing variability in rainfall frequencies and intensity, causing climatic hazards such as droughts, floods,
extreme temperatures, and prolonged dry spells.7

3. More than 80 percent of disaster impacts in Rwanda are climate-related8. From January 2015 to October 2022, the
country recorded 633 deaths, 225 people injured, 6,173 houses destroyed, and a loss of 11,893.79 ha of crops due to
disasters.9 Heavy rainfall events are especially common in northern and western provinces, and cause flooding, flash flood
events and can trigger landslides and mudslides, leading to infrastructure damage and death.10 The severe floodings and
landslides in early May 2023 caused significant damage to provincial healthcare and water, sanitation, and hygiene
infrastructure, resulting in a wide range of negative health outcomes. In addition, these climate events damaged roads
and bridges limiting and, in some cases, completely interrupting the provision of services due to inability to maintain
health equipment and distribute medication and health supplies. Regarding droughts, while they tend to take a serious
toll on production in rural areas, they also contribute to food price inflation and reduced consumption in urban areas. For
example, prices of basic foods rose significantly during a particularly long drought in 2016, affecting all major cities. The
more agriculturally dependent and drought-prone secondary city of Nyagatare is already suffering from drought, via loss
of crop and livestock and decreased production in the agro-processing sector located there.11 Likewise, climate change is
expected to increase the prevalence of water and vector borne diseases such as malaria and dengue. Malaria is already
the third leading cause of death and disability in Rwanda and one of the leading causes of under-five mortality. Changes
in heat and precipitation will make the previously malaria-free highlands more susceptible to outbreaks within the next
decades21. Epidemics, emerging, re-emerging diseases and climate change significantly impact the well-being of the
population and are of great concern to the GoR.
4. As climate change worsens, women are likely to be impacted more significantly due to pre-existing gender gaps,
such as women’s limited access to resources, networks, and skills, including in emergency situations. 12 Gender
inequalities and disparities may also lead to increased vulnerabilities to health-related pandemics. In Rwanda, for example,
occupational segregation led to women’s increased vulnerability during COVID-19 pandemic and afterwards both in terms
of livelihoods and exposure to health risks. Women are almost twice as likely to be outside the formal labor market as
men (e.g., small businesses, accommodation and food services, and domestic retail trade).13 During the COVID-19
pandemic, utilization of routine maternal and child health services declined across all five provinces in Rwanda.
5. Climate change is also expected to slow the pace of structural transformation in Rwanda, compromising future
poverty reduction and economic growth14. Poverty rates in Rwanda have declined over the past two decades but remain
high. The poverty headcount ratio, at $3.00 a day (2021 purchasing power parity), declined from 82.3 percent in 2000 to
38.6 percent in 2024. 15The impact of climate change risks could be severe for Rwanda’s growth, given the economy's
reliance on climate-sensitive sectors. Climate change is likely to increase variability in crop yields and agricultural
production, cause severe flood damage to physical capital16, reduce labor productivity, and impact demand for tourism. A

 II. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK

6. Rwanda’s macroeconomic policy framework is considered adequate for the proposed operation. Rwanda’s GDP
growth was strong over 2020-2024—averaging 9.0 percent—and is projected to grow by around 7.3 percent over 2025–
27. Inflation remains within the National Bank of Rwanda’s (NBR) 2-8 percent target range with the policy rate kept
constant at 6.5 percent for 12 consecutive months to August 2025 with no monetization of the financing requirement.
The financial sector remains stable. In early 2025, the banking sector continued to be profitable and well-capitalized, with
the capital adequacy ratio (CAR) standing at 18.8 percent, well above the regulatory minimum of 12.5 percent. Under the
IMF program, the GoR has recently adopted a tax reform package that combines the discontinuation of tax expenditures
with a new environmental tax, which should significantly increase revenue. Rwanda continues to successfully implement
performance and policy actions (PPA) agreed upon under the Sustainable Development Finance Policy (SDFP) including
measures to expand climate finance to support the GoR commitment to improve resilience of the economy to climate
related shocks and long-term degradation of Rwanda’s natural capital. This operation supports Rwanda in mitigating
macroeconomic risks by integrating disaster, climate and public health risk management as central elements of public
policies.

 A. Recent Economic Developments
7. Rwanda experienced robust economic growth in 2024 and early 2025, positioning itself among the fastest-growing
economies in sub-Saharan Africa. Real GDP growth was 8.9 percent in 2024 (5.1 percent per capita) and was broad-based.
The agricultural sector recovered, supported by improvements in food crop production following earlier climate shocks.
The industrial sector also expanded strongly, particularly in construction. The services sector performed well, driven by
trade, tourism, transport, and telecommunications, despite temporary setbacks from the Marburg virus disease (MVD).
On the expenditure side, growth was supported by consumption, investment, and a robust increase in exports. Labor
market conditions improved significantly, with labor force participation rising and the unemployment rate falling to 14.9
percent—a 2.3 percentage point drop from the previous year. Over half a million new jobs were created, marking the
most substantial reduction in unemployment since the pandemic. This led to a nearly 5-percentage point increase in the
employment-to-population ratio and a 2-percentage point decline in the unemployment rate. Growth momentum
continued in the first quarter of 2025, with the real GDP increasing by 7.8 percent, supported mainly by the robust private
consumption and strong services performance.
8. Rwanda’s current account deficit (CAD) remained wide in 2024 with the trade deficit being partially offset by strong
remittances. The CAD rose to 12.7 percent of GDP in 2024 due to continued pressure on the trade deficit (16.5 percent of
GDP), driven by sustained momentum in food and consumer goods imports as well as elevated capital goods imports—
both reflecting strong consumer and investment demand. Despite strong tourism receipts (4.1 percent of GDP) and
remittance inflows (3.5 percent of GDP), official transfers dropped by about one percent of GDP. Robust foreign direct
investment (4.0 percent of GDP in 2024) and public borrowing (9.4 percent of GDP) fully financed the CAD. The Rwandan
franc continued to depreciate against the US dollar this year, falling by 9.4 percent through end December 2024—less

11.     Prudent macroeconomic policies, structural and climate-related reforms should sustain robust growth in the
short and the medium term. GDP growth is expected to keep momentum, increasing by 7.3 percent on average in 2025–

financing will cover external financing needs. Inflation is expected to gradually return within NBR’s medium-term inflation
target of 5 percent with symmetric boundaries of ±3 percent.
12.      Fiscal consolidation efforts are expected to continue to anchor macroeconomic stability, with a mix of revenue
gains and expenditure rationalization. In the FY25-FY28 budget framework, the GoR projects tax revenue increases largely

administration, such as VAT on mobile phones, increases in fuel levy, gaming taxes, excise tax hikes, and improved customs
controls. Future reforms will focus on broadening the tax base (e.g., streamlining tax holidays and expenditures). This will
lead to a projected tax-to-GDP ratio of 17.9 percent by FY27/28. On the spending side, the GoR will continue with spending
on rationalization measures, including containing current expenditure on goods and services and reprioritization of
investments as well as with reforms aimed at improving the transparency and efficiency of public financial management
and investment practices and enhancing the management of fiscal risks. Under this baseline, public debt would peak at
86.3 percent of GDP in 2026 before gradually improving over the medium-term.
13.      Rwanda’s debt remains sustainable in the medium term, even at its elevated debt trajectory. The latest joint
World Bank-IMF Debt Sustainability Analysis (DSA) of June 2025 for Rwanda continues to indicate a moderate risk of
external and overall public debt distress. Although the stock of debt is high and projected to rise above 85 percent of GDP,
the predominance of concessional debt, about 70 percent of the external debt, keeps the debt manageable. The increasing
trend of public debt in 2025-2026 assumes an additional US$800 million in external commercial financing related to the
construction of New Kigali International Airport and the expansion of the national airline. These projects are also expected
to raise FDI inflows. Key risks include a decline in concessional financing, U.S. monetary policy tightening and U.S. dollar
appreciation, and terms-of-trade shocks. Rwanda remains susceptible to adverse market conditions and climate shocks.
14.      The outlook is subject to substantial downside risks. A prolonged or worsening crisis in the Eastern Democratic
Republic of Congo (DRC) could significantly impact Rwanda's outlook. A potential border closure with the DRC due to the
dispute could substantially affect trade, as the DRC is now Rwanda’s top export market. Tourism receipts, a major source
of foreign exchange and employment for Rwanda, could be affected by an increased perception of insecurity and the
potential damage to the country’s reputation. As in 2013, a worsened crisis has the potential to affect international aid
flows to Rwanda, creating more stress for already vulnerable fiscal and external balances. Rwanda’s public expenditures
rely heavily on international assistance, which represented 37 percent of total expenditure on average over 2020-2024
(equivalent to 10.5 percent of GDP). Even though Rwanda has limited direct trade and financial links to the Middle East,
an intensification of the dispute in the region could lead to further disruptions to the global trade and economy, thus
affecting Rwanda mainly through a reduced global demand for its main export products. Limited access to concessional
resources and lower external demand fueled by monetary tightening in advanced economies pose further downside risks.
The main risk on the domestic front is linked to the increasing frequency of weather and climate shocks, which could

 B. Triggers, Prior Actions, Expected Results and Analytical Underpinnings
25.      Cat DDO drawdown trigger: For drawdown purposes, an Eligible Event is a Category 3 or Category 4 disaster under
Law No. 41/2015, declared by The National Disaster Management Committee (NADIMAC) chaired by the Minister in
charge of Emergency Management (MINEMA). Category 3 is a disaster which occurs in any District and is managed using
resources from the Ministry and its partners, while Category 4 is a disaster which is beyond the country’s means. Eligible
Events include natural disasters as well as health emergencies. For the purpose of this operation Eligible Events do not
include any emergency that is predominantly political, technological, security, military, or fiscal in nature. The declaration
of an Eligible Event will be evidenced by a Decision signed by the Minister of MINEMA as chair of NADIMAC and posted
on the MINEMA website in the case of natural disasters, or the Ministry of Health website in the case of health
emergencies.
26.     Cat DDO drawdown period and renewal: The drawdown period for this operation will be three years. The
Borrower can renew it one time for an additional three-year period during which the Cat DDO can be disbursed (in whole
or in part). The World Bank will monitor the implementation of the DRM program (see Annex 1). If it is not being
implemented satisfactorily, the Borrower may not be eligible to submit a disbursement request. The adequacy of the
macroeconomic framework is assessed at effectiveness and reconfirmed at renewal, but it is not required for drawdown.
The extension would require the approval of the Regional Vice President of the World Bank. The renewal will need to take
place no earlier than one year and no later than six months before the expiration date.

28.      Through the reforms included in Pillar 1, the GoR is improving disaster risk information to inform decision making,

Prior Action 1: To enhance the Recipient’s capacity to manage emergencies effectively the Recipient, through its Ministry
in Charge of Emergency Management, has enhanced institutional governance for disaster risk, and has prioritized
improvements in disaster risk information, through approval and publication of the Strategic Plan 2024-2029.

29.      Rationale: In the last two decades, floods and landslides have been the most devastating events. The effects of
flood hazards have worsened as recent population growth and land scarcity have pushed people to settle in flood-prone
areas. Drought hazard is also of concern since dry spells are increasing in length, leading to food shortages. To address
this, the GoR is cognizant that ensuring available and reliable disaster risk information is still a crucial challenge in the
country. Specifically, the 2023 National Disaster Risk Reduction and Management (DRRM) Policy identifies as a critical
challenge the country’s limited capacity to understand and assess hazards, risks, exposure and vulnerability. Also, the
DRRM Policy acknowledges the need to improve Rwanda’s readiness to handle disaster events across sectors and at
various levels of government.
30.      Policy Content: The 2024/25-2028/29 strategic plan, developed by MINEMA, aims to enhance emergency
management and preparedness, fostering a safer and more resilient Rwanda. The UN Global Assessment Report on
Disaster Risk Reduction (GAR 2023) highlights that a comprehensive understanding of disaster risks is essential to building
resilience which is reflected in Rwanda’s 2023 National DRRM Policy. Thus, the first strategic objective of the MINEMA
strategic plan is to improve the understanding of disaster risk profiles at national, local, and community levels and the
strategic plan includes the review and update of the 2015 National Risk Atlas of Rwanda which is estimated to take three
years. The National Risk Atlas maps out the various hazards, vulnerabilities, and exposure factors across Rwanda. It has
served as a critical tool for planning and implementing disaster risk reduction measures, specifically to inform land use
planning, contingency planning, as well as the preparation of the National Adaptation Strategy, among others. Another
strategic objective is to build DRRM capacity at national, local and community levels. To achieve this, the plan includes
organizing training and simulation exercises (SimEx), fostering a culture of preparedness for effective response. As outlined
by the UNDRR (2020), the process of evaluating the results of a SimEX helps to identify critical areas of management and
aspects that need to be strengthened.
31.    Expected Results: This policy reform will support the GoR’s efforts to develop public policy actions aimed at
improving the availability of reliable disaster risk data to inform disaster risk reduction measures, as well as at enhancing
preparedness for effective response and building back fairer for longer-term recovery and reconstruction. Critical sectors

32.      Rationale: To respond to and mitigate the impacts caused by disasters, the GoR approved the revised National

systems (EWSs) and mechanisms to inform people at risk, limited data sharing among various stakeholders, among others.
33.     Policy Content: Developed by MINEMA in alignment with the 2015 Disaster Management Law, the 2024 National

impacts of emergencies, including natural disasters, accidents, and other crises. The Guidelines outline for each level of

search and rescue, evacuation, emergency relief, emergency communication, information management and coordination.
Likewise, they detail specific criteria for emergency categorization to serve as a foundation for activating a range of legal
and administrative processes to manage and mitigate the impact of disaster events. Furthermore, the guidelines also seek
to contribute to building DRRM capacity at national level by establishing mechanisms for effective collaboration with
DRRM stakeholders for disaster response and recovery. The National Platform for Disaster Management is one of these

agencies, International and local NGOs, private sector and civil society for co-leading and/or supporting critical emergency
response functions, including damage and need assessment, provision of emergency shelter, WASH services and other
recovery activities.

ensuring that no one is left behind. Progress made in this area will be measured through the number of seasonal
preparedness plans jointly developed by the National Platform for Disaster Management, in accordance with the new

39.       Rationale: Universal access to reliable electricity is one of the NST2 (2024-2029) 13 goals to be achieved.

management, improved response times, and investments in resilient infrastructure. The electricity distribution network
has seen improvement with a reduction in outage instances from 11,707 in 2017/2018 to 7,106 in 2023, indicating better

Prior Action 7: To strengthen the management of forests to adapt to and mitigate the impacts of climate change, the
Recipient, through its Parliament has established Protective State Forests as a new category under the legal Framework

50.      Rationale: Forestry and land use are crucial for Rwanda’s vision of transitioning to a green economy. The steady
depletion of forest and water resources has increased the country's sensitivity to climate change. Since 1990, forest cover
has declined while cropland has increased. Biomass demand for energy use, relied upon by over 80 percent of Rwandans30,
drives deforestation and forest degradation. Reduced vegetation decreases infiltration, depletes groundwater reserves,
and increases runoff, causing soil erosion and threatening food security. Addressing this, the Economic Transformation
pillar of Rwanda Vision 2050 prioritizes sustainable natural resource management. It aims to sustain forest cover at 30
percent (approx. 724,695 hectares)31, manage water catchments to mitigate disasters, and strengthen land
administration. Rwanda’s hilly and mountainous topography is prone to soil erosion, especially during heavy rainfall.
Protecting forests is vital as tree root systems stabilize soil, mitigating landslides and erosion, which threaten human life
and arable land. Forests also regulate water, reducing runoff velocity and flood risks. Their role in temperature regulation,
humidity, and precipitation patterns creates a stable microclimate, essential for agricultural planning and buffering climate
variability impacts. Moreover, forests also contribute to climate change mitigation efforts. They act as carbon sinks,
absorbing and storing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, thereby helping to mitigate climate change.
51.      Policy Content: The Law aims to provide regulations and guidelines for the management, conservation, and
protection of forests and trees in Rwanda. Regarding protective State forests, the Law establishes that this new type of
forests includes those in high-risk zones. Specifically, the Law defines ‘protective State forests’ as those forests planted in
the buffer zone of natural forests, national parks, public roadsides lakeshores, rivers banks, or other areas that may be
determined by the competent authority. Restoration and conservation of protective State forests on important and
sensitive areas such as ridge tops with very steep sloping land, riparian zones and wetland buffer zones and margins will
enhance climate resilience and reduce risks of natural hazards such as floods and landslides. While the Law establishes
this new category, it will need to be operationalized through the development of technical guidelines which will guide
management plans for these forests.
52.     Expected Results: The Law will support integrating environmental stewardship with economic and social
development goals. Furthermore, the Law will put in place a legal basis that aims to prevent degradation and ensure the
sustainable management of protective State forests. More sustainable forest practices are expected to help restore

importance of establishing robust regulatory frameworks, effective monitoring systems, and community-inclusive
safeguards to ensure equitable and sustainable water resource management. Overall, while these reforms reflect
important progress toward environmental sustainability and disaster resilience, they also present inherent risks—such as
land use change, biodiversity loss, and potential displacement, that require strong and proactive safeguards to be
effectively mitigated.
64.     However, Rwanda faces challenges such as weak institutional capacity to manage and control the negative
effects of investment projects, including a weak system for conducting Environmental and Social Impact Assessments
(ESIAs) and preparation and implementation of Resettlement Action Plans (RAPs) and enforcing compliance,
particularly in transport infrastructure. Strengthening this capacity is crucial for mitigating adverse impacts on the
environment and society. The Environment Management Authority's role in advising on natural resources management
and environmental conservation needs to be bolstered by additional measures to comprehensively address these
challenges. To reflect on key environmental and social risks associated with the operation, annex 5 has been included to
assess the individual E&S impact of each Prior Action to ensure greater transparency and to support monitoring and
mitigation efforts.

 C. PFM, Disbursement, and Auditing aspects

65.      The PFM system and public procurement framework, through the GoR’s commitment to address the PFM gaps,
provide reasonable assurance that the GoR can manage the country’s budget resources appropriately. The strengths of
the PFM system include clearly laid-out PFM legal frameworks and structures (including emergency spending), timely
public finance reporting aligned with international standards, and widespread use of Integrated Financial Management
and Information Systems (IFMIS) and e-Procurement systems across central and local government agencies; and
functioning internal audit and oversight institutions such as the Office of Auditor General (OAG) and Rwanda Public
Procurement Authority (RPPA). Gaps remain in addressing audit findings with legislative oversight; asset management;
public investment management; performance and financial monitoring of Government Business Enterprises (GBEs);
procurement regulations for commercial public institutions; preparation for working documents to implement the
provisions in the new public procurement law on sustainable procurement; and coordination gap between OAG and the
RPPA on procurement audit and reviewing complaints . The GoR is addressing these gaps through its PFM sector strategic
plan (2024-2029) that aims to support the GoR’s transformation through effective and accountable PFM. The World Bank
is supporting the GoR’s implementation of the PFM reform agenda through its Rwanda Public Finance Management
Reform Project (P164807) and continues to provide technical assistance for further reforms.
66.      The GoR supports a transparent and comprehensive annual budget process. The last Public Expenditure and

 V. SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION

73.     The overall residual risk rating for this operation is substantial. Table 6 presents the residual risk ratings by
category. The only two substantial risks to achieving the development objective of this Cat DDO relate to the technical
design of project or program, and institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability.
74.      Technical design of project or program. The substantial risk rating is driven by the multi-sectoral design of the
operation which includes a diverse set of sub-sectors and thematic areas in which actions are to be taken in parallel across
a total of eight MDAs including MINEMA, MININFRA, RTDA, Ministry of Health, RBC, Ministry of Environment, Rwanda
Forestry Authority and Rwanda Water Resources Board. To mitigate the risk the Program will make use of supporting
donor funding through, for example, the Global Facility for Disaster Reduction and Recovery, and relevant ongoing Bank
financed operations to provide targeted technical assistance to participating government entities.
75.     Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability. This substantial risk rating is driven by an identified
implementation challenge for successfully mainstreaming reforms in sectoral policies in Rwanda. The Program aims to
mitigate this risk by strengthening MINEMA’s role to provide increased support and coordination to other MDAs. This will
be supported through an inter-ministerial steering committee chaired by the Permanent Secretary of MINEMA to provide
implementation oversight, and to ensure adequate budget and staff are allocated to deliver on defined activities and
associated indicators.

Fund Relations Note
IMF Executive Board Concludes the Fifth Review under the Policy Coordination Instrument for Rwanda
June 4, 2025
•   The IMF Executive Board today concluded the fifth review under the Policy Coordination Instrument (PCI). The PCI
    continues to support Rwanda’s reform agenda aimed at maintaining macroeconomic stability, promoting sustainable
    and inclusive growth, and advancing climate-resilient development.
•   Rwanda’s economic growth remains among the strongest in sub-Saharan Africa, despite rising fiscal and external
    pressures linked to large investment projects and reduced concessional financing. Continued fiscal consolidation,
    supported by stronger domestic revenue mobilization and spending efficiency, is essential to safeguard
    macroeconomic stability and debt sustainability.
•   Program performance under the PCI has been strong. All quantitative targets were met, and most reform
    commitments were implemented, including in SOE governance, monetary statistics, and digital public financial
    management. The approval of the comprehensive tax policy package and the rollout of the Global Master Repurchase
    Agreement were implemented with a delay. Rwanda continues to demonstrate leadership in integrating climate
    considerations into macroeconomic policy and leveraging institutional reforms to mobilize climate finance.
Washington, DC: The Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) concluded the fifth review under the
Policy Coordination Instrument (PCI) for Rwanda.40
Rwanda’s economy expanded by 8.9 percent in 2024, driven by a rebound in agriculture and continued strength in the
services and construction sectors. Inflation remained within the National Bank of Rwanda’s 2–8 percent target band,
reflecting tight monetary policy and improved domestic food supply. The current account deficit widened in 2024 due to
strong consumer and capital goods imports, but reserves remained adequate at 4.7 months of imports as of end-year.
Going forward, the fiscal position will be under pressure from the large infrastructure investment in the New Kigali
International Airport and the expansion of RwandAir, as well as the recent pension reform. Public debt is expected to peak
in FY2025/26, with the PCI debt anchor now projected to be reached in 2033. Accelerating domestic revenue mobilization
and maintaining a credible fiscal consolidation path are crucial to restoring policy space and ensuring long-term fiscal
sustainability. Continued vigilance is also needed to manage risks from SOEs, rising debt service costs, and constrained
access to concessional financing.
Monetary policy should remain data-driven to contain inflation and support external adjustment. Exchange rate flexibility
will be essential to absorb shocks, while reforms to strengthen FX market functioning should continue. Close oversight of

At the conclusion of the Executive Board’s discussion, Mr. Bo Li, Deputy Managing Director, and Acting Chair, made the

“Rwanda’s economy has demonstrated impressive resilience, recording strong growth supported by robust activity in the
services, construction, and agriculture sectors. Inflation has remained within the NBR target range, aided by prudent
monetary policy and improved domestic food supply. However, the macroeconomic environment has become more
complex due to a need to implement difficult reforms against the background of worsening external conditions, including
aid withdrawals and regional tensions.
“Sustaining fiscal consolidation remains vital to preserving macroeconomic stability and ensuring debt sustainability. The
recently adopted tax reform package is a welcome step toward broadening the tax base and enhancing equity and
efficiency. Continued expenditure rationalization and close monitoring of fiscal risks, particularly from SOEs and the
ambitious priority investment project, are essential. The fiscal implications of pension reform and the financing needs for
the priority infrastructure project must be carefully managed to maintain fiscal discipline.

crucial to support external adjustment and safeguard reserve adequacy. The authorities' efforts to modernize the
monetary policy framework and strengthen FX market functioning are welcome. Enhancing risk monitoring, particularly
in the microfinance sector, and large exposures, along with building additional capital buffers, will be key to safeguarding
financial stability.

commendable. Looking ahead, Rwanda’s efforts to build a pipeline of bankable green projects and improve climate finance

engagement with development partners will be critical to sustaining progress and supporting Rwanda’s ambitious

ANNEX 5. History of Disaster Events of which the GoR has requested support from International Organization

1. May 1-8, 2016: Landslide that killed 96 people including mainly Gakenke (60) and Ngororero (26), Muhanga (19),
   Rutsiro (14). The GoR requested the support to United Nations (UN) and a project worth US$ 12.9 million named
   Emergency Response to Population and Infrastructures Damaged by Landslide in Gakenke District, was implemented
   MIDIMAR (renamed MINEMA), UNDP, WFP, IOM, FAO and Gakenke District.
2. May 2018: Floods and landslides that killed 221, injured 252 and temporary displaced 31,580 people mainly in
   Ngororero, Karongi, Rulindo and Kamonyi. A comprehensive PDNA conducted by GoR, United Nations Development
   Programme (UNDP) and the World Bank shown that total damage and loss is US$236 million while total cost of
   recovery and reconstruction is US$ 336 million.
3. April and May 2020: Floods that killed 165 people, injured 172 mainly in Ngororero, Gakenke, Nyabihu and Muhanga.
   A request to UN Resident was made by the GoR to support families affected by disasters and under CERF project
   worth US$ 1 Million was implemented in Ngororero, Gakenke and Nyabihu.
4. May 2-3, 2023: Floods and landslides where 131 people died, 104 injured and displaced over 20,000 people in
   Western and Northern Province. A comprehensive PNDA was conducted in collaboration with the World Bank and
   the assessment has shown that total damage and loss is estimated to RWF 215,8 billion (US$ 187 million) while total
   recovery needs for the physical damages and economic losses are estimated at RWF 629,38 billion (US$ 547.29
   million). The request for support was made to UN and the World Bank.
