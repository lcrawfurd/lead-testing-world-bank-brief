                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The Mozambique Urban Water Security Project aims to increase access to improved water supply services and
                     strengthen service delivery capacity in selected cities by focusing on sector reforms to increase private sector
                     participation; investing in infrastructure to expand water treatment, transport, storage, and distribution capacity in the
                     Maputo metropolitan region and other southern cities; and supporting improved performance of institutions involved in
                     water supply. The project design includes disbursement-linked indicators to support the establishment of an
                     institutional architecture for the urban water sector, the design and implementation of a service delivery policy for
                     private water providers, annual tariff review resolutions, and performance improvement of regional water utilities, with

                     the aim of modernizing urban water services, improving efficiency, increasing revenues, building resilient infrastructure,
                     and developing skilled water professionals. Ultimately, the project aims to improve public health, economic
                     development, and poverty reduction with a focus on Greater Maputo and the Southern Region, addressing water
                     insecurity and improving performance in key regions as part of the broader country investment to close the gap towards
                     universal access to water and sanitation in Mozambique.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890) aims to enhance urban water security by improving access,
                     resilience, and service efficiency in Greater Maputo and secondary cities in Gaza and Inhambane. FIPAG, with support
                     from AdRMM, AdRS, ARA-Sul, and DNAAS, will oversee the expansion and rehabilitation of water supply infrastructure.

                     The project upgrades treatment and distribution systems, rehabilitates storage infrastructure, and strengthens utilities
                     to address water scarcity, aging infrastructure, and non-revenue water losses, aligning with Mozambique’s climate
                     resilience strategy. Water sources include surface withdrawals from the Umbeluzi, Incomáti, and Limpopo rivers, and
                     groundwater abstraction.

                     Key environmental risks include disruptions to water sources and impacts on wetlands and downstream ecosystems,
                     such as the Maputo River estuary and coastal lagoons, due to altered hydrological flows and sediment transport. The
                     Corumana and Pequenos Libombos reservoirs, crucial for drinking water and irrigation, may experience cumulative
                     impacts from increased abstraction.



Public Disclosure
                     The social landscape varies from high-density urban areas to emerging urban zones, where affordability constraints may
                     affect service uptake. Vulnerable groups, including female-headed households, elderly, and informal water providers,
                     require targeted engagement to ensure equitable access.

                     Social risks include land acquisition, stakeholder engagement, and SEA/SH risks due to labor-intensive works. The
                     project will integrate climate adaptation, enhance water governance, and ensure compliance with Mozambique’s EIA
                     Decree (54/2015) and World Bank ESF, with licensing from the Ministry of Agriculture, Environment, and Fisheries.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Fundo de Investimento e Património do Abastecimento de Água (FIPAG) is the main implementing agency, with
                     oversight from AdRMM, AdRS, ARA-Sul, and DNAAS. While FIPAG has experience with World Bank-financed projects
                     under Safeguard Policies, its ESF exposure remains limited.

                     Under the PforR, E&S risks were managed through national legislation guided by the ESSA. The transition to IPF requires
                     full ESF alignment, necessitating enhanced capacity. FIPAG’s Environmental and Social Management Unit (ESMU),
                     established on June 18, 2024, has seven staff, including an OHS specialist.

                     At the regional level, AdRMM has an E&S unit with seven staff, including one Unit Manager (Environmental Engineer),
                     five Environment and Safety Technicians (two Environmental Engineers, one Environmental Manager, one
                     Environmental Chemist, and one unspecified qualification), and one Social Technician (Sociologist). AdRS has yet to
                     establish its E&S unit, with recruitment planned during implementation.

                     The project will also support small private water providers to upgrade their systems to comply with service delivery

                     Capacity-building measures will include ESF training, contractor oversight, and environmental licensing, with specific

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                        Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                 Substantial



Public Disclosure
                     The environmental risk for the Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890) is rated Substantial, considering
                     potential environmental impacts from planned infrastructure investments, water resource management challenges,
                     and the Borrower’s evolving institutional capacity to implement the ESF. The project involves water supply expansion,
                     distribution network rehabilitation, and storage improvements in urban areas, which may result in temporary,
                     localized environmental impacts. Key risks include soil erosion, sedimentation, air and noise pollution, improper
                     waste disposal, and occupational health and safety (OHS) hazards during construction. Additionally, water abstraction
                     and distribution improvements may contribute to cumulative impacts on local water resources, particularly in water-
                     scarce regions. Although the project does not involve high-risk activities such as large-scale dam construction, toxic
                     waste generation, or major biodiversity loss, some subprojects may be near sensitive wetland ecosystems, requiring
                     careful screening and mitigation. The project will comply with Mozambique’s EIA Decree (54/2015) and World Bank
                     ESF standards, necessitating ESIAs and ESMPs for substantial-risk subprojects. While FIPAG’s ESMU is in place,
                     regional gaps remain, particularly at AdRS, which has yet to establish its E&S unit. Institutional coordination
                     challenges and capacity constraints in environmental management contribute to the Substantial rating. To mitigate
                     risks, the project will adhere to ESS requirements, WBG EHS guidelines for Water and Sanitation, and Good
                     International Industry Practices (GIIP). Risk levels may be reassessed during implementation, depending on ESF
                     integration, mitigation effectiveness, and institutional capacity improvements.
                                                                                                                                   Substantial
                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The social risk rating for the Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890) is Substantial, reflecting the
                     complexity of interventions and associated risks across three main areas: Land Acquisition and Economic
                     Displacement – Planned civil works may lead to physical and economic displacement, particularly in high-density

                               The World Bank
                               Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890)



                     areas of Greater Maputo with complex informal settlements. Temporary restrictions on access during construction
                     could affect businesses, social infrastructure, and mobility, leading to economic impacts on both formal and informal
                     enterprises. Water Sector Policy Reforms – The project supports tariff adjustments and private sector participation,
                     which may impact small informal water providers and raise affordability concerns for vulnerable populations. If social
                     tariffs are not effectively implemented, some low-income households could face difficulties accessing services.
                     Additionally, stakeholder opposition may arise if engagement strategies fail to address concerns identified in the
                     stakeholder mapping exercise. Community Health, Safety, and SEA/SH Risks – Construction activities pose risks of
                     accidents, temporary service disruptions, and unsafe excavation areas. Additionally, the presence of salaried
                     construction workers could lead to increased SEA/SH risks, requiring robust mitigation measures. Cumulative social
                     impacts may include compounded economic hardships from business disruptions, tariff changes, and evolving service
                     delivery models, especially for informal vendors and low-income users. Challenges also exist in coordinating E&S risk
                     management across multiple institutional entities in secondary cities with varying capacity levels. To mitigate these
                     risks, the project will implement a Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP), Livelihood Restoration Plans (if needed), and
                     enhanced grievance redress mechanisms to ensure inclusive and transparent decision-making in line with the ESF.

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                          Relevant

                      ESS2 applies across all project components, particularly infrastructure works, requiring significant labor deployment
                      for pipeline installation, water treatment plant upgrades, and rehabilitation. A Labor Management Procedures (LMP)
                      will be developed within the ESMF by Appraisal to address ESS2 risks, ensuring fair treatment, worker safety, OHS
                      compliance, and grievance handling. The project will enforce labor contract conditions aligned with Mozambique’s
                      labor laws, ESS2, EHSGs, and GIIP, covering minimum wages, working hours, benefits, and non-discrimination policies.
                      Contractors must ensure compliance with national labor regulations, including provisions against child and forced
                      labor. The LMP will also outline grievance mechanisms for workers and SEA/SH safeguards. These measures will apply
                      to direct, contracted, and primary supply workers throughout implementation.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                           Relevant

                      ESS3 is relevant, as the project involves resource use optimization and pollution prevention. Key risks include
                      construction waste management, hazardous materials use for water treatment, drinking water quality, and potential
                      cross-contamination between water and wastewater systems. The project will ensure compliance with national
                      regulations, WBG EHS guidelines, and WHO drinking water standards. Efficient water use will be prioritized, including
                      non-revenue water reduction and sustainable extraction to protect ecosystems. A water balance assessment will
                      minimize depletion risks. Infrastructure will be climate-resilient, addressing droughts and extreme weather events. To
                      mitigate these risks, the project will integrate resource efficiency, pollution control, and sustainable water
                      management through the ESMF, ESIAs, and ESMPs, ensuring safe operations and environmental sustainability across
                      all phases.



Public Disclosure
                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                           Relevant

                      ESS4 is relevant, as construction activities may pose risks such as traffic disruptions, accidents, hazardous material
                      exposure, and waterborne diseases. To mitigate these, the project will implement health and safety measures,
                      including proper signage, community awareness campaigns, and emergency response plans. Worker health and
                      safety protocols under ESS2 will be extended to protect communities from construction hazards. The project will also
                      address social risks, including GBV and SEA/SH, through SEA/SH Action Plans for implementing agencies, supported by
                      a SEA/SH-sensitive GRM. Emergency preparedness plans will ensure swift response to unforeseen incidents,
                      safeguarding community well-being. These measures, integrated into ESMF, ESIAs, and ESMPs, will ensure
                      comprehensive risk management throughout the project lifecycle.

                      ESS5 - Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement                               Relevant

                      ESS 5 is relevant. The project may cause both physical and economic displacement. Physical displacement could occur
                      due to land acquisition needs for infrastructure development, including for pipeline expansions, storage facility
                      upgrades, water treatment plants, and associated facilities in both Greater Maputo and secondary cities. Economic
                      displacement may result mainly from access restrictions of formal and informal businesses, and of agricultural
                      activities. While the project will seek to avoid and minimize these impacts, where unavoidable, the project develop
                      and implement a Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF) to guide the preparation of site-specific Resettlement Action
                      Plans (RAPs), when needed. The RPF will outline procedures for fair compensation, relocation assistance, livelihood
                      restoration, and support for affected informal businesses, with particular attention to vulnerable groups.

                     B.3 Other Salient Features

                       Use of Borrower Framework                                                                                              No
                       The Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890) will not rely on the Borrower’s framework and will fully
                       adhere to the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) to ensure consistent, high-quality risk
                       management. While Mozambique’s ESIA system under Decree No. 54/2015 provides a regulatory basis, gaps in risk
                       screening, social safeguards, biodiversity protection, and SEA/SH mitigation necessitate the ESF approach.




                    Apr 07, 2025                                                                                                       Page 7 of 9
                               The World Bank
                               Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890)



                       Key gaps include limited provisions for land acquisition, labor rights, stakeholder engagement, and SEA/SH risks,
                       requiring compliance with ESS2, ESS4, ESS5, and ESS10. Additionally, biodiversity and water resource risks will be
                       managed under ESS3 and ESS6, addressing pollution prevention and ecosystem protection. The project will also
                       implement a SEA/SH Action Plan with worker Codes of Conduct and survivor-centered GRM. Given these limitations,
                       ESF will ensure stronger safeguards, institutional capacity, and alignment with international best practices.


                       Use of Common Approach                                                                                                 No
                       At this stage, no financing partners have been identified for the Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890).
                       The project is solely financed through IDA resources, with no confirmed co-financing from other development
                       partners. Should additional financing partners be identified, their contributions will be assessed for alignment with the
                       World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) and integrated into the project’s risk management approach.

                     C. Overview of Required Environmental and Social Risk Management Activities

                     C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or
                     required by Appraisal?
                     This project requires the following E&S risk management instruments to ensure compliance with the Environmental and
                     Social Framework (ESF). These instruments will guide the identification, mitigation, and monitoring of risks throughout
                     the project lifecycle.

                     Instruments to be disclosed before Appraisal:



Public Disclosure

                     risk assessment, and mitigation measures. It will include guidelines for ESIAs and ESMPs, incorporating climate

                     Mechanism (GRM) with SEA/SH-sensitive protocols.

                     throughout implementation.
