    B. Introduction and Context

    Country Context

1.      Uganda's economy is on a steady recovery path, growing at 6.0% in FY2023/24 from a 5.3% increase in
FY2022/23, largely driven by oil and gas investments. Growth is expected to reach 6.6% in FY261, with oil exports
anticipated by the end of 2025. The Central Bank rate is 10%, with inflation declining from 7.2% in 2022 to 5.4%2 in 2023.

2.      Uganda's population is 46 million, up from 35 million in 2014, growing at 2.9%3 annually. Urban growth is 5.2%4
annually, with urban populations expected to surpass rural ones by 20605. Urban areas are key for economic and social
transformation, with job creation higher in cities than rural areas. They provide opportunities to transform from
agricultural to non-agricultural activities often characterized by higher incomes and improved socio- economic conditions.

3.       Uganda's National Development Plan IV (2025/26–2029/30) aims to “achieve higher household incomes and
employment for sustainable socio-economic transformation." It plans to create 2.5 million jobs, focusing on service and
agricultural sectors. The plan includes operationalizing new cities, investing in solid waste management infrastructure and
systems, implementing detailed physical plans, and improving transport infrastructure, including non-motorized transport
infrastructure and acquiring of transport/utility corridors.

4.      As part of the Paris Agreement, Uganda aims to reduce emissions by 24.7% in 2030. Uganda has several policies
and strategies in place to address climate challenges, including the 2021 National Climate Change Act which gives force of
law to the Paris Agreement. The Ministry of Water and Environment, Climate Change Department is responsible for
coordination, monitoring, and evaluation, as well as ensuring Uganda’s mitigation and adaptation obligations are met6.

5.      Women are underrepresented in the labor force and decision-making roles, with 68% of women participating in
the labor force compared to 72.4% of men. Women experience higher rates of vulnerable employment, which means they
are less likely to have social protection and more likely to fall into poverty. Women represent less than 30% of those
employed in senior and middle management positions and only 34% of national parliament7. Policy reforms have
improved gender equality, but barriers to women's participation and asset ownership remain.

     Sectoral (or multi-sectoral) and Institutional Context of the Program

6.       Urban areas are central to Uganda's economic growth, generating 70% of non-agricultural GDP and nearly half
of total GDP. However, urban job creation lags population growth, with the informal sector dominating employment.
Uganda has a youthful population, with a 70% labor force participation rate, yet only 24% of men and 14% of women hold
formal jobs. Improving job creation and labor force productivity is crucial.

7.      Despite 65% of new jobs being in urban centers, formal wage growth and job creation are limited due to a large
informal sector. Furthermore, the inadequate quality of the transport infrastructure also hinders the connection between
cities and external markets. Strengthening the formal sector and improving transport infrastructure can enhance
connectivity between cities and external markets, stimulating economic growth.

8.      Lack of integrated urban planning hampers development. Rapid urbanization pressures infrastructure, services,
and natural resources. Most areas lack updated spatial plans, leading to uncontrolled growth, environmental degradation,
and increased flooding. Local governments face challenges with weak land ownership systems and insufficient personnel,
while the Ministry of Lands, Housing, and Urban Development (MoLHUD) lacks capacity to support integrated planning.
Enhancing capacity for comprehensive urban and land use planning utilizing the latest technology can enhance organized
urbanization and investing in digital land records can increase efficiency and reduce disputes.

9.      Cities and towns face inadequate infrastructure, with urban roads making up just 13% of the national network
and only 6% paved8. Public transport and pedestrian infrastructure are limited, and poorly developed systems contribute
to congestion and flooding risks. Inadequate solid waste management exacerbates these issues, with uncollected waste
blocking streets and water bodies. Local government funding shortages lead to further deterioration and high repair costs.
There is a need to prioritize investment in urban resilient and climate smart infrastructure development, focusing on
expanding and upgrading road networks and paving more roads, including improving non-motorized transport to reduce
congestion and providing effective solid waste management systems.

10.     Local governments struggle with limited technical and financial resources, affecting urban planning, service
delivery, and regulation enforcement. Promoting intergovernmental collaboration and strengthening capacity-building
and institutional mechanisms is vital for sustainable urban growth.

11.    Urban areas in Uganda face climate change impacts9 and frequent floods10 will damage infrastructure and disrupt
urban economies and livelihoods. The urban heat island effect, compounded by climate change, threatens cities' livability
and competitiveness. Urban systems are vulnerable due to high exposure, sensitive infrastructure, and limited adaptive

capacity. Implementing climate-resilient urban planning and infrastructure policies and investing in green infrastructure

including improving drainages, and putting in early warning systems is necessary.

12.      Uganda hosts the largest number of refugees and asylum seekers in Africa, with 1.7 million as of October 2024.
Refugees have equal access to public services alongside Ugandan citizens. Whereas nearly 92% of the refugees live in
settlements alongside host communities in some of the poorest and most underdeveloped districts in the country, most
of them seek economic opportunities, jobs and better services in urban areas. This creates challenges for urban
infrastructure and service delivery in refugee host districts (RHDs). Building on lessons from USMID, continued support for
urban infrastructure is crucial to i) mitigate refugee influx impacts while fostering social and economic opportunities, ii)
promote socio-economic inclusion, iii) enhance preparedness for future refugee inflows, and iv) strengthen social cohesion
and peaceful co-existence among host communities and refugees.

 Relationship to CAS/CPF

13.     The proposed Program aligns with Uganda’s 2023–2028 Country Partnership Framework (CPF), building on
lessons from USMID to strengthen city and municipal governments’ capacity for urban service delivery. It directly supports
High-Level Outcome 1 – Improved Human Capital for Inclusive Development, particularly by enhancing local governance,
revenue collection, urban planning, and financial and environmental safeguards. It also promotes human capital
development through skilling and vocational training, fostering economic growth and resilience.

14.      Additionally, the Program contributes to High-Level Outcome 2 – More Productive Employment Opportunities
for Resilience, focusing on Objective 5: Improved Strategic Infrastructure Connectivity. It will rehabilitate urban roads with
pedestrian and cycling lanes to enhance mobility, reduce emissions, and support sustainable transport. Climate-smart
designs, nature-based solutions, and low-carbon development approaches will improve urban resilience and adaptive
capacity against climate change.

  Rationale for Bank Engagement and Choice of Financing Instrument

15.     National Urban Policy (NUP) adopted in 2017 aims to create livable, inclusive, and sustainable urban areas. Key
objectives include promoting integrated urban development, balanced regional growth, urban competitiveness,
environmental conservation, and good governance. The policy emphasizes enhancing economic opportunities, providing
decent housing, and strengthening urban planning and finance.

16.     Urban Development for Socio-Economic Transformation. The Government of Uganda (GoU) sees urban
development as critical for achieving middle-income status by 2040. Urbanization is prioritized in Uganda’s NDPIV (2025-
2030) and the NRM manifesto, with a focus on physical planning and infrastructure investments. The Sustainable
Urbanization and Housing Program (SUH) contributes to building strategic infrastructure in transport, housing, energy,
and ICT.

17.     Intergovernmental Fiscal Transfers. Uganda’s fiscal transfer system channels resources to local governments,
supporting sectoral expenditures like health, education, and transport. Transfers are divided into earmarked and
discretionary categories, funding local investments through revenues from grants, local sources, donors, and loans.

18.    The Window for Host Communities and Refugees (WHR). Building on successful lessons from USMID, the
support is required to strengthen social cohesion and promote peaceful co-existence among host communities and

19.     IDA Program for Results (PforR). IDA has significant experience in Uganda through programs like USMID, UGIFT,
and the GKMA UDP. The PforR modality has successfully enhanced government systems, with a focus on performance-
based funding and citizen consultations. It has contributed to improved performance and tangible outcomes in urban

the ten municipalities to cities. The USMID Program received an overall outcome rating of Satisfactory, having
contributed significantly to the urban agenda by strengthening the institutional capacity of the Program local
governments (LGs) and delivering key urban services in targeted municipalities and RHDs.

20.     Bank Approach and National Priorities. The UCMID Program will build on lessons from previous phases,
consolidating achievements and introducing innovative approaches. It will integrate an Investment Project Financing
(IPF) component to support policy reforms, institutional strengthening, climate resilience, and economic development.
The program will align with Uganda’s NDP IV, support transformative investments, and leverage funding from
development partners and the private sector.

21.     PforR with IPF Components: While the PforR instrument is well-suited for the proposed performance-based
grant for LGs, an IPF component is proposed to ensure alignment with government systems and incentives. This approach
will focus on clear intervention areas, strengthen decentralization, and use government systems to incentivize
performance at all levels. The inclusion of an IPF component will enhance policy reforms, capacity building, and
integrated urban planning, drawing on lessons from past programs in Uganda, Kenya, and Ethiopia.

 D. Program Description

 PforR Program Boundary

The Government program

22.      The Government of Uganda program (p) is the Sustainable Urbanization and Housing (SUH) Program that covers
all the urban areas nationally. It is one of the eighteen programs under the NDPIV for the period 2025–2030, with the goal
of “achieving higher household incomes and employment for sustainable socio-economic transformation.” The
government program includes five strategic objectives covering urban infrastructure, physical planning, drainage and
waste management, economic opportunities, affordable housing and policy, legal and institutional strengthening. The
UCMID Program will cover a subset of the SUH program and select investment areas considered eligible for PforR funding.
The program will cover ten secondary cities, eleven municipalities supported under the previous USMID Program, fifteen
new municipalities and eleven districts hosting refugees. The new municipalities can only be admitted to the program if
they demonstrate having the required minimum access capacity and physical development plan. The RHD will be included
in the Program subject to availability of grants and it shall focus on urban refugees.

Proposed UCMID Program boundary

23.      Intervention areas considered risky for implementation under the PforR arrangement will be excluded. The
following areas of the government program will not be included: (i) public transport, especially the BRT and LRT, (ii) SWM
infrastructure especially “brown fields”, and (iii) housing development services and institutions.

24.   USMID2 is structured around two components: a PforR component (Component 1) and an IPF component
(Component 2).

25.     Component 1 - PforR. The PforR component focuses on national and subnational urban development
performance and investments (DLIs 1 - 6), incentivizing national institutions and local governments to implement
interventions that contribute to achieving the Program outcomes. Implementing entities will access performance grants
based on compliance with the minimum access conditions (DLIs 1 &2) and agreed performance measures (DLIs 3-5). The
PforR component will finance resilient urban infrastructure, services, local economic development and institutional
capacity strengthening at the local government. The component will also incentivize institutional development at the
central government ministries supporting urban development at local level and promote interactions and peaceful
coexistence between host and refugee communities in refugee-hosting areas.

26.      Component 2 - IPF. The IPF component will directly support the achievement of the PforR DLIs by financing
activities that contribute to the Program’s outcomes. Component 2 will support national urban planning and policies,
technical capacity building activities and program management activities executed by the central coordinating ministry,
strengthen planning, coordination, monitoring and evaluation for improved service delivery; and support the
development of Cities’ Law to operationalize the newly created cities.

31.     The Program aims to build on the past successes of USMID, bringing significant social, environmental, and
economic benefits to participating cities and municipalities. The expected potential environmental risks, mainly associated
with activities under RA2, such as soil degradation, water pollution, noise, dust, and disruption to local businesses during
construction and social risks such as land loss, economic displacement, stakeholder exclusion, labor issues, spread of
diseases like HIV/AIDS, GBV/VAC/SH/SAE issues, labor management related impacts, delayed payment or non-payment
of community suppliers, and potential cultural property impacts are expected to be adequately mitigated. Mitigation
measures from previous programs will be included, and sub-projects with adverse effects or those outside the broader
GoU program will be excluded.

32.      For the PforR component, an Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) is being undertaken as part of
program preparation to assess the country's and more specifically, the participating cities and municipalities’ capacity to
address the adverse environmental and social risks and impacts, and to propose measures to strengthen these systems
where required during Program implementation. The findings from the ESSA will feed into the project design, specifically
the DLIs, and to the overall PAP. Additionally, the Program will also have a Program Operation Manual (POM) which will
include specific mechanisms to screen, assess and manage environment and social issues related to Sub-projects. Whereas
previous ESSAs have indicated that the country’s legislative and institutional frameworks as relevant to the Program’s
activities are largely adequate to guide the management of environmental and social risks and impacts at the national and
local government levels, there are weaknesses in the country environmental system performance as respects to
enforcement of laws, acqusition of statutory approvals, monitoring, staffing level, budget allocation for environmental,
social, health and safety management. The current assessment will be used to identify weaknesses and risks which need
to be strengthened and mitigated, and the measures of strengthening these weaknesses will be explored through
stakeholder consulatation.

33.     For the IPF component, the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) and its standards will guide
the management of environmental and social risks associated with the proposed TA activities. The Borrower will prepare
the Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP) and Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) prior to appraisal, while
Labor Management Procedures (LMP), and Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF) will be prepared during Program
implementation. As the component will involve development of policies and plans, the Borrower will be required to
undertake a Strategic Environmental and Social Assessment (SESA) during Program implementation, to ensure integration
of environmental and social considerations.

34.     Since the enactment of the Anti-Homosexuality Act, 2023 all projects in Uganda will include specific measures to
mitigate the risk of discrimination against or exclusion of any affected individuals or groups in providing or receiving
project benefits. The ESSA will identify potential measures to mitigate the risk of discrimination or exclusion of Sexual and
Gender Minorities (SGM) being provided or receiving project benefits following the enactment of the Anti-Homosexuality
Act. The Stakeholder Engagement Plan and other relevant instruments will also contain measures to ensure that there is
no discrimination towards SGM. UCMID will also benefit from an Enhanced Implementation Support and Monitoring
(EISM) firm hired directly by the World Bank to support the Project Implementation Support Team (PIST) and the World
Bank following the enactment of the Anti-Homosexuality Act 2023 in Uganda.

 Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts of the IPF Component


The hybrid PforR/IPF UCMID Program will enhance urban infrastructure, including road networks, solar street lighting,
stormwater drainage, urban greening, green parks, innovation centers, transport hubs, economic infrastructure, and solid

waste management, through direct investments under the PforR component and technical assistance activities under the
IPF component to support the PforR component’s outcomes. These investments will yield social, environmental, and
economic benefits for participating cities and municipalities. However, they may also pose substantial environmental and
social risks affecting natural, built, and human environments. Physical environmental risks include pollution, erosion,
sedimentation, noise, traffic interruptions, and damage to utilities due to civil works. Occupational Health and Safety risks
stem from construction activities, site clearance, and facility maintenance. Community Health and Safety risks from
inadequate communication about road and drainage works, causing disruptions to businesses and travel. Waste
Management risks during excavation, site clearing, and operations e.g. from economic projects such as markets and
recreation areas.

USMID has not directly addressed urban liquid and solid waste management, leading to waste accumulation in
municipalities due to limited transportation and funding. Infrastructure limitations in local governments affect waste
collection, temporary storage, and disposal. Many municipalities lack adequate land and treatment facilities, restricting
sustainable waste management efforts. Biodiversity and fragile environments face risks due to urban expansion affecting
essential ecological functions, including flood retention and carbon storage, among others. Peri-urban forests are
particularly vulnerable, with many already encroached or repurposed. New municipalities participating in UCMID may
experience similar environmental threats. Stormwater and drainage infrastructure risks persist despite USMID’s role in
linking road investments with drainage improvements. Cities and municipalities continue to face frequent, severe floods
affecting settlements and markets. While urban drainage master plans have helped, further attention is needed on
downstream impacts and evolving hydrology due to climate change. Hydrological studies and flood risk assessments are
necessary to refine drainage plans and hydraulic structures in cities, municipalities, and roads. Similarly, several potential
social risks are associated with the project, including issues with land acquisition and use restrictions, particularly since
sub-projects will be undertaken in urban areas that often lack comprehensive physical development plans. There may also
be disruptions to local businesses during construction, along with the risk of excluding stakeholders and discriminating
against vulnerable groups or individuals. Other concerns include labor issues, the possibility of a nonfunctional Grievance
Mechanism (GM), potential loss of cultural resources, the spread of diseases, and risks related to gender-based violence
(GBV) and Sexual Exploitation and Abuse/Sexual Harassment (SEA/SH).

Experience from the USMID project revealed unintended consequences in managing land acquisition, including an
uncoordinated approach to land donations. Additionally, several municipalities faced challenges in handling grievances,
with some stakeholder concerns being reported directly to the Bank. For the PforR component of the Program an
Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) is under preparation by the Bank with input and support from the
GoU. The ESSA seeks to identify any gaps in the existing national system for environment and social risks management,
provide recommendations and remedial measures to strengthen system performance. Based on previous assessments,
there is a weakness in the country's environmental system performance as regards enforcement of laws, monitoring,
staffing level, budget allocation for environmental, social, health and safety management. The measures for strengthening
these documented weaknesses will be explored through stakeholder consultation. The findings from the ESSA will feed
into the project design, specifically the Disbursement Linked Indicators (DLIs) and the overall Program Action Plan. For the
IPF component, the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) and its standards will guide the management
of indirect and downstream environmental and social risks if the proposed TA informs future policies, strategies, or
