    I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT
    A. Project Strategic Context

1. Niger, a landlocked Sahelian country that stretches over 1,267,000 kilometers (km)² and is home to 26.1 million
inhabitants,1 heavily depends on its road infrastructure and the agropastoral sector to drive socioeconomic
development, fight food insecurity, prevent conflict, and mitigate forced displacement. The country’s economy relies
on agriculture, which employs about 80 percent of the workforce and is the main contributor to gross domestic product
(GDP) at 40 percent.2 Poor road connectivity and rural access, as well as logistical challenges, impact agropastoral sector
performance and economic prospects to fight the pervasive food insecurity.3 With a 50.6 percent of the population living
in extreme poverty,4 ensuring that youth, women,5 and other vulnerable groups, such as refugees and host communities
(RHC)6 are gainfully employed sets communities on a path to recovery and economic growth. Employment also lowers
fragility and reduces the incidence of conflict, especially in the context of climate change, which exacerbates these
challenges.
2. Niger relies on access to its strategic corridors to improve its international trade, particularly with Nigeria and other
neighboring countries. The National Road 1 (RN1) is the lifeline of the country and the backbone of its economy, serving
as the artery for about 70 percent of its population, supporting close to 88 percent of its agricultural activities,7 and
connecting the borders of Mali, Burkina Faso, Nigeria, and Chad. As illustrated in Figure 1, the RN1 comprises the following:
(i) the Malian border–Niamey section, which is part of the Trans-Saharan Road (TSR);8 (ii) the Niamey–Maradi section,
which is part of the Trans-Sahelian Highway (TSH); 9 (iii) the Maradi–Zinder section, which connects the TSH and TSR within
the RN1; and (iv) the Zinder–Chadian border section, which is also part of the TSR corridor. The Maradi–Zinder section of
the RN1 serves approximately 16.4 million people, 5.2 million in Niger and 11.2 million in Nigeria.10 Ensuring all-year
resilient connectivity between the RN1, the TSH and the TSR corridor will improve access, agropastoral produce flow, and
the trade potential of selected value chains at the subregional level.
3. Niger, classified as fragile and conflict-affected, is grappling with increasing forced displacement. This is due to
internal fragility and cross-border insecurity from neighboring Mali, Burkina Faso, Chad, and Nigeria. As of March 2025,
Niger hosted 985,784 forcibly displaced persons, including 431,072 refugees and asylum seekers and 507,438 internally
displaced persons (IDP), highlighting the continued cross-border spillover conflict dynamics (see map, Figure 5).11 Forcibly
displaced populations are mainly in the Tahoua, Maradi, and Diffa Regions (Nigerian refugees) and in the Tillabéry and
northern Tahoua Regions (Malian and Burkinabè refugees). More than 80 percent of refugees and asylum seekers are
women and children. Niger’s out-of-camp policy, continued by the new authorities, facilitates local integration and self-
sufficiency, with most refugees living in informal settlements or with host communities in rural and peri-urban areas.
While poverty rates are high across all groups, they are highest among refugee and IDP households. The inflow of refugees
and IDPs into these rural communities exacerbates existing challenges, straining already limited resources and increasing
poverty, unemployment, human capital deficits, and inadequate basic services. The poor infrastructure, including
degraded roads, hinders access to basic social services and disrupts agropastoral value chain activities in these
communities. Despite barriers to accessing land and assets, agriculture remains one of the most viable pathways for

4. The Government of Niger (GoN) has adopted a comprehensive approach to improve the institutional environment
and find long-term solutions for RHC. This approach includes: (i) closing refugee camps and developing humanitarian land
allocations similar to social housing and villages of opportunity,14 facilitating the controlled resettlement and integration
of over 45,000 refugees since 2019; and (ii) signing international conventions on refugees, displaced persons, and stateless
persons, and adopting legal instruments to ensure refugees have equal socio-economic rights as citizens, such as property,
security, access to courts, basic services, freedom of expression, and free movement. The GoN has internalized the
Kampala Convention through a national law passed in December 2018 for the assistance and protection of displaced
persons. Additionally, an ordinance was issued to revise the 1997 refugee law and its 1998 implementing decree to
enhance efficiency and fairness. Decree n°2019-463 was enacted to allow systematic registration of civil status events in
refugee camps, and prima facie refugee status has been granted to Nigerian nationals fleeing insecurity in certain northern
states. 15 Coordination and cooperation have been strengthened through collaboration between several governmental
institutions and the UNHCR, focusing on improving service delivery, knowledge transfer, and monitoring and evaluation
(M&E). To address land access issues, a memorandum of understanding was signed with the ministry in charge of urban
planning in January 2020 to strengthen legal access to land for housing, leading to the creation of humanitarian land
allocations or urbanized sites providing land titles or deeds guaranteeing free occupancy. Infrastructure development has
also been a priority, with over 6,426 houses built on humanitarian land allocations and plans to further develop

strategy represents a long-term vision to address the challenges faced by RHC, with continued support from the World
Bank considered essential for implementing these political reforms and institutional changes.
5. Women face major mobility barriers in accessing maternity care in Niger. In sub-Saharan Africa, the travel time for
women to access obstetric and health care is about 180 minutes.16 In comparable countries, women may travel up to 240
minutes to access obstetric care.17 It is expected that this travel time is longer in Niger particularly in the project areas
which are mainly rural. Contributing factors are among others, limited access to reliable transportation and poor-quality
roads that are safe and reliable.18 Gender-based violence (GBV) and harmful practices such as child marriage are prevalent
in the country, with 76.3 percent of girls aged 20 to 24 married before the age of 18 and 28 percent before the age of 15.19
6. Climate change can have detrimental impacts on the road network and connectivity, farming, livestock herding,
markets, and food value and supply chains. Niger's vulnerability to climate change is evident from the recent increases
in sandstorms, rising temperatures, extreme heat, droughts, and floods. The agropastoral sector in Niger is highly
susceptible to these severe climate shocks, which adversely impact water resources, soil quality, agricultural production,
livestock herding, human livelihoods, food security, and economic growth. The 2024 rainy season, which has been linked
to linked to climate change,20 caused extreme floods that led to destroyed homes, vegetation and businesses; hundreds
of deaths and injuries; and damaged drinking water and road infrastructure, including the RN1 Maradi–Zinder section.21

     B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

7. Niger faces infrastructure deficits that have been detrimental to primary-sector value chains, especially in the
Eastern Plains.22 About 95 percent of domestic and foreign trade depends on roads. Niger’s trade system is dominantly
informal with fragmented transport services.23 The lack of professionalization of the transport and logistics sector,
combined with a weak regulatory environment and a poorly enforced procedures, results in low competitiveness. These
insufficiencies combined with inadequate road infrastructure and cascading logistical challenges result in economic losses,
food shortages, and high spoilage rates in Niger's most productive agropastoral zones.
8. Poor road quality, high transport costs, and cumbersome border-crossing procedures hinder agropastoral trade,
access to basic social services, and deepen inequality and poverty among RHC and non-RHC. The road network comprises
approximately 22,787 km of roads, of which under 25 percent are paved, with a national average Rural Access Index of
38.1 percent,24 with southern Niger exhibiting considerably higher levels of connectivity compared to the northern
regions.25 In some areas, unpaved roads become impassable during the rainy season, which coincides with the peak period
when RHC transports their farm produce to markets or to storage facilities. The lack of roadside emergency assistance

10. Despite road maintenance reform efforts,28 financial sustainability as well as annual maintenance planning and
execution remain a key sectoral challenge. Managed across several institutions, namely the Road Network Management
and Monitoring Directorate (Direction de Gestion et de Suivi des Réseaux, DGSR), the Road Maintenance Fund (Fonds

asset management systems. Road maintenance faces significant challenges due to a combination of factors, including: a
vast and deteriorated road network; extreme weather conditions; limited financial resources; capacity constraints;
planning delays; and governance and contractual management issues.30 This conjunction of factors has resulted in
maintenance backlogs and an accelerated continuous loss of road asset value. Equally challenging, investment priorities
and funding are frequently directed toward reconstruction rather than targeting maintainable sections of the road
network that would maximize the value for money in a constrained budget environment.
11. A transition to medium-term planning and multi-year contracting for routine and periodic road maintenance can
improve investment efficiency. This approach would create greater efficiency and effectiveness of road maintenance
operations, ensure better infrastructure quality and connectivity in Niger, as well as strengthen preparedness and
response for urgent works due to climate change impacts. However, this requires the DGSR to transition from the current
reactive approach, which prioritizes reconstruction and rehabilitation, to proactive risk-informed strategies that
emphasize climate resilience and extend the lifespan of assets. The recently established Second Generation Road
Maintenance Fund31 managed by FER collected only about US$20 million32 to partially finance a US$75 million annual
program (2024) of periodic and routine maintenance and emergency works for the total network.33 While the road reform
process currently stipulates that 90 percent of the revenue allocation is to be dedicated to road maintenance, only about
one third of the funds collected in 2022 were allocated to FER, while the remaining stayed in treasury. Furthermore,
AMODER was only able to commit approximately 50 percent of the available resources in FER through an inefficient annual
contracting approach. To further advance Niger’s road asset management reform agenda, it is essential to enhance
programming robustness, institutionalize additional funding streams and improve contract management.
12. Improved climate-resilient road connectivity and subregional trade facilitation would enhance opportunities
between Niger and Nigeria. Trade between Niger and Nigeria is vitally important to regional economic integration. In
2022, Niger officially exported US$91.6 million to Nigeria while importing US$287 million.34 Southern Niger’s agropastoral
production represents an important share of the trade flows to northern Nigeria, especially for cowpeas, onions,35 and
livestock,36 as illustrated in Figure 3. For instance, Maradi and Zinder account for 45 percent of the total national
production of cowpeas, with average yields of 479 and 459 kilograms/hectare respectively,37 with women leading their
processing and selling. However, total trade volumes and values are likely underreported, highlighting the level of informal
cross-border activities and inefficiencies in the management of formal border facilities. As such, strategic investments in
the southern RN1 corridor basin, together with surrounding feeder road connections, can unlock the economic potential
of the rural catchment areas and densely populated border areas through improved access to larger markets.

14. Escalating security challenges and climate-related risks have altered migration patterns in the Maradi area, further
affecting food security and financial inclusion for RHC. Despite the region’s productive agropastoral resources, food
insecurity remains a challenge, especially for RHC which suffer from poor access to essential services such as healthcare,
education, and markets. This particularly affects underserved areas in need of assistance during extreme weather events,
which can lead to food shortages and consequent humanitarian crises such as forced displacement.40 Security concerns,
particularly in border areas with Nigeria plagued by insurgency and banditry, further complicate the situation. To foster
social cohesion, it is critical to include all these communities in development projects; however, limited access to improved
technologies and finance undermines opportunities for RHC and rural communities overall. All weather road infrastructure
facilitates the transport of goods and services, which is essential for the economic integration of refugees. By connecting
agricultural production zones to local markets, roads help RHC engage in trade and improve their livelihoods. Improved
road infrastructure is also critical for the delivery of humanitarian aid and relief to refugees in affected areas as it ensures
that aid can reach those in need promptly, especially during emergencies. For instance, an UNHCR report41 indicates that
all three villages of opportunity in the Maradi region lack reliable all-weather road access to markets, agropastoral
production sites and essential services, which ultimately disproportionally affects refugees in the region of Maradi. While
lack of access also affects non-refugees, refugees in Niger are more likely to live in poverty compared to the host
population due to the poor economic environment, which depends on road connectivity and rural access.

17. Niger faces significant road safety challenges due to poorly maintained and aging vehicle fleets. In 2023, the Road
Safety Agency of Niger (Agence Nigérienne de la Sécurité Routière, ANISER) reported 1,152 fatalities, while the World
Health Organization (WHO) estimated the annual mortality rate at 6,278 people. 48 Fatal road crashes in Niger are often
caused by inadequate road infrastructure, unroadworthy vehicles, excessive speeding, overloading of trucks, and poor
road user behavior. In 2020, there were 354,723 vehicle registrations, with 11.7 percent in Maradi and Zinder Regions.
Although Niger has regulations on the import of used vehicles, including a five-year age limit and required inspections,
these inspections are poorly regulated and not enforced. 49 The National Society for Automobile Control (Société nationale
de Contrôle Automobile, SNCA) operates one inspection station in Niamey, and the Niger Automobile Single Window
(Fonds d'Entretien Routier, GUAN) also has one station, both with lines for light and heavy vehicles. However, regional
directorates lack technical inspection stations and rely on visual inspections for vehicle registration. Establishing vehicle
inspection centers in Maradi and Zinder could gradually improve the quality of regional vehicle fleet stock.

 C. Project Beneficiaries
25. The project will primarily benefit communities in the RN1 Maradi–Zinder basin, including vulnerable groups such
as RHC, women, and children. An estimated 16.4 million people (5.2 million in Niger and 11.2 million in Nigeria) within
150 km of the Maradi-Zinder section of the RN1 will benefit indirectly through improved climate-resilient connectivity,
access to services and infrastructure, and increased trade and economic opportunities. The impacted communities include
2,561,000 hosts (excluding IDPs), 18,697 IDPs, and 80,078 refugees. While all beneficiaries will enjoy enhanced
connectivity, better access to rural social services, and an improved environment for value chain logistics; the project’s
RHC focus aims to amplify social benefits by integrating RHC into the transport network. This will foster economic
integration while improving livelihoods, and expanding access to social services, markets, goods, and job opportunities.
Beneficiaries also include rural, peri-urban, and urban communities in the RN1 Maradi-Zinder basin. The project is
expected to create job opportunities during construction, especially for RHC, who will exclusively benefit from training
and job opportunities in the construction industry. Upgrading the road sections will increase the movement of goods and
passengers between Niamey and Agadez in Niger and several major cities in northern Nigeria.

 D. Project Components
26. Component 1: Climate-resilient improvement of Niger’s transport backbone (US$391.9 million equivalent; US$345.4
million IDA credit, US$19.5 WHR IDA credit, US$20.0 WHR IDA grant, and US$7.0 million counterpart funding). This
component benefits indirectly 80,078 refugees, 18,697 IDPs and 2,561,000 hosts excluding IDPs in the Maradi region
through the rehabilitation/construction of key roads and drainage works to improve climate-resilient connectivity and
access to basic infrastructure, services and job opportunities towards enhanced living conditions for RHC in Niger.
• Subcomponent 1.1: Climate-resilient rehabilitation of the RN1 (US$304.0 million; US$292.5 million IDA credit, US$11.5
WHR IDA credit). This subcomponent will strengthen the resilience of Niger’s road network and the communities it serves
by rehabilitating and upgrading the 232.89 km RN1 Maradi–Zinder road section to climate-resilient standards. A detailed

climate vulnerability analysis60 identified this section as one of its most vulnerable sections to climate change, as evidenced
by recent years’ damage and indirect losses due to climate-exacerbated events. This rehabilitation and upgrade will unlock
systemic resilience of the road network by reducing climate change vulnerability in areas with high agropastoral potential
and RHC concentration, while improving all-year climate-adaptive connectivity in southern Niger. Adaptation
interventions to be implemented include mainstreaming natured-based solutions (NBS), stabilizing dunes, improving
drainage structures, and other risk reduction measures that will be reinforced during routine and periodic maintenance
phases to ensure all-year climate-adapted connectivity and accessibility.61 The project will also finance facilities for non-
motorized transport (NMT) such as cycling and walking, including pedestrian crossings, pathways, and sidewalks with
solar-powered street lighting at urban crossings. Design-stage road safety audits will ensure that upgraded roads integrate
effective speed management strategies, including traffic-calming measures and appropriate road safety horizontal and
vertical signaling for safe road connectivity. While this component will benefit both RHC and non-RHC populations,
approximately 15 percent of the indirect beneficiaries of the Maradi-Zinder road section are part of the RHC population.
Given that the RHC population will benefit from improved connectivity due to the rehabilitation of this key road section,
it is proposed that less than 5 percent of the financing needs be covered by the WHR IDA credit. The improved connectivity
to this corridor plays a vital role in enhancing the quality of life for refugees in Niger by providing better access to essential
services, economic opportunities, and ensuring their protection and integration into the host communities. Improved
corridor infrastructure helps stabilize fragile areas by enhancing the presence of the state and integrating refugees into
the national administrative system.
• Subcomponent 1.2: Climate-resilient rehabilitation/construction and upgrading of selected feeder and rural roads
and construction of drainage systems within the RN1 basin (US$69.4 million; US$41.4 million IDA credit, US$8.0 WHR IDA
credit, US$20.0 WHR IDA grant). This subcomponent will strengthen the resilience of the Niger’s road network and of
communities served through financing the rehabilitation and upgrading to climate-resilient standards of approximately:
(i) 25 km of feeder roads connecting to the RN1 Maradi–Zinder section; (ii) 500 km of rural roads connecting to the rural
hinterland of the RN1 basin in the Maradi–Zinder regions; and (iii) construction of 50 km of concrete block gutters in areas
with high flood exposure, deploying the high labor intensity (Haute Intensité de Main d'œuvre, HIMO) method.62 All roads
will be selected following a climate risk-informed prioritization methodology that considers their contribution to the
resilience of the road network, complemented by Geographic Information System (GIS) analyses and consultations with
community groups, particularly women and RHC, taking into account their specific mobility needs.63 A total of 240 km of
rural roads will be rehabilitated/constructed in areas with high refugees’ population within the Maradi region, while the
remaining 260 km of rural roads will target non-RHC populated areas within the basin. The 240 km
rehabilitated/constructed rural roads will connect areas populated exclusively by RHC with agricultural production sites,
health facilities, schools and markets, including connecting better Niger’s only three villages of opportunity, Chadakori,
Dan Dadjin Makaou and Garin Kaka, as well as other RHC within the Maradi region. It is estimated that 80,078 refugees,
18,697 IDPs and 2,561,000 hosts excluding IDPs will directly benefit from enhanced access and all-weather connectivity,
and this will lead to increased trade and economic potential of the region. This is particularly important for refugees who

construction and drainage maintenance, enhancing job prospects beyond this project for at least 2,500 refugees and 500
host community members, focusing on women and youth. Additionally, it will provide direct employment within the
project for approximately 400 refugees and 100 host community members, targeted among the most vulnerable RHC. The

 F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

30. The project's design has been informed by the findings of the West Africa Development Corridor Strategy (P180071),
which aims to foster the next generation of regional transport development projects to create “basins of integration.”
The concept of basins of integration emphasizes the importance of continued investment in major trade corridors
complemented by investments to support value chains and intraregional trade that effectively maximize local beneficiaries
and the greater resilience of communities. This approach enables improved connectivity for intraregional trade and local
productivity, with spillovers to local development and resilience of communities to climate change, food insecurity, and

                                                                                                                                             Page 14
              The World Bank
              Southern Niger Connectivity and Integration Project (P179770)


conflicts. As part of this strategy, a diagnostic was conducted on the connectivity bottlenecks that hinder Niger’s
commercial potential and its resilience to shocks. Thus, the project seeks to improve climate-resilient road connectivity
(Component 1) to leverage the enabling of value chain development, multimodality, and trade facilitation (Component 2).
31. Projects operating in a fragility, conflict, and violence context should apply an adaptive strategy and simple design
that enables project implementation in alternative sites based on the security situation, building upon dynamic security
management plans (SMP) in the existing portfolio that acknowledge the need for flexibility in conflict-affected areas. This
requires a high degree of institutional coordination, supervision, and proactiveness to mitigate potential security risks
without compromising social cohesion in these areas. The use of the World Bank’s Geo-Enabling Initiative for Monitoring
and Supervision (GEMS) to integrate remote supervision capabilities into the implementation arrangements is a valuable
lesson coming from transport operations in the Sahel. This approach has improved the usefulness and accuracy of M&E
activities, providing the client and other stakeholders better visibility of the progress and challenges at project sites. It has
also facilitated the administration of perception/beneficiary surveys and captured results on intangible outcomes such as
social cohesion.
32. RHC beneficiary–informed design ensures that vulnerable groups actively participate in the decision-making
process, leading to greater ownership and sustainability of investments. Global experience confirms that consultations
with and participation of such targeted beneficiaries have instrumental benefits, such as: (i) better needs assessments; (ii)
empowerment and capacity building of affected communities; and (iii) inclusion of marginalized groups toward improved
social cohesion. To ensure that RHC benefit from better access to socioeconomic infrastructure, agropastoral production
sites, logistics facilities, and markets within the RN1 basin, prioritization of rural and feeder roads will be informed through
beneficiary surveys and consultation. Additionally, the proposed HIMO approach for drainage that has been successfully
implemented in similar security environments within the Sahel region has proven to provide temporary job opportunities
for RHC with the prospect of gaining career skills in the construction sector.64 Lastly, insights from the study under
Subcomponent 2.1 on integrating RHC informal women’s groups into potential financing mechanisms (e.g., matching grant
schemes) for revenue-generating activities will guide future value chain development in similar contexts. This is
particularly relevant given the increasing number of forcibly displaced persons across the region and the need to foster
their socioeconomic self-reliance. This study will provide recommendations on strengthening the capacity for the
preparation of applications, including those of RHC. This is key for the success of the potential financing mechanisms for
the value chain development study being financed by the project.
33. Two World Bank operations in Niger and Uganda supported by WHR financed the rehabilitation of roads, resulting
in improved access to public services and economic opportunities for RHC. The Niger Refugees and Host Communities
Support Project (P164563) successfully provided 27 km of newly built or rehabilitated rural roads, improving access to
public services for 63,002 beneficiaries despite extreme insecurity, leading to the cancellation of certain road sections.
Therefore, this project has included in its design a long list of rural roads that exceed the total target of rehabilitated rural
roads aimed by the project to account for adjustments due to insecurity, for example, if necessary, while ensuring targeted
enhancements for RHC are met. The Uganda Roads and Bridges in the Refugee Hosting Districts Project (P171339) aims
to rehabilitate a total of 105 km of roads and during its implementation, the project has emphasized extensive stakeholder
consultations and engagements during the preparation of E&S impact assessments, coupled with a strong grievance
redress mechanism. Both projects emphasize the importance of a participatory approach to foster community ownership
and ensure that interventions are relevant and effective. Additionally, they include an extensive list of roads to be
rehabilitated, promoting adaptive implementation based on the dynamic security context. These elements are key
components of the project design.

34. The implementation approach of introducing longer-term performance-based road maintenance contracts, as

 III. PROJECT IMPLEMENTATION
 A. Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

35. The project's institutional structure includes a Steering Committee, a Technical Committee, and a Project
Coordination Unit (PCU) under the ministry in charge of transportation and equipment. The ministry in charge of
economy and finances represents the GoN on financing issues and will guide the use of counterpart funds, particularly to
ease the mobilization and allocation of counterpart funds for sustainable road maintenance. Additional key ministries
include the ministry in charge of interior affairs, the ministry in charge of agriculture and livestock, the ministry in charge
of commerce and industry, the ministry in charge of public health and social affairs, the ministry in charge of hydraulics,
sanitation and environment, and the ministry in charge of foreign affairs.
36. At the national level, the ministry in charge of transportation and equipment oversees the project, with a Steering
Committee that is chaired by the Secretary General. It will provide general strategic direction and oversee project

  Technical Analysis
43. Basins of integration project design. A technical analysis of the Maradi–Zinder zone of the Eastern Plains of southern
Niger was conducted through a Global Facility for Decarbonization of Transport (GFDT) grant in June 2024. The study
collected insights on regional value chain dynamics, trade and mobility patterns within the rural border, market prices and
structure, and climate-related or fragility, conflict, and violence risks. The collected data has been instrumental in
identifying regional development barriers and shaping the proposed investment strategy, which informed the design of a
long list of activities to be financed. Details about design considerations that contribute to climate resilience at the
systemic level, as well as resilience “through” and “of” measures are described in Annex 2. The study to be financed under
Subcomponent 2.1 will position Niger for future financing, particularly for trade and value chain logistics investments. The
study will consider a broad range of financial instruments and will justify and propose the implementation approach for
the financing mechanisms to be chosen. This study may feature the use of credit enhancement grants and PPCG in
alignment with the Livestock and Agriculture Modernization Project (LAMP, P179276) which already employs these two
financial instruments to support value chain development in the project implementation area.
44. Periodic and routine maintenance. The GoN has pledged to maintain the RN1 through a sector policy letter signed by
the MTE and endorsed by the ministry in charge of economy and finances (Annex 4), which formalizes its commitment to
enhance road maintenance departments and update laws for funding; ensure timely fund availability for the FER and
expand its financial base; and improve capacities of institutions and MSME. The multi-year climate-resilient routine and
periodic road maintenance pilot will introduce a multi-year contract modality designed to improve the long-term
sustainability of road assets. The minimum eligibility criteria for road sections to be considered for this program are as
follows: (i) the road cannot be severely deteriorated; (ii) it has to be recently rehabilitated and have an adequate bearing
capacity; (iii) it should be programed for periodic maintenance followed by routine maintenance (or vice versa); (iv) it will
be subjected to appropriate technical studies to confirm its adequacy for the proposed activity;66 and (v) the financing gap
for periodic maintenance needs to be financed by FER (in addition to its routine maintenance). This program will
implement the concept of prevention through anticipatory actions to identify the infrastructure most vulnerable to
climate change-induced events and to prevent severe quality concerns and/or infrastructure failure.
45. RN1 Maradi–Zinder design: The existing section comprises three homogeneous sections based on the latest
rehabilitation works (1994, 2005, and 2009). The road is currently in a degraded state, with a travel speed of less than 60
km/h. It does not meet international road standards; there is a high risk of accidents, and it is very vulnerable to climate
change. Detailed technical design studies have been completed, informed by a Global Center on Adaptation (GCA) study
to improve infrastructure maintenance, road safety, and resilience. Rehabilitation works will use UEMOA geometric
standards to do the following: (i) widen and upgrade the two-lane road to 7.00 m, with 1.50 m shoulders outside urban
areas; (ii) widen to an 8.00 m two-lane road with 2.00 m paved sidewalks in medium-sized urban crossings; (iii) widen to
a two-lane 2x2 road with a 2.00 m central median, totaling 14.00 m of drivable lanes plus 2.00 m paved sidewalks on each
side in large urban crossings; and (iv) build 2.50 m wide longitudinal parking areas at urban entrances. Pavement structures
have been designed using a heat-resistant Thin Bituminous Concrete Overlay (Béton Bitumineux à Module Élevé, BBME)

48. Implementation risk reduction through a feasibility studies approach. The streamlined focus on feasibility studies
and institutional arrangements makes implementation more straightforward and less risky. The project avoids direct
financial implementation risks, such as weak absorption capacity among financial intermediaries, MSMEs, or difficulties in
fund disbursement. By shifting to value chain and trade facilitation studies, the project enables private sector engagement,
ensuring that logistics operators, exporters, and traders can contribute to shaping future investments. Consequently,
under the technical leadership of the DGTPI: (i) institutional partners will consult with financial institutions involved in the
LAMP (e.g., commercial banks, the Sahelian Financial Company – SAHFI67) for the logistics value chain support program to
inform suitable recommendations applicable to the informal value chain logistics context; and (ii) the project team will
involve exporters, transporters, logistics firms and the private sector during the study phase and next-phase
design/implementation planning for multimodality and regional trade facilitation infrastructure. Given the strategic
nature of these investments, the project will establish a clear timeline for transitioning from feasibility to implementation,
determining a timeline for designing a follow-up project and a robust study dissemination strategy with key stakeholders
and development partners. This participatory approach will ensure that the recommendations and designs resulting from
feasibility studies are aligned with market needs, while providing a clear pathway for transitioning from studies to
investment implementation and scaling up the recommendations.

Paris Alignment
51. The project is aligned with the goals of the Paris Agreement on both mitigation and adaptation. The following
paragraphs summarize the Paris Alignment assessment, with additional details in Annex 2.
52. The project is consistent with Niger’s Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC)68 and National Adaptation Plan
(NAP),69 both of which emphasize the need to enhance the resilience of infrastructure, including roads, bridges, and water
access features. The NDC also underlines the importance of improving energy efficiency in internal combustion engine
vehicles, imposing restrictions on secondhand vehicles, and promoting the installation of energy-efficient, solar-powered
street lighting. This project supports both climate adaptation and mitigation objectives as it supports the rehabilitation
and upgrading of strategic road corridor,70 feeder, and rural roads to climate-resilient standards, employing both
engineering and NBS to strengthen the climate resilience of road infrastructure. In addition, the project involves enhancing
drainage systems, deploying NMT facilities, installing energy-efficient solar-powered street lighting, and strengthening
two vehicle inspection facilities to improve motorization management. The project also supports studies and initiatives to
enhance the efficiency of transport systems, logistics, and trade facilitation. The operation also supports
recommendations in the Country Climate and Development Report (CCDR) for the G5 Sahel region, which recognizes that
the region’s transport systems are extremely vulnerable to climate impacts, and the critical need to invest in roads
improvements, road maintenance, emergency works, as well as relevant policy reforms to reduce economic losses from
climate change, and improve transport efficiency and lower freight transport costs.71 The project includes a CERC, and the
task team will ensure that all eligible activities included in the CERC Manual/CERC Annex of the PIM are aligned.
53. Assessment and reduction of climate mitigation risks. The project supports activities that are universally aligned.
These include the rehabilitation and upgrading of the RN1 Maradi–Zinder (232.89 km) road section, the
rehabilitation/construction and upgrading approximately 25 km of feeder roads connecting to the RN1, and 500 km of
rural roads connecting to the rural hinterland in these regions, as well as the construction of 50 km of concrete block
gutters and drainage systems within the RN1 basin. The road rehabilitation and upgrading works will be done without
capacity expansion for private motor vehicles and with enhancements for climate resilience, safety, and accessibility
purposes. New rural and feeder modern earth roads that will be constructed to provide connectivity to rural areas do not
create barriers to NMT or long-distance public transport, do not contribute to urban sprawl or deforestation, and cannot
be substituted by lower transport modes such as railways or inland waterways. The project also invests in technical
assistance, including for a support program for agropastoral value chains through logistics, and for investments for a dry
port, border post and small onion export counter that does not hinder the country’s emission mitigation goals. All other
activities are considered low risk, more details are provided in Annex 2. The project is therefore assessed to be aligned
with the Paris Agreement from a mitigation perspective.

investments are increases in maximum temperature and increases in the frequency of dust storms, sandstorms, and
floods. The project design includes measures to reduce risks from these climate hazards and keep them from threatening

proposed PCU has vast experience and a good track record implementing World Bank–financed projects. The objective of
the FM assessment was to determine whether this PCU has adequate FM arrangements to ensure that: (i) project funds
will be used for purposes intended in an efficient and economical way; (ii) project financial reports will be prepared in an
accurate, reliable, and timely manner; (iii) project assets will be safeguarded; and (iv) the project is subjected to a
satisfactory auditing process. The assessment included budgeting, staffing, financial accounting, financial reporting, funds
flow, disbursements, and internal and external auditing arrangements.76
64. The assessment revealed that this PCU has established an acceptable FM organization that includes the following:
(i) the use of a manual of administrative, finance, and accounting procedures (currently under revision); (ii) an FM team
comprising of one accountant led by a FM Specialist; and (iii) the use of TOM2PRO accounting software, which has the
necessary FM module. In addition, this PCU has established: (i) a treasury management system that features one DA for
each financial partner; (ii) a periodic reporting system; and (iii) an annual audit requirement. The 2022 and 2023 financial
statements were submitted on time and certified with an unqualified opinion.
65. The assessment concludes that this PCU’s FM organization meets the minimum criteria required for a World Bank-
financed project. However, the fiduciary risk is assessed to be Substantial due to key risks, including the following: (i) the
security concerns in some implementing areas (especially in remote areas upcountry); (ii) the additional workload due to
the new project activities; and (iii) delays funds disbursed justification.
66. To mitigate the FM risks to the extent acceptable to the World Bank, this PCU will need to implement an action
plan consisting of: (i) recruiting one additional accountant fully dedicated to the new project; (ii) acquiring a computerized
accounting information system for the management of the proposed project, with specifications acceptable to the World
Bank (within three months after effectiveness); (iii) updating the terms of reference of the entire existing FM team,
including the internal auditor, to take into account the particularities of the new project (within one month after
effectiveness); and (iv) recruiting an external auditor (within six months after effectiveness).

77. The overall risk for this project is rated High. This reflects substantial macroeconomic risk, political and governance,
institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability, combined with high stakeholders, fiduciary and overall E&S
risks. Additionally, Niger is vulnerable to multiple fragility and conflict risks, health/pandemic risks, and climate risks (e.g.,

78. Political and governance risks: Substantial. On July 26, 2023, Niger experienced an unconstitutional change of
government, which led to economic sanctions, border closures, and the interruption of external financing flows for more
than six months. A Charter of Refoundation, with constitutional value, was adopted and promulgated on March 26, 2025.80
Currently, while financing from most development banks has resumed, most development activities funded by European
Union partners have not. Political instability could affect the implementation of this operation. Institutional changes occur,
and this could include the department in charge of refugees. The mitigation of this risk lies in the design of the overall
program, which adopts a population-centered approach, targeting the basic needs of the Nigerien population through
food and nutritional security, as well as agricultural production, which have always remained a priority for all regimes. For
example, the preparation of this project began under the previous regime and was continued under the current regime,
with similar design and priorities. Furthermore, the preparation of the project involved extensive consultations to ensure
that it addresses issues with broad consensus among a wide range of stakeholders and that it will support technical
assistance, civil society engagement, and communication. The political climate around refugee issues in Niger is influenced
by several factors, including the broader regional instability in the Sahel and internal political dynamics. The political crisis
in 2023 significantly impacted the country's economic situation, reducing GDP growth and potentially affecting the

remain contained. Public debt is projected to decline to 41.6 percent by 2027. This outlook remains subject to significant
downside risks, including a deterioration of the security situation, commodity price volatility, and climatic shocks, all of
which could impact the construction sector, and, as a result, achievement of the PDO. On January 29, 2025, Niger formally
exited Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) along with Burkina Faso and Mali, following a joint
withdrawal announcement in January 2024 and unsuccessful mediation efforts. Nigerien citizens and businesses currently
have access to key ECOWAS membership benefits, including the free movement of people and goods, pending further
negotiations. Future decisions could disrupt the free movement of goods, services, capital, and labor, potentially affecting
trade in the UEMOA zone. Furthermore, the potential currency change from CFA francs (FCFA) to a new currency would
introduce volatility, inflation, loss of investor confidence, and potential economic instability, as the FCFA is pegged to the
euro, unless it is well-managed and backed by strong economic policies. Sustained or escalated tensions between Benin
and Niger could also lead to higher trade costs and could delay oil exports, which have already had an impact on civil works
contract prices. As a mitigation measure to the fluctuation of costs in civil works contracts, bidding documents will specify
the types and terms of insurance required, typically an All-Risk form of policy. This ensures that contractors are adequately
covered for potential losses, which can help stabilize costs in the event of unforeseen circumstances.

be a challenge within Niger’s current fragile context. As such, the project is proposing targeted technical assistance to the
coordination units and technical agencies for effective implementation of the project. Besides the central PCU, regional
coordination will be established to enhance on-ground monitoring of activities and interactions with local authorities,
decentralized government agencies, partners, and contractors. Sustainability, especially in road maintenance, poses a
significant challenge that affects the longevity of road investments in Niger. The GoN has pledged to enhance road asset
management and maintenance, particularly for the RN1, as outlined in the sector policy letter in Annex 4. This
commitment is reinforced by the GoN's confirmation to allocate US$7 million in counterpart funding to fully finance
routine maintenance and co-finance periodic maintenance for an eligible RN1 section through Subcomponent 1.3 of this
project. This subcomponent aims to further reduce sustainability risks in road investments by providing a technical
assistance to facilitate the transition to medium-term planning and multi-year contracting for routine and periodic road
maintenance.

be a pull factor for armed groups, especially those operating in the Tahoua region. To mitigate inherent security risks, the
project will rely on a conflict-sensitive approach by engaging closely with local communities, facilitating access to grievance
redress systems, and promoting citizen engagement. To mitigate residual and emerging security risks, a SRA and a

specialist to support informed decision-making processes on security risk management.
84. Other Risks – Refugee Protection: Substantial. Capacity issues and lack of resources continue to be a challenge to
effective refugee protection, especially in remote border regions. Despite these challenges, Niger has continued adopting
an open-door policy for refugees, even with the numerous arrivals of refugees from Mali, Burkina Faso, and Nigeria. As
part of the WHR access, the World Bank has worked with UNHCR to develop the Refugee Policy Review Framework (RPRF),
providing a comprehensive analysis of the laws, policies, and practices relevant to refugees in different sectors to measure
the progress of projects funded through the WHR, define future areas for investment, and support policy discussions. The
updated RPRF produced on June 30, 2023, the World Bank is of the view that the relevant protection framework remains
adequate for the purpose of the WHR. However, it notes that the most significant restrictions in terms of socioeconomic
development affecting refugees are related to the difficult economic situation, the need to update the asylum legislation
from 1997, the adoption of a national strategy for durable solutions for refugees, and the need to adopt the decree to
grant prima facie refugee status to Burkinabe refugees to better manage arrivals. The project will continue working in
close collaboration with UNHCR to follow RPRF guidance and ensure alignment with WHR recommendations and
obligations. Furthermore, the project will coordinate with other WHR-funded operations in Niger and contribute to
support the policy dialogue with the Nigerien authorities, including with the departments responsible for managing
refugees, to support the implementation of the refugee legislation and the 2023 commitments of the Global Refugee
Forum.

     ANNEX 2. CLIMATE MITIGATION AND ADAPTATION CONSIDERATIONS
1. Niger is already facing the impacts of climate change, and these effects are expected to worsen over time. The country
experiences recurring droughts, floods, extreme temperatures that can reach 49.5°C, violent winds, sandstorms and dust storms, and
wildfires.82 Climate projections indicate that by 2050, average temperatures could rise by 1.5°C to 3°C, rainfall events may become
more intense, and dry periods could become longer.
2. Niger ranks among the most vulnerable countries in the world, placing 185 th out of 187 countries on the Notre Dame Global
Adaptation Initiative Country Index.83 Climate change and natural hazards in Niger disrupt essential resources, infrastructure, and
sectors, including water availability and quality, agriculture, transport, and food security. Transport plays a critical role throughout the
food value chain, improving agricultural productivity and market access at national, regional, and global levels, especially considering
that in Niger distances between production and consumption of agricultural products are significant. However, floods, extreme
temperatures, sandstorms and dust storms, and other climate hazards damage roads and bridges, disrupt transport operations, and
increase maintenance and rehabilitation costs. These disruptions lead to delays in passenger and freight transport, escalating overall
transport costs. As climate-related degradation accelerates, the road network deteriorates, affecting connectivity and in turn the
agriculture sector and access to essential goods and services.
3. The project incorporates both hard and soft climate resilience measures into its design, effectively reducing future climate and
natural hazard risk to an acceptable level. The project is informed by high-level Climate and Disaster Risk Screening, the Climate Risks
and Resilience & Adaptation Options Appraisal Report funded by the GCA and the GFDT Regional Decarbonization and Resilience
Assessment of Rural Logistics and Accessibility in the Sahel. These studies revealed that many road sections in Southern Niger become
impassable during the rainy season. Thus, the project has integrated recommended climate adaptation and mitigation measures,
reducing the physical climate risks to the project’s outcome and ensuring that the residual risks remain at an acceptable level.
Integrating Climate Adaptation and Mitigation in the Project Design (and corresponding IDA financing)
Component 1: Climate-resilient improvement of Niger’s transport backbone (US$384.9 million)
Subcomponent 1.1: Climate-resilient rehabilitation of RN1 (US$304.0 million)
4. Adaptation: This subcomponent finances the rehabilitation and upgrading of the Maradi–Zinder road section (232.89 km) to
climate-resilient standards, selected based on its criticality for transport connectivity and resilience of the road network, and its high
exposure and vulnerability to climate hazards. Damaged by the 2024 flood events, it requires urgent rehabilitation and upgrading to
enhance its climate resilience. About 25 percent of the investment targets climate resilience measures including: (a) NBS such as
planting drought-resistant vegetation and trees to (i) provide shade and a cooling effect; (ii) reduce dune migration and act as a barrier
against wind, sand and debris; and (iii) minimize sedimentation and prevent drainage systems blockages; and (b) resizing drainage
infrastructure to accommodate higher rainwater runoff. The remaining 75 percent of the costs will contribute to the overall systemic
resilience of the road network through additional resilience measures including: (i) using a layer of high-grade asphalt or concrete to
accommodate increasing high temperatures; (ii) replacing existing bridge joints to allow for sufficient expansion room to accommodate
high temperatures; (iii) stabilizing road reserves with concrete blocks; (iv) raising the road by 1 m in flood-prone sections; (v) using
masonry riprap and gabions to reduce erosion; (vi) establishing flood early warning systems in areas exposed to flood hazards; and
(vii) using the Green Roads for Water approach to improve water management and strengthen the climate resilience of road
infrastructure and also of the communities with improved access to water resources.84
5. Mitigation: The project will finance facilities for NMT, including cycle lanes, pedestrian crossings, pathways, and sidewalks with
energy-efficient, solar-powered streetlighting in the city crossings of the Maradi–Zinder road section. The planting of trees and
vegetation will also enable carbon capture and sequestration.

the resilience of the RN1 and of the road network more broadly, maintenance activities will be prioritized in climate hazard hotspots

 ANNEX 3. SYNERGIES WITH OTHER WORLD BANK–FINANCED OPERATIONS
1. Project-financed activities will complement and seek synergies with ongoing interventions that address the critical
needs of communities in and around Maradi and Zinder. These ongoing interventions, financed by Word Bank operations,
collectively aim to enhance access to essential services, including electricity, water, sanitation, health, education, and

Subject: Niger Road Maintenance Policy Letter (2025-2031).

I hereby transmit to you for approval and transmission to the World Bank the above letter worded as follows:

Niger, a Sahelian country, covers an area of 1,267,000 Km2 with a population of 25.4 million in 2023, over 80% of whom
live in rural areas. Women account for 50.2% and young people aged 15-34 for 29.4%. The road network, essential to the
economy, accounts for 95% of transport market, with 22,787 km of roads, including 5,643 km of paved roads.
Since 2017, the Government of Niger, with the support of financial partners, has launched reforms to improve the
maintenance of the road network. This includes the creation of FEI, AMODER and CACER, thereby optimizing the quality
and management of road maintenance, a task previously carried out by the DGER.

Niger's national road network faces maintenance challenges due to extreme weather conditions and insufficient financial
resources. The Government has identified areas targeted interventions.


                                                                                                                    Page 49
        The World Bank
        Southern Niger Connectivity and Integration Project (P179770)


To this end, the Ministry of Transport and Infrastructure undertakes to:

• On an institutional level, strengthen the departments in charge of road rehabilitation and maintenance, and review
the necessary laws and decrees in order to provide a clear response to the financing needs of the road network, the
driving force behind development.
• On the financial front, ensure and consolidate the timely provision of funds destined to the FER within the required
deadlines and broaden the fund's financing base by using all the levers existing in Law N° 2017-37 of 22 May 2017 which
created the FER as well as its implementation decrees. This enables the FER to start financing the periodic maintenance
of eligible roads.
• On the technical level, strengthen the technical departments in charge of maintenance works programming and
reinforce the technical capabilities of SMEs in charge of maintenance work to meet deadlines.

To this end, all those involved in road maintenance will be relentlessly mobilized to achieve the following objectives:
• From 2026 onwards, the aim is to transfer responsibility for the programming budget to AMODER, or to make DGSR
expenditures eligible for FER funding, thus enabling the programming of maintenance.
• By 2027, each structure should have implemented the CACER recommendations on the road user charges for the
interior taxes on petroleum products in order to avoid exemptions and provide technical assistance to ensure collection.
• From 2027, the aim will be to improve the rate of commitment of FER to 90% in order to, in part, maintain at least
60% of asphalt roads, 60% of feeder roads and, finally, 100% of the engineering structures and hydraulic structures; and
in another part to modernize tolls every 2 years on the RN1, and the one between Agadez and Zinder starting in 2029.
For this, multiannual maintenance that can be carried out by national enterprises will be put in place. To this end, the
capabilities enterprises, design offices and that of the administration will also be strengthened.
• Thanks to the support of financial and technical partners, financial resources will be mobilized to complement FER’s
resources to ensure periodic maintenance and rehabilitation. Specifically for Niger's most important corridor, the RN1,
these resources will enable completion of periodic maintenance and rehabilitation all eligible sections from Mali border
to the Chad border.

Faced with these challenges, the Government of Niger is committed to ensuring planned and regular mobilization of
financial resources for the maintenance of the Niger road network. In particular, the Government of Niger, within the
framework of the PICSN, is going to earmark seven (7) million dollars (around 4,200,000 XOF) to co-finance the
maintenance of the RN1.

We enclose a detailed note with this letter. We hope that through these commitments, the people of Niger and Niger's
partners will understand and support the government's relentless efforts to achieve our objectives.
