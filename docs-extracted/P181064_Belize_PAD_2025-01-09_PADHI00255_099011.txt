    I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

    A. Country Context

1.       Classified as an upper-middle-income country in Central America and a Small Island Developing State (SIDS) in
the Caribbean Region, Belize relies on its coastal and marine ecosystems for the protection of its cities, towns and
villages, the livelihoods of its fishing families, food security, and the development of its important economic sectors—
tourism, marine transport and fisheries. In 2023, Belize had a population of 410,825, a gross domestic product (GDP) of
US$3.28 billion, and a per capita GDP over US$7,988. Despite these assets, Belize maintained a high poverty rate of 35.7
percent in 2023, and infrastructure gaps are still identified as top constraints to the country’s economic growth. For
instance, the national water and sewerage utility, Belize Water Services Limited (BWS), provides water connection to
about 71 percent of the population but sanitation connection to only 18 percent of its water users.1 These infrastructure
gaps reflect the country’s low urbanization rate of 46.4 percent, which is low compared to over 80 percent for other
Caribbean and Central American countries, as well as Latin American and Caribbean Region as a whole.2
2.       Under a changing climate, Belize is exceptionally vulnerable to adverse impacts from climate-related shocks.
Ranked third in terms of disaster risk and fifth in climate change risk among all SIDS, Belize’s coastal areas are
threatened by hurricanes, storms, coastal erosion, and flooding, while the low-lying terrain of these areas exacerbates
the effects of flooding and sea level rise.3 The degradation and bleaching of coral reefs due to ocean acidification and
warmer sea temperatures threatens biodiversity, coastal and marine food webs, and the livelihoods of vulnerable
communities dependent on these ecosystems. In addition, Belize's vulnerability is further compounded by its high poverty
rates and limited capacity to respond to disasters. With over 50 percent of its population living in rural areas and
dependent on agriculture and fisheries, any climate-induced disruption, such as prolonged droughts, unpredictable rainfall
patterns, and intensified storms, could lead to significant socio-economic instability and disproportionate impacts to the
rural, poor and vulnerable population. External shocks, such as increasing energy prices and pandemics, can easily weaken
the country’s external position and reduce the purchasing power of its population. To address its climate and socio-
economic vulnerabilities, Belize will have to embark on an economic development and urbanization path that are
environmentally sustainable, socially inclusive (including women, youth and elderly people) and climate resilient.4
Recognizing unique challenges faced by Belize, the International Development Association (IDA) in its December 2023 mid-
term review granted Belize’s access to IDA financing under its Small Island Economy Exception Policy.

sustainable uses of its blue assets.5 It is endowed with 386 km of coastline, the largest barrier reef in the northern
hemisphere, offshore atolls, several hundred sand cays, mangrove forests, coastal lagoons and estuaries. According to the

4.       It is noteworthy that Belize’s Updated Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC, August 2021) has included key
climate actions to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by maintaining and restoring ecosystems and improving
waste and wastewater management, and to increase resilience of its tourism and fisheries sectors.8 Indeed increasing
the resilience of these two sectors is key for two main reasons. First, the tourism industry contributed nearly 45 percent
to Belize’s GDP in 2019 and the economic impact of climate change on Belize’s tourism sector is estimated at BZ$48.3
million (around US$24.0 million), including reduced tourism demand and loss of facilities due to sea level rise and coastal
erosion9. Second, the fisheries sector contributes an estimated US$14 to US$16 million per year to the economy and is
severely impacted by the destabilization of the barrier reef ecosystem due to higher sea-surface temperatures and more
frequent coral bleaching leading to a decline in fisheries stocks.
5.       As of 2024, Belize has two cities, Belmopan and Belize City, seven towns (mostly along its coast and in its cayes)
and 192 villages. Cities and towns are led by an elected mayor and villages are managed by village councils. Except for the
two cities, Belize’s towns and villages rely on the national government’s financial support to carry out investment projects
and provide essential public services (such as waste disposal, roads, markets and parks). The mayors and councils for these
towns and villages have limited capacity to regulate local planning and development activities and properly evaluate and
collect property taxes and other local revenues. For instance, while San Pedro Town Council has witnessed rapid
development in tourism within and beyond its administrative boundaries post COVID, it struggles to collect sufficient
revenue to provide quality services to its population. Institutionally, at the national level, the Ministry of Rural
Transformation, Community Development, Labour and Local Government (MRTCDLLG) is responsible for legislations
relating to city, town and village councils. To better manage urbanization, the national government’s 2023 Plan Belize has
called for the development of an Urban Development Strategy for the country by 2026.

development and improve the livelihood of its citizens through science-based management of the country’s aquatic
resources and space.10 Given its multisectoral nature, Belize’s blue economy development involves additional ministries
such as the Ministries of Finance, Economic Development, and Investment (overall development strategy and financing);
Sustainable Development and Climate Change (biodiversity/conservation, forest and environmental management, and
climate change); Natural Resources, Petroleum, and Mining (management of land and water resources); Tourism and

7.       Belize’s efforts on urban and blue economy development are facing several interlinked challenges, including
rapid but uncoordinated urban development at its key tourism destinations and coastal cities/communities. Due to
existing gaps in its land use and environmental policies and regulations, limited monitoring and enforcement of its existing
planning and environmental policies, lack of sustainable financing for key municipal infrastructure such as water and
sanitation and waste management, its rapid development has increased land-based pollution from its coastal
cities/communities, caused coastal erosion, and degraded its critical coastal and marine ecosystems.12 Despite the
contributions that women make to the blue economy sector, gender norms hamper them from productively engaging in
the sector and reduces their impact in environmental sustainability efforts. If continued, such development will undermine
the long-term sustainability of Belize’s coastal and marine ecosystems and consequently, the sustainability of the country’s
blue economy. In fact, the 2022 Report Card on the Health of the Mesoamerican Reef reveals that Belizean reef health
has dropped from the ‘Fair’ rating attained five years ago to a ‘Poor’ rating due to overfishing, critical habitat loss, and
potential biodiversity loss.13 After overfishing, high concentrations of nutrients, primarily from inadequately treated
sewage from urban and coastal communities, are the main cause of widespread death of coral cover across the region.14
8.       Recognizing these challenges, the Government of Belize (GoB) has requested the World Bank (WB)’s support to
develop a three-phase program to promote sustainable urban and blue economy development and mobilize sustainable
financing to finance its critical blue economy investments at key coastal cities and communities. The intention is to use
the first operation to (a) improve the enabling environment, (b) finance priority infrastructure investments that are
investment ready, and (c) develop an investment program with sustainable financing mechanisms that can be financed by
the two follow-up operations and other donors. In response to this request, the WB has developed this first project
through extensive consultations with key national ministries/agencies, local governments, and development partners to
(a) tackle key policy and capacity gaps in urban and blue economy development at both national and local levels, (b)
finance priority climate-resilient water and sanitation infrastructure at priority urban areas, and (c) support the
government to develop a blue economy investment program that will promote sustainable urban and blue economy
development. It is expected that the preparation of the second project will be initiated in FY27 and the third in FY29.

2030 (Report No. 191013), especially strategic objective 2 (Expanding and enabling economic opportunities) and strategic
objective 3 (Engaging women as leaders) through activities that aim to promote the participation of women in the blue
economy sector, provide capacity building activities to women-led enterprises, and incorporate gender-sensitization
training for public and private sector stakeholders.
10.      By supporting resilient coastal development and blue economy investments, the project is also fully aligned
with the country’s economic development plans and strategies highlighted in Paragraph 4 and is consistent with Belize’s
NDC and Long-Term Strategies (LTS). In the latest NDC submitted to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate
Change in August 2021, the country commits (a) on the mitigation front, to reduce deforestation and promote the
sustainable use of ecosystems, and cumulative emissions across all sectors by 5,647 KtCO2e between 2021 and 2030; and
(b) on the adaptation front, to build climate resilience, particularly in the coastal and marine resources sector. This
Project’s support to policy development and capacity building will improve Belize’s capacity to manage climate-resilient
and low-carbon development of its coastal areas and blue economy in the long run. Its investments in (a) the Belize City
Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) will help reduce GHG emissions from poorly managed operations of the facility and
improve its treatment efficiency under a changing climate, (b) water supply will improve climate resilience of San Pedro,
and (c) the waste management pilot will promote recycling and reduce waste leakage. Such reduced pollution discharges
from Belize’s coastal cities will help protect environmental quality, reduce threats to and promote sustainable
management and uses of globally significant biodiversity, and increase climate resilience of Belize’s precious coastal and
marine ecosystems. Ultimately, these efforts will contribute to the implementation of the Kunming-Montreal Global
Biodiversity Framework Fund15 at the national, regional, and global levels. The project is consistent with the National
Adaptation Plan by targeting improvements in resilience for coastal communities and ecosystems, among other sectors,
and the LTS by aligning with the goal of developing a low carbon and carbon neutral development pathway by 2050. The
Climate Finance Strategy maps out how to mobilize financial resources to implement priority actions between 2021 and
2026, directly linked to the climate change efforts outlined in the NDC.
11.     The project is fully aligned with objectives of the Global Environment Facility (GEF) 8 Sustainable Cities
Integrated Program (SCIP). The SCIP supports “cities and local governments to undertake integrated urban planning,
implement policies and invest in nature-positive, climate-resilient, and carbon-neutral urban development.” The
project’s activities, namely, the development of national spatial and local integrated land-use planning and critical
environmental policies, and the strengthening of stakeholders’ technical capacity to implement such policies and
regulations, contribute to Component 1 of the SCIP (supporting integrated and inclusive urban planning, strategies and
policy development). Its investments in climate resilient urban water and sanitation infrastructure and circular economy
interventions and its efforts to explore sustainable mechanisms to finance the proposed blue economy investment
program contribute to Component 2 of the SCIP (promoting investments in sustainable, nature positive, and resilient
urban development and adopting innovative financing mechanisms). Its capacity building activities will support for Belize’s
effective participation in knowledge sharing activities of Component 3 of the SCIP (strengthening knowledge-sharing and
capacity building). As an integral part of the SCIP, the project will contribute to the implementation of GEF 8 focal area
strategies for climate change, biodiversity, international waters, and Chemical and Waste.

 the final upgrade activities will be determined during the detailed design of the upgraded treatment plant). The WWTP’s
 upgrade will include an expansion of its treatment capacity to accommodate the forecasted population growth for at

 standards, and inclusion of climate resiliency considerations in its design, including energy efficient pumping systems
 and promoting less energy-intensive treatment processes. Throughout the project interventions, the GHG emissions
 from the upgraded wastewater treatment process are expected to reduce by 37,184 tons CO2eq.20 Proposed activities
 include:
       (a) desludging of the existing lagoons, including the sludge holding lagoon,;
       (b) addition of a preliminary treatment unit;
       (c) construction of a properly sized manhole to facilitate the disposal of septic tanks sludges;
       (d) construction of a new anaerobic lagoon (or lagoons);
       (e) upgrade of the facultative lagoons and polishing lagoons;
       (f) construction of sludge drying beds;
       (g) installation of a Ultra-Violet system to disinfect the treated effluent;
       (h) improvement to the effluent discharge canal and its berm system integrating climate-resilient infrastructure
           design to withstand extreme weather events to ensure the facility remains operational during climate stressors,
       (i) improvement to the access to and the internal roads of the compound,
       (j) construction of an operation office, a mechanical workshop, a basic on-site laboratory and an equipment
           storage room,
       (k) installation of security and safety measures (fencing, lighting, camera systems),
       (l) improvement to the site drainage system and landscaping; and
       (m) proper conservation and management of the mangrove area to ensure it continues to act as a buffer against
           storm surges and sea-level rise and to effectively serve as an NBS for nutrient removal before the treated
           wastewater reaches the Caribbean Sea, conserving its important dual role in enhancing climate resilience and
           protecting coastal ecosystems.

 F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

39.     As blue economy and coastal development involves multiple sectors and stakeholders from both the public and
private sectors, it is important to design a blue economy project with good coordination mechanisms for all relevant
stakeholders. The design of this project has emphasized coordination at the levels of policy development, capacity
building, and investments. All activities under Component 1 will promote broad stakeholder participation, including, but
not limited to, relevant national ministries/agencies and local governments, civil society organizations, and donor
agencies. These coordination efforts will ensure project stakeholders’ endorsement to project outputs and sustainability
of project interventions.
40.     Project design needs to match the existing financial and technical capacity of the country. Per the request of the
Government, the WB considered the possibility of developing a multiphase programmatic approach program to provide
long-term support to sustainable development of the country’s blue economy. Recognizing that Belize has yet to develop
a concrete blue economy program and has limited fiscal space to commit to a long-term program with a high loan amount,
this project has elected to focus on priority interventions on policy development, capacity building, and water and
sanitation investments. In addition, it proposes to support the Government to develop a blue economy investment
program and use the program as a platform to coordinate domestic stakeholders and development partners to raise
necessary financing for the implementation of this investment program beyond the scope of this project.
41.      As highlighted in the April 2023 PLR (report number 180534-BZ), Project Implementation Unit (PIU) capacity
and sufficient project management funding are needed to ensure proper delivery of expected outputs and outcomes
from WB operations in Belize. The proposed project design mitigates such project management capacity constraints
proactively by supporting proper staffing and adequate budget support for the CEU to manage Component 1 (except for
subcomponent 1.4(b)), Subcomponent 2.3, and Component 3 activities. The CEU is expected to rely on key national
authorities to coordinate technical issues of these project activities. As the existing PIU of the BWS has worked effectively
with technical departments of the BWS to manage the firm’s annual investments, amounting to between US$10 million
and US$22 million in the last six fiscal years, the project will use the BWS PIU to manage activities under Subcomponents
1.4(b), 2.1 and 2.2.

 IV. PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY
 A. Technical, Economic and Financial Analysis (if applicable)

49.      Technical design of the project activities was developed through extensive stakeholder consultation. It has
considered ongoing and planned activities of key stakeholders, assessment of their capacity, and readiness of proposed
investment activities. It emphasizes the importance of policy actions to improve the regulatory framework for coordinated
and sustainable blue economy development. TA, training, and awareness-raising activities are proposed to help Belize
develop sufficient capacity to manage technical aspects of project activities and for blue economy development in the
long run. The investment program is proposed to help the Government prioritize its blue economy investments through a
well-coordinated mechanism supported by a robust financing and fund-raising plan. Investments in San Pedro will help
BWS to bring reliable, high quality and low-cost water supply to a specific area of the town that are so far relying on bulk
water truck sales, rainwater harvesting and beach wells. Finally, investment in Belize City will help stop uncontrolled
discharges of insufficient treated wastewater from its current WWTP. It is worth noting that these activities will not resolve
all challenges faced by Belize but will support the Government to initiate efforts to improve and operationalize its blue
economy strategies. Successful project implementation will prepare Belize for next phases of interventions for sustainable
development of its blue economy.
50.      Technically, the proposed upgrade of the Belize WWTP will not change its existing treatment technology—
stabilization lagoon treatment, which is widely adopted usually in small and medium-size cities but also in large cities
such as Melbourne and Nairobi due to its easiness and low costs to construct and operate. Project investment will
improve the current design and operating conditions of the WWTP with an objective of improving its treatment
efficiencies. Its feasibility study is being developed with the support of Korean Environmental Industry and Technology
Institute. The Bank team will participate in the review of this study outputs to ensure climate resilience and cost-
effectiveness of project investments.
51.      Two economic and financial analyses are conducted (see Annex 2), one for Subcomponent 2.1 and one for
Subcomponent 2.2. Estimated benefits include (a) ecological and environmental benefits; (b) producer surplus; (c)
consumer surplus; (d) job creation; and (e) health benefits, depending on the subcomponent analyzed. Preliminary
estimates of investment costs and operations and maintenance (O&M) costs are provided by the BWS. An economic
internal rate of return (EIRR) and net present value (NPV) of investment are estimated based on benefits and costs
calculations, using a discount rate of 6 percent. Sensitivity analysis is carried out to assess the viability of the project when
some of the assumed variables change, specifically, when there are 20 percent overruns on investment costs or O&M
costs. For the WWTP upgrade, an EIRR of 12 percent has been calculated, which is fair above the discount rate and comes
with a positive NPV. The sensitivity analysis shows that the upgrade is sensible to investment costs overruns of 20 percent,
resulting on a 9 percent EIRR, while a 20 percent O&M costs overrun minimally affects the EIRR. As both EIRRs are still
above the discount rate, the project would still be economically viable. The South San Pedro water network expansion

                                                                                                                         Page 14

investment has an EIRR of 15 percent, which is also fair above the discount rate of 6 percent. The sensitivity analysis shows
that the water expansion subcomponent would have EIRRs of 12 percent in case of investments or O&M overruns, which
are still above the discount rate and means that the project would still be economically viable.
52.      An assessment of the BWS’ overall financial situation, using their latest audited reports to analyze financial

 D. Environmental and Social
64.      Overall, the Project is expected to result in positive E&S outcomes by reducing land-based pollution, promoting
sustainable management of coastal zones, and improving the health and wellbeing of the population. The
Environmental and Social Risks Classification (ESRC) is Substantial with a Substantial environmental risk rating, and a
moderate social risk rating. The key environmental risks are associated with Component 2 activities and include (a) soil,
water, and air pollution resulting from the desludging of facultative ponds and septage disposal; (b) leakages and emissions
of greenhouse gases from anerobic lagoons; (c) generation, management and disposal of waste; (d) occupational health

main social risks of the project activities are: (a) the social exclusion of the disadvantaged and vulnerable groups (fishers,

of stakeholder participation and public information disclosure; (c) inadequately addressing and promoting E&S capacity
building and institutional strengthening; (d) potential moderate risk associated to labor influx and low Sexual Exploitation
and Abuse/Sexual Harassment risks; (e) low-moderate risk for child labor, along with labor safety concerns; and (f) weak
capacity of contractors to manage E&S risks and impacts. Currently, no physical or economic displacement related to
Environmental and Social Standard 5 (ESS5) is envisaged as a result of project activities. Overall, the E&S risks are likely
to be temporary, short-term, and limited in extent and can be managed through routine preventive and mitigation
measures and capacity strengthening. The ESRC will be reviewed regularly during implementation to reflect the project's
risk level.

milestones. Thematic review meetings will be organized periodically and based on actual needs of project implementation.

E&S, and project management. Implementation support to project management will focus on technical capacity of project
teams, internal coordination between the two PIUs, and coordination and cooperations between the CEU and
participating ministries and local governments. As needed, the WB will provide technical inputs to support the PSC’s
review and decision-making processes.

implementation. This exercise will review progress of project implementation, identify interim implementation results and
assess performance of the project’s technical design and implementation arrangements. The MTR will provide
recommendations for any course correction or restructuring necessary to improve the project’s relevance, efficacy or
efficiency. At the end of project implementation, the WB and MFEDI will prepare the ICR report to summarize project
achievements, evaluate implementation performance, and identify concrete lessons learned to inform future operations.
5.      Throughout project implementation, consultations with development partners, NGOs and private sector
participants will be maintained to ensure coordination on project activities and ongoing and new initiatives of all
stakeholders. Such coordination will also help mobilize the endorsement of all stakeholders to project outputs and results.

                                                     ANNEX 2: Financial and Economic Analysis

                                                                     COUNTRY: Belize
                                                               Belize Blue Economy Project

Belize City WWTP Upgrade
1.       The objective of the WWTP upgrade in Belize City is to restore and increase its current treatment capacity. As this
project has quantifiable benefits, the Economic and Financial Analysis comprises a cost-benefit analysis that assesses
whether economic and financial benefits of the project outweigh the economic costs. The treatment plant is expected to
accommodate the expected population growth for at least the next 25 years, so the analysis assumes a 25-year life of
infrastructure assets. The assessment of benefits and costs is made at constant year 2022 prices, using an exchange rate
of 2 BZD per 1 US$. An estimation of the net benefits is calculated, which is the difference between the incremental
benefits and the incremental costs of two scenarios: with project (WP) and without project (WoP). The WP scenario
considers the proposed project and its targets. The WoP scenario considers that the plant will stay the way it is, and the
water treated will continue to diverge from national standards.
2.       Estimation of Benefits. The following benefits from the upgrade of the wastewater plant in Belize City have been
identified:
          (a) Ecological and environmental benefits – project activities will contribute to the conservation of coastal
              ecosystems, specifically the mangrove area surrounding the WWTP and the area through which the treated
              water passes before reaching the sea, and the GHG emissions reduced because of a correct treatment of
              wastewater30. These benefits can be quantified as the value of the services provided by the mangrove area
              that are being conserved31 and the shadow price of carbon, respectively. Excessive nutrients from wastewater
              affect mangroves’ ability to store carbon and contribute to deterioration of the ecosystem.32
          (b) Potential producer surplus – BWS is in consultation phase for charging a tariff to contractors that deposit the
              septic waste in the WWTP.33 If the upgrade does not happen, BWS would not be able to charge, as the current
              treatment capacity of the plant is minimal, and they would not have elements to charge.
          (c) Job creation – jobs will be created for the upgrade and operations of this project. It is estimated that 30 jobs
              will be created during upgrade phase and five additional permanent jobs will be created for operations and
              maintenance.34
          (d) Public health and other benefits – reduction of the risk of diseases will be achieved by improving treatment
              of wastewater.
          (e) Security and safety – the WWTP is located in a conflictive area. As part of the project, security and safety
              measures will be improved (lighting, camera systems, etc.), which will have a positive impact in the
              community.

San Pedro Water Supply Expansion
7.       The objective of the San Pedro Water Supply Expansion is to increase access to potable water for residents in the
South of San Pedro, located in Ambergris Caye, Belize. To reach this objective, the project aims to connect households and
businesses to the drinking water distribution system in the next 25 years to meet current and future water demand.
Installation of distribution water mains will be part of the project scope.
8.       Estimation of Benefits. The following benefits from the water supply expansion in South San Pedro have been
identified:
         (a) Additional revenue for BWS – when people and businesses get connected to the new expansion, BWS will
             have additional revenue from installation fees and paid tariffs.
         (b) Money savings – households and businesses who are not currently connected must pay tankers to provide
             water, but still pay a tariff to BWS. When they become connected to the pipeline, they will save the money
             paid to tankers or other water providers, and they will pay only for the gallons of water used, instead of paying
             for the bulk.
         (c) Enhanced livelihoods – it is estimated that the project will provide jobs for 12 people for a duration of 9
             months. We assume the minimum wage, which is US$2.5 per hour.

Financial Management
1.      Staffing. CEU will recruit two full-time FMO under the project who will be responsible for FM activities. The CEU’s
finance department head will supervise the two FMO. BWS’ FMOs will be supervised by its Chief Financial Officer.
2.      Accounting. The CEU and BWS will incorporate distinct project codes for recording, tracking, and reporting of the
Project’s transactions in their respective accounting systems. These include QuickBooks and SmartStream under the
CEU/MFEDI, and SAGE 300 under the BWS.
3.     Financial Reporting. Each entity will submit IFR to the WB within 45 days after each quarter, in content and format
acceptable to WB.
4.     Planning and Budgeting. Each entity will prepare a master budget for the project's life. The annual work plan and
budget will be derived from this master budget, which will be submitted to WB, for approval. Each entities’ approved
budget will be included in GoB or BWS’ budget approval, e.g., for MFEDI/CEU, approval by Parliament, and for BWS, by its
Board.
5.       Internal Controls. The project’s implementation will be governed by POM to be developed by each entity, which
will be approved by the WB. The MoF’s internal audit will review CEU’s internal controls annually, from Year 2 while that
of BWS will be reviewed by its internal audit department, yearly.
6.      Disbursement. The Project’s disbursement process will follow WB disbursement procedures which will be
outlined in the DFIL and Funds Control or operating accounts, acceptable to WB.
7.      External Audit. Each entity will be audited by independent audit firms on ToRs approved by the WB. Each entity
will submit the annual audited financial statements and management letter to the WB within 6 months after the end of
the fiscal year. The project will support the capacity building for the Office of Auditor General of Belize under CEU’s
component.
8.    Governance and Accountability. Both CEU and BWS must comply with WB Guidelines on Preventing and
Combating Fraud and Corruption, promptly reporting any suspected cases to the WB.
9.     Supervision Plan. The WB will review the adequacy of the agreed FM arrangements during project
implementation through FM support supervision, biannually. These reviews will focus on various aspects such as project
accounting, internal controls, budgeting, financial planning, and audit reports.
10.     FM Risks and Mitigation. The main risks include delayed finalization and submission of the Project’s audit reports,
noncompliance with WB procedures, and weak internal control environment. These risks will be mitigated through
detailed POMs, hiring of dedicated FMOs, developing project-specific codes in accounting systems, training of project staff
on WB FM procedures, subjecting the project to auditing by independent auditors, and provision of capacity building for
the Auditor General’s Office on auditing of WB operations. The FM risks after mitigation are assessed as Substantial.

1.       Component 1: Policy Development and Capacity Building (US$17.93 million, of which IDA US$10.35 million, GEF
US$2.61 million, PROBLUE US$4.0 million, and Global Facility for Disaster Reduction and Recovery [GFDRR] US$0.97
million). This component will support four subcomponents: policy development, capacity building, carbon market
readiness, and blue economy investment program. It aims to address climate-related and socio-economic challenges that
are inhibiting sustainable development and management of Belize’s coastal communities and blue economy through
policy development, regulatory reforms, institutional strengthening, capacity building, carbon market readiness and
investment preparation. Mitigation and adaptation measures for expected climate impacts, such as hurricane, floods,
droughts and sea level rise, will be fully incorporated into policies developed and capacity building activities under this
Component.

2.      Subcomponent 1.1. Policy Development (US$2.85 million, of which IDA US$0.24 million, GEF US$1.61 million and
PROBLUE US$1.0 million) will support Belize to review and refine its spatial planning, tourism and environmental policies
that are critical to sustainable development of Belize’s coastal communities and its blue economy development, and to
address the country’s vulnerability to climate change. Development of these policy actions will require cross-sectoral and
cross-jurisdiction collaboration and active public participation from a wide range of key stakeholders including women,
youth, and vulnerable and disadvantaged groups.

3.       For land-uses, this subcomponent will support (a) the Ministry of Natural Resources to develop national geospatial
and spatial planning policies and legislations and an associated national spatial development plan and (b) selected local
governments and township councils (Belize City, San Pedro and Placencia) to develop their respective urban land-use
planning. Ecological urban planning and nature-based solutions (NBS) will be integrated into these national and local
planning policies and plans. These policies will be informed by geospatial tools to identify areas susceptible to flooding,
sea level rise, coastal erosion and other climate-related impacts and to propose suitable and tailored NBS such as
mangrove restoration that would increase resilience to storms and at the same time provide additional benefits as carbon
sinks. This information will be integrated into local land-use planning and zoning to promote climate-resilient
infrastructure design and to support vulnerable people’s adaptation to climate-induced impacts.

4.        For environmental policies, this subcomponent will focus on the monitoring and protection of the country’s
critical blue assets – water and fisheries. It will first support the Ministry of Natural Resources to lead the development of
a national water master plan and prescribe the country’s water quality control strategy and implementation plan. It will
also support MPUELE, the Public Utilities Commission, BWS and other relevant stakeholders to develop a national
sanitation master plan. These plans will incorporate climate risk assessments (particularly from sea level rise, hurricanes,
storms, coastal erosion and ocean water temperature), which will guide the implementation of water efficiency measures
across different sectors, account for ecosystem services that provide buffers against climate-related impacts, and focus
on provision of climate-resilient water resources management and sanitation services to urban and rural population of
the country, in particular those living in underserved communities. Moreover, the subcomponent will support the Ministry
of Sustainable Development and its Department of Environment (DOE) to work with the MHW to develop surface water,
marine water, and groundwater quality standards; and MPUELE and its Public Utilities Commission to work with the MHW
to develop potable water standards and regulations. Finally, it will support the DOE to revise effluent discharge standards
to minimize environmental impacts of such discharges to Belize’s coastal and marine ecosystems, and the MBEDRM and

13.       Subcomponent 2.1. Belize City WWTP Upgrade (IDA US$8.8 million) will help BWS renovate the existing and only
WWTP of Belize City, which consists of a facultative lagoon, followed by a polishing lagoon, a sludge storage lagoon and
an effluent discharge canal conveying treated effluent through a mangrove area to the Caribbean Sea. Due to the lack of
maintenance over many years, the existing lagoons are filled with sludge, so the treatment system currently has a minimal
level of treatment effect with uncontrolled methane releases. The proposed investments will be performed within the
physical boundaries of the existing WWTP and will include, in principle, the units detailed below (although the final
upgrade activities will be determined during the detailed design of the upgraded treatment plant). The WWTP’s upgrade
will include an expansion of its treatment capacity to accommodate the forecasted population growth for at least the next
25 years, an improvement of its treatment performance to meet the national effluent discharge standards, and inclusion
of climate resiliency considerations in its design, including energy efficient pumping systems and promoting less energy-
intensive treatment processes. Throughout the project interventions, the GHG emissions from the upgraded wastewater
treatment process are expected to reduce by 37,184 tons CO2eq.43 Proposed activities include:

(n)       desludging of the existing lagoons, including the sludge holding lagoon, to prepare them to be part of a new layout
and flow pattern of the upgraded plant, which will be determined during the design activities;
(o)       addition of a preliminary treatment unit consisting of a grit chamber, bar screens and micro strainers, to prevent
large solid matter from entering the lagoons;
(p)       construction of a properly sized manhole to facilitate the disposal of septic tanks sludges which will be delivered
by sludge hauling trucks;
(q)       construction of a new anaerobic lagoon (or lagoons) in the treatment train upfront of the facultative lagoons, to
anaerobically decompose a significant part of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD) entering the plant and thus decrease
the BOD load on the following facultative lagoons and consequently decrease the total area required for the treatment
plant. The design for covering the anaerobic lagoons for the purpose of collecting the biogas (mostly methane which is a
strong GHG gas), to prevent its emission from the treatment system, will be developed along with options for possible
utilization of the biogas for energy generation, as appropriate. Notably, there are no feasible alternatives with lower GHG
emissions that can achieve the same objective in Belize;
(r)       upgrade of the facultative lagoons and polishing lagoons using the existing lagoons with possible modifications
based on the new design of the upgraded treatment plant, which will consider future climate scenarios, such as changes
in temperature and precipitation patterns, to maintain treatment efficiency and avoid overloading the system during
extreme weather climate events;

14.      Subcomponent 2.2. Expansion of water supply in South San Pedro (IDA US$1.5 million) will help BWS expand the
existing drinking water distribution system to meet current and future potable water demand for the residents and
tourism development in South San Pedro area.44 As the main tourist destination in the country, San Pedro faces challenges
to provide safe, potable water to meet growing demand. Residents that are outside BWS’s distribution network rely on
rainwater harvesting, beach wells and other water supply means that are vulnerable to the impacts of climate change,
such as droughts, tropical storms, and saltwater intrusion caused by sea-level rise. Expanding the water supply
infrastructure will enhance resilience against climate-induced water security threats by ensuring a reliable and clean water
source, even during extreme weather events and periods of high demand. The scope of works to be implemented includes
(a) about 2.84 km of 8-inch distribution water main; (b) 0.61 km of 6-inch distribution water main; and (c) 1.68 km of 4-
inch distribution water main. The design, construction and operation will consider climate-resilient measures such as
appropriate pipe depth, incorporating proper sealing and waterproofing of below ground structures to protect against
floods and contamination, and regular maintenance for the timely detection and repair of leaks. The expansion activities
will result in GHG emissions reduction of 886 tons CO2eq45, primarily attributed to a reduction in the use of diesel-powered
trucks to deliver water to potential beneficiaries.
