                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The proposed Central African Power Pool Regional Interconnection and Access (CAPP-RIA) Program is a Multi-Phase
                     Programmatic Approach (MPA) operation that aims to expand regional electricity trade and increase access to
                     electricity services in the Central African region. Supporting the establishment of a regional electricity market will
                     enhance energy security, reduce costs of electricity supply and improve the resilience of the energy system. CAPP-RIA
                     comprises two phases that focus on three thematic pillars: 1) augmenting regional integration, 2) expanding electricity
                     access, and 3) strengthening institutional capacity. Over the next 10 years, CAPP-RIA will support regional integration in
                     Central Africa through investments and technical assistance in developing the regional transmission network,
                     reinforcing and expanding national and cross-border transmission and distribution systems, and strengthening the


Public Disclosure
                     enabling institutional and regulatory environment for fficient power trade. Phase 1 includes preparing and
                     implementing on-grid access projects in the Central African Republic (CAR), Chad, Gabon, Republic of Congo, and
                     Cameroon, as well as Medium-Voltage (MV) cross-border projects. It also involves pre-investment studies for High-
                     Voltage (HV) interconnections. The estimated budget for the first phase is US$330.5 million. For Phase 1, the CAPP
                     Secretariat will be the implementing agency for the technical assistance aspects of the project whilst the national power
                     utilities in the involved countries shall execute the on-grid access and MV cross-border investments.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     This MPA is a regional program in Cameroon, CAR, Gabon, Equatorial Guinea, and the Republic of Congo aimed at
                     strengthening the Central African Power Pool and expanding reliable electricity access. The participating countries face
                     shared challenges: low and uneven electrification, especially in rural and border zones; fragility and conflict risks (CAR &
                     parts of Cameroon); high‑value ecosystems within the Congo Basin; growing climate impacts such as flooding and
                     hydrological variability; and significant gaps in transmission and distribution infrastructure. The program operates in
                     diverse environments urban, peri‑urban, rural, and forested border areas characterized by poverty, insecurity, and rich
                     biodiversity. Indigenous Peoples are present in several locations, and complex land tenure systems require culturally
                     appropriate engagement and careful compensation processes.

                     Phase 1 focuses on institutional strengthening, regional market governance, and priority MV/LV distribution
                     investments, including MV cross‑border links between Cameroon–CAR, CMR–Gabon, CMR- Eq-G. and CMR–RoC. It also
                     finances feasibility studies and environmental and social assessments for future high‑voltage interconnections. E&S risks

                     Phase 2 will construct and commission the HV interconnections prepared under Phase 1. These works pose higher risks

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     Institutional capacity to manage environmental and social risks is constrained across all participating entities, resulting
                     in a High institutional capacity risk rating. The program depends on multiple implementing agencies national utilities,
                     sector ministries, and the CAPP Secretariat with uneven technical, fiduciary, and managerial experience in World Bank
                     operations. Weak project management, procurement delays, limited monitoring capacity, and unfamiliarity with
                     cross‑border coordination pose risks to effective and timely implementation.

                     This will be the first World Bank–financed operation directly implemented by the CAPP Secretariat, and an institutional
                     assessment confirmed that its Project Management Unit has no prior experience applying the ESF. Similarly, although all
                     five countries have established national E&S institutions, none has extensive experience with ESF‑based
                     implementation. Capacity gaps are most pronounced in monitoring, compliance, documentation, stakeholder
                     engagement, and grievance management. Targeted institutional support will therefore be required for national

                     coordination units, line ministries, and utilities.
                     To mitigate these challenges, implementation responsibilities will be shared across: (i) the CAPP Secretariat for regional
                     coordination, technical assistance, joint procurement, and M&E; (ii) national utilities for country‑level MV/LV works and
                     safeguards implementation; and (iii) a Project Management Consultant and Hands‑on Expanded Implementation
                     Support (HEIS) to guide the preparation and implementation of ESF umbrella instruments.
                     The program will also adopt joint procurement arrangements to improve efficiency and ensure harmonization across
                     countries. Embedded specialists, targeted ESF training, and strengthened project management systems will support the
                     CAPP Secretariat and PIUs in building the operational and safeguards capacity necessary to meet ESF requirements.

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                                 High


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                          High
                     The program Environmental Risk is considered high due to its large scale, cross-border nature, diverse ecosystems,
                     and the range of natural resources potentially impacted by project activities. These environmental concerns are
                     typical for regional power interconnection efforts, highlighting the need for comprehensive assessment and
                     mitigation planning. All five beneficiary countries are contiguous Central African states and members of the Congo
                     Basin Forest, recognized for his unique and rich biodiversity. The program will operate nationally across urban, peri-
                     urban, rural, and forested border regions. In Phase 1, Technical Assistance (TA) will address high voltage (HV)
                     transmission lines, which could have several downstream environmental effects, as early decisions on routing,

                     corridor width, and technology choices may lock in future impacts on biodiversity, land use, and ecosystems. Phase 1
                     also funds medium- and low-voltage (MV/LV) distribution investments and household connections, choosing from
                     vetted sub-projects. All countries except Equatorial Guinea already benefit from Bank-financed energy projects,
                     providing useful experience for selecting technical solutions that protect ecological and social integrity. Meanwhile,
                     Equatorial Guinea faces added challenges because it lacks recent Bank-backed initiatives. Future HV corridors (Phase
                     2/3) and associated facilities imply large right of way clearing and associated impacts on vegetation, fauna,
                     landscapes, land acquisition, pollution risks, community resettlement, cumulative environmental issues, and
                     transboundary effects. Phase 1 TA must therefore integrate ESS consistent mitigation hierarchy to avoid hotspots. All
                     risks are compounded by low and uneven institutional capacity among the CAPP Secretariat and national
                     implementing agencies to apply the Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), as well as the complexity of
                     harmonizing environmental standards and mitigation measures across multiple jurisdictions.

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The CAPP RIA MPA is a multi country regional energy infrastructure program in Central Africa, designed to strengthen
                     power interconnections and expand electricity access. The project involves the construction and rehabilitation of
                     transmission lines, substations, and related facilities across multiple countries and social contexts, resulting in
                     extensive interaction with communities and land users. The linear, transboundary nature of investments entails
                     engagement with diverse environments characterized by varying cultural, legal, and institutional settings. Key social
                     risks include: (i) land acquisition and economic or physical displacement, potentially complicated by customary land
                     tenure systems; (ii) impacts on vulnerable groups, including risks of exclusion and livelihood insecurity; (iii) labor
                     influx risks, such as pressure on local services, GBV/SEA/SH risks, and community–worker tensions; (iv) potential




Public Disclosure
                     impacts on cultural heritage; and (v) community health and safety risks related to construction activities, traffic, and
                     work sites. Under Phase 1, the MPA will finance project preparation and Environmental and Social (E&S) studies for
                     high voltage transmission lines. An assessment of the Project Management Unit indicates no prior experience with
                     the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF). Given the limited institutional capacity and the nature of
                     anticipated risks, the project’s social risk rating is High. Risk mitigation will rely on the preparation and
                     implementation of ESF instruments to be disclosed prior to appraisal, including an ESMF, RF, SEP, LMP, IPPF, and an
                     ESCP defining responsibilities and timelines. As subprojects are yet to be identified, only framework instruments will
                     be prepared for appraisal. Site specific instruments will be developed during implementation and applied as
                     disbursement conditions. All framework instruments will be umbrella documents covering all participating countries,
                     with country-specific annexes.

                      ESS10 - Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure                                                     Relevant

                      ESS10 applies as the project requires continuous, inclusive engagement across five countries and regional institutions.
                      A Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) and project-level Grievance Mechanism (GM) will be prepared and disclosed
                      before Appraisal. Engagement will involve project-affected communities along transmission corridors, national
                      authorities, civil society, vulnerable groups, and regional bodies such as the CAPP Secretariat and ECCAS, given their
                      coordination roles. The SEP will outline stakeholder mapping, consultation methods, information disclosure, and
                      tailored outreach to ensure meaningful and culturally appropriate participation. The GM will provide accessible and
                      confidential channels for grievance resolution. Engagement will continue throughout the project lifecycle, supported
                      by adequate capacity, resources, and clear responsibilities within national and regional institutions.

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                           Relevant

                      ESS2 is relevant as the project will engage multiple categories of workers, including direct PIU staff, contracted
                      workers for transmission and distribution works, primary supply workers, civil servants, and possibly community
                      workers. Key labor risks relate to OHS, fair terms of employment, labor influx, GBV/SEA risks, and the need for strong
                      worker grievance mechanisms. A Labor Management Procedures (LMP) will be prepared to define requirements for




Public Disclosure
                      all worker types, including non-discrimination, prohibition of child and forced labor, OHS measures aligned with WBG
                      EHS Guidelines, Codes of Conduct, and a dedicated worker GM. Contractors and suppliers will be required to comply
                      with ESS2 and the LMP, with monitoring by the PIU. Capacity-building will support effective labor risk management,
                      and the LMP will be updated as project activities evolve. The project will adopt a robust approach to labor
                      management, consistent with ESS2 and good international practices.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                            Relevant

                      ESS3 is relevant due to the Project’s construction and operation of regional power infrastructure, which will involve
                      significant resource use and pollution risks. MV/LV network expansion and household connections require materials
                      such as poles, conductors, transformers, concrete, and fuels. HV preparation impacts future material intensity and
                      footprint via corridor design and technology. Distribution design choices (loss reduction, transformer sizing) affect
                      operational efficiency, with early TA decisions influencing long-term outcomes for HV assets. Construction activities
                      need water across varied climates and may cause pollution (dust, noise, vibration, spoil, wastewater), with risks
                      differing by setting. Hazardous materials and waste, including oils (possible legacy PCB risks), fuels, solvents,
                      batteries, and e-waste, will result from upgrades and replacements. The project will comply with ESS3 requirements
                      that will be detailed in the ESMF, ESMP, monitoring plans and protocols.

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                            Relevant

                      ESS4 is relevant as the project will construct and operate transmission lines and substations near communities,
                      creating risks related to construction hazards, traffic and road safety, labor influx, communicable diseases, GBV/SEA,
                      hazardous materials, and potential emergency events. Infrastructure design and works will follow international safety

                      ESS5 - Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement                                  Relevant

                      ESS5 is relevant as the project will require land acquisition for transmission corridors, substations, and associated
                      facilities, which may lead to physical and/or economic displacement. The construction of new T-lines will require new
                      rights-of-way and related restrictions. An RPF will be prepared prior to appraisal to establish principles for
                      resettlement compensation. Site-specific RAPs will be developed during implementation once project footprints are
                      confirmed. The project will prioritize minimizing displacement through careful routing and design optimization, with
                      special attention to vulnerable groups. Institutional roles and capacity needs will be outlined in the RPF, and
                      Voluntary Land Donation if used, will follow strict ESS5 criteria. The project will ensure affected persons receive
                      compensations and livelihoods restoration support in line with ESS5 requirements. Targeted measures will be
                      implemented to address the needs of vulnerable Project-Affected Persons (PAPs).

                      ESS7 - Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional                              Relevant

                      ESS7 is considered relevant to the project at this stage. The project will be implemented across five countries,
                      Cameroon, Gabon, Central African Republic, Republic of Congo, and Equatorial Guinea and will involve the
                      construction of medium voltage (MV) transmission lines, including cross border interconnections between Cameroon
                      and CAR, Cameroon and Gabon, and Cameroon and the Republic of Congo. Available contextual information,
                      indicates that these border areas are territories traditionally occupied and used by Indigenous Peoples. Project
                      activities, particularly along MV transmission corridors and interconnection points, may therefore intersect with
                      communities meeting the ESS7 identification criteria. Although transmission alignments are not yet finalized, the
                      known presence of Indigenous Peoples in the relevant border zones is sufficient to trigger ESS7 and warrants further
                      assessment and planning in accordance with the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework.

                      ESS8 - Cultural Heritage                                                                                       Relevant

                      ESS8 is relevant as project works excavation, earth movement, and construction of transmission lines and substations
                      may affect tangible or intangible cultural heritage in a region known for archaeological sites, historic structures, and
                      culturally significant landscapes. The project will identify and assess cultural heritage through desk studies, field
                      surveys, and consultations with communities and heritage authorities. The mitigation hierarchy will guide
                      management, prioritizing avoidance, then minimizing and mitigating impacts, including documentation or restoration
                      where needed. A Chance Finds Procedure will be included in all contracts, requiring work stoppage, expert review,
                      and protection measures for unexpected discoveries. If significant heritage is identified, a Cultural Heritage
                      Management Plan will be prepared. Consultation, confidentiality of sensitive sites, and compliance with legal
                      requirements for protected cultural areas will be ensured throughout implementation.

                      ESS9 - Financial Intermediaries                                                                  Not Currently Relevant

                      The project is described as an Investment Project Financing operation in the energy and extractives sector, with no
                      indication that Bank funds will be provided through an FI mechanism. Therefore, Environmental and Social Standard 9
                      (ESS9): Financial Intermediaries is not relevant to this project.
