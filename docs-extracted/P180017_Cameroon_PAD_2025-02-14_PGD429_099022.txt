    1. INTRODUCTION AND COUNTRY CONTEXT


1.        This proposed IBRD Loan in the amount of US$200 million (equivalent) is the second operation
in a programmatic series of three Development Policy Financing (DPF) operations supporting inclusive
and sustainable growth and economic resilience in Cameroon. The program development objective is
to: (i) improve fiscal sustainability, governance, and public sector management; (ii) improve inclusion by
ensuring equitable access to services and empowering women and girls; and (iii) lay foundations for
sustainable infrastructure development. This operation is closely aligned with the country’s economic
priorities and the medium-term structural reforms underpinning its aspiration laid out in the National
Development Strategy 2020–2030 (SND30), which underscores the necessity of accelerating structural
transformation, improving fiscal management, addressing disparities, particularly in terms of gender and
regions, and enhancing climate change resilience, in line with the Paris Agreement.
2.       Economic growth in Cameroon has been low due to a host of structural bottlenecks, with 38.6
percent of the population below the national poverty line in 2021, underscoring the importance of
accelerating reforms to boost productivity and address fragility. Around 23 percent of the population
lived on less than US$2.15 per day (2017 Purchasing Power Parity) [extreme poverty threshold] in 2023.
Cameroon faces significant fiscal pressures compounded by weak domestic revenue mobilization and
considerable expenditure needs. Despite being categorized as a lower middle-income country (LMIC),
Cameroon's health and education indicators are closer to those of low-income countries, marked by
significant regional and rural-urban disparities: the country's Human Capital Index stands at 0.4, below
the averages of Sub-Saharan Africa and LMICs. The state of the agriculture and transport sectors not only
make them vulnerable to climate change, but also undermine growth prospects and livelihoods. As a
result of these factors, economic growth per-capita has averaged only 1.1 percent between 2020-2023,
with GDP per capita still 20 percent below the last peak in 1986. Accelerating growth and reducing poverty
in Cameroon thus requires: (i) structural reforms to address these bottlenecks to productivity growth and
private sector-led economic diversification,2 and (ii) addressing fragility from conflicts and insecurity, in
particular in the Anglophone regions and the Far North region. Growing insecurity and displacement stress
the urgency for socioeconomic inclusion, especially for youth vulnerable to crime and radicalization.
Improved service delivery through decentralization, as supported by DPF2, is essential to strengthening
the social contract and addressing the sources of conflict and fragility, as suggested by the conclusions of
the 2019 Grand National Dialogue.3
3.      Cameroon remains highly vulnerable to the impact of climate change, threatening economic
prospects and the livelihoods of millions of people who depend on land. The 2022 Climate Change and
Development Report (CCDR) identified four sectors as highly vulnerable to the effects of climate change:
agro-forestry, transport, energy, and urban development. Coastal areas are vulnerable to floods and rising
sea levels, while the northern regions risk frequent droughts and floods. In recent years, higher

4.       The reform agenda supported by the proposed DPF series promotes inclusive and sustainable
growth while strengthening Cameroon’s resilience to climate change. The series is organized around
three pillars aligned with the government's reform program. Pillar 1 aims to improve medium-term fiscal
sustainability, governance, and public sector management by supporting the decentralization process,
improving the governance and oversight of state-owned enterprises (SOEs), and increasing revenue
mobilization. Pillar 2 aims to improve inclusion by ensuring equitable access to services and empowering
women and girls by promoting equitable access to education across the country, providing better
identification systems, improving women's access to education, and strengthening the social protection
system. Pillar 3 aims to lay foundations for sustainable infrastructure development by improving the
maintenance of road assets and enhancing water resources management. The pillars are priorities in the
SND30, build on World Bank core analytics, and complement investment projects in key sectors (public
sector governance, social protection, digital economy, education, health, agriculture, and transport).
5.       This operation is grounded in important progress in the implementation of key reforms towards
the achievement of targeted results of this ambitious programmatic series of three operations.
However, progress in some areas has been slower-than-expected, reflecting Cameroon’s complex political
and bureaucratic system as well as the country’s fragility and exposure to multiple domestic and external
shocks. Cameroon is a unitary presidential republic, with a high degree of centralization of decision making
in the Presidency. These issues had to some extent been anticipated in the flexible and encompassing
design of this DPF series. This allowed adjustments to the DPF-supported reform program, with
completion of two prior actions (PA) (Law against Gender-Based Violence – GBV, and Road Fund reform)
now moved to the third operation in the series but with credible commitment from the government to
the continuation of the reform efforts. Nonetheless, the overall reform program has remained essential
and will be even more so in 2025, when the Presidential elections (scheduled for October 2025) will claim
significant attention of policy makers. In the complex policy and bureaucratic environment described
above, the World Bank’s DPF series plays an important role in keeping fundamental governance and
structural reforms on government’s policy agenda.
6.       An exception of the requirements of World Bank Procedure for programmatic DPF series
exceeding 24 months between approvals has been granted for this operation. The Cameroon’s First
Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable DPF (P175249) was approved on December 19, 2022. The
exception granted for this operation extends the period of Board consideration from December 20, 2024,
to June 30, 2025, to avoid a lapse in the programmatic series. The key justification for the exception is the
critical need to continue supporting important progress on reforms in Cameroon’s unique governance
environment. The operation is already showing significant impact in the supported priority areas, with
Cameroonian authorities consistently engaging with the World Bank to overcome political and
bureaucratic obstacles that have led to the delays.


 2. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK
7.      The macroeconomic policy framework is adequate for the purpose of the proposed operation.

The growth outlook has improved (4.2 percent annually on average), anchored in a continued recovery of
domestic activities, in particular the services sector and projected increased of public investment in
infrastructure. Both fiscal and monetary policies remain credible. Cameroon’s membership of Central
African Economic and Monetary Union (Communauté Économique et Monétaire de l’Afrique Centrale -
CEMAC) and the regional monitoring framework provide additional comfort, especially as the World Bank
of Central African States (Banque des États de l’Afrique Centrale, BEAC) stands ready to tighten monetary
policy if inflation increases further or regional foreign exchange reserves come under pressure. BEAC has
responded to rising inflation with monetary tightening in line with its mandates, while maintaining a fixed
peg against the Euro (EUR). The government remains committed to fiscal consolidation, as reflected in
revenue mobilization measures in the 2024 finance bill and the second increase in February 2024 in retail
fuel prices to reduce fuel subsidies. The financial sector remains stable, but vulnerabilities remain in the
banking sector. Public debt is sustainable owing to expected moderate growth in 2024-2027 and
continued fiscal consolidation.

 2.1. RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS
8.       Cameroon’s economic growth is estimated at 3.7 percent in 2024 after a deceleration in 2023
at 3.3 percent, down from 3.6 percent in 2022. The growth deceleration in 2023 spanned all economic
sectors—primary, secondary, and tertiary—amidst fiscal consolidation, rising domestic inflation, and
ongoing internal conflicts. However, a modest increase occurred over the first half of 2024, with real Gross
Domestic Product (GDP) growth of 3.4 percent and 3.7 percent for the first and second quarters. The
service sector was the main contributor to this while primary sector growth fell due to reduced logging
activities. Oil field depletion continues to dampen the dynamism of the secondary sector, which has been
somewhat strengthened by steady improvement in energy supply. On the demand side, private
consumption and investment remain the main drivers of growth in 2023 while public investment and net
exports lagged with a negative contribution to real growth; public consumption remained contained even
though the authorities increased public servants’ salaries and settled salary arrears.
9.        However, Cameroon’s growth performance remains significantly below its potential and also
below the government’s own growth ambition to become an upper-middle income by 2035. Reforms
supported by this operation address selected governance and structural issues that are necessary to
enhance Cameroon’s growth trajectory. However, more fundamental reforms that would foster private
sector activities through an improved business environment and adequate infrastructure and government
services are needed. Such reforms would also have to support technological upgrading in the private
sector and improvements in the international trade regime for exports to become an engine of growth,
fully utilizing Cameroon’s rich natural and human resource endowments. The World Bank is working with
the authorities on a Country Growth and Jobs Report that will identify specific reforms needed to generate
much needed higher and more inclusive economic growth and employment. This work will also feed in
government’s review of its National Development Strategy SND30.
10.     The current account deficit increased to 4.1 percent of GDP in 2023 from 3.4 percent in 2022
driven by a widening trade deficit. After the hydrocarbon price boom which boosted export revenues in
2022, prices declined in 2023 which, combined with the decline in hydrocarbon production, led to a
widened trade deficit of 3.9 percent of GDP against 2.6 percent in 2022. Over the first eight months of
2024, skyrocketing cocoa beans prices have significantly improved Cameroon’s export performance: while
hydrocarbon exports continue to drop, non-hydrocarbon exports increased by 4.3 percent in quantities
and 29.2 percent in value. With the easing global prices, the import bill should be contained in 2024.

11.     Average inflation in Cameroon is cooling, reaching 4.9 percent as of end September 2024,
brought down by a restrictive monetary policy stance, easing global prices and price controls for select
products. In 2023, inflation was primarily driven by domestic supply factors, particularly the effects of
higher fuel prices on transportation, fresh products, and energy price dynamics. The impact of increased
fuel prices permeated throughout the economy, raising mostly food prices (10.9 percent) and
transportation costs (15.0 percent), which significantly contributed to the final prices of domestically
produced foods. These effects disappeared steadily and since the second half of 2023, average inflation
has started to decline, and the recent fuel price increase in February 2024 has not reversed this trend.
12.     The monetary policy stance has remained restrictive to strengthen the region’s external
position and bring back average inflation below the 3.0 percent target. The policy rate has been kept at
5.0 percent since March 2023 by the regional central bank (BEAC). At the CEMAC level, reserve coverage
of money was far above the minimum 15 percent as of end July 2024, at 71.9 percent. However, CEMAC’s
net foreign assets (NFA) have shown a concerning recent pattern: since June 2024, they have been below
the targets agreed with the IMF. Ongoing discussions between IMF, BEAC and national authorities might

result in sustainable corrective measures like a further hike in key rate or a stricter enforcement of forex
regulations. Cameroon’s real effective exchange rate (REER), which has been broadly stable since the 1994
devaluation, depreciated by 3.1 percent in 2022 due to the 12.5 percent depreciation of the euro against
the US dollar and higher domestic inflation. In 2023 and the first half of 2024, the REER bounced back,
reflecting the euro’s appreciation against the US dollar.
13.       Although Cameroon’s banking system remained robust, vulnerabilities persist and are related
to solvency challenges and high sovereign exposure. Liquidity ratios remained steady at 29.8 percent,
and the banking sector’s capital adequacy ratio increased from 15.0 percent at the end of 2022 to 16.3
percent by the second quarter of 2023, surpassing the regulatory requirement of 8 percent set by the
regional banking commission (Central African Banking Supervision Agency - Commission Bancaire de
l'Afrique Centrale -COBAC). However, solvency risks are persistent: non-performing loans reached 15.4
percent of total gross loans by the second quarter of 2023, up from 13.0 percent at the end of 2022, due
to (i) insufficient governance and risk management frameworks in commercial banks and (ii) continued
government’ arrears accumulation which harms companies’ financial situation. Risks to financial stability
also arise from the banking system’s exposure to sovereign bonds, which has increased in recent years to
reach about 35.3 percent of total assets in 2022.
14.     The fiscal deficit should remain at 0.8 percent of GDP in 2024, same as in 2023 but down from
1.1 percent in 2022, supported by reduced fuel subsidies, improved tax collection and capital spending
cuts. Non-oil revenues surged to 13.3 percent of GDP in 2023, up from 12.1 percent in 2022, reflecting
the impact of revenue measures introduced in the 2023 and 2024 budgets. The reduction in fuel subsidies
through two successive retail price adjustments in February 2023 (21 percent increase) and February 2024
(15 percent increase) significantly lowered the fuel subsidy bill from 2.9 percent to 0.8 percent of GDP
between 2022 and 2024. Nevertheless, the resulting saving seems to have been used to expand the State’s
support to SOEs and public establishments, underscoring the need for bolder SOE reforms like the State’s
ownership policy that will be supported under DPF3. Capital spendings have significantly declined over
recent years: they represented 3.5 percent of GDP in 2023, down from 4.6 percent in 2022. For the first
time in fifteen years, the primary balance turned positive in 2023, marking a noteworthy milestone in
Cameroon's fiscal trajectory.
15.      Public debt fell from 44.6 percent of GDP to 42.0 between December 2023 and June 2024, amid
ongoing fiscal consolidation. Cameroon’s public debt is deemed sustainable, although with a high risk of
debt distress due to liquidity ratios breaching the thresholds. Exports proceeds (which have declined from
22.2 percent of GDP in 2010 to 13.5 percent in 2022, due to internal conflicts, the trade disruptions caused
by the Covid-19 pandemic and the declining oil production) and fiscal revenue are insufficient to cover
adequately debt servicing. Moreover, tighter international and domestic financial conditions led to a rise
in interest charges from 0.8 to 1.0 percent of GDP between 2022 and 2023. Important steps have been
taken recently in terms of debt coverage and reporting from SOEs and Public Private Partnerships (PPP)
projects, while gaps remain regarding domestic arrears. Arrears accumulation results from the extensive
use of treasury advances without budget allocation and exceptional budget procedures such as imprest
accounts, provisional commitments, and cash advances. Moreover, the budget system lacks robust
monitoring and evaluation mechanisms to track actual spending against the budget plan.

 2.2. MACROECONOMIC OUTLOOK AND DEBT SUSTAINABILITY
16.      Cameroon’s economic prospects show an improvement over the medium term, but economic
growth remains below the country’s potential and peers and insufficient to meet the country’s
development objectives. Real GDP growth is projected to reach 4.2 percent, on average, over 2025–2027,
grounded in improved energy supply and higher public investment. The full commissioning of the
Nachtigal hydropower plant that started to inject power into the grid since June 2024 and the completion
of the transmission lines from it towards the industrial zones in Douala will provide an additional 420 MW
(30 percent of country’s total energy generation) to ease energy supply shortages and boost activities in
the manufacturing sector. Moreover, the construction sector will directly benefit from the stepped-up
public investment in the medium-term, while there will be a knock-on effect on the other economic
sectors, especially the service sector. Average inflation is expected to gradually converge to the BEAC’s
medium-term target of 3 percent.
17.       The current account deficit is expected to remain around 4.0 percent of GDP over the medium
term, reflecting a decline in oil production, and higher imports needed for the scaled up public
investment. Government policies to reduce constraints faced by the private sector and ensure fairer
competition would expand the production of some cash crops and development of value chains for wood
and agricultural products, and thereby bolster Cameroon’s export performance in the medium-term. The
BEAC will enforce further the foreign exchange regulations, limiting capital outflows. The current account
deficit is expected to be covered mainly by concessional external financing and foreign direct investment,
as tighter global financial conditions are likely to constrain Cameroon's ability to tap international and
CEMAC’s capital markets.
18.     Regional monetary policy is likely to remain restrictive over the medium term to bring NFAs
closer to the IMF target, despite regional inflation being likely to fall below the 3 percent target.
Commercial banks are expected to progressively reduce their sovereign exposure, as CEMAC’s
governments and BEAC have started to enforce the of bank’s obligation to sell a minimum share of

       3. GOVERNMENT PROGRAM
24. Cameroon's Vision 2035 aims to achieve upper-middle-income status by 2035, focusing on poverty
reduction, industrialization, and consolidating democracy and national unity. The SND30 adopted in
November 2020 supports this vision by b promoting economic growth, improving social services,
strengthening climate resilience, and enhancing governance. SND30’s four pillars: (1) structural economic
transformation, (2) human capital development; (3) job creation and economic inclusion; and (4) governance,
decentralization, and strategic state management. Pillars 2 and 3 emphasize social inclusion, while Pillar 4
seeks to address various drivers of conflict through: (i) advancing decentralization and local development; (ii)
strengthening rule of law and the security of people and property reduction; (iii) enhancing public services;
(iv) strengthening economic and financial governance by rigorously fighting corruption, embezzlement, and
conflicts of interest;4 and (v) improving regional planning. As Cameroon faces multiple crises, adhering to
these long-term goals is essential for achieving sustainable, resilient, and inclusive development.

 Pillar 1: IMPROVING FISCAL SUSTAINABILITY, GOVERNANCE, AND PUBLIC SECTOR MANAGEMENT
 Policy Area 1: Supporting the decentralization process.
 Prior Action #1. To improve the financing of decentralized entities and strengthen their capacities the
 Borrower has: (i) submitted the draft law on local taxation to Parliament for adoption setting the definition
 of the Dotation Générale de la Décentralisation (DGD), as evidenced by transmission letter dated October

 30.      Context and challenges. Decentralization is critical for improving service delivery and building
 resilience to fragility in Cameroon, as emphasized in the 2019 Major National Dialogue. Recognizing this,
 the Risks and Resilience Assessment (RRA) identified the concentration of resources and authority at the
 central level as a key conflict driver. Progress has been made in defining the mandates of municipalities
 and regions in specific policy domains, particularly under DPF1, but the legal framework remains
 incomplete. While the 2019 Decentralization Code expanded local competencies, central entities continue
 to deliver most services through deconcentrated entities. Limited financial resources further constrain
 subnational authorities' capacity to function effectively. Municipalities primarily rely on funding from the
 Ministry of Decentralization and Local Development (MINDDEVEL)’s DGD, sector-specific allocations from
 sector ministries and fiscal revenue subject to equalization. While the Decentralization Code assigns
 certain revenue sources to regions, only transfers from the DGD have reached them thus far, and the local
 taxation law has clarified the 15 percent of public revenue coverage and distribution mandated by the
 Decentralization Code. Fiscal transfers to subnational authorities also vary with the poorest regions
 receiving disproportionately low per capita funding in sectors, exacerbating disparities. Local tax revenue
 potential is hindered by poverty and informality, and perfectible fiscal transfers to local authorities.

Addressing these challenges is essential to empower subnational entities and ensure equitable
development across Cameroon’s regions.
31.      Substance of the Prior Action. This prior action promotes increased fiscal revenue for local
governments and the transfer of competencies and resources to regions, especially health and secondary
education, to improve human capital, productivity, and economic growth. The DGD covers State budget
allocations coming from MINDDEVEL and the sectors who have transferred functions. In the proposed
Local Taxation Law, the definition of the DGD has been clarified as excluding fiscal revenue transferred to
Local Governments (LG), which means potentially more resources for LGs. An effort has been made to
rationalize the number of communal taxes and fees for more efficiency and some ways have been
identified through which to increase the revenue of communes. The Law could, however, have benefitted

partially mitigated through increased sensitization efforts on the Law. (Prior Action #1.i). Also, Decree No.
2023/223 of April 27, 2023, specifies the terms and conditions for transferring certain powers related to
secondary education from the state to regions. These include regional planning, school infrastructure,
recruitment and support staff management, procurement of materials, scholarships distribution,
representation in state school management, municipal actions related to primary and nursery education
and enhancing high schools’ and colleges’ functioning through local involvement of parent-teacher
associations. Decree No. 2023/132 of February 10, 2023 also transfers health competencies such as
creation and management of regional health facilities, equipment and maintenance, organizing and
management of essential drugs, and management of regional nursing and paramedical personnel and
district hospitals staff (Prior Action #1.ii).

distributing the DGD (DPF3 Trigger#1).
33.     Expected results and indicators. Reforms support decentralization process by effectively
transferring competencies and increasing equitable regional financing for better service delivery. Decrees
outline these transfers, raising the share of regional and municipal budgets in the national budget from 1
percent in 2019 (under the DGD) to 7 percent in 2026.
Policy Area 2: Improving governance of state-owned enterprises.
Prior Action #2. To improve the governance and monitoring of SOEs, the Borrower has: (i) adopted the
requirement of the use of a detailed methodology for preparing SOE performance contracts (including
financial and non-financial targets and mechanisms to ensure compliance) to assess the performance of
SOEs, as evidenced by the Minister in charge of finance’s circulaire Number 0000861/MIFI dated October
6, 2023, (ii) concluded and signed performance contracts with two large SOEs, as evidenced by the contracts
signed between the ministries responsible for finance, transport, and economy on one hand, and the Port
of Douala on January 31, 2024, and CAMAIR-CO on December 20, 2024 on the other, and (iii) adopted the
restructuring plan for CAMWATER based on international best practice and in accordance with the
Diagnostic study adopted by the Technical commission on the rehabilitation of SOEs, as evidenced by
CAMWATER’s board of directors decision Number 001/CA/CAMWATER dated November 18, 2024.
34.   Context and challenges. Weaknesses in governance and ambiguous state relations make
Cameroon’s SOEs fiscally risky. With over 60 SOEs in sectors like electricity, oil and gas, irrigation, finance,

           The World Bank
           Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth Development Policy Financing (P180017)


and transportation, their high debt levels (12.6 percent of GDP in 2021) and poor financial performance6
create fiscal vulnerabilities. These SOEs rely on government support via direct subsidies for operations and
capital investments, recapitalizations, loan guarantees, and on-lending, since many face losses due to a
lack of transparency, ineffective oversight, and quasi-fiscal activities. Governance issues are exacerbated
by outdated recruitment methods, lax reporting standards and delayed audits with only 23 out of 68 SOEs
publishing annual financial information in 2021. More than 68 percent of SOE board directors continue to
serve despite expired mandates, contravening legal provisions limiting their terms of office. 7 Late and
incomplete financial reporting, coupled with the absence of analytical financial or operational reports,
hinder effective monitoring of SOE performance. The IMF in 2021 recommended performance-based
contracts to enhance efficiency of public enterprises.8 In response, the Ministry of Finance (Ministère des
Finances, MINFI) issued an instruction in July 2022 mandating all SOEs to enter into performance contracts
with the state by end-2024, though specifics remain unclear in terms of indicators and target calculation
methods, posing a challenge to its effective implementation. Addressing these SOEs governance issues is
critical for improving their fiscal and operational stability.
35.       Substance of the Prior Action: This PA aims to enhance SOE governance and improve their
financial stability amid operational and debt challenges. To boost governance, the Technical Commission

Policy Area 3: Improving Domestic Revenue Mobilization
Prior Action #3. To increase domestic revenue mobilization, the Borrower has: (i) streamlined tax
exemptions to be aligned with the 2022 CEMAC Directive by reducing the number of basic necessities and
the list of staple foods benefiting from tax exemptions, as evidenced by the enactment of Law No 2023/019
dated December 19, 2023, (ii) adopted country-by-country reporting (CbCR) standard and the framework
for the automatic exchange of financial information as required by the Base Erosion and Profit Shifting
action plan, in compliance with Article 6 of the convention, and strengthened the sanctions regime related
to the reporting obligation of companies under the Department of Large Tax Payers, as evidenced by the
enactment of Law No 2023/019 dated December 19, 2023, and (iii) authorized the sharing of data on
international financial transactions related to foreign trade with the BEAC to verify accuracy of customs
declaration, as evidenced by the Memorandum of Understanding between the General Directorate of
Customs and the BEAC, dated March 29, 2023, regarding the establishment of a formal framework for
consultation, collaboration, and exchange of information and data between the two parties.
38.     Context and challenges. Cameroon's non-oil revenue-to-GDP ratio is below the 15 percent
benchmark needed to support basic government functions. The primary factors contributing to its low tax
revenues include tax expenditures, a substantial informal sector, and insufficient revenue from the CIT as
well as personal income tax. Tax base erosion is mainly due to value-added tax (VAT) exemptions and
preferential rates undermining productivity. Over the past decade, the CIT revenue amounted to only
about 1.6 percent of GDP, due to profit shifting by multinational companies. Despite major anti-avoidance
provisions in the tax code, weaknesses in tax administration persist, including a lack of automated
information exchange, risk-based audit approaches, and systematic data collection for identifying tax
evasion risks.

tax revenue. Under Prior Action #3. ii, the government has established the country-by-country reporting

Policy Area 4: Improving cash management.
Prior Action #4: To improve cash management, the Borrower has (i) established the Treasury Single

42.     Context and challenges. Government cash and Treasury management currently relies on manual
processes involving check- and cash-based payments, maintaining multiple bank accounts, holding large
cash floats, and allowing idle balances that earn little to no interest. This contributes to the lack of visibility
within the Treasury of funds available to various budget entities. Government entities maintain deposits
across more than 1,000 active accounts at Cameroonian commercial banks, estimated at FCFA 1,186 billion
(4.6 percent of GDP). As a result, budget execution is impaired by frequent liquidity constraints, leading to
delays in the flow of funds to spending units and the accumulation of payment arrears. These weaknesses
in cash management resulted in the accumulation of domestic payment obligations and delays in external
payments in 2022-23, leading to a deterioration in Cameroon’s credit rating by credit agencies. 11

Policy Area 5. Improving Equity in the Education System
Prior Action #5. To improve efficiency and equity in the education sector, the Borrower has (i) set and
strengthened the modalities and the conditions for redeployment and/or assignment of teachers to
administrative position in line ministries or in other ministries, as evidenced by Borrower’s Prime Minister
circular Number 003/CAB/PM dated December 29, 2023, (ii) regulated and set a mandatory cap on the
costs of school projects managed by the Student Parents’ association and other contributions from parents
as evidenced by Borrower’s Minister of Secondary Education’s Circular letter No 14/23/LC/MINESEC/CAB
dated December 28, 2023 and Circular Letter No 11/B/1464.L-C/MINEDUB/IGS dated November 27, 2023.
46.     Context and Challenges. Despite recent progress, education costs remain unaffordable for many
families in Cameroon. This is due to limited public resources allocated to the education sector, inefficient
and inequitable resource allocation within the sector, and ineffective management of public spending.
Public education spending is only 18.3 percent of total spending, below recommended benchmark levels,
leaving parents to cover up to 31 and 21 percent of primary education and secondary education costs.
Parent association fees account for 78 and 31 percent of parental payments in public primary and
secondary schools, respectively. Extra costs like books and uniforms add further strain (about nearly 10
percent of total education spending). Although registration fees were officially abolished in public primary
schools, they still make up over 15 percent of education expenses due to inconsistent weak regulated
nature of non-payable fees. There are also equity issues in the distribution of teachers’ and schools across
regions. Despite substantial efforts made by the government over the last five years to improve the
recruitment process, human resource management in education continues to face multiple challenges
related to teacher deployment, retention, career development, and attrition. The Ministry of Basic
Education’s (Ministère de l’Éducation de Base, MINEDUB) latest census reveals that 86 percent of primary
teacher placements are unrelated to actual needs. A 2022 study found random teacher assignments in
secondary schools, with regional disparities reaching 30.1 percent. Furthermore, the higher migration of
women to other professions in Cameroon had previously negatively impacted the education system.
Maintaining a higher proportion of female teachers is critical to support inclusive educational environment
which will encourages girls to pursue their education and helps in bridging the gender gap in the
educational system.
47.     Substance of the Prior Action. Under Prior Action #5.i, the Prime Minister Circular n 003/CAB/PM
(December 29, 2023) clarifies rules for redeploying or assigning teachers to administrative roles in various
ministries. Reinforcing circular n#005/CAB/PM (June 07, 2011), it introduces two new conditions for
approving teacher assignments outside the education sector or secondments to SOEs. These conditions
require prior authorization from the relevant minister and notification of the Prime Minister’s Services and
Public Service Minister. The reforms undertaken by the Government of Cameroon within the framework
of the Teacher Policy, reinforced by circulars number 003/CAB/PM (December 29, 2023) and number
005/CAB/PM (June 07, 2011), have significantly improved the proportion of female teachers in the
educational system. These reforms have led to an increase in the percentage of women among the total
teaching staff to 51.75 percent in 2024, with 35,150 female teachers out of a total of 67,921. Studies show
that having women math teachers, for example, improved girls’ math outcomes (Evans and Nestour 2019),

Policy Area 8: Improving access to services and fostering the digital economy
Prior action#7. To improve access to services to all, including women and other vulnerable groups, through
better identification means, the Borrower has submitted the draft law on the digitalization of civil status
to Parliament for adoption, as evidenced by transmission letter dated October 31, 2024, to introduce
simplified, computerized and inclusive processes for the registration of births as well as other civil status
facts.
60.      Context and Challenges. Nearly 7.5 million Cameroonians - almost one-third of the population-
lack official identification, with over one-third of children unregistered at birth and over half lacking birth
certificates. Additionally, 2.5 million adults are unable to obtain national identification due to the
requirement of a birth certificate. This limits access to basic services and economic opportunities, such as
education, social assistance, financial services, or formal employment, disproportionately affecting
women, rural residents, the poorest 40 percent of the population, and Internally Displaced Persons (IDPs).
The legal framework governing Cameroon's civil registration (CR) ecosystem, governed by Law 2011-011
amending provisions of Ordinance 81-02,17 inadequately addresses Cameroon’s heavily paper-based CR
processes for recording events, archiving records, and issuing certified copies. This hampers efforts to
digitalize these processes and recognize the legal value of electronic records, impeding access to vital
services and increasing the risk of duplicate registrations and fraud (which in turn leads to leakages and

Policy Area 10. Improving Water Resource Management
Prior Action #8. To improve water resources management and increase the irrigated areas, the Borrower
has defined the modalities of agricultural water management and maintenance of hydraulic infrastructure
in irrigated areas in Cameroon allowing as well the delegation of their management by legal entity under
private law, as evidenced by Borrower’s Prime Minister Decree Number 2024/00176/PM dated of February
26, 2024.
67.       Context and Challenges. With over half of Cameroon's households spending more than 40
percent of their income on food purchases, along with over 15 percent of households vulnerable to food
insecurity, effective irrigation is essential to ensure the country’s food supply. Unfortunately, irrigation
development and management in Cameroon has not improved much since the 1980s. There is currently
no specific legal framework for agricultural water use and the maintenance of hydraulic infrastructure in
irrigated areas. The heavy involvement of the Company for the Expansion and Modernization of Rice
Production (SEMRY), an SOE, in all stages of rice production, from land preparation to commercialization,
has resulted in poor outcomes. In some regions like Far North, farmers can only harvest once per year in
irrigated areas due to inefficiencies. To enhance efficiency, SEMRY aims to focus on the maintenance of
large infrastructure, while water users and producer organizations will be responsible for rice production.
The recent Country Climate Diagnostic Report (CCDR) suggests that adopting climate-smart production
practices, alongside institutional and governance reforms in irrigation and water resource management,
can support sustainable agricultural intensification and enhance the resilience of agricultural production
systems. Irrigation schemes offer advantages over both public agencies and individual efforts, as they can
contribute to better irrigation system performance through coordinated and organized activities.
Improving water availability for households engaged in farming can reduce waterborne diseases, enhance
agricultural productivity, and decrease the time spent by women and children fetching water, thereby
bolstering the income prospects of rural communities, in particular women.

and equitable cost-sharing among users. The reform is crucial for enhancing agricultural productivity and
resilience against climate variability and water scarcity.

Policy Area 11. Improving the Performance of Electric Utilities
73.       Context and challenges. The Cameroonian electricity distribution company (The energy of
Cameroon - ENEO) is in financial distress, contributing to high operational inefficiencies which constitute
major bottlenecks for electricity service delivery. The distribution utility has been in the hands of the
private sector for the last twenty years without improvement in service delivery due to lack of investment
to upgrade the infrastructure and weak management system. The majority shareholder of the company,
Actis, has expressed interest in exiting the Cameroonian market and the government, with TA financed by
the World Bank, is in the process of acquiring its shares. The decision to acquire the shares was motivated
by the failure of Actis to attract a private buyer and the urgency to stop further deterioration of the sector.
Once the distribution company is nationalized, the major challenge ahead would be to put in place a
professional independent management system that would ensure efficiency, transparency, and
progressive elimination of government subsidies. The sector is at a critical juncture especially with the
commissioning of the 420 MW Nachtigal hydropower plant that started to inject power into the grid since
June 2024 with full commissioning expected in March 2025. The plant is likely to increase financial losses
in the sector in the short term if distribution and transmission are not upgraded. It is estimated that each
month of delay in acquiring Actis’ shares in ENEO and implementing its turnaround plan will cost the
government about FCFA 10-15 billion. In addition, the sector is in a liquidity crunch position, putting the
distribution company in an almost-bankruptcy situation. The situation is likely to get worse with full
commissioning of Nachtigal in March 2025 that will increase the bill of strategic suppliers by at least FCFA
10 billion per month, putting at high risk the call on WBG guarantees (Multilateral Investment Guarantee
Agency - MIGA and IBRD) related to the Independent Power Producers (IPPs) owned by Globeleq 19 as well
as Nachtigal Hydro-Power Company. The arrears situation calls for an urgent financial restructuring as part
of the turnaround plan for the sector.

           5.3. PFM, DISBURSEMENT AND AUDITING ASPECTS
87.      Despite improvements made in recent years, Cameroon's PFM system remains weak due to
persistent shortcomings in budget execution, internal controls, procurement, and budget transparency.
The 2023 Public Expenditure Financial Assessment (PEFA) report highlights several critical public financial
management (PFM) challenges in Cameroon. Key issues identified include among others less reliable
budget execution, transparency gaps in reporting government financial activities, challenges in controlling
payroll and procurement processes, limited internal audit coverage and weak legislative scrutiny of audit
reports limit the government’s capacity to detect and correct financial mismanagement. The budget
documents and reports are published annually on the MINFI’s website (https://minfi.gov.cm/). The 2024-
2027 PFM reform strategy and action plan have recently been developed drawing on the PEFA to enable
the implementation of reforms for «effective and accountable financial governance throughout the
country, supported by a high performing integrated information system».
88.       The dematerialization of procurement procedures is the main leverage for reforming the public
procurement system. Cameroon's public procurement system under the 2018 Public Procurement Code
faces persistent inefficiencies despite reforms aimed at decentralizing responsibilities and improving
complaint mechanisms. Challenges include lengthy contracting processes (120-197 days), low budget
execution rates (55 percent) and gaps in coordination between regulatory entities. While compliance has
improved thanks to stricter enforcement and independent oversight by ARMP and the Appeals
Commission, delays in decision-making and the under-utilization of online procurement platforms, such as
Cameroon Online E-Procurement System (COLEPS), are hampering progress, with less than 2 percent of
contracts awarded online. To address these issues, reforms are underway, including the assessment of the
procurement system using the MAPS approach with the support of the AfDB and other stakeholders. The
government plans to dematerialize all stages of procurement, improve platform interoperability between
COLEPS and Pridesoft software (Open, multi-platform integrated management system -Système intégré de
gestion ouvert et multiplateformes), and provide training to increase the adoption of e-procurement. The
World Bank's PforR initiative aims to significantly increase the percentage of fully digitized contracts by
2025, while promoting sustainable and climate-sensitive public procurement. Complementary measures,
such as the simplification of review procedures and the revision of complaint mechanisms, aim to improve
the efficiency, transparency and management of public spending.
89.       The BEAC has introduced measures to improve its governance and control functions. Since 2013,
it has launched a series of reforms to enhance the capacity of its accounting system, including: (i) upgrading
practices to conform with international financial reporting standards; (ii) strengthening its computerized
accounting system (SYSCOBEAC) to improve information management; and (iii) implementing a


                                                                                                 Page 33 of 71
         The World Bank
         Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth Development Policy Financing (P180017)


computerized system for integrated risk management (SIRISBEAC) to better manage risks arising from the
diversification of activities and the integration of new technology. Meanwhile, the scope of external audits
of BEAC activities has expanded to include its national directorates and other agencies. The BEAC has
implemented priority recommendations from the 2017 safeguards assessment, and it issued its FY’22 and
FY’23 audited financial statements in full compliance with International Financial Reporting Standards, with
the auditor issuing an unmodified opinion on the financial statements for both years. The 2022 update
safeguards assessment found that the BEAC maintained strong governance arrangements following legal
reforms in 2017.
90.      Disbursement, reporting, and audits. The Borrower is the government represented by MINEPAT.
The loan will be released in a single tranche equivalent to US$200 million upon effectiveness and disbursed
based on a withdrawal application. The proposed operation will follow IBRD standard disbursement
procedures for DPF operations. Upon approval of the operation and the effectiveness of the financing
agreement, the proceeds of the loan will be disbursed by IBRD into an account designated by the
government for budget support at the BEAC, which will form part of the country's foreign exchange
reserves. The loan proceeds shall not be used to finance expenditures excluded under the financing
agreement. The Borrower shall ensure that upon the loan's deposit into the said account, an equivalent
amount shall be credited in the Borrower's budget management system in a manner acceptable to IBRD
and denominated in XAF based on the market rate effective on the date of the transfer. The Borrower will
promptly report to the World Bank within 30 days by fax or email: (i) the exact sum received into the
account; (ii) the details of the account to which the local currency equivalent of the loan proceeds was
credited; and (iii) confirmation that an equivalent amount has been accounted for in the government's
budget management system. IBRD reserves the right to require an audit of the account designated by the
government for budget support by independent auditors acceptable to it. If the proceeds are used in a
manner inconsistent with the provisions in the financing agreement, the Borrower shall be required to
refund an amount equal to the amount of the payment directly to IBRD promptly upon notice. Amounts
refunded to the World Bank upon such request will be credited to the loan account and canceled. The
closing date for the operation is December 31, 2026.

 6.       SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION
93.      The overall risks associated with this operation are substantial but manageable through
established mitigation measures. Political and governance; and Macroeconomic risks categories are rated
high.
94.        Political and governance risks are rate as high. Political and governance risks are heightened by
Cameroon’s sociopolitical crisis in the Northwest and Southwest, and Boko-Haram attacks in the Far North.
Upcoming elections in October 2025 may reduce the pace of the reforms. These challenges threaten
stability and may divert resources from reform. The authorities have also taken essential steps to curb the
adverse social impacts of shocks by extending social safety net programs and providing targeted subsidies
for fertilizers to improve crop yields. Prior actions under this DPF aims at improving decentralization, social
inclusion and digitalization of CR systems and will contribute to address the drivers of fragility and conflict
and reinforce the social contract.
95.      Macroeconomic risks are rated as substantial risks stem from Cameroon’s reliance on oil and raw
materials exports, as well as imports of key goods like fuel and rice. These dependencies heighten
vulnerability to external shocks, impacting fiscal stability. Low global commodity prices could cut export
earnings and government revenue, potentially forcing reductions in capital and social spending. High prices
for food, energy, and fertilizer may keep inflation high, adding to fiscal pressures via increased subsidies
and tax exemptions. Monetary tightening in advanced economies could worsen the economic outlook and
debt sustainability. Declines in exports and tax revenue, coupled with weak SOE governance, add further
fiscal and debt risks, threatening program implementation by reducing the government’s fiscal space.
While these risks cannot be fully mitigated, fiscal controls and tax reforms—including reduced fuel
subsidies—partially address them. Additionally, new tax and customs measures under the national budget,
supported by the IMF’s ECF-EFF program, anchor prudent policies to help manage these risks. The
government is implementing several PFM reforms under the PFM Reform Strategic Plan for 2024-2027.
96.      Institutional capacity risks are rated as substantial. There are often lengthy delays between
program decision and execution, as executive power is highly centralized at the Presidency which delays
the decision-making process. To mitigate these risks, the team engages with high-level authorities,
including the Prime Minister’s office, key ministers, and the office of the President from the beginning of
the program, and the team keeps them regularly informed on the program’s development to ensure their
buy-in and willingness to champion DPF-supported measures.
97.      Fiduciary risk is deemed substantial. This assessment results from a combined evaluation of the
performance of the country's PFM system and the BEAC’s safeguards framework, accounting systems, and
auditing arrangements. While the BEAC has successfully implemented institutional and technical reforms
to improve its processes and practices, Cameroon's PFM system still suffers from various shortcomings,
especially in terms of implementing the new budget and procurement reforms. With support from the IMF,



                                                                                                 Page 35 of 71
         The World Bank
         Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth Development Policy Financing (P180017)


the WBG, and other donors, the government continues to implement several PFM reforms under a
comprehensive PFM reform action plan for 2022-2023.

Mr. President
This letter of development policy is part of the second operation of your institution's budget support, in conjunction with
the Fiscal Sustainability and Inclusive Growth DPF series for the Republic of Cameroon. It describes the recent performance
of economic activity at the national level and specifies the reforms implemented, as well as those envisaged in the short
term by the Government, aimed at: (i) improving fiscal sustainability, governance, and public sector management; (ii)
improving inclusion by ensuring equitable access to services and empowering women and girls; and (iii) laying foundations
for sustainable infrastructure development.

    1. Recent macroeconomic performance.

Despite a particularly difficult international context, the Cameroonian economy is expected to register a revival of activity
in 2024 with growth estimated at 3.8% compared to 3.2% in 2023. This development is explained in particular by the
dynamics of the non-oil sector, whose growth is projected at 4.1% in 2024, after a slowdown observed in 2023. Indeed,
the real growth rate of the non-oil sector in 2023 was 3.6% after reaching 3.9% in 2022. Oil activity continued to decline
in 2024 (-2.6%) due to lower yields from existing oil wells.
As for inflation, it has started to decelerate after the sharp pressures experienced in 2022 and 2023, due to the combined
effects of higher fuel prices at the pump and the rising costs of imported goods. Thus, the inflation rate is expected to be
around 5% in 2024 compared to 7.4% in 2023 and 6.3% in 2022. This deceleration is mainly explained by the reduction of
inflationary pressures at the global level and the adoption of specific government measures aimed at reducing speculative
behavior.
With respect to public finances, the fiscal consolidation policy continued in order to free up the margins needed to finance
capital expenditure. Thus, the overall fiscal balance should continue to improve with a surplus of 0.1% of GDP in 2024,
after deficits of 0.6% in 2023 and 1.1% in 2022. Similarly, the non-oil fiscal balance deficit declined from 3.9% in 2022 to
2.5% in 2023 and is projected at 1.3% in 2024.
These developments reflect the combined effects of an increase in non-oil revenue mobilization and a rationalization of
current expenditures. Indeed, the tax ratio is expected to rise to 13.3% of GDP in 2024, after having stood at 13.1% in 2023
and 12.1% in 2022. As a result, the ratio of current expenditure to GDP fell from 13.3% in 2023 to 12% in 2024.
As far as debt dynamics are concerned, they have remained under control. As of end-September 2024, the Central
Government's outstanding debt is estimated at CFAF 12,658 billion, or about 40% of GDP. This stock is made up of 67.9
percent of external debt (26.6 percent of GDP) and 32.1 percent of domestic debt, including overdue payments older than
three months (12.7 percent of GDP). Projections indicate that this debt should reach 38.0% of GDP (CFAF 12,141 billion)
by December 31, 2024 compared to 39.4% of GDP (CFAF 11,786 billion) at the end of December 2023, a decrease of 1.4
points.
Regarding the monetary situation, the evolution of the main aggregates remained strong in 2023 and is expected to
continue in 2024, despite the tightening of the Central Bank's financial conditions necessary to combat inflation. The
money supply grew by 3.2% between December 2022 and December 2023, in line with the strong growth in credit to the
economy (+16.5%). Similarly, at the end of July 2024, this aggregate increased by 9.0% to reach CFAF 8779.0 billion.
For official reserves, they stood at CFAF 2,729 billion at the end of July 2024, thus recording declines of 6.9% and 12.2%
respectively compared to the situation at the end of December 2023 and the end of July 2023.

       The World Bank
       Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth Development Policy Financing (P180017)


In terms of external trade, the current account deficit widened in 2023 due to the combined effects of higher imports and
lower exports of goods and services. Indeed, the said deficit deteriorated by 279.8 billion FCFA between 2022 and 2023
to 1,221.1 billion FCFA (4.1% of GDP). The deficit is projected to gradually decline over the 2024-2025 period to average
around 2.9 percent of GDP, in line with the implementation of measures to promote non-oil exports, substitute imports
with local products, and strengthen regional integration.
The economic outlook remains positive, despite uncertainties in the international environment. Growth is forecast at 4.1%
in 2025 and 4.5% on average over the 2026-2027 period. As for inflation, it is expected to show a downward trend in 2025
and gradually rise below the community threshold of 3% in the medium term. With regard to public finances, efforts
should be continued to improve revenue mobilization by raising the tax burden to nearly 14.5 percent by 2027. The fiscal
deficit is expected to remain contained at less than 1 percent of GDP.


    2. Government program

The Government is committed to speeding up implementation of the National Development Strategy (SND30) with the
support of the technical and financial partners, including the World Bank. To this end, its program of short-, medium-, and
long-term economic and social reforms sets out the following priorities: (i) structural transformation of the economy; (ii)
Human capital development; (iii) job creation and economic inclusion; and (iv) governance, decentralization, and strategic
management of the State.
These priorities are reflected in the three pillars of the reform package supported by this budget support operation:
improving fiscal sustainability, governance, and public sector management (pillar 1); improving inclusion by ensuring
equitable access to services and empowering women and girls (pillar 2); and laying the foundation for sustainable
infrastructure development (pillar 3).
Pillar 1: Improving fiscal sustainability, governance, and public sector management.
The actions envisaged under this pillar are aimed in particular at: (i) speeding up the decentralization process under way
and improving the financing of decentralized entities and strengthening their capacities; (ii) Improve the governance and
monitoring of state-owned enterprises; (iii) increase domestic revenue mobilization; and (iv) improve the management of
the State's cash flow.
About improving the financing of decentralized entities and building their capacities, the Government has continued
the process of transferring responsibilities from the central government to the decentralized territorial authorities and
increasing their financial autonomy. To this end, the President of the Republic has signed two decrees. The first, which
was signed on 10 February 2023, sets out the terms and conditions for the exercise of certain competences transferred
by the State to the Regions in the field of health. The second, signed on 27 April 2023, sets out the terms and conditions
for the exercise of certain competences transferred by the State to the Regions in the field of secondary education. Also,
with a view to increasing the financial autonomy of the decentralized local authorities, the Government plans to adopt
the law on local taxation. The draft law has already been submitted to and adopted by Parliament, and the President of
the Republic enacted the law on December 23, 2024.
Regarding enhancing governance and monitoring of public enterprises, the Government has continued actions aimed
at improving the performance of public enterprises. To this end, the Minister of Finance signed on October 6, 2023 a
circular letter specifying the terms and conditions for the development, conclusion and implementation of performance
contracts between the State and Public Establishments and Enterprises. In addition, a performance contract has been
signed with the Autonomous Port of Douala with a view to operationalizing the circular. The signing of the performance
contract for Camair-CO is still awaited. In addition, with a view to reducing the fiscal risks associated with CAMTEL and
CAMWATER, diagnostic studies have been conducted.

Regarding increasing domestic revenue mobilization, the Government intends to continue actions aimed at
strengthening cargo controls and combating base erosion (BEPS). In this regard, the General Directorate of Customs
signed with the BEAC, on March 29, 2023, a memorandum of understanding aimed at creating a framework for
consultation, collaboration, exchange of information and data. In addition, work continued to improve transfer pricing
controls and combating base erosion (BEPS). To this end, the Government ratified the OECD's BEPS Convention on 21
April 2022. Therefore, on January 4, 2023, the Minister of Finance signed a decision setting out the content and format of
transfer pricing documentation.
About actions aimed at improving the management of the State's cash flow, the Prime Minister, Head of Government
signed on February 28, 2024, a decree setting out the modalities for managing the cash flow of the State and other Public
Entities, as well as an order on the reorganization and operation of the State Cash and Budgetary Regulation Committee.
In addition, with a view to continuing the reform on electronic payments, the Minister of Finance signed on February 28,
2024, an order setting out the terms and conditions for the approval and cessation of activity of electronic payment service
providers in Cameroon.
Pillar 2: Improving inclusion by ensuring equitable access to services and empowering women and girls
The objective of this pillar is to support the Government's actions to ensure equitable access to services and improve
women's economic participation. To this end, the measures implemented are aimed at: (i) guaranteeing equity in access
to education; (ii) reduce gender-related inequalities; and (iii) strengthening the social protection system.
With regard to the need to ensure equity in access to education, on November 27, 2023, and December 28, 2023,
respectively, the Ministers of Basic Education and Secondary Education signed the circulars relating to the supervision and
operation of parents' associations within schools. In addition, on December 29, 2023, the Prime Minister and Head of
Government signed a circular on administrative mobility and transfers of teaching staff within the Cameroonian public
administration. This circular requires the approval of the Ministers of Education before any transfer or appointment of
teachers to another administration.
Regarding the reduction of gender-related inequalities, particularly in terms of reproductive health, education and
access to employment, the Ministers in charge of basic education and secondary education signed circulars on November
27 and 10, 2023 respectively, relating to measures to protect young girls against GBV in the school environment. These
measures are part of a process that should lead to the adoption of the law against GBV, the preliminary draft of which, as
well as the explanatory memorandum, have been finalized. In addition, work on revising the Labor Code has been initiated
in collaboration with the social partners, employers and workers.
About strengthening the social protection system and adapting to shocks, the Government's interventions have aimed
to finalize the process of building a single social registry constituting the central element of the national social
protection system. In this regard, the Minister of Social Affairs signed on November 10, 2023, a decision establishing the
implementation manual of the USR of Cameroon. This manual sets out the procedures for registering socially vulnerable
persons and households, as well as the methodology, mechanisms and criteria for targeting.
To improve access to services for all, especially women and other vulnerable groups, through better identification, the
Government is planning to adopt two laws on the digitization of CR and on the protection of personal data, respectively.
Draft laws have been submitted to and adopted by Parliament, and the President of the Republic enacted the laws on
December 23, 2024.
Pillar 3: Laying Foundations for Sustainable Infrastructure Development
Through this pillar, the Government intends to sustainably improve the condition of the road network and transport
infrastructure, as well as the management of water resources.

Regarding the sustainable improvement of the condition of the road network and transport infrastructure, the
Government plans, following the adoption of the 2022 law on road assets, to set up a second-generation Road Fund that
will ensure that the country has appropriate and controlled programming of its interventions for the effective protection
of road assets.
About improving water resource management, the Government's objective is to regulate the management of
agricultural water in the hydro-agricultural schemes established by the State and entrusted to public entities. In this
regard, the Prime Minister, Head of Government signed on February 26, 2024, a decree setting out the modalities for the
management of water used for agricultural purposes and the maintenance of hydraulic infrastructures in irrigated
perimeters in Cameroon. This text aims to give public entities the means to better organize the maintenance and upkeep
of the facilities entrusted to them by the Government. A management delegation may also be granted to private sector
entities.

ANNEX 5: PARIS ALIGNMENT ASSESSMENT

Step 1: Assessing Consistency with Country’s NDC, LTS and NAP
Is the operation consistent with (does not hinder) the implementation of the country’s NDC, LTS, and NAP? Identify and review
the most recent Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC), National Adaptation Plan (NAP), and Long-term Strategy (LTS) (if
available).

The project is consistent with the Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) and National Adaptation Plan (NAP) of Cameroon.
The Government of Cameroon (GoC) committed to achieving an ambitious climate change agenda. The GoC revised its NDCs in
September 2021, calling for 35 percent conditional Green House Gas (GHG) emissions reduction by 2030 (including an
unconditional contribution of 12 percent), primarily from actions taken in the agriculture and infrastructure sectors. The
proposed project is consistent with Cameroon revised NDC which highlights (i) adaptation actions such as the adaptation of
agricultural production techniques to climate change risks, the promotion of climate resilient transport infrastructure, and the
reinforcement of the population’s resilience to climate shocks; as well as (ii) mitigation actions such as the promotion of low-
emissions modes of transportation and agricultural processes. Similarly, the Project is consistent with the National Adaptation
Plan of Cameroon (2016-2021), which expands focus on (i) the promotion of water management systems to handle extreme
climate conditions (heat waves, droughts, floods); (ii) the promotion of climate-smart agricultural techniques, notably through
the integration of climate change in the education, training and awareness programs targeting farmers, in particular in agro-
ecological regions; (iii) the empowerment of local authorities to address climate-related issues, enabling administrative regions
to elaborate and implement adaptation and mitigation strategies; (iv) the protection of the most vulnerable to the risks and
effects of climate change.

Mitigation goals: assessing and reducing the risks

Under Pillar 1, the proposed Prior Actions 1-4 focus on improving fiscal sustainability, governance, and public sector
management, and are not expected to introduce physical infrastructure or policy framework that would lock Cameroon in
carbon-intensive patterns.

Under Pillar 2, the proposed Prior Actions 5-7 aim at improving the efficiency of education sector and social protection, as well
as digitization of public services. The proposed reforms are unlikely to cause a significant increase in GHG emission or create
carbon lock-in.

Under Pillar 3, the proposed prior actions align with the Paris Agreement’s mitigation goals as they could actively contribute to
support GHG emissions reduction. Prior Action 8 could support decarbonization efforts, as it promotes the efficient use of water
resources through measures that could directly affect energy savings, which can lead to reduced GHG emissions.

Mitigation Conclusion
All prior actions are aligned with the Paris Agreement’s mitigation goals as they are unlikely to cause a significant increase in GHG
emissions, nor to reinforce significant and persistent barriers to transition to Cameroon’s low GHG emissions development
pathway.

Adaptation and resilience goals: assessing and managing the risks

Under Pillar 1, the proposed actions align with the Paris Agreement’s adaptation and resilience goals, as the reforms will
strengthen the country’s public sector management, generating larger fiscal space and enabling authorities (including at the local
level) to face potential climate shocks.




                                                                                                                        Page 62 of 71
        The World Bank
        Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth Development Policy Financing (P180017)


Under Pillar 2, the proposed actions align with the Paris Agreement’s adaptation and resilience goals as the reforms will aim at
increasing the climate resilience of vulnerable households. Prior Action 6 aims at improving the targeting and efficiency of the
social protection system, notably by ensuring that the most vulnerable households benefit from an assistance program in case
of a climate shock. This prior action will pave the way to a structural approach to address vulnerability to climate shocks, which
will be promoted through a dedicated institutional framework (Indicative Trigger 7).

Under Pillar 3, the proposed actions align with the Paris Agreement’s adaptation and resilience goals as the reforms will
strengthen the country’s resilience to climate effects, particularly in the transport and water sectors. Under Prior Action 8,
sustainable water management will help Cameroon adapt to climate change by addressing the increasing water stress faced by
agriculture and industry, as well as contributing to the protection of ecosystems from drought and flood hazards.
The remaining prior actions under the proposed operation are deemed to present no or low risks to the Paris Agreement’s
adaptation and resilience goals.

Adaptation Conclusion
All prior actions are aligned with the Paris Agreement’s adaptation and resilience goals as climate hazards are not likely to have
an adverse effect on the prior actions, and risks are reduced through the reforms that aim to strengthen climate resilience in
social protection and water management.

Conclusion
The assessment of the proposed “Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth” DPF (P180017)
concludes that the project is Paris Aligned. The project is consistent with the Cameroon’s NDCs and NAP and is expected to
contribute to the decarbonization and climate change mitigation agenda of Cameroon, notably through the promotion of climate-
smart agricultural processes, better quality road transportation networks, and sustainable water management systems.
Furthermore, the project is expected to support government’s efforts on adaptation and mitigation (i) by reinforcing local
authorities’ prerogatives on key matters to design, fund and implement climate change mitigation strategies (such as taxation);
(ii) by increasing households’ resilience to climate shocks in the form of social safety nets; (iii) by promoting climate resilient
processes and infrastructure, in the agricultural, water and transport sectors.
