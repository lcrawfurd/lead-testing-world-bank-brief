                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     Tocantins, Brazil’s youngest state, has an area of 277,620 km² and a population of 1.6 million. Despite its significant
                     agricultural potential, it faces structural challenges, including socioeconomic disparities, rural poverty, and vulnerability
                     to climate change. The state's Human Development Index (HDI) of 0.731 is below the national average, and 50 percent
                     of its municipalities are classified as highly vulnerable. Migration from rural areas to urban centers has been
                     accelerating due to limited economic opportunities and deteriorating living conditions in the countryside, placing
                     pressure on urban infrastructure. The state's road network, critical for agricultural logistics and regional integration,
                     comprises 5,841 km of paved roads connecting major corridors like BR-153 and the North-South Railway. However, 31

                     accidents and climate-induced damage further challenge the network’s sustainability. To address these issues, Tocantins

                     tourism infrastructure, diversify offerings, and integrate local communities into the value chain. The Tocantins
                     Productive, Resilient, and Inclusive Development Project (PDPRIS) aims to address these challenges through investments

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The State of Tocantins is in the central part of Brazil, in the transition zone from the Cerrado (Savanah) – occupying
                     about 90% of its territory – to the Amazon rain forest (notably in the northern portion). The Cerrado is the 2nd largest
                     biome in Brazil, holds 5% of the planet's biodiversity, is a relevant recharge area for major aquifers and encompasses
                     the springs of the main hydrographic basins.
                     Around 15% of the State territory is covered by conservation units, including ten environmental protection areas (APAs)
                     (IUCN Category V), five Parks (IUCN Category II), one Ecological Station (IUCN Category Ia), and two natural monuments
                     (IUCN Category III). Two regions deserve special attention. The Jalapão region has unique environmental and
                     sociocultural characteristics, including the Jalapão State Park, the Serra Geral do Tocantins ecological station, the
                     Canyons and Rapids of the Sono River national monuments and the Jalapão APA. The Bananal Island is the largest fluvial
                     island in the world (23,000 sq.km.), encompasses wetlands of international importance and includes the Bananal APA
                     and the Cantão State Park.
                     Hosting a population of 1.6 million inhabitants (21.2% living in rural areas), Tocantins ranks 13th among the 27 Brazilian
                     federative units in the Human Development Index (HDI). In 2023, the poverty rate reached 28.6% and the extreme
                     poverty rate equaled 4.4% in the state. The unemployment rate was 14.2%.
                     There are around 45,000 family farmers in the state (IBGE: 2017). They hold 70% of the farms in the state, but just 19%
                     of the area under agriculture. Women comprise only 18% of the holders of family farms. Family farm is responsible for
                     40% of the state’s agricultural production.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The World Bank has supported the State of Tocantins for over three decades. Lately, the State implemented the
                     Sustainable Integrated Regional Development Project (PDRIS - P121495), which included road maintenance, road safety,
                     climate resilience and agricultural development activities. Its implementation unit developed a series of E&S
                     instruments (E&S assessment forms, E&S technical specifications for road works, resettlement and indigenous peoples
                     plans, and a guide for E&S incident response) with the Bank support and many of the state agencies involved in the
                     Project acquired experience with the Bank’s E&S risk management policies and procedures. The PDRIS was closed with
                     minor E&S pending issues and the Borrower has shown strong commitment with the implementation of the post-closing
                     action plan.
                     The Project Management Unit (PMU), hosted by SEPLAN, will be composed by a team of professionals who handled E&S
                     procedures under PDRIS. It will coordinate the implementation of Component 1 with AGETO, Component 2 with
                     SEAGRO, RURALTINS and SEPEA, and Component 3 with SEMARH and SETUR. As part of PDRIS, AGETO, SEAGRO and
                     RURALTINS have particularly developed a good record dealing with vulnerable social groups (traditional
                     communities/Indigenous Peoples) and the environmental licensing procedures in activities that are like those to be
                     supported by the Project.

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                       Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                Substantial
                     The Substantial risk rating accounts primarily for the potential impacts related to the road rehabilitation inside or
                     adjacent Protected Areas in Tocantins. Whereas road rehabilitiation works are not likely to generate a wide range of
                     significant adverse risks and impacts to the environment, the location an sesnitiveness of some areas accounts
                     primarelly for the Substantial risk. Other Moderate Impacts from road rehabilitationworks may also include: the
                     generation and disposal of construction wastes; water pollution from construction runoff, wastewater from
                     construction sites and land or ground water contamination from waste streams from asphalt mills; soil and air
                     pollution from the asphalt mills; noise, dust emissions and land degradation from the quarries, construction orkplace
                     and traffic hazards for the workers and communities; and workplace construction OHS. Similarly lower and diffuse
                     impacts are also expected from smallholder’s (family) agriculture activities supported by the project (component 2)
                     and community-based tourism (component 3). These impacts could include environmental contamination due to
                     farm wastes disposal, use of pesticides, soil erosion and so forth, air emissions from small furnace and grain dryers,

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The social risk classification is Substantial. Benefits are expected from the project's implementation, especially by
                     increasing the earnings and improving the livelihoods of 12,000 beneficiary family farmers (including Indigenous
                     Peoples, Quilombola and Traditional Communities), and by improving the state's logistical capacity (a key bottleneck
                     for economic growth) and road safety (reducing the high rate of accidents). Component 1 may require land
                     acquisition and the clearing of right-of-ways in selected sections of the road network. At the Concept stage, these
                     sections have not been selected yet, but the possibility of building the Rio do Sono bridge on the TO-425 road – near
                     the Xerente indigenous land – was suggested.. Furthermore, the improvement or creation of access roads to
                     previously inaccessible locations in the Jalapão region may indirectly lead to increased predatory tourism with
                     adverse socioenvironmental impacts to local and traditional communities. Component 2 may face the risk of
                     excluding the most vulnerable groups of family farmers as well as female farmers who lack behind on access to
                     information, technical assistance and participation in producers’ organizations. Aiming to promote community-based
                     tourism in the Jalapão region, Component 3 may foster the use of (tangible and intangible) cultural heritage of
                     indigenous and traditional communities. As many works and activities will occur in areas that are hard to supervise
                     and highly vulnerable, there are concerns related to SEA/SH and gender-based violence in the daily interactions
                     between project workers and between them and members of vulnerable communities. Material measures to address
                     these risks following the mitigation hierarchy will be defined during preparation, incorporated in the Project’s social
                     risk management instruments and consolidated in the Project’s Environmental and Social Commitment Plan.

                     B. Relevance of Standards and Policies at Concept Stage

                     B.1 Relevance of Environmental and Social Standards

                      ESS1 - Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts                                Relevant

                      The project will foster the rehabilitation of the state's road network, boosting logistics and road safety, resilient
                      family farming and community-based tourism. The risks and impacts from this project are expected to be temporary,
                      reversible, moderate and limited to the subproject sites, and they are expected to be mitigated by the use of
                      Borrower’s framework and adoption of GIIP. The Borrower has developed its capacity to manage E&S risks through
                      the implementation of a previous operation and, during preparation, they must complete an ESMF covering all
                      components, including: a) an E&S impact assessment (focusing on biodiversity, critical and natural habitats;
                      vulnerable/traditional social groups; gender equity; community health and safety; working place conditions and
                      relationships between project workers and community members); b) the definition of subprojects selection/exclusion
                      criteria and E&S requirements for contractors; and c) the description of the E&S institutional capacity.

                      behavior against fraternization, SEA/SH and promoting respect for distinct cultural values and worldviews, and

                      facilities will be developed as part of the ESMF and Contractors ESMP (C-ESMPs).
