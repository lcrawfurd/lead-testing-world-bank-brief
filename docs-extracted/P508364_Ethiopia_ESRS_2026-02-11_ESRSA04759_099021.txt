                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The CRISP project aims to improve access to resilient irrigation services in selected areas of Ethiopia. The project will
                     strengthen the performance, reliability, and climate resilience of irrigation systems by rehabilitating and modernizing
                     key large- and medium-scale schemes, upgrading irrigation infrastructure down to on-farm systems, and expanding
                     small-scale irrigation in vulnerable and food-insecure areas. It will introduce climate-smart and energy-efficient
                     technologies to enhance water-use efficiency and sustainability, and pilot innovative approaches such as irrigation
                     service fee systems, private service provision, water allocation and monitoring mechanisms, and salinity management
                     practices. The project will further support agricultural production and value chain development by improving farmers’

                     access to inputs, mechanization, and markets, and by strengthening agri-business linkages to increase productivity and
                     incomes. It will promote inclusive and participatory management models, empower water user associations and female
                     farmers, and foster private sector engagement in irrigation services, financing, and agricultural commercialization. In
                     addition, the project will establish digital tools for national irrigation information and asset management, enhance dam
                     safety and environmental and social risk management, and build institutional and technical capacity at federal and
                     regional levels to sustain climate-resilient irrigation development.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The project is a nationwide operation in Ethiopia focused on irrigation landscapes. Activities span federally managed
                     large- and medium-scale irrigation schemes and regionally implemented small-scale irrigation (SSI) schemes, alongside
                     dam safety and small water storage works, and sector-wide digital and quality systems (NIIS and Quality Assurance) led
                     by the Ministry of Irrigation and Lowlands (MILLs). The project will operate across diverse agro-ecological zones and in a
                     context with varying levels of fragility, conflict, and violence (FCV), including regions such as Amhara, Oromia,
                     Benishangul-Gumuz, Afar, Somali, and Tigray, where access constraints may affect stakeholder engagement,
                     supervision, and security management for field operations.

                     Ethiopia has a considerable geographical diversity and the country’s topography is highly varied, featuring rugged
                     mountains, plateaus, deep gorges, river valleys, and vast lowlands. The climate is primarily tropical though it varies with
                     altitude. The country has adequate average annual rainfall, 12 major river basins, several major rivers, wetlands, and
                     lakes, and significant groundwater resources. The country has a number of major ecosystems and agro-ecological zones,



Public Disclosure
                     supporting a rich diversity of flora and fauna. Ethiopia has several protected areas, including national and regional parks,
                     wildlife sanctuaries/reserves, controlled hunting areas, and national priority forests. The main environmental challenges
                     of the country include land degradation, deforestation, water pollution, and climate vulnerability.

                     The project operates nationwide across rural irrigation landscapes that are socially diverse and predominantly
                     smallholder-based. Local livelihoods are organized around farming and irrigated agriculture, with Irrigation Water Users
                     Associations (IWUAs), kebele administrations, and customary institutions playing key roles in everyday resource
                     governance. The program intersects with regions that have varying fragility, conflict, and access dynamics—such as
                     Amhara, Oromia, Benishangul‑Gumuz, Afar, Somali, Tigray, Gambella, and areas of the former SNNP. Social settings are
                     multilingual and multiethnic, and community organization often combines formal structures with customary leadership
                     around land, water, and rangeland use, which influences participation in irrigation scheme management and benefit-
                     sharing.

                     Pastoral and agro‑pastoral populations—recognized as Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities under
                     ESS7—are present in several lowland and emerging regions where mobility, and clan/lineage structures organize social
                     roles and access to common resources. These communities contribute substantially to national livestock economies and
                     inhabit arid and semi‑arid territories where water points and seasonal grazing patterns shape settlement and
                     production, alongside emerging mixed farming around irrigation opportunities.
