I. PROGRAM STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING

A. Program status

1. The Burkina Faso Water Supply and Sanitation Program - P164345 (Programme d’Approvisionnement en Eau et
    d’Assainissement- PAEA) was approved in June 2018 and became effective in December 2018. The IDA financing of
    US$300 million, combining a Credit of US$250 million and a Grant of US$50 million, funds a larger Program of a
    total cost of approximatively US$365 million. The PAEA is implemented by the Ministry in charge of water and by the
    national urban water utility (Office National de l’Eau et de l’Assainissement – ONEA). Each of those two implementing
    entities have established fully staffed implementation units. The Program has disbursed so far US$68.3 million
    corresponding to a first advance. Progress towards achievement of Program Development Objective (PDO) and
    Implementation Progress are rated respectively Satisfactory and Moderately Satisfactory.


2. The implementation of the various Result Areas of the PAEA has experienced mixed results. As of March 2020, the
   rural water supply’s Result Area has recorded good progress: (i) nearly 500 boreholes (45% of the Program end
   target) have been drilled and are ready to be equipped with pumps, (ii) a new rural water tariff structure has been
   adopted by the Government of Burkina Faso (GoBF), (iii) the construction of nearly 40 rural water supply schemes
   is starting. Technical and Training, Doctoral and Short-term programs have also been successfully initiated.
   Likewise, procurement for large water resources studies and activities has significantly advanced. In addition, the
   procurement for the rural sanitation’s Result Area is on-going. On the contrary, ONEA-led procurement for the
   urban sanitation and water supply of secondary cities’ Result Areas have been delayed since the Program’s
   effectiveness. An action plan with clear deadlines to accelerate the delayed activities of the Program has been
   agreed with ONEA and with the Ministry, and is currently being executed by ONEA. It is expected that all the
   activities of the Program will be under implementation by the end of 2020 unless the on-going COVID-19 crisis,
   whose impacts are being carefully monitored, becomes a significant and more durable shock.

3. The PAEA will support Burkina Faso’s short-term and medium-term’s response to the COVID-19 pandemic, as safely
   managed water, sanitation, and hygiene services are a central part of preventing transmission and protecting
   human health during infectious disease outbreaks such as the current COVID-19 pandemic. One of the most cost-
   effective strategies for increasing pandemic preparedness, especially in resource-constrained settings such as
   Burkina Faso, is investing in core public health infrastructure, including water and sanitation systems. Good WASH
   and waste management practices, that are consistently applied, serve as barriers to human-to-human transmission
   of the COVID-19 virus in homes, communities, health care facilities, schools, and other public spaces. Safely
   managed WASH services are also critical during the recovery phase of a disease outbreak to mitigate secondary
   impacts on community livelihoods and wellbeing. These secondary impacts—which could include disruptions to
   supply chains, inability to pay bills, or panic-buying—have negative impacts on the continuity and quality of water
   and sanitation services, the ability of affected households to access and pay for WASH services and products (for
   instance, soap, point of use water treatment or menstrual hygiene products) and the ability of schools, workplaces
   and other public spaces to maintain effective hygiene protocols when they re-open. If not managed, secondary

    impacts can increase the risk of further spreading water borne diseases, including potential disease outbreaks such
    as cholera, particularly where the disease is endemic.

4. The PAEA, which expands access to water and sanitation services and promotes hygiene practices in both urban
   and rural areas, will therefore be instrumental to prevent and respond to the threat posed by COVID-19 in Burkina
   Faso. In addition, it is anticipated that the resources disbursed from the PforR could provide ONEA with emergency
   liquidity support in order to buffer the impacts of the various social measures taken by the GoBF (such as free water
   at standpipes and first 8 m3 of the monthly bill free of charge) on the financial situation of the utility that may lead
   to interruption of service in urban and peri-urban areas if cash shortage is not immediately addressed. This will be
   of particular relevance as Burkina Faso is experiencing a three-fold--security, humanitarian and, more recently,
   public health crisis linked to the COVID-19 global spread. This triple combination of challenges is increasing
   expenditures to handle the COVID-19 emergency, and to manage security and humanitarian costs, thus generating
   fiscal pressures. Given the security requirements stemming from the pressure of the armed extremist groups, security
   spending is likely to continue rising. Subsidies to utilities and transfers to local governments will increase in tandem
   with outlays related to COVID-19. Under those circumstances, the emergency facility that could provide the PAEA
   to cope with ONEA’s cash shortage will be an important element in the current fiscal and public spending context.

5. The International Institute of Water and Environmental Engineering (2iE) has been recruited as the Program’s
   Independent Verification Agent (IVA). The first campaign of verification of the Disbursement Linked Indicators (DLIs)
   achievement is expected to start by April 2020. In addition, by the end of FY20, it is expected that results equivalent
   to DLIs amounting at least US$10 million should be achieved: these relate to DLI 3 (Adjustment of urban water tariff
   and adoption of rural water supply tariff structure, already completed), DLI 1.2 (People provided with access to an
   improved water source in rural areas), DLI 2 (Water Production capacity), DLI 7.1 (Beneficiaries registered in
   Technical Training Programs) and DLI 7.4 (Beneficiaries of short term trainings).

6. The implementation of the PAEA has also been impacted since 2019 by a deteriorating security environment
   affecting the Sahel region and the Burkina Faso in particular. At the end of March 2020, more than 30,000 Internally
   Displaced People (IDPs) were identified in the Program’s rural areas, whilst 80,000 IDPs were estimated in the
   Program’s urban areas, more specifically in the city of Kaya. As of March 2020, no security incident affecting the
   implementation of the Program has been reported. However, the Ministry in charge of the implementation of the
   rural water supply’s Result Area has announced that the planning of some activities could be adjusted to respond
   to the evolution of the security situation in certain areas, though it is expected this will not change the boundaries
   of the Program for the time being.

7. At this stage of implementation, the Program has not faced substantial fiduciary, environmental and social issues.
   The Task Team also confirms that at the time of this proposed restructuring (i) there is no audit overdue and (ii)
   neither the loan, nor the country, is subject to an ongoing suspension of disbursements.

B. Rationale for Restructuring

8. As the PAEA’s implementing units and the IVA are preparing the first campaign of verification of the DLIs, various
   changes are proposed to the DLIs matrix of the PAEA’s financing agreements due to the following reasons:
       The majority of the Program’s Disbursement Linked Results (DLRs) are defined with respect with the year of
        implementation of the Program. Linking results with the implementation timeframe of the Program is however
        not considered as a good practice given changes of implementation schedule that can occur during the execution
        of the Program and that can lead to misalignment and difficulty to disburse; DLIs concerned: 1, 2, 4, 5, 6.1, 7.1,
        7.2, 7.3, 7.4, 7.5.
       Some activities of a different nature are bundled under the same DLR. This prevents scalable disbursements
        and/or makes results achievement associated with diverse activities implemented at different pace dependent
        on each other when they should not be; DLIs concerned: 6.1, 6.2.
       The DLIs matrix of the Program Appraisal Document indicates that disbursements can be requested for both the
        registry and the completion of training programs (except short-term training), however this was not reflected
        into the DLIs matrix of the Program’s financing agreements; DLIs concerned: 7.1, 7.2, 7.3.
       Given the above changes, there is also the need to make minor adjustments to the amounts associated with the
        DLRs, so the numbers line up and match the Credit and Grant’s total amounts; DLIs concerned: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6.1,
        6.2, 7.1, 7.2, 7.3.

9. By delinking the DLRs to the implementation timeframe of the Program, making additional DLRs scalable, and
   allowing disbursements at the registry of training programs, the restructuring will also accelerate disbursement of
   the PforR and thus allow providing more quickly emergency liquidity to ONEA to respond to the financial impacts of the
   COVID-19 crisis that may rapidly deteriorate its ability to deliver water supply services in urban and peri-urban areas.


10. Section II of this Restructuring Paper describes the proposed changes in more details. The DLIs matrix of the PAEA’s
    financing agreements, as well as the Terms of the Reference of the IVA and the Program’s Operational Manual will
    be amended to reflect the agreed changes and make them fully consistent.

11. The World Bank received on March 10, 2020 a letter from the Ministry of Economy, Finance and Development from
    the GoBF dated March 4, 2020 requesting an amendment of the Program’s financing agreements for the changes
    mentioned above and described in more details in the Section II below.

12. In addition, the mixed results experienced by the implementation of the various Result Areas of the PAEA make
    necessary to update the intermediate targets of the Results Framework as well as the Program’s Disbursement
    Estimates. This update aims at reflecting the actual implementation of the Program’s activities since the approval
    of the operation, as well as the revised projected implementation of the Result Areas of the Program through the
    closing of the operation.

D. Change in Results Framework

17. The intermediate targets of the indicators of the Program’s Results Framework are updated in order to reflect the
    actual implementation of the Program’s activities since the approval of the operation, that has differed from the
    trend anticipated at appraisal and negotiations. Similarly, the intermediate targets of the indicators are also
    updated in order to reflect the revised projected implementation of the Result Areas of the Program onwards.

18. None of the formulation of the indicators is changed. Neither the baseline nor the final targets of the Program’s
    indicators are amended as part of this restructuring, as the latter are still considered as achievable. Details of the
    updated intermediate targets are presented in Annex 1 below.


E. Change in Disbursements Estimates

19. Likewise the change in Results Framework, the mixed results experienced by the implementation of the various
    Result Areas of the PAEA make necessary to update the Disbursement Estimates of the Program. This update aims
    at reflecting the actual disbursement of the Program during FY19 as well as the projected disbursements during the
    subsequent Fiscal Years (FY) of the implementation of the Program from FY20 through its closing.
