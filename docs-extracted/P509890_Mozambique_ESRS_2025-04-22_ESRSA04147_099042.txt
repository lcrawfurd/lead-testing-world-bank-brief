                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The Mozambique Urban Water Security Project aims to increase access to improved water supply services and
                     strengthen service delivery capacity in selected cities by focusing on sector reforms to increase private sector
                     participation; investing in infrastructure to expand water treatment, transport, storage, and distribution capacity in the
                     Maputo metropolitan region and other southern cities; and supporting improved performance of institutions involved in
                     water supply. The project design includes Performance-based conditions to support the performance improvement of
                     regional water utilities, with the aim of modernizing urban water services, improving efficiency, increasing revenues,
                     building resilient infrastructure, and developing skilled water professionals. Ultimately, the project aims to improve
                     public health, economic development, and poverty reduction with a focus on Greater Maputo and the Southern Region,

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The Mozambique Urban Water Security Project (P509890) is an Investment Project Financing (IPF) operation designed
                     to increase access to improved water supply and enhance service delivery capacity in selected urban centers. The
                     project supports a transition from a previous PforR operation and is now aligned with the World Bank’s Environmental
                     and Social Framework (ESF). The project will be implemented in the Greater Maputo Metropolitan Area (GMMA), and
                     the cities of Xai-Xai and Chibuto in Gaza Province, and Inhambane and Maxixe in Inhambane Province.

                     Project activities span urban and peri-urban areas, where population density, aging infrastructure, and climate
                     vulnerabilities converge. The environmental setting includes surface water bodies such as the Umbeluzi, Incomáti, and
                     Limpopo Rivers, and sensitive ecosystems like coastal lagoons and wetlands near estuaries. Groundwater is also a vital
                     source, with known issues related to over-abstraction and saline intrusion, especially in coastal areas. These areas face
                     recurring droughts, floods, and cyclones that have significant implications for infrastructure resilience and water
                     security.

                     The project comprises four components:

                     Component 1: Access to sustainable and climate-resilient water and sanitation services – This includes integrated water



Public Disclosure
                     resources and watershed management; expansion of water treatment and distribution capacity; output-based grants
                     for connecting low-income households; upgrading of PWP (Private Water Providers) infrastructure; and completion of a
                     fecal sludge treatment plant in Tete.

                     Component 2: Improvement of performance and efficiency of services – Focuses on performance improvement of
                     AdRMM and AdRS utilities, NRW reduction, energy efficiency, governance, digitalization, and implementation of
                     turnaround plans.

                     Component 3: Water sector development support for enabling private sector participation (PSP) – Supports
                     development of the legal framework for integrating PWP services, strengthening AURA’s regulatory role, enhancing
                     ARA-Sul’s capacity for water resource management, and preparing future WSS investments.

                     Component 4: Project management support – Provides technical assistance and operational support to FIPAG, DNAAS,
                     ARA-Sul, and AURA-Sul to ensure fiduciary, environmental, and social compliance.

                     The planned civil works encompass the construction and rehabilitation of water treatment plants, the expansion of
                     piped networks, the installation of bridge crossings over rivers, and upgrades to fecal sludge treatment facilities. Water
                     sources include the Umbeluzi, Incomáti, and Limpopo rivers, as well as aquifers, with known risks of over-abstraction,
                     saline intrusion, and contamination. The region encompasses sensitive ecosystems such as wetlands, estuaries (e.g.,
                     Maputo River estuary), and coastal lagoons. Construction-related risks include erosion, sedimentation, hazardous
                     material use, solid waste generation, and pollution from wastewater or sludge. During the operational phase, potential

                     rehabilitation of water treatment plants, transmission pipelines, and distribution networks in urban and peri-urban
                     areas across Greater Maputo, Gaza, and Inhambane. These activities are expected to result in a range of construction-
                     phase impacts, including soil erosion, sedimentation, air and noise emissions, traffic disruptions, improper disposal of
                     construction and hazardous waste, and occupational health and safety (OHS) risks due to trenching, concrete works,
                     and heavy machinery use. Operational-phase risks have also been identified, including risks to water quality from
                     improper sludge management, untreated effluents, and chemical leaks related to water treatment processes. The

                     which may impact small informal water providers and raise affordability concerns for vulnerable populations. To
                     mitigate these risks, the project includes output-based payments for low-income household connections—building on
                     the successful experience of WASIS II (P149377)—to ensure that connection costs do not become a barrier to access.

                     reinforcing affordability and inclusion. If social tariffs are not effectively implemented, some low-income households

                     engagement. Additionally, stakeholder opposition may arise if engagement strategies fail to address concerns
                     identified in the stakeholder mapping exercise. Community Health, Safety, and SEA/SH Risks – Construction activities

                     salaried construction workers could lead to increased SEA/SH risks, requiring robust mitigation measures. Cumulative

                     C. Overview of Required Environmental and Social Risk Management Activities

                     C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or
                     required by implementation?
                     To ensure compliance with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), the Mozambique Urban Water
                     Security Project (P509890) requires a comprehensive set of environmental and social (E&S) instruments to guide the
                     identification, mitigation, and monitoring of risks across all project phases. These instruments are anchored in the
                     Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP) and will be prepared, disclosed, and implemented in accordance
                     with defined timelines. The World Bank’s due diligence will focus on the adequacy, timely preparation, and quality of
                     these instruments and their implementation by the Borrower and implementing entities.

                     Instruments to be prepared and disclosed prior to Appraisal:

                     1) Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF): Serves as the umbrella framework to guide E&S
                     screening, classification, risk assessment, and mitigation measures for subprojects. The ESMF provides procedures for
                     preparing site-specific Environmental and Social Impact Assessments (ESIAs) and Environmental and Social
                     Management Plans (ESMPs), incorporating guidance on climate resilience, sustainable water use, waste management,
                     pollution prevention, and biodiversity protection. Key annexes include:




Public Disclosure
                     - A SEA/SH Prevention and Response Action Plan outlining prevention measures, mandatory training, Codes of Conduct,
                     and survivor-centered response and referral protocols.

                     -A Cultural Heritage Management Plan, including legally compliant chance find procedures aligned with ESS8 and
                     Mozambican heritage laws.

                     - A Capacity-Building Plan for strengthening the E&S capacity of FIPAG, DNAAS, AURA, and ARA-Sul.

                     2) Labor Management Procedures (LMP): Defines labor categories, terms and conditions of employment, occupational
                     health and safety (OHS) provisions, grievance redress mechanisms, and child and forced labor prohibitions. It applies to
                     all project workers, including direct, contracted, and primary supply workers.

                     4) Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF): Establishes eligibility, compensation, and livelihood restoration procedures for
                     households and businesses potentially affected by physical or economic displacement, in line with ESS5 and
                     Mozambican legal frameworks. The RPF will guide the preparation of Resettlement Action Plans (RAPs) during
                     implementation.

                     3) Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP): Outlines a strategy for inclusive stakeholder consultations throughout the
                     project cycle, including provisions for engaging women-headed households, vulnerable groups, informal service
                     providers, and private operators. The SEP includes a project-wide, SEA/SH-sensitive Grievance Redress Mechanism
                     (GRM) to be operational across FIPAG, DNAAS, AURA, and ARA-Sul.
