STRATEGIC CONTEXT


1.        Tunisia ranks among the most water-stressed countries in the world. Water underpins its socio-economic
development, directly impacting livelihoods, jobs, food security and environmental sustainability. Tunisia is experiencing
increasing water demand; deteriorating water quality; dwindling water supply due to climate change; and increased
variability and unpredictability of extreme weather events. Water restrictions are becoming more frequent, intense and
erratic, risk to impact daily lives of Tunisians, their health and economic activities. By 2050, it is predicted that water supply
could only meet around 75 percent of the projected demand. The Country Climate and Development Report (CCDR,
2023) estimates that, left unaddressed, this water deficit would cause the economy to shrink by 3.4 percent of Gross
Domestic Product (GDP) in 2030 and by 6.4 percent in 2050, with direct impacts on poverty levels. Improving the water
security and resilience of the country requires acting at scale to ensure integrated water resources management (IWRM)
with concerted engagement across sub-sectors (potable water, irrigated agriculture, sanitation). A combination of
investments and structural measures is needed to improve the quality of water services in a context of growing water
scarcity, rising marginal costs of resource mobilization, and deteriorating water quality; strengthen resilience 1 to climate
and other shocks through supply diversification towards non-conventional sources; ensure satisfactory technical and
commercial performance of service providers; and maximize the economic and social value generated per unit of water
used.

2.      This document presents the Tunisia Water Security and Resilience (RESEau, after its French acronym) Multi-
phase Programmatic Approach (MPA), in the main section; and its Phase 1 comprising two projects presented
concomitantly for approval: the Irrigation Water Security, Resilience and Value-Addition Project, presented in Annex 1,
and the Potable Water Security and Resilience Project, presented in Annex 2.

A. Country Context

3.        Tunisia’s economic recovery has been moderate since the sharp, pandemic-related contraction. After stalling in
2023 (0.2 percent growth) due to limited demand, drought, and barriers to entry, the economy slightly recovered in 2024
(1.6 percent growth). Economic growth increased to 2.5 percent in 2025, driven by a rebound in the agricultural sector
and increased construction and tourism activities. This has translated into lower levels of unemployment (15 percent by
end 2025) and increased labor force participation compared to a year earlier.2 External balances remained broadly stable
in 2025; however, the trade deficit remains significant and is partially offset by resilient services inflows and remittances.
The budget deficit narrowed from 9 percent of GDP in 2020 to 6.3 percent in 2024 due to stronger tax collection, wage
bill restraint, and lower subsidies, but public debt remains elevated (84.5 percent in 2024).

4.        Government priorities for the next five years are being formalized through the 2026-2030 National
Development Plan under preparation. Human capital development remains a central focus, with particular emphasis on
improving employability. The plan also targets a range of structural challenges, including water scarcity, energy transition,
digital transformation, and business environment reforms, alongside broader efforts to foster private sector growth.

5.     The Tunisian economy is highly vulnerable to water scarcity, which is increasingly exacerbated by climate
change. Most climate change projections predict higher temperatures (1.9-2.9°C increase this century) and lower

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

6.       Tunisia successfully developed its water supply to the benefit of its population and economy, but the traditional
supply-oriented approach is increasingly constrained with respect to conventional water resources. The 1970s and
1980s marked the peak in the construction of large hydraulic infrastructure: dams were built in the water-endowed
northwest, as well as significant inter-basin transfers linking these dams to the drier areas in the northeast (Tunis and Cap
Bon) and the center (Sfax). The country achieved near universal access to drinking water, and the provision of some form
of sanitation for 97 percent of the population. Irrigated agriculture – covering 10 percent of the agricultural land –
generates 36 percent of the agriculture value added. Surface water is mobilized to almost full capacity (92 percent), 5
leaving little opportunity for further supply-side augmentation through construction of new dams. Groundwater is
overexploited (133 percent),6 which can contribute to saltwater intrusion along the coastline.7 To increase its water
availability, Tunisia has been investing in non-conventional sources, although their contribution to the water balance
remains lower than that of other North African countries: at present desalination and reuse of treated wastewater (TWW)
contribute 2 percent and 1 percent, respectively, to the total water supply of Tunisia.

7.       Tunisia’s growing water gap is a major challenge for the country's socio-economic development: without action
to address water insecurity, a quarter of the country’s water demand could go unmet by 2050.8 Tunisia is extremely
water-scarce: per capita water availability in 2021 was 395 m 3, below the conventional definition of the absolute water
scarcity threshold of 500 m3. With rapidly depleting resource endowment and sustained growth of demand across all
sectors, service interruptions are common, particularly in the more water-stressed Central and Southern regions. Tunisia
urgently needs implementation of both demand-side and supply-side management actions to close the gap, and to
manage the mismatch between supply and demand in a coherent and structural manner, rather than on a reactive
emergency basis. Currently, when water supply is insufficient, water from the dams is prioritized for human and industrial
demands, while restrictions are imposed on public irrigation schemes with far-reaching consequences for agricultural
production. Inter-basin transfers are key to managing the imbalance, but they are also expensive to construct and operate.
The exceptionally limited water supply of 2024 caused the energy costs of the Northern Water Canal and Pipeline
Exploitation Company (Société d’Exploitation des Canaux et Conduites d’Eau du Nord, SECADENORD) to double. Despite
the prioritization of potable water supply needs and severe restrictions applied to public irrigation schemes, the national
water utility (Société Nationale d’Exploitation et de Distribution des Eaux, SONEDE) does not receive sufficient freshwater
to meet domestic needs. In response, SONEDE has started developing seawater desalination capacity (exploring
opportunities to do so through public-private partnerships, PPP), while intensifying efforts to reduce physical water losses
and curb per capita consumption, which currently stands at 128 liters per day.

8.      Climate change further threatens Tunisia’s water endowment. Tunisia is among the countries most vulnerable
to climate change in the Middle East, North Africa, Afghanistan and Pakistan (MENAAP) region. Since 2015, the country
has been grappling with a multiyear drought, which has resulted in dam filling levels significantly below historical averages 9
and in reduced recharge of aquifers. Temperature increases lead to increases in water demand and evapotranspiration,
which intensifies water needs for agriculture. With decreasing availability of surface water sources, compounded by rising
temperatures, water users shift increasingly towards groundwater, adding to the pressure on already overexploited
groundwater resources. In addition, reduced water availability has adversely affected water quality, complicating efforts
to maintain safe and reliable supply standards for both urban consumption and agricultural use. Across multiple climate
change scenarios, it is projected that by 2050, increases in temperature and in the frequency of dry years, coupled with
changes in the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events (including both floods and droughts), will further
decrease water availability. Moreover, climate change is leading to sea level rise in coastal Tunisia, which exacerbates
saltwater intrusion into aquifers, and higher temperatures could exacerbate wildfire risk.

9.       Agriculture is often cited as using 75 percent of Tunisia’s water resources; however, this has not been the case
over the past decade.10 In years with low water levels in the dams, the allocation to irrigation is reduced. Restrictions
prevent production of high-value crops, while safeguarding fruit trees and ensuring production of strategic crops (e.g.
cereals, forages, sugar beets). The timing of the water allocation to irrigation is not always aligned with the agricultural
season, which limits the opportunity to optimize water use for agriculture. Indeed, over the past five years, farmers in
public irrigation schemes were able to use only two-thirds of their allocations.11 Farmers’ limited visibility into quantity
and timing of water availability discourages agricultural intensification and investments in higher-value production, with
cascading effects on incomes, employment, and food security - and ultimately contributing to the overexploitation of
groundwater resources.

10.      Demand for water has been substantially increasing. Tunisia’s population has tripled since 1950 and has become
increasingly urbanized; with only one in three Tunisians now living in rural areas. Irrigated areas have similarly tripled since
the early 1970s, driven by the construction of large dams in the northwest (where most of the public irrigation schemes
are located) and by growing exploitation of groundwater resources. Consequently, annual freshwater withdrawals have
increased from 1.1 billion m3 (1975) to 3.9 billion m3 (2021). This surge in water consumption has also generated greater
volumes of wastewater, intensifying the demand for the management of water quality, adequate sanitation and treatment
infrastructure: TWW volume grew from just 5 million m3 in 1975 to 300 million m3 in 2024.12 Taken together, these
dynamics underscore the necessity of IWRM to safeguard livelihoods, public health, and environmental sustainability.

11.      Pollution and declining water quality pose a growing risk to reliable water service delivery. Tunisia has achieved

unconnected to wastewater networks. About 27 percent of effluent treated by the sanitation utility (Office National de
l’Assainissement, ONAS) fails to meet discharge standards,14 reflecting aging infrastructure, insufficient pretreatment of
industrial wastewater, and uneven application of regulations. These shortcomings increase operating costs and complicate

12.      Tunisia’s water management challenges are not only about scarcity but also about efficiency and governance.
About 23 percent of drinking water and 30 percent of irrigation water in public schemes do not reach consumers due to
leakages, undermining both the efficient use of scarce resources and the revenues of the operators. In southern Tunisia,
lower-quality services are more pronounced, with the quality of supplied water exceeding in certain cases the
recommended maximum salinity levels due to limitations in treatment capacity of brackish water or to desalinate
seawater. While substantial investments have been made in efficient on-farm irrigation technologies, rationalizing
agricultural water consumption has been constrained by the limited rollout of complementary governance measures
(quotas, volumetric pricing, and incentives). Across all sub-sectors, the cost of water in Tunisia does not fully reflect the
scarcity of this resource.

13.      The sector’s performance is challenged by aging infrastructure and insufficient human resources. For SONEDE,
non-revenue water (NRW) is now twice the level of two decades ago. Staffing efficiency in SONEDE is high by international
standards, with only about two staff per 1,000 connections, which sometimes generates difficulties in the management
of the operation. SONEDE’s efficiency is constrained by the centralization of its operational functions and complex internal
processes to execute routine operational activities. Human resources are often strained, such as for the operation of
desalination plants, due to delays in the recruitment process. ONAS's performance is increasingly constrained by aging
infrastructure, limited means to carry out the required Operation and Maintenance (O&M), and with many wastewater
treatment plants (WWTPs) operating beyond capacity and requiring rehabilitation. ONAS’ limited financial resources,
institutional capacity and human capacity undermine its ability to properly staff, monitor, and upgrade facilities. ONAS’
dire financial position is at the heart of the hiring freeze that has been in place since 2008, with consequent adverse impact
on expertise in the sub-sector. Together, these constraints result into service gaps and heightened environmental risks.
Public irrigation schemes face ongoing challenges in moving toward higher value addition, which in turn impacts cost
recovery, maintenance levels, and service reliability, resulting in operational disruptions. The Regional Offices for
Agricultural Development (Commissariats Régionaux de Développement Agricole, CRDAs) are equally understaffed and
underfunded.

14.      Water management practices in Tunisia struggle to optimize economic returns, particularly within the
agricultural sector. About 10 percent of cultivated land is irrigated, contributing up to 36 percent of the agricultural value
added.15 Yet, Tunisia records lower agricultural water productivity than the regional average. Despite the proven benefits
of climate-smart agriculture (CSA) practices, uptake among smallholder farmers remains limited, due to limited access to
finance and extension services, limited availability and high cost of certified seeds, and low awareness of innovative
techniques. Despite successive plans and strategies prioritizing value chain development, value added remains
suboptimal: citrus fruits are still mostly exported in bulk, and 75 percent of tomato production is used as low-value input
in the industry of concentrated tomato paste instead of exported as fresh tomatoes. This is largely related to fragmented
production systems, limited modalities to promote aggregation and limited farmer organizations unable to effectively
manage storage, processing and commercialization of their members’ production.

effectively address water scarcity. The Ministry of Agriculture, Hydraulic Resources, and Fisheries (Ministère de
l’Agriculture, des Ressources Hydrauliques et de la Pêche, MARHP) oversees urban potable water through the SONEDE;
rural potable water and irrigated agriculture through its General Directorate for Rural Engineering and Water Management

16.      Modernizing the water sector’s legislative framework is key to more effectively addressing the growing water
gap. Efforts to modernize the institutional and legal framework in the water sector have not yet delivered significant
results. Tunisia’s Water Code dates to 1975, when the country was not suffering from water deficits. The revision of the
Water Code has been under discussion since 2009. Utilities are being pressured to assume new roles (e.g., SONEDE as
producer of desalinated water, ONAS as producer of TWW) that are not aligned with their original mandates,
competencies and/or financial structures, for which they have insufficient human and financial resources. Attempts to
improve management of public irrigation schemes illustrate the challenges of advancing complex institutional changes
that involve multiple stakeholders and policy makers. For TWW reuse, which has been identified as a priority, efforts to
strengthen the regulatory and institutional framework are also challenged by the complexity of coordinating policies
across multiple departments.

17.      Water utilities and public operators in Tunisia face increasingly strained budgets; despite this, their overall
financial positions remain relatively resilient. The government’s approach to State-Owned Enterprise (SOE) governance
challenges consists of sector-specific strengthening measures, with tailored interventions adapted to each public utility.
For the water sector operators, proposed measures include improving operational efficiency (e.g., reduction in NRW) and
financial sustainability, addressing institutional bottlenecks, and increasing private sector participation. Except for
SONEDE, subsidies are needed to compensate for the shortfall in revenues collected from customers, which are
insufficient to cover O&M costs, let alone the capital investments for the renewal and expansion of infrastructure. Yet
these subsidies are insufficient to help address pressure on service quality, aging infrastructure, and limited technical and
commercial performance. At the same time, utilities have limited power to increase their revenues. The challenge of
financial sustainability in the sector is further impacted by inflation. More specifically:
 For potable water, SONEDE recovers 107 percent of O&M costs and 102 percent of its total costs including financial

 For wastewater treatment, ONAS recovers 70 percent of O&M costs. No tariff adjustments have been implemented

18.     Private players are making their way into the water sector in Tunisia, notably in sanitation. The ONAS is
increasingly leveraging private sector participation in O&M to overcome its operational challenges, outsourcing its O&M
functions for 32 percent of WWTPs, 56 percent of pumping stations and 47 percent of networks. This is a significant
progression compared to 2022 values of 14, 18 and 17 percent, respectively. The ONAS is exploring concession contracts
that involve more advanced forms of private sector engagement, including performance-based compensation, extended
contract durations, and targeted infrastructure improvements with a modest private contribution to capital investment.
There is increasing interest from SONEDE, MARHP and CRDAs to explore other management models. SONEDE is exploring
private sector involvement through a PPP study under preparation by the World Bank and in collaboration with the
national PPP Authority (Instance Générale de Partenariat Public Privé, IGPPP). Through the Sanitation PPP Support Project
(ONAS PPP, P162957), which combines International Finance Corporation (IFC) transaction advisory services with Bank
financing, ONAS is also strengthening its PPP Unit, responsible for monitoring all PPP contracts and facilitating
coordination with both the concessionaires and the IGPPP. The ONAS now aspires to go even further by leveraging higher
shares of private sector financing through PPPs, as indicated by the ongoing tender for El Hessiane WWTP Design, Build,
Finance, Maintain, Operate (DBFMO) contract (final bid expected in June 2026) and the preparation of a new wastewater
PPP in Gabès, through technical assistance from the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD).
However, given the weak financial situation of ONAS, strong State commitments are required to make such projects
bankable, which has translated into implementation delays.

19.     The Plan Eau 2050 (Water 2050 Plan) aims to close the water demand-supply gap by 2050. The Plan, presented
to the Higher Water Council in 2023, serves as a cornerstone for the country’s economic growth, human development,
and environmental sustainability. The Plan Eau 2050 advocates for infrastructure development, strengthening institutions
and governance, improving the legal framework, and enhancing interministerial coordination. Prioritization still needs to
be sharpened, and several proposals have yet to gain broad institutional ownership. The Plan aims to amplify non-
conventional water supply, increasing contribution of TWW reuse to the water balance by 20 times, and of desalination
by three times by 2050. In the face of its ambitious targets, the Plan estimates a total financing need of US$23 billion, or
US$900 million per year. This is well beyond the financing capacity of the state budget, as it corresponds to 60 percent of
total public annual investments.

20.     The RESEau Program aims to support the Government of Tunisia in the coordinated implementation of
investments, performance improvements and structural measures needed to shift the country towards greater water
security and resilience. The Program adopts a holistic approach, focusing on key productive uses such as potable water,
sanitation and irrigation services coupled with broader water resources management interventions. Program
interventions aim at making services more resilient in the face of climate change; tackling both demand management and
supply increase and diversification; and spanning across conventional (surface and groundwater) and non-conventional
(desalination and TWW reuse) water sources. The RESEau Program complements ongoing efforts supported by the World
Bank through the Integrated Disaster Resilience Program for Results (P173568) and by sector partners in improving water
resources management through improved monitoring and planning (systems modeling, decision-making tools and

21.      Success of the RESEau Program is linked to the following performance improvement actions:
 Performance improvement #1: Improved technical and commercial performance of water operators: SONEDE, ONAS
    and CRDAs need to optimize use of existing finite resources, enhance the quality of the water services, while reducing
    O&M costs and increasing revenues. This requires implementation of technical and digital solutions, improved
    commercial management, and reinforced human resources and organizational structures to – among others – reduce
    NRW, ensure volumetric billing, maximize revenue collection, and pursue debt recovery.
 Performance improvement #2: Enhanced private sector participation: Engagement of the private sector has potential
    to enhance technology transfer, supplement limited implementation capacities of public operators, and complement
    public funding. The ONAS has been experimenting with private sector mobilization for O&M with contracts spanning
    ten years. The SONEDE has notable experience with framework contracts for maintenance of its network, and the
    CRDAs aim to follow a similar approach as public irrigation schemes are modernized. In addition, SONEDE is looking
    into the establishment of PPP schemes for desalination, potentially with mobilization of private capital. Progressive
    tariff increase (Measure#1, see next paragraph) alongside technical and commercial performance improvement
    (Performance improvement #1) are key to fostering an enabling environment for private sector engagement.
 Performance improvement #3: Enhanced industrial pre-treatment: The quality of the wastewater entering the ONAS
    treatment facilities is sometimes compromised by industrial pollution from facilities that do not comply with the
    wastewater discharge regulation (regulatory thresholds as per Decree of March 26, 2018) in relation to toxic and non-
    biodegradable pollutants. This puts undue burden on ONAS, making it more challenging and expensive to meet
    effluent quality standards, posing an environmental risk as well as a challenge for the successful reuse of TWW and
    sludge. Fines imposed are limited relative to the cost of carrying out the treatment (hence, provide limited incentive
    to the industrial polluters to comply with standards). Improvement of the industrial pre-treatment requires a
    combination of effective incentives for the industrial stakeholders to achieve compliance, as well as political will to
    enhance monitoring and inspection capacity of the National Agency for Environmental Protection (Agence Nationale
    de Protection de l’Environnement, ANPE) and fast-track enforcement of penalties.

D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach

Rationale for Using MPA

25.      The MPA is the most suitable modality to support the Government of Tunisia in its envisioned long-term, holistic
transformation of the water sector for sustaining the country’s socio-economic development. The scale and complexity
of the water challenges in Tunisia – encompassing a multiplicity of institutions at various government levels, a complex
governance framework, and large investment needs - go well beyond the scope of a single project or even a sequenced
series of follow-on projects. The programmatic structure makes it possible to:
 elevate the engagement beyond each individual institution and each sub-sector: the MPA will mobilize a wide array
    of stakeholders within and above the water sector, thereby addressing the bottlenecks that undermine water security
    and resilience in Tunisia. Projects will be strategically accompanied by technical assistance to strengthen linkages
    among institutions and ensure the sustainability of investments and services. The MPA embraces a holistic approach
    of the sector and offers multiple entry points to transform Tunisia's vision of IWRM.
 build a flexible and adaptive platform: the MPA allows phased implementation and course correction, changing
    prioritization of investments across sub-sectors based on evolving situations, while providing critically needed
    predictability of continued support and investments from the WBG and other development partners.
 gradually mobilize private financing: the MPA will allow a longer-term engagement to foster the creation of a
    conducive environment for the private sector to increase its role in this traditionally public-led sector.

Program Framework

29.     The Program consists of three mutually reinforcing phases. The proposed scope, packaging and timing of each
phase considers the readiness of investments, maturity of the governance and institutional context, and optimization of
implementation modalities. The Program design makes it possible to tackle the deep-rooted challenges across the water
sub-sectors, with a common lens and approach to achieve impact at scale, while ensuring an operationally implementable
set-up.

30.      Phase 1 will address security and resilience in the water sector, with a specific focus on potable water and
irrigated agriculture. This phase aims to optimize the use of existing water resources (focusing on water quality
management) and incorporate efficiencies to enhance the use of non-conventional sources like desalination. Support to
potable water will be focused in the central and southern regions, where water constraints are more acute; while support
to irrigation will be in the northwest, where surface water availability is higher and public irrigation schemes have been
developed in parallel to large dams. While the sub-sector engagements are geographically distant, the regions are
hydraulically linked by the inter-basin transfer from the northwest to the Sfax region. Diversifying sources for potable
water by including non-conventional water will reduce the competition for shared groundwater resources among sub-
sectors, hence reducing the pressure and the risk of restrictions on irrigated agriculture.

31.      Phase 1 will be structured in two projects presented concomitantly to the Board for considera on:
 The Irrigation Water Security, Resilience and Value-Addition Project (Annex 1) is expected to directly benefit 3,776
    farmers through improved irrigation water service and to benefit 9,026 farmers through support for agricultural
    extension and value addition and indirectly benefit Tunisian consumers with higher agricultural production of
    better quality. It will contribute to the achievement of the PrDO, the improvement of the technical and commercial
    performances of the CRDAs (Performance improvement #1), as well as the transfer of the management of modernized
    public irrigation schemes from the CRDAs to a utility (Measure #3). This will be enabled by the modernization of the
    infrastructure, the improvement of the internal capacity of the CRDAs, the enhancement of the modalities for the
    outsourcing of some services, and the operationalization of a central unit in MARHP for centralizing monitoring and
    evaluation (M&E), cross-CRDA learning, and coordinated capacity building. Finally, the project will support the
    increased value addition and commercialization of irrigated agriculture. Value addition will also increase farmers’
    capacity to pay for the irrigation services, creating a virtuous cycle benefiting the commercial performance of the
    irrigation operators. Strengthening irrigation operators’ performance will create the conditions for cost-effective use
    of higher-cost TWW reuse in agriculture (to be supported in Phase 3).
 The Potable Water Security and Resilience Project (Annex 2) is expected to benefit 2.3 million people through a
    better performing, more reliable and sustainable potable water service. It will contribute to the achievement of the

projects is operationally preferable to merging them into one project. The Government of Tunisia highlighted that
packaging all Phase 1 activities under one loan would create significant implementation challenges, as MARHP and
SONEDE have substantially different budgeting processes and fiduciary requirements. The option of two loans under one
project was also considered: while this could simplify day-to-day implementation within SONEDE and MARHP,

performance would still be jointly evaluated at the project level. Separating the projects allows each institution to be
assessed on its own results, which is necessary for governance and accountability. Clear accountability mechanisms are
essential to overcoming implementation hurdles. Dual or hierarchical decision-making arrangements blur mandates and
delay critical decisions, often leading to slow and suboptimal outcomes. The MPA enables optimal packaging of projects
so that each implementing agency is fully accountable for the administration, implementation and results of activities
under its mandate. The MPA’s learning agenda and the Program Coordination Committee are embedded to ensure
coherence of the programmatic approach and support incremental improvement measures in the sector. This approach
aligns with the conclusions of the World Bank’s 2025 Performance and Learning Review (PLR) for Tunisia, which notes that
inter-institutional coordination remains a significant challenge for the Bank-supported portfolio in Tunisia.

33.     Phase 1 will lay the groundwork for subsequent Phases by supporting preparatory studies and performance
improvements that enable future investments and advance key sector measures. In preparation for Phase 2, the World
Bank, in coordination with the EBRD, has supported ONAS in the preparation of a Financial Sustainability Action Plan
(related to Performance improvement #1 and to Measure #1). Priority measures include revision of pollution coefficients
for industrial and commercial customers, including funded sampling and laboratory campaigns; business intelligence
system to automate commercial performance monitoring and improve data quality; and Computerized Maintenance
Management System (CMMS) to optimize technical interventions. The Plan is structured around 22 actions grouped under
three strategic pillars: (i) revenue optimization; (ii) operational efficiency improvement; and (iii) cost reduction. The plan
sequences short-, medium-, and long-term measures, and clarifies institutional responsibilities. The implementation of
the Action Plan – supported in parallel to Phase 1 roll out – will improve the financial viability of ONAS and facilitate the
investments planned under Phase 2. Advancing on the implementation of the Action Plan represents a prerequisite for
the approval of the project in support to ONAS, notably with reference to: (i) the optimization of ONAS’ customer billing
at the correct tariff while maintaining an up-to-date and reliable database; and (ii) updating the tariffs for customers with
polluting activities. In preparation of Phase 3, preparatory studies are carried out with a combination of resources from
Government, World Bank and Trust Funds, jointly tackling infrastructure feasibility studies and the related institutional
aspects (related to Measure #4).

34.      Phase 2 will address water security and resilience, expanding the focus on aspects of sanitation and water
quality. Support to ONAS is expected to benefit 550,000 people nationwide through better performing sanitation services,

achievement of the PrDO and provide incentives for implementation of some of the Program’s improvement measures. It

and commercial performance of ONAS22 (Measure #1 and Performance improvement #1). Phase 2 will extend beyond
ONAS to include pertinent stakeholders such as the Ministry of Environment and Ministry of Health to address broader
aspects of water quality regulatory standards and regulatory oversight, industrial treatment and sludge reuse; the Ministry
of Finance to address aspects of financial sustainability; as well as other stakeholders such as the Ministry of Industry for
industrial discharge and reuse; and MARHP for reuse and groundwater recharge (Performance improvement #3). These
efforts aim to produce treated effluents suitable for reuse, contributing to environmental protection, and increase value
within the water treatment cycle. Based on the evolving dialogue, Phase 2 might also build on activities and models

management. Building on prior phases, this project will also explore opportunities to integrate value chain development,
job creation, and value addition. Phase 3 will benefit from the increased clarity on the institutional management of
irrigation gained under Phase 1 (Measure #3). It will contribute to reducing the pressure on groundwater resources

strategy.

Coordina on structure of the Program

38.       A Program Coordination Committee will be established within six months of Effectiveness of Phase 1 projects
through a decision by the Minister of MEP. Program-level coordina on is necessary to ensure a uniﬁed approach to
tackling the deep-rooted challenges across the water sub-sectors with a common lens, and in turn to achieve impact at
scale. It will include the sectoral stakeholders (MARHP, Ministry of Environment, SONEDE, ONAS), as well as MEP and MF.
The terms and conditions for its functions, staffing, and resources shall be agreed between the Borrower and the Bank.
The Program Coordination Committee will coordinate with other technical structures, such as the Coordination
Committee for TWW reuse in agriculture established by the MARHP in 2025 and serving all partner-supported projects on
TWW reuse.

Role of partners in the Program

39.      Water security and resilience is a core area of engagement for Tunisia’s development partners, with several
major institutions already active through financing and sector dialogue. This creates a highly engaged environment
where priorities and investment pipelines often overlap. The inter-agency water working group provides a valuable
platform for exchanges and coordination, helping to manage this complexity. Within this context, the World Bank’s
programmatic engagement is designed to complement ongoing initiatives and foster partnerships. Dialogue with key
partners is ongoing to firm up opportunities for strategic alignment and co-financing across the Program’s Phases. KfW
and the African Development Bank (AfDB) co-chair the inter-agency working group and, together with the Japan
International Cooperation Agency (JICA), are long-standing partners that have established a broad track record across
Tunisia’s water sub-sectors. The Program complements parallel efforts addressing aspects of water resources
management by partners such as the German Agency for International Cooperation (GIZ). In the wastewater reuse space,
the Italian Agency for Development Cooperation (AICS) and the French Development Agency (AFD) are among the most
active partners, and the World Bank is engaging closely with them on governance frameworks and optimization studies.
Coordination with EBRD, particularly around support to ONAS and performance improvement in the wastewater sector,
is also advancing. The preparation of the RESEau program has also been enabled by generous trust fund support, notably
through the Quality Infrastructure Investment (QII) partnership (financed by Japan), the Tunisia Economic Resilience and
Inclusion (TERI) country umbrella program (financed by Switzerland, the United Kingdom, the Netherlands, Italy and the
European Commission), and the Global Water Security and Sanitation Partnership (GWSP) (financed by several countries
and philanthropic organizations).

Role of the private sector in the Program

40.    Opportunities for private sector involvement will be deepened during the MPA implementation through a One
WBG (IBRD, IFC and MIGA) approach aiming at bringing innovative solutions, improving service delivery to clients,
reducing financing constraints, implementing tools to mitigate risks and contributing to job creation.

41.     Private sector engagement in the management of water infrastructure will be supported across all three Phases.
Under Phase 1, SONEDE is exploring outsourcing the O&M of desalination facilities for all its existing and future plants,
while the CRDAs intend to expand the use of service contracts to outsource the O&M of specific elements of the system
under defined performance metrics. Under Phase 2, enhanced use of innovative performance-based contracts is being
discussed, building on ONAS’ experience in attracting private sector participation in O&M. Phase 3 will ensure private
sector engagement in O&M, building on ONAS experience in Phase 2 for treatment aspects, and on MARHP experience in
Phase 1 for reuse in irrigation.

42.      Private Capital Mobilization (PCM) for construction will be pursued across all three Phases. As part of project
preparation, IBRD and IFC are supporting SONEDE and the national PPP Authority (IGPPP) in the review of private sector
partnership options (including the financial implications of investments with and without PPPs) in desalination

recovery, TWW reuse and sludge management can generate additional revenue streams that can be monetized to
leverage PCM. Fundamental to increasing private sector financing is the improvement of the technical and commercial
performances of the utilities (Performance Improvement #1), which the Program will support across all phases. The
consequent increase in creditworthiness, together with Viability Gap Funds (VGF) and guarantees through the WBG
Guarantees Platform, would open opportunities for accessing private capital, including in subsequent MPA phases.
International carbon and adaptation funds could further close financing gaps and augment investment attractiveness.
Technical assistance will strengthen operators’ capacity to identify PPP opportunities, structure contracts, and manage
their transactions.

43.      Private sector opportunities will be explored along the agricultural value chain using WBG AgriConnect
approach via IFC’s Africa Agriculture Accelerator Program (AAAP). The AAAP five themes that directly reinforce
AgriConnect priorities are: digitization of farmer payments and financial flows, digitalization of agricultural value chains,
agri-insurance and climate risk management, private sector investment and financing as part of AgriConnect, and value
chain mapping. IFC support can be structured around the five main themes aligned with the Program’s focus on irrigated
agriculture and private sector participation, while using AAAP as the main execution platform. AAAP uses the full range of
IFC’s investment and advisory tools—including risk-sharing, financing, and targeted technical assistance— with each
intervention designed to advance AgriConnect's focus on private sector participation. Specifically, and in coordination with
AgriConnect, AAAP will (i) build a network of agro-industrials and AgTechs capable of servicing selected agricultural value
chains across Africa; (ii) connect these actors to IFC’s network of financial institutions to facilitate financing for farmers,
AgTechs, off-takers, input distributors, and service providers; and (iii) provide capacity building throughout the value chain
to increase farmer’s productivity and close the food security gap. Interventions around agri-insurance and climate risk
management will leverage outputs of the Tunisian Rural and Agricultural Chains of Employment Project (TRACE, P175641
– closed in December 2024) and AAAP to support the design of crop and weather-index insurance products tailored to
irrigated schemes targeted by the project, using new project-generated data to improve product design and pricing. Other
opportunities to partner with insurers and aggregators will be explored to develop bundled products combining credit,
inputs and insurance for farmers benefitting from the irrigation modernization and value addition interventions.

Program Learning Agenda

44.     The Program provides a platform conducive to promoting adaptive learning across water sub-sectors, in support
of strengthening the sectoral framework. For each sub-sector, the corresponding project will include a learning and
training budget. In addition, grant resources (both recipient- and Bank- executed) throughout the Program lifespan will
contribute to the sector-wide learning agenda. A coherent M&E system deployed across the projects will facilitate the
consolidation of outputs and outcomes, thereby informing progress on the learning agenda, in turn guiding the design of
subsequent phases and allowing for course correction during implementation as needed.

45.    The learning agenda will support short-, medium- and long-term learning. Regarding short-term learning, the
agenda will encompass technical thematic areas that are targeted at a particular sub-sector or crosscutting. In terms of
medium- and long-term engagement, the agenda will prioritize activities intended to support the evolution of the sectoral
framework, improve inter-sectoral coordination and enhance institutional capacity. The learning agenda will also explore
broader opportunities to strengthen integrated solutions that address Tunisia’s burgeoning water security challenges,

PROJECT DESCRIPTION


A. Project Development Objective

    (i)          PDO Statement

1.     To improve the quality of irrigation water services; to strengthen the operational performance of the irrigation
operators; and to increase crop yields.

    (ii)         PDO level indicators

 2. The PDO indicators are:
 People supplied with improved quality of water service (#);
 Reduction in non-revenue water (m3/yr), as an indicator of operator performance;
 Revenue collection efficiency (%), as an indicator of operator performance;
 Increase in yield of selected crops (%);
 Number of direct project beneficiaries (#).

B. Project Components

3.       Agriculture in Tunisia is highly vulnerable to water scarcity, which is increasingly exacerbated by climate change.
Agriculture is essential to the country’s food security, producing on average 50 percent of wheat, 65 percent of coarse
grains, 90 percent of red meat, and the entirety of fruits, vegetables, poultry and eggs. It contributes on average 10 percent
of the GDP and 14 percent of the jobs (50 percent in rural areas). The agricultural sector would be particularly affected by
climate inaction, with its value added projected to fall by 29 percent by 2050 (compared to 7 and 3 percent for industry
and services, respectively),25 with dire implications for the country’s food security and rural jobs.

4.       The arid climate of Tunisia makes irrigation essential to secure production and increase agricultural value
added. Only 10 percent of the agricultural land is irrigated, but it generates 36 percent of the value added by agriculture
GDP26. Public schemes represent half of the irrigated area in Tunisia. They are largely concentrated in the northwest,
across the four governorates of Beja, Bizerte, Jendouba and Siliana. Their construction dates mostly to the ‘70s and ‘80s,
in parallel to the development of the dams upstream: these large investments were a national priority to ensure the
development of the agricultural sector, to contribute to food security, exports and jobs.

5.       Farmers in public irrigation schemes face constraints in irrigation service delivery, meaning they are unable to
access water in time and in the quantity needed for their agricultural production. This is a deterrent to agricultural
intensification and higher value production. Service challenges stem from aging infrastructure, often poorly operated and
maintained: at least 30 percent of the water does not reach the farmers due to leakages, and the system faces frequent
breakdowns. Water is provided on rotation to farmers, limiting crop diversification. Meters are sometimes broken,
preventing volumetric charges. This negatively affects the operators’ financial performance and removes incentives for
farmers to use scarce water resources more productively. Another element influencing the quality of the service is the
mounting competition over water, due to higher demand combined with climate change: irrigated agriculture is often

reduced water levels in the dams and consequent restrictions to irrigation, in favor to domestic, industry and municipal
uses. Not only is the allocation to irrigation reduced, but suboptimal water resource planning limits efficient water use by
farmers.

6.       Over the past 25 years, the management of public irrigation schemes has generally demonstrated limited
performance. The CRDAs face significant challenges as public administrative bodies to deliver a commercial service: they
lack financial autonomy, operate across a wide range of activities spanning both irrigated and rainfed areas, and they have
limited staff. The CRDAs continue to struggle with scarce financial resources for irrigation activities, due to the constraints
imposed on them to increase tariffs. Tariffs have been frozen since 2002. In 2021, a gradual tariff increase was launched,
but shortly after suspended due to the restrictions on water allocation for irrigation. As an additional challenge, the CRDAs
struggle to collect from the GDAs their share of the farmers’ payment. Overall, the CRDAs recover only 50 percent of O&M
costs.

7.      The delegation of the water service from the CRDAs to the GDAs is often a contributor to the modest
performance of public irrigation schemes. The GDAs are tasked by the CRDAs to provide the water service in 85 percent
of the public irrigation schemes in Tunisia. However, CRDA/GDA relationship results in dilution of responsibilities and
accountabilities towards the farmers. Less than half of the GDAs have a valid management contract with the CRDAs. The
GDAs have limited technical capacity, with only 25 percent employing a technical director. They are in principle tasked
with maintenance of the distribution networks, but they oftentimes resort to the CRDAs due to their lack of technical
competence and financial means. Most GDAs have accumulated significant debt with the CRDAs. They have usually poor
recovery rates, due to their lack of leverage and willingness to implement consequences for farmers not paying their bills.
Only 37 percent of the GDAs manage to cover half of the O&M costs. The GDAs often fail to fairly represent farmers’
interests and facilitate communication, and indeed only 30 percent have an elected board.27

8.        To overcome these challenges, the MARHP has been exploring the opportunity of transferring the management
responsibility of public irrigation schemes from the CRDAs to a utility (Measure #3) in parallel to the modernization of
the irrigation infrastructure. A study conducted in 2017 as part of the preparation of the Tunisia Irrigated Agriculture
Intensification Project (Projet d'Intensification de l'Agriculture Irriguée en Tunisie, PIAIT P160245) identified the
opportunity of decoupling the irrigation management function for public irrigation schemes from the CRDAs. It compared
various options for the management of the public irrigation schemes. The analysis indicated that the most suitable option
for the majority of public irrigation schemes in Tunisia is a public company, which could possibly open its capital to private
investors over time, once key conditions are met (proper technical and financial records available, reasonable level of cost
recovery). This autonomous, financially viable, client-oriented utility would progressively consolidate the management of
the public irrigation schemes across the northwest as they get rehabilitated. The study included extensive consultations
at all levels (farmers, GDAs, CRDAs, MARHP, MEP, MF and other key stakeholders), which recorded a broad consensus on
the need to improve irrigation services.

9.       The establishment of the utility is proving slower than anticipated. Over the past seven years, the draft decree
has been submitted twice to the Ministerial Committee, each time requiring revisions. This is in part due to the related
fiscal implications and the need for a progressive tariff increase (Measure #1). The MARHP remains committed to the long-
term institutional evolution towards the utility, but it also recognized the need for intermediate actions, which can provide
quick wins in terms of service provision, safeguard the modernized infrastructure, and create a more enabling
environment fostering the long-term institutional shift.

10.      The MARHP conceptualized an incremental approach with “no regrets” actions, as the institutional framework
keeps evolving, with improvement of technical and commercial performance of the CRDAs (Performance improvement
#1) achieved through a combination of:
 enhanced technical capacity of CRDAs’ staff to manage the modernized infrastructure: The management of the
    modernized schemes requires a new approach and way of working. The CRDAs will need to upgrade their internal
    capacity, adopt improved IT tools, and ensure data-driven decision making.
 outsourcing of some of the O&M functions through service contracts with defined performance metrics: The
    outsourcing by the CRDA will have the double benefit of: (i) mobilizing additional human resources with specialized
    skills, in recognition of the chronic understaffing of the CRDAs; and (ii) allowing more responsive actions compared to
    what is possible by the CRDAs within the limits of the public administration. Tasks to be outsourced can include: the
    O&M of large production infrastructure (pumping stations, reservoirs and water transfer); the surveillance and
    maintenance of the distribution network and hydrants; reading of the meters; and the management of water
    resources distribution in case of reduced allocation. The CRDAs can decide to establish framework contracts to
    outsource these tasks to GDAs and/or to private companies. Outsourcing to the GDAs will only be possible when a
    minimum level of performance is demonstrated, and it will need to be formalized through an updated management
    contract reflecting the new operational requirements of the modernized infrastructure.

11.     To enhance the CRDAs technical and commercial performance, a central monitoring unit in DGGREE/MARHP
has recently been established28 to foster synergies, economy of scale, and data-driven decision making in the
management of modernized public irrigation schemes. The CRDAs will fully retain their management responsibilities in
the public irrigation schemes, with the central unit offering support as needed. As the utility is created, the central unit
could evolve towards a regulatory role. The central monitoring unit would have the following core functions:
 Forging synergies across CRDAs for optimization of resources: The unit will pool and rationalize resources across
    CRDAs. It will support the CRDAs in optimizing their O&M tasks and improving their technical and commercial
    performances. The unit will manage a central stock of replacement parts (located in Bou Salem, Jendouba) to reduce
    unit costs and enhance the CRDAs’ responsiveness for preventive and corrective maintenance, helping break the
    perverse cycle of “build-neglect-rebuild”. The establishment of the consolidated stock (valves, suction cups, hydrants,
    pipes and hydromechanical equipment) is possible thanks to – among others - the standardization of the technical
    designs for modernization works, which has been carried out in the irrigation schemes modernized under the ongoing
    PIAIT (P160245) and which will be further enhanced by the central unit. The central unit will also ensure optimization
    of the structuring of contracts for outsourcing of some O&M activities, ensuring appropriate definition of the
    performance metrics, also leveraging the experience of SONEDE and ONAS in this domain. The central unit will also
    spearhead the conceptualization and implementation of a program for renewal and modernization of the modernized
    infrastructure.
 Enhanced IT solutions: The central monitoring unit will coordinate the use of IT solutions to optimize irrigation
    management. Under the ongoing PIAIT (P160245), the DGGREE has already carried out the creation of a centralized
    database; the establishment of an online Geographic Information System (GIS) - management information system;
    and the identification of a first set of key performance indicators allowing the comparison across schemes.
 Coordinated M&E for data-driven decision making: The central monitoring unit will inform key performance
    indicators across all modernized public irrigation schemes, to quantify technical (NRW; functioning meters; hydraulic
    and energetic performances of the systems…), commercial (collection rate; debts; share of water sold on volumetric
    basis, unit costs for O&M functions…), and agricultural (crop water productivity…) performance. These indicators will
    be consolidated annually in a report to MARHP management, becoming a benchmarking tool across CRDAs and

pillars. The proposed Irrigation Water Security, Resilience and Value-Addition Project builds on these same pillars,

E. Lessons Learned

32.      It is important to strike the right balance between supporting the evolution of the sectoral framework over the
long term and committing to short-term attainable advancements. The irrigation sub-sector suffers from intrinsic
institutional, governance and financial challenges that need to be tackled well above the technical level, considering the
complex political economy and social implications typical of the agricultural sector. Over the past two decades, the World
Bank’s engagement in Tunisia’s water sector has demonstrated the capacity to deliver significant physical outputs but has
often fallen short of advancing the evolution of the sectoral framework envisaged during preparation. The PIAIT was
restructured in 2023 to reflect the lack of progress towards the creation of the irrigation utility. This highlights the
concomitant need for the proposed project to: (i) frame the institutional engagement within a long-term, high-level
trajectory of sectoral framework improvement that could allow overcoming the fundamental challenges of the sub-sector;
while (ii) commit to gradual “no-regret” achievements within the timeframe of project implementation. In this spirit, the
proposed project supports the long-term goal of switching the management of public irrigation schemes from the CRDAs
to a utility, while in the short-term focusing its activities on: (i) improving the technical and commercial performance of
the CRDAs, including through outsourcing some O&M functions to performing GDAs and the private sector; and (ii) forging
synergies, economy of scale, and data driven decision-making in the management of public irrigation schemes through
the creation of a central monitoring unit in DGGREE/MARHP.

33.     The project’s implementation arrangements should build on lessons learned from the PIAIT, in order to ensure:
 ownership and accountability of DGGREE and DGPA for the implementation of activities under their respective purview:
    under PIAIT, the fiduciary responsibility of the whole project was ensured by the DGGREE, including for the activities
    under the technical purview of the DGPA. This overburdened the DGGREE while limiting the engagement of DGPA;
    and ultimately resulted in the downsizing of the Component 3 to only about 5 percent of its original amount and in
    the corresponding portion of the PDO related to value addition being dropped. By making DGPA directly responsible
    as the driving force for agricultural valorization activities, the proposed project aims to catalyze the chances of
    successful implementation and full PDO achievement.
 establishment of a well-equipped Project Implementation Unit (PIU): the complexity of project implementation
    requires dedicated, full-time staff throughout project implementation. The progressive reduction in staff allocated to
    PIAIT throughout its implementation, and notably the lack of PIU extension in line with the prolonged project duration,
    progressively led to implementation difficulties and delays. The proposed project aims to establish a PIU with
    dedicated, full-time staff throughout to reflect the complexity of project implementation.
 definition of interim arrangements until PIU established: PIU establishment under PIAIT was a lengthy process taking
    about 12 months from Effectiveness. The proposed project will establish interim arrangements during the transition
    period to ensure project effectiveness equates project readiness.

addition and market access, so that irrigation investments translate into higher farmer incomes. PIAIT’s support to

whom may still require additional support —whether in the form of technical assistance, capacity building, or improved
market linkages—to fully realize their potential.

PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY

A. Technical Analysis

46.      The rehabilitation of the remaining part of the BouHertma irrigation scheme will follow principles similar to
those applied during the modernization of the first part of the scheme under PIAIT. The BouHertma scheme was
developed in the 1970s and 2000s, in line with the modern standards of that period, including pumping regulated with
reservoirs, pressurized distribution, and on-demand service. The scheme is functioning, yet only less than half of its area
has been recently rehabilitated (under PIAIT), while the remaining part faces unreliable service. Design studies for
BouHertma 2 have been conducted under the PIAIT by local engineering consultants, under the purview of Jendouba CRDA
in collaboration with the PIAIT PIU. The scheme – once rehabilitated - will provide on-demand irrigation service. This type
of service requires that flow limiters be installed at each individual hydrant, in relation to farmers’ flow requirement
defined as per contract. A probabilistic approach is then used to determine the resulting flow in the primary and secondary
conveyance lines of the distribution system. Minimum pressure requirements also need to be determined, and the
hydraulic system designed accordingly. To control the use of water, meters need to be installed on each hydrant and a
volumetric charge applied to each individual user. One of the main technical issues faced by the system operators to
provide continuous service is the daily interruption of pumping during electricity supply peak hours during the months of
June, July and August. Sufficient storage capacity needs to be provided to ensure adequate pressure and flow in the
system, as well as to ensure adequate operation of the system for its optimal life cycle. The pumping cost can be
significantly reduced when sufficient storage is available and using an optimized design of the pump station and rising
main. Water hammer protection needs to be properly designed in order to reduce the risk of breakdown and reduced
lifecycle of the network. SCADA systems are necessary and will be provided for the complex schemes with several pump
stations and/or reservoirs. Appropriate attention will be given to O&M requirements, ensuring that detailed technical files
and instructions be made available to the operator, that safety considerations are incorporated in the design, and that
maintenance operations are facilitated.

47.    The project is aligned with the goals of the Paris Agreement on mitigation and adaptation as specified in Tunisia’s
updated Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) and Long-term Climate Change Strategy (LTS). On adaptation,

                        Official Use Only                                                                               Page 40
      The World Bank
      Water Security and Resilience Program


identified inherent risks include more intense droughts and extreme heat, as well as floods which are addressed by project
activities. The operation promotes climate adaptation and resilience through the modernization of irrigation infrastructure
and services. Proposed activities include rehabilitating irrigation and drainage networks, introducing O&M tools including
SCADA systems, expanding roadway access, and agricultural production valorization. Thus, residual risks have been
reduced to an acceptable level and pose no material risk to operations objectives. On mitigation, proposed activities
reduce energy use and water loss through real time monitoring (SCADA systems), network rehabilitation and improved
operational performance thereby lowering GHG emissions. None of the proposed activities poses an increased emissions

B. Economic and Financial Analysis

48.      The project is expected to generate net incremental benefits in terms of production increase for farmers,
income increase and job creation, through: (i) yield increases; (ii) higher cropping intensity; (iii) evolution of cropping
patterns towards higher value crops; (iv) enhanced quality of products and reduced post-harvest losses; and (v) enhanced
connectivity to markets. An economic and financial analysis was carried out to validate the viability from the standpoint
of the government, which is providing the infrastructure and technical assistance in the form of public and common goods.
In addition, a farm model was developed to evaluate the benefits and financial viability from a farm perspective.

Economic and financial analysis (EFA) for the BouHertma 2 scheme

49.    The economic and financial analysis for the BouHertma 2 scheme uses a structured discounted cash-flow model.
The model spans a 20-year horizon and consolidates investment costs, operating expenses, and projected benefits,
drawing on detailed engineering cost estimates, field surveys, and local market data. The model also captures investment
breakdowns, cropping patterns, yield assumptions, and price structures, which are aggregated into annual cost and benefit
streams. Prices for outputs and inputs are expressed in 2025 Tunisian dinar without inflation adjustment, and economic
conversion factors were applied to exclude subsidies.

50.     Projected benefits derive from a diversified cropping pattern designed to optimize water use and market

Contribution to income

54.      A farm model was developed to test the financial viability of project’s investments, using a discounted cash
flow across 12 representative farm profiles. The model covers six smallholder (small-scale) and six large-scale archetypes,
mirroring Tunisia’s dual agricultural structure: 0.5 ha (or 5-head dairy) smallholder units versus 10 ha (or 50-head dairy)
large farms. The value chains included are potatoes, dairy, fruits, vegetables, olives and fodder. The analysis distinguishes
between the farmers within the public irrigation schemes (benefitting from a combination of improved irrigation and
drainage services and agricultural enhancement and value addition) and those outside the irrigation schemes (benefitting
from agricultural enhancement and value addition but assumed in rainfed conditions). The model runs over a 20-year
horizon. The farm models were calibrated using data on yields, prices, and costs, combined with macro assumptions on
inflation and discounting. Inflation is projected at 6–7 percent per year based on IMF projections, and a 6 percent real
discount rate is used to compute NPVs.

55.     The project increases farmer income over the investment period. The financial feasibility of the investments
shows that the profitability for large farms increases, while having mixed financial effects for smallholder farms, which
would make the investments unviable without government support. There are important variations across value chains,
with some activities clearly beneficial and others reducing financial attractiveness for smallholders. These patterns
indicate that the project is financially very attractive for commercial farms, but requires more selective and carefully
designed support for smallholders’ producers, hence the need for government intervention and reflecting the public good
nature of the investments.

56.      The incremental NPV distribution reveals a strong asymmetry: all large farm profiles exhibit positive
incremental NPVs, while three of six smallholder profiles show negative incremental NPVs. For large farms, all modeled
value chains show positive incremental NPVs and IRRs comfortably above typical discount rates, with short payback
periods, which opens the door for introduction of matching grants, concessional credit, and private co-financing. For
smallholder farms, only dairy, fruits, and fodder show clearly positive incremental NPVs (from already negative NPVs in
base case), while investments in potatoes, vegetables, and olives are financially unattractive under current assumptions.
This means that in the case in which smallholder farmers were to finance by themselves the proposed investments, the
NPV would be negative in recognition of the heavy investment compared to the benefit, which justifies the government
intervention in the project. Targeted support differentiated by farm type and value chain would therefore yield significant
efficiency gains.

may cause site degradation, landscape disturbance, or habitat fragmentation. While these risks are largely predictable
and site-specific, the spatial scale of the infrastructure components and their proximity to sensitive areas (watercourses,
forested patches, agricultural lands) require systematic environmental screening and careful planning. An Environmental
and Social Management Framework (ESMF), which will guide environmental due diligence for all activities, was prepared

effectiveness. The ESMF includes screening criteria and environmental classification procedures, institutional roles and
responsibilities, and templates for site-specific Environmental and Social Management Plans (ESMPs). The ESMF will
ensure that all activities comply with the World Bank Group’s Environmental, Health, and Safety (EHS) Guidelines including
national legislation and good international industry practice. It will also provide specific guidance on pollution prevention
and control, including measures to manage construction-related nuisances (dust, noise, traffic), reduce water
contamination, protect soil and vegetation, and ensure proper waste handling.

their voices heard throughout the project cycle and components, especially women, the elderly and people with disability.
The project will carefully craft activities for the engagement of GDAs. The project will engage with performing GDAs to
further strengthen their capacity to provide the water service under the new requirements of the modernized schemes.
The project will equally support underperforming GDAs as they are phased out of the provision of the water service, in
favor of other services, most likely related to agricultural value addition and commercialization.

KEY RISKS

78.    The residual risks to achieving the PDO are rated Substantial for Political and Governance, Macroeconomic
environment, Sector Strategies and Policies, Institutional capacity, Fiduciary, and Environmental and Social.

79.      Political and Governance Risk is rated Substantial. Tunisia’s political context remains stable, and water security
is widely recognized as a strategic national priority, even as government priorities may evolve over time. The water sector
represents a structural constraint to economic development and remains a core government priority, given its central role
in growth, food security, and climate resilience. Agriculture is critical for rural livelihoods and social stability, particularly
in lagging regions such as the northwest, including Jendouba where project investments will be concentrated. Given the
structural importance of water security and irrigated agriculture, the operation and its scope are expected to remain
relevant even in the event of changes in sector governance. Political and governance risks are mitigated through alignment
with the Plan Eau 2050 and sustained high-level dialogue supported by the Program Coordination Committee.

80.      Macroeconomic Risk is rated Substantial. Economic growth is expected to average 2.4 percent over the medium
term, providing moderately favorable macroeconomic prospects. Furthermore, the Government has committed to fiscal
consolidation, thereby creating budgetary space to finance priority public policies. Nevertheless, elevated external deficits
and public debt levels leave residual macro-fiscal risks, particularly in light of the country’s vulnerability to shocks,
including climate-related shocks. The budget allocation of MARHP is insufficient to support investments and maintenance
of public irrigation schemes. The decision for the creation of a utility for the management of public irrigation schemes
(with the related fiscal implications) is being delayed, considering the overall macroeconomic context of the country and
fiscal constraints affecting SOEs expenditures. The project will mitigate this risk by focusing on short-term “no regrets”
actions, which will increase the commercial performance of the irrigation operators, such as: decrease of O&M costs due
to modernization of the infrastructure; identification of economy of scale for maintenance expenditures; increase in the
share of water sold on a volumetric basis; and plans for debt recovery from farmers who are expected to generate higher
incomes from improved irrigation services and productivity measures.

81.      Sector Strategies and Policy Risk is rated Substantial. The project is solidly grounded in the Plan Eau 2050.
However, the Plan does not yet set out detailed pathways for the regulatory and legal frameworks, compounded by the
awaited approval of the new Water Code. While the MARHP remains committed to the long-term institutional evolution
in public irrigation schemes, the risk that the creation of the irrigation utility will not materialize needs to be accounted
for. The project will mitigate this risk by taking an adaptive approach with flexibility in support depending on the evolving
institutional set up. The improvement of technical and commercial performances of the CRDAs, alongside the creation of
the central monitoring unit for modernized public irrigation schemes in DGGREE, are no-regret actions, providing support
for immediate improvement of service provision to farmers, while also paving the road to a future utility and building
capacity for strong oversight capacity in the longer term. To take into account the changing role of GDAs, the project
incorporates resources to support their shift towards other areas of activity, like agricultural value addition and
commercialization.

82.      Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability Risk is rated Substantial. Given the multiplicity of
institutions involved in the program, the chairmanship of the Program Coordination Committee will be entrusted to the

                        Official Use Only                                                                                  Page 51
      The World Bank
      Water Security and Resilience Program


MEP, in order to ensure proactivity and timely contributions from pertinent members and entities. Additionally, while
several departments of MARHP and the CRDAs have extensive experience in donor-funded projects in irrigated agriculture,
substantial risks remain. The collaboration among departments within MARHP has often proved challenging. The PIUs will
be established after the loan agreement is ratified by the Tunisian parliament and its staff appointed thereafter. The
establishment of the PIUs and of the project Steering Committee for the project risks to be time-consuming (estimated 12
months), delaying project implementation. The project will mitigate this risk by ensuring appointment of staff by Decision
of the Ministry of MARHP until PIUs in place. Capacity building programs for staff will be provided through the US$0.5
million Grant Facility for Project Preparation mobilized for the project.

83.       Fiduciary Risk is rated Substantial. From a procurement perspective, major works procurement packages are not
complex and will be procured using national competitive bidding processes. Some contracts for goods and consulting
services will approach the international market. The recurrent delays in procurement observed under PIAIT and the new
relatively complex implementation arrangements involving DGPA in procurement implementation of Component 3 with
limited experience and technical involvement of regional CRDAs suggest substantial risk of procurement. The project will
mitigate this risk through procurement consolidation, which has been optimized in the PPSD; and by ensuring transfer of
the procurement experience gained by DGGREE during PIAIT implementation to DGPA. From an FM perspective, the most
critical risk stems from DGPA, which has no prior exposure to World Bank FM requirements and currently lacks dedicated
fiduciary staff, heightening the likelihood of delays and non-compliance during implementation. In addition, the project’s
multi-institutional structure introduces significant coordination and fiduciary capacity challenges and risk of recurrent
delays in transmitting supporting documentation to the central level, resulting in slow initiation and processing of
payments and impacting the project overall disbursement. Moreover, the absence of internal audit structure provides
limited oversight capacity. To mitigate these gaps, each implementing agency will appoint dedicated and full-time
procurement staff and World Bank to provide them with enhanced training and support in the early stage of project
implementation. From an FM standpoint, and for DGPA, the mitigation measures will include, in addition to assigning
dedicated FM staff, the recruitment of an FM consultant to strengthen the FM team. For DGGREE and the participating
CRDAs, mitigation measures will similarly include dedicated FM support. In addition, the project will mobilize a consulting
firm to ensure engineering and construction quality oversight and to monitor the financial progress of works. Moreover,
the project will: (i) strengthen oversight mechanisms by operationalizing the internal audit function. This will be achieved
through the recruitment of an internal audit consultant with strong experience in World Bank procedures, while also
supporting the MARHP in establishing this function, and (ii) establish a detailed POM describing the roles and
responsibilities of the central and regional actors and the financial reporting procedures.

84.     Environmental and Social Risk is rated Substantial. Project activities may generate construction-related impacts
such as noise, dust, waste, runoff, and soil erosion, as well as risks from heavy machinery, borrow pits, and vegetation
clearance. While predictable and site-specific, the scale and proximity of works to sensitive areas (watercourses, forests,
farmland) require systematic screening and planning. The ESMF includes screening procedures, roles and responsibilities,
and templates for site-specific ESMPs. It will ensure compliance with national legislation, World Bank EHS Guidelines, and
good practices, covering pollution prevention, waste handling, and protection of soils, water, and vegetation.
Environmental and social requirements will be integrated into bidding documents, obliging contractors to prepare C-
ESMPs, comply with codes of conduct, manage labor conditions, and ensure occupational and community health and
safety. Risks also include land acquisition, impacts on livelihoods, and community health and safety concerns such as
accidents or SEA/SH linked to labor influx. Mitigation will include Resettlement Action Plans (RAPs), livelihood restoration,
a SEP, and a SEA/SH action plan.

PROJECT DESCRIPTION


A. Project Development Objective

(i)     PDO Statement

1.      To improve the quality and resilience of potable water services; and to strengthen the operational performance
of the national water utility.

(i)     PDO Level Indicators

2.   The proposed PDO indicators are:
 People benefiting from climate resilient infrastructure (#) (corporate scorecard indicator);
 People provided with safely managed water (#) (corporate scorecard indicator);
 Reduction in non-revenue water (m3/yr).

3.      The proposed project includes three components, for a total project cost of US$248 million. The IBRD loan will
total US$208.5 million, financing all the activities proposed net of taxes. The government contribution is estimated at
US$39.5 million, covering all project related taxes and fees. SONEDE will also contribute in kind to the project cost in the
form of salaries for employees assigned to the project.

B. Project Components

Component 1. Operational performance and governance of SONEDE

Subcomponent 1.1. Technical performance improvement program

4.      This subcomponent will support the improvement of the technical performance of SONEDE, with a focus on
Sfax (Tunisia's second largest city), Tozeur and Kebili representing 1.2 million people supplied. Activities under this
subcomponent will reduce the level of non-revenue water (NRW), currently at 31 percent of the water distributed in the
three governorates, thus reducing energy needs and GHG emissions per volume supplied from the system. The water
saved from loss reduction will contribute to improving water availability (adaptation) and resilience to droughts, meeting
the growing demand of the population, and in particular offering continuous water supply services to 60,000 people in
the South of Sfax Governorate currently experiencing intermittent water supply during peak water demand months, while
reducing SONEDE’s operational costs. The saved water will also reduce the need for desalination for the equivalent volume
of water of about 8.8 Mm3/yr, equivalent to a 100% reduction of GHG emissions (baseline 229,316 tCO2e; project 0 tCO2e).

5.     Strengthening of SONEDE’s technical performance in Sfax, Tozeur and Kebili will include: 40
 Restructuring the water distribution networks in 160 district metered areas (DMAs) that will be fitted with flow
   meters, pressure reduction valves, and sensors communicating data to a supervisory center (US$8 million) and that
   can increase efficiency and reduce energy use. This involves updating the network hydraulic model.
 Updating a Geographic Information System (GIS), collecting and analyzing flow, pressure data, and incidents with the
   implementation of Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition equipment (SCADA – hardware, software, and
   communication infrastructure) to allow for supervising the network’s real-time functioning (US$4 million) contributing
   to reduced emissions and reduced energy use.
 Rehabilitating and/or replacing part of the water distribution network and house connections (US$60 million) for
   adapting to water scarcity and droughts.
 Provisioning of leak detection equipment (US$1 million) to reduce NRW and thus energy use.
 Technical assistance to train and assist SONEDE in the identification and reduction of physical water losses through
   combinations of analytical and onsite investigative methods. The technical assistance will also support SONEDE with
   the design, implementation and supervision of activities of this component and train utility personnel (US$5 million).

Subcomponent 1.2. Commercial performance improvement program

6.       Activities focusing on strengthening SONEDE’s commercial performance will include the implementation in Sfax
of Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) to enhance commercial performance in monitoring, measuring, billing, and
collecting accurate water usage data leading to reduced apparent water losses (US$11 million). The project will fund the
supply and installation of 100,000 electronic smart meters equipped with data communication capabilities, the
implementation of data transmission systems, and the provision of meter data management applications. Given SONEDE’s
limited experience with AMI, the first batch of 100,000 meters will provide insights for a future scale-up to all clients in
Sfax and nationwide.

Subcomponent 1.3. Internal governance and customer management improvement

7.       This subcomponent will support SONEDE’s organizational improvement for enhanced internal governance,
customer orientation and efficiency (US$1.1 million). SONEDE has been actively engaged in a transformative process for
several years with the objective of enhancing its operational efficiency. One of SONEDE’s current priorities is to transfer
most of its operational functions to regional directorates. This initiative aims to streamline bureaucratic processes and
improve SONEDE’s responsiveness to the specific needs of local communities in terms of investments, operations,
maintenance, and service delivery. This measure towards greater decentralization must be meticulously designed,
considering the national regulations and laws, the financial stability of the company, and the quality of service provided
to its customers. Specialized experts will be engaged to assist SONEDE in designing the blueprint for this transformation.
Should the decentralization measure be legally enacted during project implementation, this component may be
restructured to support SONEDE in its implementation. The subcomponent will also finance the preparation and
implementation of priority measures of a plan to promote gender equity within the institution.

8.      Finally, activities will focus on strengthening customer relationships (US$1 million) through (i) a communication
and advocacy strategy with stakeholders, including an institutionalized complaints and feedback system for SONEDE that
can also be used to manage project-related feedback; (ii) customer satisfaction surveys; and (iii) improvements in billing
procedures, including options such as digital billing and mobile payments. The technical assistance supporting the
framework for its customer opinion survey will also support SONEDE to expand customer “management” to customer
“care” to explore potential policies such as including bill discounts, flexible payment terms, temporary financial assistance,
water efficiency upgrades, and educational resources on water conservation and how to use water more efficiently to
lower bills.

9.       In parallel to project activities, SONEDE took the initiative to modernize its customer relationships and
digitalization strategy through the improvement of its customer billing and management information technological (IT)

multiplicity of systems that could strain capacity and create interoperability challenges, a more integrated approach, such
as Enterprise Resource Planning (EPR) or modular systems, will be explored to improve efficiency and sustainability.
Opportunities to link these systems to government-wide platforms will also be considered to enhance transparency and
service delivery. A phased rollout starting with core modules (billing and customer relationship) and supported by
capacity-building will help reduce implementation risk.

Component 2. Reliability and resilience of water supply services

10.     This component will aim to improve the quality and drought resilience of water supply services in the water-
scarce governorates of southern Tunisia, where service delivery indicators lag those of urban centers in the North and in
the Center of the country. In the governorates of Gabès, Médenine and Tataouine, representing 1.1 million people
supplied through the interconnected network of south-eastern Tunisia, the project will strengthen and secure the
production of drought-resilient drinking water from a non-conventional water source, potentially partially replacing
overexploited groundwater resources and improving service reliability and drinking water quality. These investments will
help reduce the water supply deficit in these areas, reduce drought vulnerability, restore service continuity and reduce
the salinity of supplied water. The component will include the provision of support to SONEDE for studies and works
supervision.

Subcomponent 2.1. Expansion of the Zarat Seawater Desalination Plant

11.      This subcomponent will finance the expansion of a desalination plant in Zarat, increasing its production capacity
from 50,000 to 100,000 m3 per day, and the O&M of the entire plant during the semi-industrial running phase for a
year, and potentially during the construction period. The water supply to the southeastern region of Tunisia
(governorates of Gabès, Médenine, and Tataouine) originates from brackish water desalination plants situated in Gabès,
Zarzis, Jerba and Ben Guerdane, boreholes, and a seawater desalination plant in Jerba and Zarat (respectively in May 2018
and July 2024). Without any increase in desalination capacity, the inhabitants would face a climate-change-exacerbated
water deficit from about 9 percent to 18 percent by 2040 during peak demand months, aggravating service interruptions
and water quality (already exceeding national salinity targets for 70 percent of the region’s customers). Also, energy use
for freshwater abstraction would increase significantly due to the significant lowering of the groundwater table due to
shortages and over-abstraction (depletion risk), while some beneficiaries may resort to household-level water supply




                                                                                                                         Page 70


                                                                                                                  Official Use Only
       The World Bank
       Water Security and Resilience Program


alternatives that would often be energy intensive but ineffective. Expanding the Zarat desalination plant with an additional
capacity of 50,000 m3 per day is essential for south-eastern region of Tunisia given its high drought vulnerability and to
increase climate adaptation and resilience of water supply.

12.     The Engineering, Procurement and Construction (EPC) contract for the Zarat desalination plant, covering a first
phase of 50,000 m3 per day, was awarded at the end of 2018. The plant was inaugurated in 2024 and has been operated
for one year by the contractor. The plant, using reverse osmosis technology, was designed to accommodate an extension
of the same capacity. Most civil infrastructure (seawater intake, buildings, pipeline for brine disposal, etc.) has already
been constructed for the final capacity, which will reduce the costs of the expansion. The expansion of the plant to its final
capacity of 100,000 m3 per day will encompass in particular the installation of seawater pumps, pre-treatment layer filters,
reverse osmosis units with energy recovery devices, treated water pumps and electrical transformers. The construction
period is estimated at 18 months. The subcomponent will also fund engineering studies and technical assistance for works
supervision.

Subcomponent 2.2. Strengthening of treated water transmission systems from the Zarat desalination plant

13.      This subcomponent will finance the strengthening of large diameter water transmission infrastructure from the
desalination plant to the distribution centers of Gabès, Médenine, Tataouine and Ben Guerdane, and associated
engineering studies and technical assistance for works supervision. The plant will generate an additional volume of water
that will be transported via two primary pipelines and associated pumping stations to Gabès, Médenine and Tataouine.
The transmission main towards Gabès consists of 5 kilometers of pipeline of 500 mm diameter (and associated civil works)
for an estimated cost of US$3 million. The branch towards Médenine and Tataouine will consist of 24 kilometers of 600
mm diameter pipeline and a pumping station along its way for an estimated cost of US$18 million. Finally, the branch
towards Ben Guerdane will consist of 50 km of 500 mm diameter pipeline and a pumping station, for an estimated cost of
US$25 million. SONEDE has extensive experience in the design, procurement, and supervision of these types of works.
When the emissions from the trucking of bottled water and the energy required for sourcing are partially offset by the
energy requirement of the desalination expansion, GHG emissions increase by only about 10% (baseline 2,562,123 tCO 2e;
project 2,814,913 tCO2e; net 252,789 tCO2e).

C. Project Beneficiaries

16.      The main project beneficiaries will be people living in water-scarce areas in southern and central Tunisia. It is
estimated that the expansion of the Zarat desalination plant and associated transmission systems, and the reduction of
non-revenue water in Sfax, Kebili and Tozeur will make it possible to provide 224,000 people with continuous water supply
services, and to improve the quality (through reduced salinity) of supplied water for 440,000 people. The resilience of
services to drought shocks will be improved for about 2.3 million water users: 1.1 million across Gabès, Médenine and
Tataouine governorates through the development of drought-proof, non-conventional water production, and 1.2 million
in Sfax, Tozeur and Kebili through the significant reduction of water losses. In addition to these consumers, beneficiaries
will also include service-oriented and tourism enterprises that rely on SONEDE’s potable water to drive their economic
activities.

17.     At the national level SONEDE will directly benefit from institutional strengthening activities under the project.
This will include improvement in operational performance measures to enhance the utility’s financial standing and
adjustments to its internal organization to improve responsiveness to customers’ needs. Other agencies and institutions
at national and local levels will directly and indirectly benefit from the project through coordination, supervision missions,
and capacity-building activities.

D. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners

18.     The World Bank, with its global experience in water supply services, will focus on funding the most cost-
effective investments. This project will draw on the Bank’s Water Global Practice’s experience in strengthening urban
water security and resilience in water-scarce areas, as well as promoting efficient O&M of water services. It will focus on
modernizing utility management systems and procedures, strengthening organizational arrangements to align internal
incentives, and introducing benchmarking and other performance-enhancing measures. Tools such as Utilities of the

Future, Irrigators of the Future, and Water Utilities Creditworthiness training frameworks will be key to designing
institutional strengthening activities for SONEDE and to tailoring its learning needs.

19.      Component 1 is verified as Maximizing Finance for Development (MFD-e) and Component 3 as Private Capital
Enabling (PCE). The project will improve the overall financial position of SONEDE, potentially making private sector
financial participation more viable in the future. Implementation of the operational performance improvement program
will boost SONEDE’s operational efficiency and strengthen its capacity, thus improving the utility’s operating ratio, which
ultimately leads to improved creditworthiness and enables access to commercial capital (Performance improvements #1
and #2). This effort will complement the World Bank’s ongoing technical assistance to SONEDE to define a strategy for
involving private operators in future financing, construction and/or operation of desalination plants. The project has been
tagged as MFD-e. Component 3 develops SONEDE's capacity to identify and structure projects mobilizing private capital.
This is captured under the non-monetary indicator measuring the water production capacity of new desalination facilities
(at least partly) privately financed. The targeted desalination capacity has been estimated based on the planned size of
future desalination projects which could mobilize private capital (such as in Zarzis, Mahdia or Sfax). The volume of private
investment expected as a result of Component 3 activities is estimated at US$ 60 million. It has been calculated based on
the cost of recent desalination plants in Tunisia, and on an assumption of hybrid financing in which private debt and equity
would cover 60 percent of financing needs. The project has been tagged as PCE.

20.      Potable water security is a core area of engagement for multilateral and bilateral development partners in
Tunisia. The KfW Development Bank (KfW) is currently financing, together with the European Union (EU) activities
supporting the improvement of SONEDE’s operational performance in seven Southern districts of Tunisia. Furthermore,
SONEDE is discussing with the African Development Bank (AfDB) the financing, together with the German Cooperation
and the Republic of Tunisia, of operational performance improvement in Greater Tunis. The French Development Agency
(AFD) and JICA are also helping to strengthen SONEDE’s capacity in specific areas, such are NRW reduction and projects’
environmental and social management. During project preparation, the Bank closely collaborated with KfW, which
financed the original Zarat desalination plant in 2019, and is now supporting a review of SONEDE’s tariff plan and financial
equilibrium.

E. Lessons Learned

21.      Elevating the project-based approach into a higher-level, long-term programmatic engagement significantly
increases the achieving meaningful and sustained improvements in the potable water sector’s performance. Beyond
infrastructure investments to improve and expand services, deep-rooted governance challenges continue to impede
sector performance improvement efforts. Comprehensive sectoral improvement measures need to be tackled
strategically and in a phased approach, consistently with institutional capacity to implement proposed changes. The MPA
is well-suited to thus purpose: it supports infrastructure and expansion of existing services, while concurrently creating a
roadmap and phased actions that foster sectoral coordination and refinement of the sector’s enabling environment. The
policy dialogue established through the Program will include issues such as responsibility for financing investments in
potable water, private sector involvement and structured measures conducive to enhanced utility customer orientation
and efficiency such as decentralization.

22.      Improving the financial sustainability of public water utilities requires a combination of operational efficiency,
institutional strengthening, and a calibrated sector financial model involving the private sector at an early stage. The




                                                                                                                       Page 73


                                                                                                                Official Use Only
       The World Bank
       Water Security and Resilience Program


expansion of desalination capacity may undermine SONEDE’s financial position and, hence, increase its reliance on public
funding for capital investments While tariff adjustments may be needed to reflect changes in SONEDE’s production and
distribution infrastructure, project focus should at the same time be geared towards improving operational performance
through a combination of investments and technical assistance and adopting more efficient corporate practices (defining
and monitoring performance can directly impact financing performance by reducing costs and increasing revenues). These
measures also provide a solid foundation and enabling environment to attract strategic private sector participation. The
project design builds on the lessons learned from the last Bank-financed project with SONEDE (Tunisia Urban Water Supply
Project, P064836, closed 2018), such as the need to involve more the private sector in the design of complex infrastructure,
the importance of using advanced metering and payment technologies, and to strengthen customer satisfaction
mechanisms.

23.       Improving water supply services is not an isolated goal but a critical part of a comprehensive strategy to ensure
water security. Diversifying supply sources, enhancing operational efficiency, and embedding climate resilience in the
design and operation of services are critical to addressing broader water security challenges. Investments in non-
conventional sources such as desalination and treated wastewater (to be supported in a later phase of the MPA) are
critical to reducing dependency on over-exploited or highly climate-vulnerable sources. To ensure that existing and new
supplies (often produced at a higher marginal cost) are utilized efficiently, it is essential to improve the technical and
commercial performance of the utility through interventions such as reducing NRW, improving customer management,
and adopting advanced technologies for monitoring and managing water use.

C. Sustainability

30.      The project will help strengthen the sustainability of water resources management in southern Tunisia.
Groundwater availability and quality are declining steadily due to increased water abstraction due to reduced
groundwater recharge as a result of climate change. The deterioration of this strategic resource for SONEDE in southern
Tunisia, and its exposure to potentially irreversible saline intrusion, represent not only a production capacity constraint
but also a major water security threat for the entire region. The introduction of non-conventional water supply solutions
could enable SONEDE to decrease groundwater abstraction levels and alleviate pressure on groundwater resources.
Further measures to enhance groundwater recovery such as recharge using TWW will be assessed through parallel
technical assistance and implemented in Phase 3 of the MPA.

31.       The project will help SONEDE define its O&M strategy for desalination plants and lay the groundwork for its
implementation. This investment will increase SONEDE’s nationwide desalination production capacity by 14 percent,
raising the share of potable water produced through desalination to 13 percent. The World Bank and IFC are currently
supporting SONEDE in the review of strategic options for the financing, construction and operation of future desalination
plants and the expansion of existing ones. SONEDE is studying the opportunity to outsource O&M of water production
facilities, and this analysis will help identify and compare approaches that either involve the private sector or rely on
strengthened internal capacity. The expansion of the Zarat desalination plant will include a one-year O&M period, within
the project timeframe, during which SONEDE will decide whether to (i) have the contractor train its teams and transfer
O&M responsibility, or (ii) establish longer-term private management of the plant, either through a competitive bidding

PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY


A. Technical Analysis

33.     The project design offers a technically viable approach to improving water security in the targeted areas,
including the expansion of water production and transmission capacity and the strengthening of SONEDE's operational
performance. The proposed technical solutions for reducing NRW, and for expanding water production systems as well as
pressurized distribution transmission systems in urban areas, are considered adequate to achieve drinking water service
delivery standards and reduce inefficiencies in utilization of finite water resources. Engineering designs and the
technologies considered follow proven and well-established approaches. Complementary activities focusing on the
management and recharge of groundwater resources will be explored under Phase 3 of the MPA.

34.      In Gabès, Médenine and Tataouine, the current supply capacity is 6 percent short of water demand and
continuously deteriorating. The regional water system relied exclusively on groundwater resources with salinity levels (up
to 2.8 g/l) largely exceeding internationally recommended levels, and heavily overexploited by an average of 125
percent.41 Seawater desalination was identified as the only technically and financially viable supply augmentation option
to bridge this demand-supply gap, leading to initiating the construction of the first tranche of the Zarat desalination plant
starting 2019. Its implementation complements other ongoing efforts to reduce the water deficit, including the
Performance Improvement Project in Seven Districts (including Gabès, Médenine, Tataouine) financed by KfW, the EU,
the German Cooperation and the Government of Tunisia to reduce water losses and modernize water system
management tools, and demand management (awareness campaigns) by SONEDE to minimize wasteful water use. The
plant’s civil works were sized and constructed to allow a doubling of its capacity, which was then anticipated to be needed
by 2027 to address water supply needs until 2040, while decreasing pressure on coastal aquifers vulnerable to saline
intrusion.

35.    The project is aligned with the goals of the Paris Agreement on mitigation and adaptation. The project is aligned
with Tunisia’s key climate change strategies, including its Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) to the United Nations
Framework Convention on Climate Change and its Long-term Climate Change Strategy (LTS). Tunisia’s Updated NDC

36.     Assessment and reduction of adaptation risks. As discussed above in the context section, the major risks posed
to Tunisia by climate change include changes in precipitation patterns including longer and more intense floods and
droughts, reducing aquifers recharge; increased temperatures leading to higher evapotranspiration; and sea level rise and
extreme heat which could exacerbate wildfire risk. The project’s activities will promote adaptation to (i) changes in
precipitation patterns by offering alternative water production solutions to water abstraction from overexploited aquifers
already showing significant decreases in productivity (with water tables now dropping by about one meter per year); (ii)
increases in temperatures and evapotranspiration by reducing pressure on regional aquifers which will be able to satisfy
more easily higher demand for agriculture water users; and (iii) sea level rise which, together with coastal aquifers
overexploitation, poses the risk of irreversible saltwater intrusion, deteriorating further the quality (salinity) of
groundwater resources exceeding more and more often the targeted water salinity levels (1.5 g/l). Reducing water losses
and improving operational performance in Sfax, Tozeur and Kebili will also provide drought resilience by reducing water
withdrawals necessary to meet demand. Project-financed infrastructure will include design measures to ensure resilience
against floods, sea level rise and extreme temperatures.42 Thus, the residual risks have been reduced to an acceptable
level and pose no material risks to operational objectives.

37.      Assessment and reduction of mitigation risks. The expansion of the Zarat desalination plant is expected to
increase GHG emissions due to the additional energy required to process the increased water volume. This risk is partially
offset, as the plant uses reverse osmosis technology (coupled with the use of energy recovery technology), which is
considered less emissions-intensive than thermal and most other desalination technologies. All other Component 2
activities are on the Universally Aligned List and contribute significantly to lowering GHG emissions, reducing energy
consumption and increased resource efficiency. These improved operational performances measures include water loss
prevention, network rehabilitation, smart metering, as well as real-time monitoring and management.

38.       Gender. The gender employment gap extends to SONEDE, where women comprise only 18 percent of
staff. Women occupy 45 percent of senior and mid-level executive positions compared to only 25 percent of supervisor
positions and 5 percent of executive positions, indicating persistent barriers to women’s progression into senior decision-
making and technical leadership roles. Female employees at SONEDE are also more likely to be aged 40-65 years age group
(63 percent) compared to 37 percent in the 20-39 years group, reflecting the overall pattern of an aging workforce at the
utility. Without external recruitments for leadership positions or women’s career development support, SONEDE will face
difficulties in sustaining the relatively high share of women currently in leadership. In 2025, only 31 percent of promotions
granted in senior and mid-level executive positions have benefited women (whereas they represent 45 percent of the
workforce at those levels), underscoring potential constraints in advancement pathways.

56.      Scope of procurement, PPSD and procurement plan: Major procurement packages will include the expansion of
the Zarat seawater desalination plant to double its capacity from currently 50,000 to 100,000 m³/day and covering one
year of O&M; reinforcing transfer and distribution networks in the Southeast region (Tataouine, Médenine, Gabès);
rehabilitation of potable water networks in three regions (Sfax, Kebili, and Tozeur); supply and installation of smart meters
in Sfax; information system for water supply telemanagement/SCADA and related network equipment; and consulting
services for technical assistance and support throughout the procurement process and works supervision. To the extent
feasible, contracts will be consolidated to enhance competition, reduce interface risk, and streamline contract
management. As per World Bank procurement regulations, SONEDE will conduct early market engagement for contracts
to be procured approaching the international market (e.g., desalination works of Zarat station estimated at US$69 million,
supply of ductile iron pipelines estimated at US$ 25 million), and consider integrating evaluation-specific rated criteria and
weightings in bidding documents and bids, including where applicable life cycle costs as well as local labor participation
requirements and related skills development. SONEDE has prepared the PPSD covering all the abovementioned aspects
and related initial procurement plan. The PPSD and procurement plan have been reviewed and approved by the World
Bank.

57.     Procurement implementation capacity and risk: SONEDE’s implementation capacity was assessed in detail during
preparation of the PPSD. While SONEDE managed a World Bank project in 2018 and is currently implementing other
donor-financed projects, the complexity of the envisioned procurement packages, the recurring country-level
procurement delays, and the mandatory use of new features of procurement regulations, represent the main risks.
Mitigation measures will include World Bank support and technical assistance to SONEDE, with implementation and
decision-making (including all procurement activities) ring-fenced within the agency. With these mitigation measures, the
procurement risk is considered Moderate. The procurement risk and the procurement plan will be reviewed every six
months.

effectiveness timelines, this readiness level is expected to translate into disbursements exceeding US$25 million in FY28
and US$45 million per year onward.

Financial management

59.     A Financial Management Assessment (FMA) was carried out in accordance with the World Bank Policy on IPF to
evaluate the implementing agencies’ FM capacity and the adequacy of FM arrangements for Project implementation. The
objectives of the assessments were to determine whether SONEDE has adequate FM arrangements. The FM functions for
the Project will be provided by the PIU at SONEDE.

60.     Capacity and risk assessment. The overall residual FM risk of the Project has been rated Moderate provided the
mitigation measures described below are implemented. The FMA of SONEDE capacity carried out during project
preparation considered SONEDE’s experience with the World Bank’s FM procedures under the Tunisian Urban Water
Supply Project (P064836). The following key risks were identified: (a) limited capacity in executing the investment budget,
leading to low disbursement rates, (b) lack of dedicated FM personnel and absence of a tailored procedural manual to
manage project activities, (c) excessive workload for staff in both financial and technical units, and (d) insufficient
effectiveness of the internal audit function.

61.      Mitigation measures. To mitigate the identified risks and weaknesses, the following measures will be carried out:
(a) strengthening of SONEDE’s organization for effective project implementation, including by hiring appropriate human
resources staff, and reinforcing FM capacities, oversight and control mechanisms according to needs jointly identified; (b)
development of FM procedures as part of a detailed POM, which describes the roles and responsibilities of the
implementing entity including on cash and assets management procedures ; (c) submission to the Bank of annual internal
audit plans covering project transactions and submission of the internal audit unit of the project; (d) provision of training
on World Bank fiduciary procedures; and (f) expansion of the mandate of SONEDE’s external auditor to include all project
transactions, per terms of reference agreed and acceptable to the Bank, which will be annexed to the POM.

62.      FM arrangements. The proposed project includes three components, for a total project cost of US$248 million all
taxes included, representing US$208.5 million without taxes. The government's contribution is estimated at US$39.5
million, covering all project-related taxes and fees. SONEDE will be responsible for the overall financial management of
the Project. SONEDE will establish a PIU and mobilize a dedicated FM officer who has already been involved in previous
projects and has experience with FM procedures under World Bank-financed operations. Roles, responsibilities, and
procedures for FM will be described in the POM. The FM team will be required to prepare and submit an annual work plan
and budget (AWPB) no later than November 30 of the year preceding the year the AWPB should be implemented.
Unaudited IFRs will be prepared every six months and submitted to the World Bank 45 days after the end of each semester.
SONEDE’s external audit arrangement will be applied to the Project. SONEDE’s external auditor will produce a separate
audit report on the Project’s activities based on TOR approved by the Bank. Furthermore, the audit reports of the Project's
annual financial statements will be submitted to the World Bank within nine months following the end of each calendar
year. In addition to these arrangements, the project will use the Bank’s financial monitoring platform Funds Chain.

E. Environmental and Social

67.     The environmental risk rating is assessed as Substantial, based on the nature and scale of the planned physical
interventions, the sensitivity of the project’s coastal setting, and the current SONEDE capacity limitations. Activities
carry the potential for adverse environmental impacts, including brine discharge, localized air and noise pollution,
temporary leaks during pipe replacement, the generation of construction debris and excavated materials, and the
production of solid waste. High energy consumption during both construction and operation phases also contributes to
the overall environmental footprint of the project.

68.      In Sfax, the renovation of potable water networks will involve replacing old conduits with new ones to reduce
losses and improve service reliability. Asbestos-cement pipes present in the existing network will not be removed, but left
in place to avoid the health, safety, and environmental risks associated with their handling. To ensure safe operations, it
will be critical to: (i) map and document the locations of asbestos sections; (ii) train SONEDE staff and contractors on safe
working protocols when operating near such pipes; and (iii) include specific health, safety and environmental (HSE)
requirements in bidding documents, including clauses prohibiting intervention on asbestos pipes, mandatory protective
equipment, and emergency procedures in case of accidental exposure.

69.    The deployment of approximately 100,000 smart meters is not expected to generate immediate e-waste, as the
mechanical meters being replaced will either be reallocated to other areas of the network or sold through auctions in
accordance with national regulations. However, because smart meters have a lifespan of about 15 years and contain non-

replaceable batteries, the project will need to anticipate long-term end-of-life management. Technical specifications and
Environmental and Social Management Plans (ESMPs) should therefore include provisions for traceability and compliance
with Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) regulations once replacement is required.

70.       While the planned works are not located in protected or critical habitats, the coastal location of the desalination
facility introduces risks to marine ecosystems, and the terrestrial network reinforcements will traverse multiple urban and
rural settings where impacts on land use, community health and safety, and traffic disturbance need careful management.

71.     Although some of these risks are expected to be site-specific, they involve considerable technical complexity,
require strict compliance with international environmental standards for desalination and water distribution operations,
and necessitate robust, sustained monitoring and mitigation. At this stage, the implementing agency, SONEDE, does not
have an operational Environmental and Social Management System (ESMS) aligned with ESF and has not previously
implemented Bank-financed projects under ESF. This may limit its ability to meet relevant standards without targeted
capacity support.

72.      To address these gaps, the project will include institutional strengthening measures, such as training for SONEDE
staff in ESF implementation, and technical assistance for Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) and ESMP
preparation and supervision of works. An Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) was reviewed and
accepted by the Bank. The screening of sub-projects (transfer conduits, smart metering program, network
rehabilitation/renovation activities) will identify the appropriate E&S instruments to be developed during implementation
(ESIAs, ESMPs, monitoring plans, etc.). Emphasis will be placed on brine management, asbestos risk management, energy
efficiency, long-term smart meter disposal planning, and occupational and community health and safety.

73.       Social risks are rated as Substantial, reflecting a wide range of potential challenges identified in the project’s
initial social risks assessment. Key social risks will entail: (i) Risks related to unmet expectations for access to modernized
and efficient water services, especially when individuals and communities perceive that they do not benefit from the
improvements brought about by the project, particularly in relation to its geographical coverage and/or prioritization of
interventions. (ii) Risks related to labor and working conditions are anticipated for the water systems’ modernization
activities. These risks may cover external labor influx, unequal employment opportunities, and gaps in occupational health
and safety, which may result in tensions around job opportunities created by the project. To mitigate these risks, the
project will implement robust labor management procedures and adopt a grievance mechanism for workers to ensure fair
integration of the workforce, compliance with labor standards, and protection of workers’ rights. (iii) Community health
and safety risks may arise during works on water distribution networks due to accidents, increased traffic, or exposure to
hazardous materials and heavy engines. (iv) The risk of Sexual Exploitation and Abuse/Sexual Harassment (SEA/SH),
anticipated for this project. An action plan is planned to manage these risks through proportionate mitigation measures.
(v) Physical and economic displacement may arise because of rehabilitation activities to be undertaken in urban centers
(in Sfax, Tozeur and Kebili) and Medina (Sfax), potentially leading to disruptions in livelihoods. Women engaged in shellfish
gathering in Zarat will not be affected, as the project does not involve any additional physical investments along the marine
coastal zone. The scope of land acquisition for this project is expected to be limited, and small-scale acquisition is
anticipated only for the expansion of Zeuz pumping station. Preventative measures will be essential to avoid negative
impacts on livelihoods and, if needed, additional mitigation strategies will ensure that affected parties receive fair
compensation and comprehensive support.

74.     Stakeholder engagement is essential to manage effectively the substantial social risks associated with the project.
The project will prioritize inclusive consultations and accessible communication to ensure that customers, citizens, people
affected by the project activities and vulnerable groups are included, integrating their feedback into both project
preparation and implementation phases. These efforts will be supported by a comprehensive Stakeholder Engagement
Plan, robust grievance redress mechanisms, capacity building for implementing agencies, and ongoing monitoring and
reporting to foster trust and ensure equitable outcomes for all stakeholders.

75.     Finally, construction and excavation activities, especially in historically rich urban centers (for example, the

are reported immediately to the relevant authorities, notably the National Cultural Heritage Institute (INP) and managed
in accordance with national laws and best international practices.

76.    Both environmental and social risks are considered manageable with appropriate mitigation measures, strong
implementation support, and a clear commitment to building institutional capacity. The project offers a valuable
opportunity to enhance E&S practices and strengthen inclusive stakeholder engagement processes.

KEY RISKS

78.    The residual risks to achieving the PDO are rated Substantial for Political and Governance, Macroeconomic
environment, Sector Strategies and Policies, Institutional capacity, and Environmental and Social.

79.      Political and Governance Risk is rated Substantial. Tunisia’s political context remains stable, and water security
is widely recognized as a strategic national priority, even as government priorities may evolve over time. The water sector
represents a structural constraint to economic development and remains a core government priority, given its central role
in growth, food security, and climate resilience. Agriculture is critical for rural livelihoods and social stability, particularly
in lagging regions such as the northwest, including Jendouba where project investments will be concentrated. Given the

structural importance of water security and irrigated agriculture, the operation and its scope are expected to remain
relevant even in the event of changes in sector governance. Political and governance risks are mitigated through alignment
with the Plan Eau 2050 and sustained high-level dialogue supported by the Program Coordination Committee.

80.     Macroeconomic Risk is rated Substantial. Economic growth is expected to average 2.4 percent over the medium
term, providing moderately favorable macroeconomic prospects. Furthermore, the Government has committed to fiscal
consolidation, thereby creating budgetary space to finance priority public policies. Nevertheless, elevated external deficits
and public debt levels leave residual macro-fiscal risks, particularly in light of the country’s vulnerability to shocks,
including climate-related shocks. SONEDE remains heavily dependent on public resources for capital expenditures. While
mobilizing private capital is a priority, SONEDE has limited experience in this area. The project will mitigate this risk by
enhancing SONEDE’s financial sustainability and capacity to explore mechanisms to enhance the bankability of future
investments, such as through VGF and guarantees.

81.      Sector Strategies and Policy Risk is rated Substantial. The project is solidly grounded in the Plan Eau 2050.
However, the Plan does not outline regulatory and legal frameworks, including the new Water Code. SONEDE’s capacity
to take on key measures to improve sector performance – in particular tariff adjustments and opening to the private sector
for desalination expansion - remains subject to governmental decisions. A continuous sector dialogue with the Project
Steering Committee and with the Program Coordination Committee will help mitigate these risks.

82.      Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability Risk is rated Substantial. Rising costs from
desalination without a clear tariff and subsidy roadmap could create financial pressures on SONEDE. The project aims to
enhance SONEDE’s performance through the introduction of improved governance measures, support to the
decentralization agenda to improve customer orientation, and technology and capacity transfer from the private sector.
Following several tariff adjustments amounting cumulatively to 63 percent between 2020 and 2024, SONEDE is now
showing a solid financial position. Zarat desalination plant (including the first tranche) will incur a 5 percent increase in
SONEDE's operating costs. In the perspective of desalination capacity expansion across the country (and other
investments), SONEDE plans to develop a new tariff adjustment program for 2026-2030 with the support of KfW, with
careful consideration for affordability aspects and targeted subsidy options. The Bank will closely support SONEDE in this
dialogue with the Government to ensure the project's and SONEDE's financial viability.

83.      Environmental and Social Risks are rated Substantial. The project is expected to bring environmental impacts
such as noise, dust, waste generation, increased energy use, and impacts on marine life from desalination plant discharges.
It may involve land acquisition and access to land for infrastructure development. These impacts are generally predictable,
limited to specific sites and manageable with the appropriate mitigation measures. Construction activities may pose risks
to community health and safety, including accidents and SEA/SH, due to labor influx and project socio-economic dynamics.
An ESMF has been prepared to outline how to assess and manage risks, including preparing screening tools to determine
site-specific instruments, following the World Bank’s Environmental, Health and Safety (EHS) Guidelines. Also,
Resettlement Action Plan(s), livelihood restoration plan(s), and a SEA/SH action plan will be prepared. Since SONEDE has
limited experience with the World Bank’s ESF, the project presents a strategic opportunity to strengthen SONEDE’s
institutional capacity.
