                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     With Phase II of the Horn of Africa - Groundwater for Resilience Project (P511492) Djibouti will be joining the Horn of
                     Africa - Groundwater for Resilience Project (P174867), which started with Kenya, Ethiopia, Somalia, and the
                     Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD). This regional project aims to increase sustainable access and
                     management of groundwater resources in the Horn of Africa's borderlands, addressing urgent water supply needs and
                     fostering transboundary cooperation. The project's development objective is to enhance sustainable groundwater
                     access and management in the region's borderlands. It comprises four main components: delivering inclusive
                     groundwater services to priority areas, generating groundwater information and strengthening regional and national

                     institutions, supporting project management and operations, and a Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC)
                     for rapid response to emergencies. Expected outcomes include improved groundwater access for communities,
                     strengthened regional and national groundwater management institutions, enhanced cooperation among participating
                     countries, and increased resilience to water scarcity. Key features include regional cooperation facilitated by IGAD, a
                     focus on borderland areas to improve water supply and foster cross-border cooperation, and consideration of complex
                     land rights and access issues, particularly for women. The project aims to ensure equitable access to land and water
                     resources, benefiting from regional cooperation and addressing both shallow and deep groundwater needs.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     Djibouti faces severe environmental constraints due to its arid desert climate, receiving only below 200 mm of rainfall
                     annually. This extreme aridity, coupled with limited permanent surface water sources, forces the country to rely almost
                     entirely on groundwater for its freshwater needs. Per capita water availability is critically low at 185 cubic meters/year,
                     far below the global water scarcity threshold. Aquifer systems consist of two main sources: the transboundary Afar Rift
                     Valley Aquifer and smaller local sedimentary aquifers. Groundwater quality is a major concern, with over half of
                     boreholes exceeding salinity levels of 900 mg/L, making water unfit for drinking or irrigation. Djibouti is highly exposed
                     to climate extremes, including frequent droughts, exacerbating water shortages and increasing flood risks, which
                     further strain fragile water infrastructure and contaminate limited freshwater reserves. With rising temperatures and
                     erratic rainfall, sustainable water management and regional cooperation on shared aquifers are essential for Djibouti’s
                     future resilience.




Public Disclosure
                     Djibouti’s population is very urbanized (79% urban, 21% rural ). Livelihoods depend on agropastoralism and nomadic
                     herding. However, deep inequalities persist, with 21% of the population below the national poverty line and 44% below
                     the international lower-middle-income threshold. Only 47% of the rural population has access to basic drinking water,
                     forcing many to rely on unsafe sources. Women and children bear the burden of water collection, spending hours daily
                     fetching water, limiting education and economic opportunities. Nomadic communities face extreme water insecurity
                     due to their mobility and reliance on scarce groundwater. Much of the rural population in the borderland areas rely on
                     cross-border pastoral livelihoods. This is also the case in Djibouti whose rural population is highly dependent on
                     nomadic herding.




                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     MAEP-RH will be responsible for supporting the implementation of all project components. A new Project
                     Implementation Unit (PIU/UGP) will be established and housed within MAEP-RH. Project management will be
                     supervised by the Ministry, and all fiduciary aspects of the project will be centralized in this PIU, which will also handle
                     procurement and environmental and social management for all project activities. The PIU will implement the project in
                     close collaboration with existing institutions at the regional level. The PIU will coordinate with all beneficiary agencies to
                     ensure coherent implementation while maintaining centralized oversight of environmental and social alignment with
                     the ESF and ESCP. Regional institutions, although not fully determined at this stage, is likely to provide technical
                     support and facilitate community engagement, complementing rather than replacing MAEP-RH's oversight role.
                     MAEP-RH has experience with donor-funded projects, including WB projects under the ESF and the OPs. Currently they
                     are implementing the PURCSA, A $20 million emergency response to the food security crisis, approved in October 2022,

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                         Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                   Moderate
                     The environmental risk is rated as Moderate environmental risk given the potential for localized but potentially
                     significant impacts, particularly related to groundwater resources and waste management from desalination, and the
                     need for detailed site-specific assessments during preparation. Key potential environmental risks associated with the
                     project include those typically linked to construction activities (drilling, excavation, building) and operation of water
                     infrastructure in arid and semi-arid environments. These encompass pollution,resource consumption, waste
                     generation, potential impacts on biodiversity and habitats , occupational and community health and safety.The
                     magnitude and spatial extent of these risks are anticipated to be primarily site-specific and localized, associated with



Public Disclosure
                     individual infrastructure development sites (boreholes, wells, surface water harvesting structures, desalination units,
                     regional center construction/rehabilitation). Most potential impacts, such as dust, noise, and construction waste, are
                     expected to be temporary and reversible with the implementation of standard mitigation measures. Given Djibouti’s
                     relatively small and sparse rural population, the overall demand on groundwater resources is expected to be modest,
                     which helps reduce many of the typical environmental risks associated with groundwater abstraction. However, the
                     potential for groundwater contamination or improper brine disposal from desalination units could have more
                     significant and longer-term consequences if not adequately managed. The project's focus on rural and borderland
                     areas, some of which may be ecologically sensitive or support nomadic populations, necessitates careful site selection
                     and screening. Component 2 will provide technical assistance to enhance the MAEP-RH's capacity in water resource
                     management with a new Master Plan, and strengthen Djibouti water resource knowledge base.

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The social risk rating at Concept is Substantial, although it may be raised to High during Appraisal pending additional
                     information of potential affected parties, such as the presence of groups which meet the criteria of Indigenous
                     Peoples (IP/SSAHUTLCs) in ESS7 in the borderlands, as well as an assessment of security/conflict risks. Activities under
                     Component 1 (groundwater infrastructure and small-scale irrigation development), could lead to a range of social
                     risks and impacts. These include: conflict and social cohesion risks of both endogenous and exogenous communities
                     due to natural resource access and which can impact traditional livelihoods (nomadic herders in Djibouti and those of
                     border communities); land acquisition affecting rural communities exacerbated by complex land systems, including
                     customary and communal land rights; physical and/or economic displacement especially of vulnerable groups who
                     rely on traditional access to land and water for their livelihoods and cultural identity; the potential presence of

                     Indigenous peoples in the border areas with Ethiopia; exclusion of vulnerable groups including migrants from other
                     countries (both in search of water and accessing traditional customary land use systems), women, youth and persons
                     with disabilities, and internally displaced person; labor risks including informal labor (workers with no contracts
                     especially for vulnerable laborers such as IDPs, migrants), child labor and forced labor, as well as exposure of local
                     communities to communicable diseases through labor exposure; SEA/SH risks due to labor influx exacerbated by
                     limited ability to regularly monitor the project due to remoteness and client capacity; weak stakeholder engagement
                     practices including exclusion or limited access or participation of vulnerable groups; weak grievance management
                     inclusion SEA/SH-GM management. Weak client monitoring and reporting is also a risk as the PIU is very stretched,
                     and the project is located in remote areas.

                      ESS1 - Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts                                 Relevant

                      The project involves complex interventions that require thorough E&S risk assessments. Key environmental risks and
                      impacts during construction and operation phases may include generation of waste (construction, domestic, and
                      hazardous waste); noise and dust pollution; water and soil contamination; occupational and community safety;
                      impacts on biodiversity and natural habitats within the project areas; resource efficiency, including water and energy
                      use. Key social risks and impacts may include the potential presence of Indigenous peoples and traditional
                      communities depended on resource access for their livelihoods; physical and/or economic displacement especially of

                      ESS10 - Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure                                                      Relevant

                      As the PDO focuses on benefits to communities and reslient livelihoods, the use of CDD approaches will help mitigate
                      risks by promoting community buy-in. Central to this is ensuring active, inclusive, transparent and accessible
                      consultation throughout the project lifecycle. Likely project affected stakeholders include nomadic herders (in
                      Djibouti as well as groups who meet the criteria of IPs/SSAHUTLCs under ESS7 from Ethiopia) residing near proposed
                      infrastructure sites, particularly in rural and borderland areas, as well as women, elderly, youth and persons with
                      disabilities to ensure they receive project benefits. Interest parties include relevant government agencies at national
                      and local levels, civil society organizations active in the water sector and rural development, humanitarian/refugee
                      sectors, and women's organizations. A SEP, including a project-level Grievance Mechanism fand a GM process for
                      SEA/SH incidents will be prepared by Appraisal and disclosed.
