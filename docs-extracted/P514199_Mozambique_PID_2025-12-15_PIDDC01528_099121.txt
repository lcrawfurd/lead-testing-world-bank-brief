B. Introduction and Context

Country Context

1.      Despite its rich natural resource base, Mozambique suffers from high poverty and inequality rates, as well as
the compounding impact of climate shocks in a context that is also afflicted by conflict and fragility. An estimated 81
percent of Mozambique’s 33 million population live under the international poverty line of US$3 per-day, and inequality
(Gini of 0.50) are among the highest in the world. The benefits of the economic boom and peace dividend that followed
the end of the civil war in 1992 were not distributed in an inclusive manner and growth has since been derailed by a
series of shocks, including the hidden debt crisis (2016), the escalation of insurgency in Northern Mozambique (since
2017), cyclones (such as Idai and Kenneth in 2019, and Freddy in 2023), and the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2022). Within
this context, Mozambique is experiencing fragility challenges, including historical exclusion that underpins socio-
economic grievances and regional divides (north-south and rural-urban), youth marginalization, the impacts of climate
change and natural hazards on livelihoods, particularly land-based resources, and challenges related to governance of
natural resources. As 70 percent of the population relies on low-productivity subsistence agriculture, impacts on
agriculture, such as crop failures and disruptions in market access, not only directly impact the incomes of households
but also reduce per capita food consumption of affected households by 25–30 percent, aggravating food security (CCDR,
2023). Climate change is projected to reduce GDP by 9 percent in 2050, primarily due to decline in agriculture and labor
productivity. Worst-case scenarios increase poverty by 5 percent, pushing 1.6 million more people into poverty by 2050.

2.     Mozambique’s deep-seated structural fragility and the unfinished process of inclusive state building, have
manifested in different ways, including in the conflict in Cabo Delgado and in the 2024 post-election unrest. The eight-
year conflict in Mozambique’s northernmost province has its origins in the inequitable distribution of the benefits of
growth and lack of political and economic inclusion. The conflict has resulted in a humanitarian crisis, with over 6,000
people killed to date, and displacement at 461,745 as of mid-2025, down from their peak of 1.2 million in November
2022. Approximately 701,462 people have returned to their places of origin as of mid-2025. Although the situation on
the ground has somewhat stabilized, it remains volatile. Attacks have risen since mid-2024 and escalated in 2025 covering
the territory of Cabo Delagado and spreading into Nampula and Niassa provinces, prompting repeat displacements and
disruption of provision of basic services.

3.      Mozambique faces deep-rooted gender and youth development challenges, with women and young people
disproportionately affected by poverty, and with limited access to education, healthcare, and economic opportunities.
Women experience high rates of gender-based violence (GBV), one-third report physical abuse and cultural norms
perpetuate early marriage and unpaid labor, leaving most employed in vulnerable agricultural work highly exposed to
climate risks. Mozambique ranks 118 out of 165 on the UNDP Gender Inequality Index, reflecting systemic barriers to
empowerment. Youth, who make up nearly two-thirds of the population, face high unemployment rates—8.1% overall
and even higher among young women—due to poor access to quality education and vocational training. Most rural youth
rely on subsistence farming or informal work with little chance for income stability or social mobility, limiting their
participation in decision-making and economic growth. Addressing these gaps is critical for inclusive development and
resilience in Mozambique.

4.     Lack of infrastructure and basic services hinder access to jobs and economic opportunities for already
vulnerable communities. There are significant differences in access to basic services between urban and rural areas. In
2019/2020, access to improved water and sanitation was far greater among urban households (above 60 percent) while
for rural households this figure is below 40 percent. The urban/rural gap is higher in access to electricity with greater
access among urban households (70.3 percent) than rural households (8.9 percent). Access to primary education is high
among urban households where enrolment rates of children in primary school age (6 to 11 years old) reach 90 percent
in urban areas against 76 percent in rural areas. Destruction of infrastructure and economic assets since October 2024
due to post-election protests, conflict, and cyclones have widened the infrastructure gap and cost 50,000 jobs across the
country. Overall damages to economy are estimated at US$504 million. Nampula was the northern province that was
most affected by these events. The districts of Erati and Memba were most impacted, with all public infrastructure
damaged or vandalized, businesses closed, and jobs lost.

5.      Natural hazards and climate change are a threat amplifier in the presence of pre-existing vulnerabilities, and
this is especially so in northern Mozambique and affects the most vulnerable. Floods affected 8.5 million people and
storms affected 6.2 million people between 1980 and 2020. In 2019, cyclones Idai and Kenneth caused damages and
losses of US$3.0 billion and recovery needs of US$3.4 billion in central and northern regions. In 2023, cyclone Freddy
caused damages of nearly US$1.5 billion. These climate-related events are increasing in frequency and intensity, with
severe compounding impacts. Between December 2024 and March 2025, the northern region of Mozambique was hit by
three consecutive storms affecting over 1 million and causing significant infrastructure damage and livelihood losses.
Sea-level rise is likely to exceed half a meter by 2090, putting coastal infrastructure, agriculture, key ecosystems, and
fisheries at risk for the 60 percent of Mozambicans who are living in low-lying areas. If mitigation measures are not taken
between now and 2050, climate change is estimated to generate economic losses in the range between US$2.3 billion
and US$7.4 billion.

Sectoral and Institutional Context

6.      Addressing the drivers of fragility and conflict in northern Mozambique requires a holistic approach to
strengthening social cohesion. The approach is anchored in rebuilding the social fabric, strengthening trust between
and within groups, and opening spaces for engagement between citizens and local authorities, as well as investing in
infrastructure and the provision of basic services. It requires a specific focus on supporting young people to access
economic and livelihoods opportunities, and investment in community mechanisms and resilience to help prevent youth
radicalization towards violent extremism. It focuses on inclusion of internally displaced people (IDPs) and host

vulnerabilities. This is critical given that many IDPs in Cabo Delgado, Nampula, and Niassa have been displaced multiple
times and largely consist of women and children, while returnees contend with specific risks related to return to place of
origin.

7.      The continuation and escalation of conflict and fragility across the north have hindered government efforts to
deliver infrastructure and services to communities in the conflict-affected areas. As of September 2021, more than 220
schools and multiple water systems in Cabo Delgado were impacted, and more than 130 health units closed or destroyed.
More than 300,000 school-age children have been displaced and rely on emergency schooling. Nearly all public buildings
in the most affected districts have been severely damaged or destroyed. While government launched recovery efforts in
the south and reconstruction actions in the North of Cabo Delgado, insurgent attacks resumed in mid-2024 and escalated
to districts in northern Nampula and eastern Niassa, generating a new wave of displacements and destruction of public
and private infrastructure. This has exacerbated grievances related to the lack of access to public services and jobs in the
region.

8.     To improve accountability and transparency of public decision making, support local economies and prevent
the spread of conflict, the Government is reinstating the decentralization, community participation, and local
economic development agendas. From 2005 to 2015, under the leadership of the National Directorate of Planning (DNP)
in the Ministry of Planning and Development (MPD), and guided by the Mozambique Local State Organs Law (Law
n.8/2003, of May 19), the Government established participatory consultative councils at village, locality, administrative
post and district levels to collectively set local development priorities and jointly monitor implementation and results.
Under the leadership of the former National Directorate for Rural Development (DNPDR) at (MPD) and anchored under
the Rural Development Strategy (EDR), the Government implemented local development initiatives with the support of
community-based economic organizations. Despite their recognized contribution to local economic development and job
creation, promoting decentralized district planning, consultative councils and financing of local development initiatives,
these programs were discontinued in 2016. The Government is now reinstating these programs, building on the good
lessons of the past, and MPD will lead on their implementation.

9.     To support an integrated approach to infrastructure and basic service provision that tackles major gaps and
inequalities at provincial levels, the Government has strengthened the mandate of the Ministry of Public Works,
Housing and Water Resources (MOPHRH) and established National Public Works Administration (ANOP). Until recently,
construction of public facilities was directly led by each line ministry/sector, provinces and districts, while MOPHRH was
responsible for technical assistance and quality assurance for construction, as well as maintenance and rehabilitation of
public infrastructure. Moving forward, ANOP will take over this role across the country, with support of its provincial
delegations.

10.    The mandates of provincial decentralized entities are being adjusted to devolve powers and responsibilities to
elected Provincial Governors. This will include the reduction of powers and responsibilities of the Provincial Secretary of
State and return of all staff of provincial services (under the Secretary of State) to provincial directorates under the
Provincial Governor. These changes will enhance the Provincial Governor’s ability to lead local development initiatives.
This includes advocating for the identification and negotiation of community priorities, improving transparency and
accountability within local governments, and strengthening the capacity of provincial and district institutions to plan and
deliver public services efficiently. Additionally, these reforms aim to support job creation and expand economic

11.     The drive towards decentralization in Northern Mozambique is not only a governance reform but a critical
strategy for mitigating conflict risks and boosting equitable, inclusive, and transparent service delivery. Rural districts,
where infrastructure gaps are most severe and community-led initiatives often provide the bulk of essential services,
face heightened vulnerability due to weak state presence, poor coordination, and competition over scarce resources.
These conditions create fertile ground for grievances and social tensions. To counter this, the proposed project will
promote participatory planning and execution at the district and locality levels, ensuring communities have a voice in
prioritizing investments and building trust in local governance. At the same time, stronger coordination between
communities and provincial authorities is essential to guarantee a minimum level of service provision and equitable
resource allocation across rural and urban areas. Currently, provincial and district authorities have challenges in
delivering services to local communities, undermining planning and accountability. The proposed project will therefore
support mechanisms for inclusive planning, transparent information flows, and joint monitoring, enabling government
systems to function more effectively and fostering social cohesion as a foundation for resilience and stability.

12.     The current World Bank portfolio has successfully built the foundations to address social cohesion and close
some gaps in service delivery in most conflict-affected districts in Northern Mozambique. The Northern Mozambique
Rural Resilience Project (MozNorte) focuses on improving livelihoods and natural resource management through
community-driven development approaches, empowering local groups to identify priorities and implement small-scale
infrastructure and livelihood activities. The Northern Crisis Recovery Project (NCRP) has supported social cohesion,
livelihoods and investments in basic infrastructure services, fostering trust between communities and local authorities
while promoting gender and youth engagement through the creation of peacebuilding committees. These interventions
have begun to bridge divides between provincial and district administrations and the communities they serve,
demonstrating that locally led solutions combined with improved coordination and transparency are essential for
reducing grievances, strengthening resilience, and restoring confidence in state institutions in areas where conflict has
eroded the social fabric and disrupted basic services.

Relationship to CPF

13.     The proposed operation is aligned with the World Bank Group (WBG) Country Partnership Framework (CPF) for
Mozambique. The proposed project contributes to achievement of Outcome 1 (Improved macro-fiscal stability), by
improving effectiveness of IDA resources in social sectors towards improved socio-economic development, and to
achievement of Outcome 2 (A better skilled workforce), by financing community-led business initiatives and public
infrastructure to improve youth and women’s access to sustainable and inclusive business opportunities and jobs.
Moreover, the proposed operation will address drivers of fragility by enhancing accountability and transparency in
decision making processes, reinforcing both horizontal and vertical cohesion and intra and inter-group trust, while
supporting inclusive and equitable access to basic services and to economic opportunities, all of which will help to
strengthen the social contract.

14.     The operation is aligned with the World Bank Group Jobs Strategy. The proposed Project will finance economic
activities under the community grants window and large public infrastructure that present high potential for job
creation. The project will promote access to public services necessary to boost the local economy by prioritizing key

D. Concept Description

16.      The proposed project is the first phase of a programmatic approach conceived to allow scalability of Bank
support for northern Mozambique. This first phase will focus on addressing urgent needs for strengthening social
cohesion and livelihoods, while providing basic infrastructure and services to communities most affected by conflict and
fragility in the North. This operation will build local government capacity to engage in participatory planning processes
led by the Locality Consultative Councils that aim to deliver community and public infrastructure that improve
communities’ access to better public services, jobs and economic opportunities. Moreover, the proposed project helps
to reduce social tensions, by adopting a social cohesion approach rooted in lessons learned from the engagement in
northern Mozambique under the NCRP and MozNorte projects, and the successful experiences and outcomes of
decentralized planning and local economic development in Mozambique. Subsequent phases will leverage the
strengthened capacity of local governments to scale up activities across the northern provinces.

17.     Project beneficiaries will include: (a) local communities in targeted districts; (b) district and provincial
authorities; and (c) youth, women, IDPs, returnees and other vulnerable groups in targeted districts. The project will
cover safer areas in 22 districts (to be confirmed during preparation) in the provinces of Cabo Delgado, Nampula, and
Niassa. The project is expected to benefit around 1 million beneficiaries, through enhanced community inclusion and
access to spaces of participation, livelihoods and economic opportunities, increased capacity at local, district, and
national level, and delivery of resilient community and public infrastructure. At the local level the added value of the
project will be the targeted social cohesion interventions for peacebuilding and conflict recovery and the promotion of
community engagement by adopting participatory approaches to identify communities’ needs and priorities, bringing
communities together and reducing existing tensions over limited resources and assets, and lack of access for voice and
participation in community and district decision-making and planning process.

18.    Geographic targeting: The project will target all safer and accessible districts of Cabo Delgado, Nampula and
Niassa that form the buffer zone for conflict escalation towards western Niassa or southern Nampula (see Fig 1). Those
include 13 districts covered by NCRP and MozNorte, and three districts of eastern Niassa Province (Mecula, Marrupa and
Nipepe) and six northern districts of Nampula Province (Memba, Erati, Mecuburi, Malema, Ribuae and Muecate) that
have endured recent insurgents’ attacks or that were heavily affected by 2024 post-elections crisis. In each district, social
cohesion activities and community infrastructure works will target localities with the highest poverty rates, low access to
public services, and high exposure to natural hazards.

Project Components

19.      Component 1: Improved social cohesion through community level socio-economic integration and livelihood
development (US$37 million). The component will prioritize youth and women to increase their overall resilience to
fragility risks of exclusion, climate shocks, inequality, and radicalization. These activities will also promote citizen
engagement, enhance voice and agency of vulnerable and marginalized groups and facilitate communities to identify
their priorities to inform community level development planning processes. The component activities will be
implemented in an estimated 400 communities covering 12,000 households, within targeted localities to be confirmed
during preparation) selected based on an agreed and transparent criterion including poverty, scale of returnees and IDPs,
and climate vulnerability.

20.     Sub-component 1.1: Community mobilization for improved social cohesion (US$5 million). This subcomponent
uses culturally anchored social cohesion activities, GBV/psychosocial awareness, and participatory priority setting to
foster bridging ties across diverse groups (host communities, IDPs, returnees, women, youth) within each locality
targeted by the Project while also reinforcing bonding within cohorts formed through mobilization and collective action.
It will finance the following activities: (a) design and implement social cohesion activities integrating key sexual
reproductive health, mental health, and other locally identified topics using culturally appropriate activities; (b) create
and register community based-organizations; (c) deliver capacity building and awareness activities on GBV and
psychosocial support for trauma survivors; and (d) identify community priorities through a participatory approach. These
activities will be delivered through existing and established community structures (such as peace building committees,
village development organizations, women groups and youth groups) and will improve community interaction and
connectivity to foster a sense of trust. Experienced local civil society organizations (CSOs) and non-government
organizations (NGO) will be hired to support local governments in the implementation of activities under this
subcomponent.

21.    Sub-component 1.2: Community subgrants for socio-economic development initiatives (US$29 million). This

including construction and equipment of incubators. A service provider hired by National Directorate for Promotion of

management of their business plans. A community grants manual will be prepared and will adapt the eligibility criteria

22.    Sub-component 1.3: Building readiness of national, provincial and district institutions to lead decentralized

and manuals, to include gender, inclusion, and climate resilience; (ii) support the updating of DEL regulations, guidelines

decentralized planning and local economic development during the second phase of the Project.

component will address the challenges related to limited delivery of critical basic services, while improving capacity of

24.      Sub-component 2.1: Support for construction and rehabilitation of climate resilient community infrastructure

Implementation arrangements

28.     The Ministry of Planning and Development (MPD) will lead the overall Project Coordination (PC) through the
National Directorate for Promotion of Integrated Development. Implementation will be led by provincial and districts
authorities supported by central agencies under the Ministry of Planning and Development (MPD) and the Ministry of

implemented across three provinces, planning and prioritization of social cohesion activities and community
infrastructure will be conducted at locality level through consultative meetings of Locality Consultative Councils, and
aggregated at district level, and endorsed at provincial level. Planning and prioritization of construction or rehabilitation
of public infrastructure will be conducted at district level, aggregated at provincial level and endorsed at national level.

 Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts




1.   Environmental Risks
The environmental risk of the project is rated Substantial. Although the project will finance primarily small-scale
community and public infrastructure works—these activities will take place across a broad geographic area
affected by fragility and limited institutional presence. Potential environmental risks include soil disturbance,
erosion, improper disposal of construction debris and solid waste, localized air and noise emissions, interference
with natural drainage patterns, and risks related to material sourcing, particularly where contractors may rely
on unregulated borrow areas or quarries. In addition, the project may involve the rehabilitation or installation
of small water supply systems, which could generate risks related to water quality, wastewater management,
and resource efficiency. Occupational health and safety (OHS) risks are expected during community works and
small-scale construction activities, especially given the likely use of local labor, variable contractor capacity, and
existing gaps in OHS management across public works agencies. Community health and safety risks may arise
from increased traffic and movement of construction materials, inappropriate site demarcation, or insufficient
management of work areas in densely populated village environments. While the project is not expected to
support activities in protected areas or critical habitats, some subprojects may be located near wetlands,
seasonal streams, or modified natural habitats, requiring screening and mitigation measures under the ESMF.
Cumulative environmental impacts are expected to be limited due to the dispersed and small-scale nature of
investments. In view of these factors—small-scale but widespread works, moderate intrinsic environmental
impacts, weak institutional capacity, operational challenges in FCV settings, and the need to finalize ESF
instruments during implementation—the environmental risk is considered Substantial.

2. Social Risks
The social risk of the project is rated Substantial. The project will operate in areas deeply affected by fragility,
conflict, displacement, and weak state presence. While the project’s activities are anticipated to generate
important social benefits—including improved access to livelihoods, basic services, and community
infrastructure—the operating context presents significant challenges and heightened risks. Key social risks stem
from: (i) Labor risks, including the use of local labor in community works, uneven contractor capacity, and
potential gaps in enforcing labor management requirements, OHS practices, and worker GRM mechanisms; (ii)
SEA/SH risks, which are substantial in FCV settings characterized by displacement, weakened social protection
structures, and limited availability of survivor support services. Labor-intensive public works and interactions
between workers and communities may heighten these risks if not managed proactively; (iii) Community health
and safety risks, including localized safety concerns around worksites, increased movement of materials, and
potential exposure of communities—particularly children and women—to unsafe construction zones; (iv)
Exclusion risks, as vulnerable groups such as IDPs, returnees, unemployed youth, female-headed households,
and remote communities may face barriers in accessing project benefits or participating in planning processes,
especially given the uneven presence of local government and variable community governance structures; (v)
Security risks, resulting from ongoing insurgent activity in parts of Cabo Delgado and in select districts of
neighboring provinces of Nampula and Niassa. These may affect the ability to conduct consultations, maintain
project continuity, supervise activities, or ensure safe engagement with communities. At this time, land
acquisition needs have not been defined and thus ESS5 screening criteria and procedures will be included as
part of the ESMF.

3. Sexual Exploitation and Abuse/Sexual Harassment (SEA/SH) Risks
The SEA/SH risk for the project is rated Substantial. The project will be implemented in areas affected by
prolonged conflict, displacement, and weakened social protection systems, where pre-existing vulnerabilities
significantly heighten risks of sexual exploitation, abuse, and harassment. The presence of large numbers of
internally displaced persons (IDPs), returnees, and female-headed households—combined with high poverty
and limited access to services—creates conditions in which SEA/SH risks can be amplified. Although the project
will finance primarily small-scale community and public infrastructure and labor-intensive public works, these
activities involve close interaction between workers and community members, including young women and
adolescent girls, which can increase exposure to SEA/SH if not properly managed. Contractors may recruit
temporary or unskilled labor locally, and in some FCV districts, labor supervision capacity is limited. Where
access is constrained by insecurity, oversight of worker behavior and code-of-conduct compliance may be
further challenged. Availability of survivor-centered services (medical, psychosocial, legal, and shelter support)
is limited or uneven across the 22 targeted districts. Referral pathways are fragmented, and coordination among
service providers is often disrupted by insecurity. These factors increase the importance of strong preventive
and response measures, simplified and confidential reporting channels, and close coordination with specialized
NGOs operating in the region. Institutional capacity for SEA/SH risk management within MPD/DNPDI and
MOPHRH/ANOP is evolving, and experience implementing SEA/SH mitigation under ESF operations is limited.
The project will prepare a SEA/SH Action Plan in early implementation, and reinforce the PIUs through a
dedicated GBV specialist, training for government and district teams, and integration of SEA/SH requirements
in bidding documents and supervision arrangements.
