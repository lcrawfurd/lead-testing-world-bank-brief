 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT


1.     This Project Appraisal Document (PAD) covers Phase III of the Accelerating Access to Water, Sanitation And
Hygiene Services in the Eastern and Southern Africa Region Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) (P512763), with
an overall IDA/IBRD financing envelope of US$ 1,580 million, approved by the World Bank’s Executive Directors on
September 30, 2025.

 A. Country Context

2.       The Republic of Burundi is a low-income country whose economic growth is recovering. As of 2024, 74.2 percent
of the population lives below the international poverty line of US$3 per day, 1 and 75 percent live in multidimensional
poverty. 2 Burundi is one of the most densely populated countries in the world, with a population density of 442 people
per square kilometer, exacerbating pressure on arable land. It ranks near the bottom for all global measures of human
capital, with a Human Development Index of 0.44, making it one of the least developed nations. 3 Gender disparities remain
high in Burundi, ranking low on the Gender Inequality Index, placing it 132 out of 172 countries. 4
3.       The country has an undiversified economy heavily reliant on public services and agriculture.5 It’s
multidimensional fragility results in political and economic risks. 6 The political crisis of 2015 halted 11 years of growth,
and Burundi’s gross domestic product (GDP) per capita—declining over 2016–2024—was only US$154, 7 roughly one-tenth
of the Sub-Saharan Africa average (US$1,441). 8 A peaceful transfer of power in June 2020 marked a turn toward peace
and stability, but challenges remain. Although Burundi’s near-term outlook has improved, with real GDP growth projected
at 4.6 percent in 2025, macroeconomic pressures from foreign exchange shortages, fuel distribution constraints, and fiscal
monetization persist. The influx of refugees from the Democratic Republic of Congo into Burundi contributes to economic
pressure.
4.       Burundi remains largely rural, with one quarter of the total resident population of 12.3 million people living in
urban areas. 9 However, it is one of the fastest-growing urban populations, growing over 5 percent per year in the last two
decades. Approximately 58 percent of the urban population lives in unplanned areas, often under-resourced, exposed to
disaster risks, and without adequate basic services. Bujumbura, the largest city and economic capital, plays a key role in
supporting the economic drivers of the country 10. Gitega, the country’s political capital and second-largest city, faces a
similar rapid urbanization process.
5.       Burundi has embarked upon a territorial reform that took effect after the June 2025 municipal and
parliamentary elections. The administrative set-up has been consolidated from 18 provinces and 119 communes to five
provinces and 42 communes with enlarged competencies. The aim is to streamline governance, improve resource
allocation at the local level, and enhance service delivery, though such reforms may take several years to mature.

6.      While Burundi has the lowest per capita greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions globally, climate change poses a
serious threat to its weak infrastructure and livelihoods. Changes in rainfall patterns have led to increased frequency of
extreme weather events such as floods, storms, landslides, and prolonged dry spells, which threaten food security,
especially in the northeastern part of the country. Water levels in Lake Tanganyika surged to their highest level in 60 years
due to heavy rains in early 2024, forcing over 230,000 people to relocate. The existing pumping station that provides 90
percent of the water supply to the city of Bujumbura was under threat of inundation during the 2024 floods, and part of
the existing water distribution network was damaged during flash floods in October 2024.


 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
7.       Despite the country’s considerable water endowments, a lack of investment and poor governance have led to
widespread water insecurity. 11 Over the past decade, access to water supply, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) services
barely saw improvement, with population growth absorbing gains. As of 2024, only 65 percent of the population has
access to basic water supply (urban: 91 percent; rural: 61 percent), and 45 percent has access to basic sanitation (urban:
42 percent; rural: 45 percent). 12 Achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) for universal access by 2030
requires acceleration rates of seven to ten times the rate of progress in the 2010–2020 period. In urban areas, only 58
percent of the population has access to water on premises, and only 53 percent receive a regular daily water supply. 13 In
the cities of Bujumbura and Gitega, water production capacity is insufficient, with water rotations to different quarters
every few days. In rural areas, only 3 percent of the population has access to water on premises. Only 2 percent of the
urban population is connected to a sewer, and even then, sewage treatment is not guaranteed.
8.      Lack of WASH services disproportionately affects women and girls, with 78 percent of women responsible for
water collection versus 17 percent of men 14. This highlights a deeply rooted inequality in the distribution of WASH-related
domestic labor, with 40 percent of women fetching water for longer than 30 minutes per trip, exposing them to risks of
gender-based violence (GBV). 15 Poor WASH services are a main contributor to diarrheal disease, the leading cause of child
mortality in Sub-Saharan Africa. 16 Moreover, drought further exacerbates the time burden on women and girls, compared
to boys and men. 17 Access to water and sanitation in schools is about 45 percent, 18 and 18 percent of schools have water
and soap, negatively affecting school attendance, especially of girls. Malnutrition, linked to poor WASH, stands at 53
percent. 19

11.     A key bottleneck for the sustainability of the sector is the lack of cost-reflective tariffs, both in urban and rural
areas. Since 2021, fee collection at public standpipes has been halted, causing financial strain and the dismantling of rural
community operators, leading to the collapse of services. 23 The DGRP-EPA has drafted a tariff policy 24 that will guide the
Authority for Regulation of Water and Energy Sectors (Agence de Régulation des Secteurs de l’Energie et de l’Eau Potable,
AREEN) to develop instruments for tariff setting and review. The rural arm of MRMEICT is the Rural Water Supply and
Sanitation Agency (Agence de l’Hydraulique et de l’Assainissement en Milieu Rural, AHAMR), responsible for the
construction and rehabilitation of rural water systems; implementation of sanitation activities, using the Community-Led
Total Sanitation (CLTS) approach; and support to communes and water operators. Going forward, it is critical that by-laws
and national guidelines for service delegation contracts clearly define responsibilities for operation and maintenance
(O&M), capital maintenance, and rehabilitation, and that adequate financing mechanisms are put in place to guarantee
sustainability.

 C. Project Beneficiaries

34.      The beneficiaries are people provided with access to water supply, including basic water services through public

household connections for low-income households. In addition, the project will educate 6,000 girls on menstrual health
and hygiene.

operators are beneficiaries of an incubation, mentoring, and a skills-building program to support their professionalization.

 D. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners

36.     The World Bank’s engagement is grounded in its comparative advantage in supporting complex, infrastructure
programs in FCV and low-capacity environments. The World Bank brings extensive global and regional experience in
designing and implementing WASH programs, particularly in contexts that require institutional and governance reforms.
Burundi will benefit extensively from Phase I of the regional MPA (P506439), implemented by AUDA-NEPAD. AUDA-NEPAD
will engage global and regional knowledge partners to deliver TA, analytics, and peer-to-peer exchanges through a learning
agenda tailored to the Burundian context. Hence, the World Bank’s involvement is critical to assist the GoB with knowledge
to (a) advance institutional reforms and regulatory competencies; (b) professionalize rural water supply management
models; (c) lift utility performance through energy efficiency, digitalization, and NRW measures; (d) prepare hybrid PPPs
in the medium term; and (e) integrate climate adaptation and mitigation measures in infrastructure design and operations.
37.     The World Bank is an active participant in the WASH sectoral coordination group, which ensures alignment of

and leverage the efforts of key development partners active in Burundi’s WASH sector, specifically the EU, UNICEF, AfDB,

improving the legislative framework (EU and AfDB); supporting the preparation of the national WASH program and the
sanitation roadmap (UNICEF); and coordinating investments and support for REGIDESO (KfW).

 E. Lessons Learned and Progress on Learning Agenda

38.     The project design reﬂects lessons drawn from the World Bank’s global and regional experience in the WASH
sector, particularly in FCV contexts, informing the scope, implementation arrangements, and risk mitigation measures.
Key lessons learned include the following:
         •      Institutional capacity and reform require long-term engagement. Experience from previous World Bank-
                ﬁnanced operations in Burundi and similar contexts has shown that institutional reform and capacity building
                are gradual processes. The project incorporates sustained TA and phased implementation to support the
                operationalization of the new WSS law and strengthen regulatory oversight and tariﬀ review procedures
                (AREEN).
         •      Performance-based management enhances sustainability. The use of service delegation contracts in rural
                areas and a PIP for REGIDESO is informed by successful models in other countries. These approaches aim to
                improve service quality, ﬁnancial sustainability, and accountability through KPIs and incentives. The project
                supports the regular monitoring of KPI achievement under the PIP, while ensuring engagement of citizens.
         •      Sustaining rural water services through domestic private operators. Global experience shows that
                community-based management models have a high risk of sustainability and that the private sector—under
                the right enabling conditions—brings impetus for professionalization and higher service levels. 41 The project
                incorporates lessons from Sub-Saharan Africa on service delegation and the setting up of capital
                maintenance funds to ensure better asset management.
         •      Private sector engagement through blended ﬁnance. Lessons from hybrid-PPPs in the region highlight the
                importance of cost-reﬂective tariﬀs for the public oﬀ-taker, viability gap funding, transaction advisory
                support, and other de-risking instruments to attract private investment. The project supports preparatory
                studies for a hybrid PPP for Gitega and/or other urban centers to be ﬁnanced under future phases.

Economic Assessment
54.     An economic analysis has been carried out to assess the economic viability of the project, and the results
support the robustness of the project investments. The economic evaluation considers activities that generate benefits
for which an economic value can be quantified. The project will reduce vulnerability to public health risks among rural and
urban residents by improving access to water supply and reducing fecal exposure. The economic analysis estimates the
non-monetary, financial, and economic costs of poor access to WSS services in comparison to the benefits derived from
project activities. It includes (a) project costs under Components 2 and 3, including estimated replacement and O&M costs,
and (b) all measurable benefits, including benefits from reduced time spent collecting water and accessing sanitation and
reduction in incidence of associated diseases. Other non-quantifiable benefits include improved health and well-being and
increased safety and dignity, specifically for women and girls.
55.      Although the dual exchange rate regime reduces the project’s financial efficiency, its overall economic viability
remains strong. 50 The efficiency analysis, using a discount rate of 6 percent, yields a robust net present value (NPV) of BIF
1,980,013 million (US$669 million equivalent), an economic internal rate of return (EIRR) of 99.5 percent, and a benefit-
cost ratio of 35.2—indicating substantial economic gains for Burundi. However, the persistent gap between the official
and parallel exchange rates—estimated at more than a 150 percent premium in October 2025—distorts financial
efficiency by lowering the real value of imported inputs financed at the overvalued official rate. To account for these
distortions, a counterfactual simulation incorporating the observed exchange rate premium was conducted. The adjusted

consistent with Burundi’s Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) and National Adaptation Plan (NAP). The NAP
identifies flooding, changes in river morphology, loss of water bodies, drought and low flows, and intense rainfall as areas
of climate-related hazards. Burundi does not have a Long-Term Low Emissions Development Strategy. On mitigation, the
project supports the NDC and NAP objectives and priorities by promoting the use of renewable energy (for example, solar-
powered pumping), reducing methane emissions from unimproved sanitation, and improving energy efficiency measures
through NRW measures and targeted energy improvements of the existing pumping stations and water treatment plants.
Diesel-powered pumps will not be used as primary sources of pumping unless alternatives are not technically or financially
feasible. As discussed in the context sections, Burundi is highly vulnerable to climate change-exacerbated floods and
droughts. Floods put WSS infrastructure at risk of inundation, including the mixing of untreated fecal sludge with
floodwaters, increasing exposure to waterborne disease. Droughts can cause water supply systems to fail due to lack of
water supply. Project activities enhance climate change adaptation and resilience of water infrastructure to climate risks
through the development of climate-resilient water supply infrastructure for both urban and rural areas, which are
designed to withstand floods, droughts, and landslides, with solar-powered pumping and gravity-fed systems prioritized
in rural areas. WSS infrastructure will include design elements to reduce risks from floods and droughts, such as avoiding
building key components in floodplains or areas with frequent flooding from shallow groundwater and considering
multiple water supply sources. 54 Thus, the climate hazards have been reduced to an acceptable level and are not likely to
have a material impact on the PDOs.

Gender
75.      The project will address gender disparities by reducing the time women and girls spend collecting water.
Currently, 78 percent of Burundian households rely on women and girls as the primary collectors of water, with some
spending up to two hours daily, 57 especially in rural communes where water access is extremely low. Providing basic access
can cut this time burden to 30 minutes, boosting education and productivity. First, the project will increase the number
of women who are able to access water from an improved source within 30 minutes or less, with a target of 240,000
women/girls 58 (gender indicator), corroborated through representative user-based surveys. This would be achieved
through investments in new private connections and public standposts, consulting women on the functioning and location
of public standposts and improving feedback on reliability of standpost/water services. Second, the project will educate
6,000 girls on menstrual hygiene management, removing stigma and increasing girls’ school attendance during their
periods. Moreover, increasing sanitation access in rural areas will reduce the risks of GBV for women and girls during open
defecation. Third, the project will create economic opportunities through skills development, targeting male and female
staff of private water operators. REGIDESO will benefit from TA to further advance female employment in technical and
leadership positions, through the Equal Aqua 59 initiative.

 VI. KEY RISKS


78.     The residual risk rating for the project is Substantial. Several mitigation measures are integrated into the project
design and complementary TA is provided during implementation.
79.      Political and Governance risk is Substantial. Although corruption and transparency risks persist, the country’s
governance environment is marked by a gradually stabilizing political context, the consolidation of commune
administrations following the local elections in June 2025, and the early stages towards fiscal decentralization. Therefore,
the project places significant emphasis on providing TA to the new commune administrations, particularly in relation to
their responsibilities for awarding affermage contracts and promoting community engagement at the local level in
Bujumbura. Investments in new infrastructure will be carefully supported by communication campaigns around necessary
tariff adjustments to ensure long-term sustainability.
80.      Macroeconomic risk is High. Public debt remains sustainable, but the country faces a high risk of debt distress
due to limited concessional financing and external imbalances. Persistent fuel shortages and exchange rate misalignment
threaten growth prospects, potentially reducing export coverage, fueling inflation, and widening fiscal deficits—with
additional pressures stemming from refugee inflows. Forex exchange controls have raised administrative costs for
exporters, encouraged capital flight, and posed risks for project implementation. Mitigating factors include ongoing efforts
by the Government, in dialogue with the International Monetary Fund, to consolidate fiscal accounts, modernize monetary
policy, address exchange rate disparities and further liberalize the foreign exchange market. The project incorporates price
contingencies to mitigate cost escalations. The project leverages the GoB’s contribution—made available in Burundian
franc before the commencement of civil works for the treatment plant—toward the utilization of local materials and labor.
Moreover, the project has accounted for the disparities of the exchange rate market as part of the economic analysis.

and long-term sustainability. Additionally, recent decentralization reforms may strain local governments’ capacity for
