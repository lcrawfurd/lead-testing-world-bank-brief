                           B. Introduction and Context

                           Country Context

                          Addressing rural poverty in Mexico is essential for equitable and sustainable growth. Poverty is almost double for rural
                          population and households whose head works in agriculture. They also endure more chronic poverty, which hints at a
                          more entrenched problem to solve. These groups represent almost 40 percent of the extreme poor, so poverty eradication
                          cannot happen without a substantive change in living conditions of rural/agricultural households. Despite notable
                          reductions over the past two decades, rural areas continue to face vulnerability to falling back into poverty, limited access
                          to basic services, and deeper social deprivations—particularly in health, education, and housing. These challenges are
                          compounded by a high reliance on low-productivity agriculture, and heightened exposure to climate hazards. Also, around
                          20 percent of the rural population is indigenous, who double the poverty rates compared to non-indigenous populations.
                          This Operation aims to promote social inclusion and contribute to poverty reduction by prioritizing interventions in rural
                          landscapes that not only preserve and sustainably manage forests—strengthening the adaptive capacity of local

                          communities—but also contribute to income generation of rural populations through a benefit sharing plan under the
                          Program.

                          The country's efforts to tackle rural poverty are deeply intertwined with its climate change agenda, notably its
                          commitment to achieve net-zero deforestation by 2030 under the 2022–2030 Nationally Determined Contribution
                          (NDC). Mexico's approach prioritizes the most excluded groups, particularly underserved rural populations, through
                          targeted interventions and programs designed to improve their quality of life. By promoting sustainable forest
                          management and integrated landscape approaches, Mexico is strengthening its adaptive capacity against natural hazards
                          and climate-induced hydro-meteorological phenomena. Forests play a crucial role in achieving these mitigation and
                          adaptation goals, with broader landscape activities, including improved management in grasslands, and silvopastoral and
                          agroforestry systems, contributing significantly to reducing emissions and enhancing carbon stocks.

                           Sectoral and Institutional Context

                          Mexico's diverse ecosystems, encompassing vast forestlands, play a critical role in the country's environmental and
                          economic landscape. These vital resources face significant threats from deforestation and forest degradation, driven by
                          factors such as agricultural expansion, illegal logging, and unsustainable land-use practices. The Land Use, Land-Use




_For Official Use Only_
                          Change, and Forestry (LULUCF) sector is therefore central to Mexico's climate change mitigation and adaptation efforts,
                          as recognized in its Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). Effective management of this sector requires a multi-
                          faceted approach that integrates sustainable forest management, agroforestry, and silvopastoral systems to enhance
                          carbon stocks and reduce emissions, while also addressing the socio-economic needs of forest-dependent communities.

                          The National Forestry Commission (CONAFOR) stands as a key institution in addressing these challenges, with over two
                          decades of experience in promoting sustainable forest management and conservation through various incentive
                          programs. These programs, demand-driven and voluntary, channel resources to ejidos, communities, and small
                          landowners, emphasizing social organization, capacity building, and land-use planning, particularly in Indigenous Peoples
                          and Local Communities (IP&LC) owned lands. While CONAFOR's budget has seen fluctuations, its established operational
                          rules and long-standing collaboration with the World Bank provide a robust framework for implementing forest-related
                          initiatives. The Ministry of Environment and Natural Resources (SEMARNAT) provides overarching policy leadership and
                          coordination to align forestry actions with Mexico’s broader environmental and climate priorities.

                           C. Proposed Development Objective(s)


                           Development Objective(s) (From PAD)
                           The program will generate payments for measured, reported, and verified Emission Reductions (ER) from the Land
                           Use, Land Use Change, and Forestry (LULUCF) sector in the jurisdiction and will distribute ER payments in accordance
                           with an agreed benefit sharing plan.

                            Key Results
                          The program is designed to generate measurable, reported, and verified emission reductions from the Land Use, Land
                          Use Change, and Forestry (LULUCF) sector. Resulting payments will be distributed transparently through an agreed
                          Benefit Sharing Plan (BSP), ensuring that local communities receive direct benefits.

                          Emission reductions will result from activities supported through CONAFOR’s public incentive programs (ROP), where
                          communities and Indigenous Peoples implement sustainable forest management measures that help halt deforestation
                          and forest degradation under an integrated land management approach.

                          Benefit sharing of the emission reduction payments will be channeled through a Jurisdiction-Specific Demand-Driven
                          Program , known in Spanish as Lineamientos Especiales. Participation will be voluntary, with particular attention to
                          removing barriers that prevent women and youth from accessing government support in the forest sector.

                          The BSP will be finalized through participatory consultations to ensure that local interests and priorities are reflected in
                          the Lineamientos Especiales. Benefits—both monetary and non-monetary—will be allocated to eligible individuals and
                          groups. These consultations will guide the final BSP design and ensure transparent, equitable distribution of funds.

                          The BSP functions as an incentive-based framework for emission reductions, promoting diversity, equity, and inclusion.
                          The BSP will strengthen the jurisdiction’s emission reduction model and complement the ROP, with an emphasis on the
                          inclusion of vulnerable groups. It will define eligibility criteria, types of support, and mechanisms for transparency and
                          oversight. Given limited BSP resources, the program will prioritize vulnerable groups and reinforce territorial approaches
                          based on demand, eligibility, and stakeholder consultations—aligning with and enhancing the objectives of the ROP.

                            D. Project Description

                          The Mexico BioCarbon Emission Reduction Program is a results-based payment operation designed to advance Mexico’s
                          climate change commitments. The program provides financial incentives for the Government of Mexico, through
                          CONAFOR, to implement inclusive and sustainable forest management activities. These efforts are intended to reduce
                          emissions from the Land Use, Land Use Change, and Forestry (LULUCF) sector and foster climate resilience. The Program
                          will be implemented across four states comprising the Program’s jurisdiction and accounting for 30 percent of the
                          country’s total area and 23 percent of its national forestlands. Social inclusion is a core priority, with special attention
                          given to Indigenous Peoples, women, and those without land tenure, ensuring equitable distribution of benefits through
                          a transparent benefit-sharing plan.

                          The program is organized around three main components: Landscape Investments, Emission Reductions payments
                          distributed in accordance with an agreed Benefit Sharing Plan (BSP), and Program Operating and Coordination Costs.
                          Landscape Investments, partially funded by a US$3.6 million grant from the BioCarbon Fund Plus Initiative, support
                          activities that generate emission reductions and strengthen sustainable forest value chains. The Emission Reductions
                          Payments component, with US$18 million committed by the World Bank, will disburse funds for verified emission
                          reductions generated from the LULUCF sector, following the agreed BSP. The third component covers operational and
                          coordination costs, including monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) systems, stakeholder engagement, and
                          environmental and social management.

                          This programmatic approach begins with targeted LULUCF interventions and is designed to target enabling factors that
                          would support a transition to the broader Agriculture, Forestry, and Other Land Use (AFOLU) sector. The program
                          leverages CONAFOR’s extensive experience and established systems, building on more than two decades of collaboration
                          between the Government of Mexico and the World Bank in the forest sector. It is aligned with Mexico’s National

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts




                          The overall Enviromental and Social (E&S) risk is rated as substantial: moderate for environmental risks and substantial
                          for social risks. The ERP, focusing on forest conservation and sustainability through landscape-level investments, aims to
                          address deforestation and forest degradation through activities that generate largely positive environmental benefits. To
                          minimize the risk of lack of understanding of the ERP by local communities, the finalization of the BSP will involve potential




_For Official Use Only_
                          beneficiaries through a strong consultation and participatory process to determine the distribution of benefits derived
                          from the ER. Both underlying investments and the BSP will benefit from a forest sector gender and social inclusion strategy
                          already developed with support of the WB. A WB directive for the environmental and social management of ERPs was
                          issued in December 2024 to clarify the scope and requirements for managing these programs in accordance with the
                          Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) and the Bank’s earlier safeguards policies. The directive emphasizes a systems
                          approach to managing E&S risks, and mandates that CONAFOR implements a robust environmental and social
                          management system (ESMS). It outlines detailed processes for E&S assessments, monitoring and reporting, including
                          periodic self-reporting by CONAFOR and third-party monitoring. Additionally, the directive applies to BSP, ensuring the
                          fair and inclusive distribution of benefits in alignment with the ESF. Environmental and social management for the ERP
                          will build on the systems established for the WB-supported project Strengthening Entrepreneurship in Productive Forest
                          Landscapes (PROFOEM; P164661), implemented by CONAFOR. During six years of implementation, the PROFOEM project
                          satisfactorily applied the WB’s safeguards operational policies and developed required management instruments.To
                          comply with the Bank’s 2024 Directive on Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts for the ERPs under the
                          Forest Carbon Partnership Facility and the BioCarbon Fund Initiative for Sustainable Forest Landscapes, particularly the
                          requirement to have an ESMS to identify, manage, and monitor E&S risks associated with the ERP, CONAFOR will build
                          upon its existing legal, institutional, and operational E&S arrangements and infrastructure. These arrangements have been
                          institutionalized through CONAFOR’s ROP and have been progressively strengthened over the past decade through its
                          engagement with the World Bank in forestry-related operations and the application of the Bank’s environmental and
                          social safeguard policies. The ERP will include a retroactive period, in line with ISFL program provisions and subject to the
                          ERPA negotiations. Potential retroactive ERP activities are expected to comply with the Bank’s safeguard policies, as they
                          have been implemented in accordance with CONAFOR’s Operational Rules during 2019-2025, including E&S management
                          considerations. Nevertheless, the task team will carry out an E&S audit for the retroactive period in line with the above-
                          mentioned directive and the ESF. CONAFOR’s ESMS—applied to both the underlying activities of the ERP and the BSP—
                          will comprise Mexico’s environmental and social legislation and its regulations, CONAFOR’s Operational Rules, institutional
                          manuals, and additional measures designed to address any gaps between CONAFOR’s current framework and the
                          requirements of the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF). The ESMS will also establish detailed
                          monitoring and reporting processes, including periodic self-reporting by program entities, third-party monitoring
                          requirements in accordance with the Bank’s Directive, an exclusion list to prevent activities with significant adverse E&S

                           E. Implementation

                           Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

                          Institutional and implementation arrangements for the Mexico BioCarbon Emission Reduction Program center on the




_For Official Use Only_
                          Ministry of Environment and Natural Resources (SEMARNAT), which is responsible for signing the Emission Reductions
                          Purchase Agreement and transferring ER titles. The National Forestry Commission (CONAFOR) will oversee the
                          management and implementation of the program, including MRV activities, benefit distribution through the Mexican
                          Forest Fund (FFM) in line with the Benefit Sharing Plan, and coordination of private sector grants. CONAFOR also acts as
                          the recipient and implementing agency for the BioCarbon Fund Grant, managing procurement and ensuring compliance.
                          The program leverages CONAFOR’s established structures for cross-sectoral coordination, with the FFM functioning as the
                          transparent financial instrument for receiving and transferring results-based payments.
