                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The project has a national scope, with interventions concentrated in southern governorates of Sfax, Tozeur, Kebili,
                     Gabès, Médenine, and Tataouine. These areas face acute water scarcity, climate-related drought vulnerability, and

                     growing urban and industrial water demand pressures. According to the 2024 census (INS), the governorate of Sfax has
                     1,047,468 inhabitants, of which 89.3% of 304,562 households are connected to the potable water network. Tozeur and
                     Kebili represent together about 250,000 inhabitants, with similar access rates to the water network but lower service
                     continuity due to aging infrastructure and water stress.
                     Key environmental settings include coastal zones and arid inland areas, characterized by fragile aquifers, high
                     evapotranspiration, and salinization pressures. Project interventions will take place primarily in dense urban and peri-
                     urban areas of Sfax, Tozeur, and Kebili, where potable water distribution networks will be restructured and
                     rehabilitated, and in southern coastal and desert regions (Gabès, Médenine, Tataouine) supplied by the Zarat seawater
                     desalination plant and transmission systems.
                     Urban network rehabilitation works are expected to be implemented through District Metered Areas (DMAs) and will
                     involve trenching, pipe replacement, and reconnections along existing streets. In Sfax, preliminary intervention zones
                     include central and peri-central neighborhoods, some of which are located near sensitive receptors such as local
                     markets, schools, health centers, major transport corridors, and the Sfax Medina. In Tozeur and Kebili, works are
                     expected mainly within urban cores and oasis-adjacent neighborhoods, where narrow streets, mixed residential–
                     commercial uses, and proximity to traditional housing increase sensitivity to construction disturbances.
                     While the expansion of the Zarat seawater desalination plant from 50,000 to 100,000 m³/day will be carried out entirely
                     within the existing facility footprint, the marine environment of the Gulf of Gabès remains environmentally sensitive,
                     and incremental risks associated with increased brine discharge volumes and energy demand.

                     and cultural contexts. Poverty rate in the South regions tend to be higher than the national average (16.6%), but rates
                     vary across the six southern governorates: Sfax has a much lower rate (5.8–6.3%), while Kebili has the highest poverty

                     II. SUMMARY OF ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL (ES) RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                         Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                  Substantial
                     The environmental risk rating is assessed as Substantial, reflecting the nature and scale of the planned physical
                     interventions, the geographic spread of activities across urban, coastal, and semi-arid environments, and the limited
                     institutional capacity of the implementing agency to fully meet ESF requirements. The Project entails inherently
                     significant environmental risks due to its technical complexity, nationwide footprint, and the cumulative nature of its
                     investments. The Project involves significant physical works of varying scale across urban and semi-arid regions,
                     including (i) rehabilitation and restructuring of potable water adduction and distribution networks; (ii) construction of
                     water transmission infrastructure linking production and consumption centers; (iii) installation of approximately
                     100,000 electronic smart meters; and (iv) the capacity expansion of the existing Zarat seawater desalination plant
                     within its original design footprint. The desalination component consists of an incremental capacity expansion of the
                     Zarat plant from 50,000 to 100,000 m³/day, achieved through the installation of additional electromechanical
                     equipment (reverse osmosis units, pumps, filters, transformers, and energy-recovery devices) within the existing
                     facility footprint. No major civil works are required, as the seawater intake, buildings, and brine discharge
                     infrastructure were originally constructed to accommodate the ultimate capacity. While this substantially reduces


                    Feb 04, 2026                                                                                                      Page 4 of 15
                              The World Bank

                     risks related to physical disturbance of terrestrial or marine habitats, operational environmental risks remain, notably
                     increased brine discharge volumes, higher electricity demand, handling and storage of treatment chemicals,
                     membrane-cleaning effluents, and associated waste streams. The construction and rehabilitation of water
                     transmission infrastructure present additional environmental risks, including soil disturbance and erosion along linear
                     corridors, temporary occupation of land, spoil generation, dust and noise emissions, traffic-related impacts, and
                     potential interference with existing utilities. In densely populated urban areas such as Sfax, rehabilitation of existing
                     networks may also result in temporary service interruptions, sediment transport, accidental leaks, and localized
                     flooding risks. In semi-arid areas (notably Tozeur and Kebili), works may intersect fragile soils exposed to erosion and
                     windblown dust, requiring context-specific mitigation measures. The Project’s nationwide footprint increases the
                     complexity of environmental risk management, particularly in relation to contractor performance variability, waste
                     handling practices, and consistent enforcement of pollution prevention and OHS standards across multiple
                     governorates. The installation of electronic smart meters introduces additional environmental risks related to
                     occupational safety during installation, handling of battery-powered devices, and the generation of electronic waste.
                     These risks require structured procedures for collection, temporary storage, transport, and disposal in line with
                     national waste regulations and ESS3 requirements.

                      commitment to avoid involuntary resettlement by exploring viable project alternatives, to prevent forced evictions,
                      to mitigate negative social and economic impacts by ensuring compensation at full replacement cost and supporting
                      the restoration of livelihoods and living standards. This process should be evidence-based and context-specific,
                      requiring detailed socio-economic surveys and asset inventories to establish a baseline of affected persons, including
                      those with formal, informal, or customary land rights, and mapping the spatial distribution of affected households,
                      businesses, and community facilities to clarify the scale and nature of economic displacement. Particular attention is
                      given to assessing the degree of vulnerability among affected populations, with a focus on those at heightened risk of
                      impoverishment or marginalization. Accessible and transparent GM is required to enable vulnerable persons to safely
                      raise concerns or seek redress.

                      ESS8 - Cultural Heritage                                                                                         Relevant

                      ESS8 is relevant given that several Project components involve excavation, trenching, and installation of water
                      network and transmission infrastructure in southern governorates of Tunisia, which are characterized by a rich
                      cultural heritage legacy. These areas include archaeological remains, historic urban fabrics, oasis systems, traditional
                      irrigation structures, and culturally significant landscapes, as well as intangible cultural heritage linked to local
                      practices, crafts, and community life. Activities related to the capacity expansion of the Zarat desalination plant are
                      confined to the existing operational footprint and involve only the installation of electromechanical equipment and
                      reverse osmosis units. As no new excavation or ground disturbance is envisaged, cultural heritage risks for the Zarat
                      desalination plant expansion are considered negligible, and no specific cultural heritage screening is required under
                      the Zarat capacity-expansion–specific ESMP. For other Project components involving civil works—namely the
                      rehabilitation of potable water distribution networks and the construction of adduction and transmission pipelines in
                      Sfax, Tozeur, Kebili, Gabès, Médenine, and Tataouine-cultural heritage risks will be managed through the ESMF. The
                      ESMF establishes procedures for early screening of works to identify potential interactions with known or unknown
                      cultural heritage resources, including archaeological sites, historic structures, oasis landscapes, and traditional water-
                      related features. Although no legally protected cultural heritage sites have been identified along preliminary
                      alignments, the potential for chance discoveries remains, particularly in desert and oasis contexts. Accordingly, a
                      Chance Finds Procedure consistent with ESS8 will be included in all bidding documents, works contracts, and site-



Public Disclosure
                      specific ESMPs. This procedure will require immediate suspension of works, securing of the site, notification of the
                      relevant national authorities (INP), and implementation of agreed measures prior to resumption of activities. Where
                      screening identifies potential risks to tangible or intangible cultural heritage, including indirect impacts on cultural
                      heritage entailing historic monuments and aeras that may be brought about by the use of heavy machinery and
                      engines, site-specific ESMPs will include proportionate mitigation measures, prioritizing avoidance, and, where
                      necessary, documentation, consultation with competent authorities and affected communities, and culturally
                      appropriate management measures.
