1. Project Description
1.1 Country/Sector Context
Sri Lanka, a lower-middle-income country, has faced significant political and economic challenges in recent
years. Following the 2019 Easter Sunday attacks, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the 2022 financial crisis, the
economy contracted sharply, leading to severe disruptions in livelihoods, public services, and private
sector activities. Despite these setbacks, the government has embarked on an ambitious program of
recovery and reform aimed at restoring macroeconomic stability, improving governance, strengthening
social protection systems, and promoting long-term, inclusive growth.

The Northern and Eastern Provinces, once severely affected by the decades-long civil conflict, continue to
grapple with socio-economic vulnerabilities, infrastructure deficits, and limited access to quality public
services. Revitalizing these regions is essential for achieving balanced national development, fostering
reconciliation, and addressing poverty. Recent national priorities, including the 2026 Budget and the
National Physical Planning Policy and Plan (2017–2050), emphasize accelerated development in these
lagging regions, particularly through climate-resilient infrastructure, regional connectivity, and sector-
driven economic revitalization.

In this context, the World Bank, in partnership with the Government of Sri Lanka, is formulating a new
project — provisionally referred to as the " Regional Empowerment through Vibrant, Inclusive, and Viable
Economies (REVIVE)", a multi-sector investment program— aimed at supporting integrated regional
development, enhancing resilience, and improving livelihoods in the Northern and Eastern Provinces. The
project is designed to unlock the economic potential of these provinces by focusing on sectoral
development, infrastructure improvement, and the promotion of private sector participation. REVIVE
SOP1 focuses on strengthening enabling infrastructure in selected high-potential clusters, improving value
chains in tourism and fisheries, and building institutional capacities to catalyze regional economic
development and private sector participation.

Sri Lanka, like many countries, faces regional disparities in economic growth, particularly between its
western and other provinces. The Northern and Eastern Provinces, rich in natural resources and cultural
heritage, possess significant untapped potential. By strategically targeting key sectors such as tourism,
fisheries, and industries, this program seeks to foster local economic growth, enhance livelihoods, and
reduce regional inequalities.

While Sri Lanka’s post-conflict recovery has led to notable improvements in infrastructure and socio-
economic conditions, the Northern and Eastern Provinces still face challenges in achieving sustainable,
inclusive development. This project builds on existing provincial development plans, strengthening
climate-resilient infrastructure, supporting priority value chains (tourism and fisheries), and enabling
coordinated regional planning to address critical constraints. The project will be implemented through a
collaborative approach involving local and national government bodies, private sector stakeholders, and
civil society organizations to ensure balanced, sustainable, and community-driven outcomes.

The development objective of the Regional Empowerment through Vibrant, Inclusive, and Viable
Economies (REVIVE) project is to improve access to enabling infrastructure in project-supported clusters
in Sri Lanka’s Northern and Eastern Provinces to enhance regional economic development.
REVIVE represents the first phase (SOP 1) of a broader Series of Projects (SOP) approach, structured as a
two-phase series spanning 8 years. SOP 1 focuses on catalytic, high-impact investments in selected clusters
anchored in the tourism and fisheries sectors, while simultaneously strengthening institutional
coordination and the enabling environment for private sector participation. This SEP corresponds to SOP
1, the first phase of the 8-year SOP, which prioritizes foundational investments in selected clusters and
value chains in the Northern and Eastern Provinces.
The REVIVE project comprises three major components designed to catalyze inclusive, climate-resilient
regional economic growth. These components are strategically aligned to: (i) improve regional economic
planning and institutional capacity; (ii) finance catalytic public infrastructure investments in selected
clusters to attract private sector participation; and (iii) support local enterprises through business
development and skills enhancement linked to key value chains.
Component 1: Improving Regional Economic Planning and Strengthening Institutional Capacity
This component supports the preparation of a coordinated regional planning framework and strengthens
institutional capacity at national, provincial, and local levels. It aims to guide long-term regional economic
development by improving planning systems, identifying priority infrastructure needs, and preparing
future investments for subsequent phases of REVIVE.
Sub-components:
    •    Regional Economic and Development Planning: Preparation of a Regional Development Plan
         (RDP) for the North–East region, including value-chain diagnostics, identification of priority
         climate-resilient infrastructure, and guidance for public and private investment over a 5–10 year
         horizon.
    •    Preparatory Studies and Transaction Advisory Support: Feasibility and scoping studies for priority
         future investments such as air connectivity, Kokkilai–Pulmodai linkages, and sustainable
         development options for outer islands and fragile ecosystems, including advisory support for
         future phases of REVIVE.
Component 2: Catalyzing Investments, Supporting Enterprises, and Promoting Private Sector
Participation in Clusters
This component finances climate-resilient, area-based infrastructure and service improvements in five
priority clusters to strengthen tourism and fisheries value chains. It also supports local enterprises through
value-chain strengthening, business development services, and targeted interventions to attract private
investment and improve market access.
Sub-components:
    •    Climate-Resilient Infrastructure and Site Improvements: Integrated investments such as tourism
         and cultural site upgrades, improved pedestrian and waterfront spaces, fisheries infrastructure
         enhancement, and basic service upgrades (roads, drainage, water, waste) within cluster areas.

The project will be implemented in selected districts across the Northern and Eastern Provinces, with
specific project locations to be finalized during early implementation based on needs assessments,
technical feasibility, and stakeholder consultations. The Series of Projects 01 (SOP-01) will initially cover
the Jaffna District in the Northern Province and all three districts of the Eastern Province—Trincomalee,

1.3 Potential Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
The project involves the development of key infrastructure and facilities that may have both positive and
negative environmental and social impacts. While the overall objective is to enhance economic
development and improve livelihoods, the following risks and impacts will need to be carefully managed:

Environmental Risks:
• Potential degradation of coastal ecosystems (e.g., mangroves and sensitive marine habitats)
    associated with tourism and fisheries infrastructure.
• Increased pressure on scarce natural resources, particularly water supply in high-demand tourism
    areas such as Arugam Bay and Passikudah.
• Accumulation of solid waste and wastewater in tourism clusters where service capacity is already
    limited.
• Habitat disturbance from construction activities such as walkways, jetties, beaches, and access roads.
• Climate-related risks (coastal erosion, flooding, heat stress) that may affect both infrastructure and
    local communities.
• Potential risks associated with preliminary field investigations in ecologically sensitive areas such as
    the Kokkilai Wildlife Sanctuary, including disturbance to migratory bird habitats and lagoon
    ecosystems.

Social Risks:
• Temporary or permanent economic displacement of informal vendors, small businesses, and market
    users in revitalization areas such as Jaffna Fort, Gurunagar, and Passikudah.
• Risks to the livelihoods of small-scale fishers and fisheries workers due to changes in access, temporary
    construction works, or reorganization of harbor/landing areas.
• Community health and safety risks during construction, including dust, noise, access restrictions, and
    safety hazards in dense coastal settlements..
• Risks of social exclusion of vulnerable groups (women, youth, informal workers, low-income
    households) if benefits are not equitably distributed.
• Potential SEA/SH risks associated with labor influx, tourism development, and increased interactions
    between project workers, private sector actors, and communities.
    • Potential concerns from lagoon-dependent communities in Kokkilai and Pulmoddai related to
        future access, mobility, or livelihood implications arising from feasibility study findings.

The project will proactively address these risks through environmental and social safeguards, including
comprehensive stakeholder engagement, impact assessments, and the establishment of grievance redress
mechanisms.

2. Objectives of the SEP and Requirements for Stakeholder Engagement
2.1 Objectives of the SEP
The overall objective of this SEP is to define a program for stakeholder engagement, including public
information disclosure and consultation throughout the entire project cycle. The SEP outlines the ways in
which the Ministry of Transport Highway and Urban Development (MOTHUD)will communicate with
stakeholders and includes a mechanism by which people can raise concerns, provide feedback, or make
complaints about the project and any activities related to the project. The SEP specifically emphasizes
methods to engage groups considered most vulnerable and that are at risk of being left out of project
benefits.

2.2 National and World Bank Requirements for Stakeholder Engagement
The project will be implemented in full compliance with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social
Framework (ESF), as the national framework is only partially aligned with ESS10 requirements .

2.2.1 Requirements under the Country Legal Framework
In Sri Lanka, the national framework only partially aligns with ESS10: while the Constitution (Section 14A)
and the Right to Information Act, No. 12 of 2016 operationalize the right to access information, disclosure
is largely request-driven and stakeholder engagement sits outside the RTI mandate. The National
Environmental Act establishes EIA/IEE processes with a formal 30-day public disclosure and comment
period, integrating community feedback into approvals. However, the Land Acquisition Act focuses on
procedural acquisition and compensation with minimal scope for early consultation, and the National
Involuntary Resettlement Policy—though well aligned with ESS10—remains a non-binding policy, not law.
Sectoral laws (UDA Act, Coast Conservation Act, Sustainable Development Act, and Local Government
Acts) offer participatory provisions in specific domains, but application is uneven, especially outside large
or externally funded projects. Please refer to Annex for more details on stakeholder engagement
requirements under the country legislation.

2.2.2 Requirements under the World Bank’s ESF
The World Bank’s Environmental and Social Standard 10 (ESS10) outlines the requirements for Stakeholder
Engagement and Information Disclosure under its Environmental and Social Framework (ESF). ESS10
emphasizes that meaningful and inclusive engagement with stakeholders is a central component of
responsible project design and implementation. It applies to all projects supported by the World Bank
through Investment Project Financing (IPF)1. Stakeholder engagement is an inclusive process conducted
throughout the project life cycle2, from early preparation through implementation and closure.
ESS10 recognizes that early and sustained stakeholder engagement contributes to:
   • Enhancing the environmental and social sustainability of the project,
   • Strengthening project outcomes and effectiveness,
   • Minimizing risks and adverse impacts, and
   • Fostering transparency and accountability in project delivery.

Key Requirements of ESS10:
    •    Stakeholder Identification and Analysis – Identify affected and interested parties, including
         vulnerable groups.
    •    Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) – Prepare and disclose a SEP detailing stakeholders,
         consultation methods, information sharing, feedback integration, and grievance handling.
    •    Information Disclosure – Provide timely, relevant, and accessible information on risks, impacts,
         mitigation, and benefits.
    •    Meaningful Consultation – Ensure inclusive, gender-sensitive, culturally appropriate, two-way
         engagement, with special measures for vulnerable groups.
    •    Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) – Establish an accessible, responsive system for complaints,
         proportionate to project risks.
    •    Ongoing Engagement and Reporting – Maintain engagement throughout implementation,
         monitoring, and evaluation; update SEP as needed.
In addition to ESS10, several other Environmental and Social Standards (ESSs) of the World Bank are
directly relevant to stakeholder engagement and information disclosure under the REVIVE Project.
    •    ESS1: Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts requires that
         stakeholder engagement be integrated into environmental and social risk assessments, ensuring
         that the concerns of affected and interested parties are reflected in project planning and
         mitigation measures.
    •    ESS5: Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement mandates
         meaningful consultation with affected persons and the preparation of Resettlement Action Plans
         (RAPs) or Livelihood Restoration Plans (LRPs), promoting participatory approaches to
         compensation and relocation.
    •    ESS7: Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local
         Communities requires Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) for activities affecting Indigenous
         Peoples, thereby reinforcing the principle of self-determination and cultural respect.
    •    ESS8: Cultural Heritage emphasizes the importance of engaging with communities regarding the
         identification, preservation, and management of cultural heritage, particularly when it is tangible,
         intangible, or of spiritual significance.

3. Stakeholder Identification and Analysis
The success of the project depends significantly on early, continuous, and meaningful engagement with a
wide range of stakeholders. To ensure that no stakeholders are overlooked, a systematic process was
undertaken to identify, categorize, and analyze all stakeholder groups linked to various components,
activities, and clusters proposed under the program. This chapter outlines the methodology used for
stakeholder identification, the categorization of stakeholders into Project-Affected Parties (PAPs), Other
Interested Parties (OIPs), and Disadvantaged and Vulnerable Groups (DVGs), and presents a detailed
analysis of each category.

3.1 Methodology and Stakeholder Classification
Stakeholders were identified through a combination of document review, field visits, consultations with
government and community representatives, and an analysis of the proposed activities under each
program component. Special attention was paid to identifying those directly or indirectly affected by
project interventions, those with interests in project outcomes, and those needing special support due to
vulnerabilities.

Stakeholders were broadly classified into three categories:
    • Project-Affected Parties (PAPs): Individuals, groups, and communities directly affected by the
       project.
    • Other Interested Parties (OIPs): Individuals, groups, and organizations with an interest in the
       project because of their role, mandate, or expertise.
    • Disadvantaged and Vulnerable Groups (DVGs): Individuals or groups who, due to their particular
       circumstances, may be disproportionately impacted or have limited ability to participate or benefit
       from the project.
3.1.1 Project-Affected Parties
Project-Affected Parties are those who are directly impacted (Positively/negatively) by specific project
activities. Under REVIVE SOP 1, interventions are concentrated in five Priority Investment Areas (PIAs)
across the Northern and Eastern Provinces, primarily under Components 1 and 2. Each PIA involves a
distinct set of local communities, economic groups, and institutional actors.

Jaffna Fort and City Center Area (Northern Province)
Stakeholders include residents, businesses, and cultural actors in and around the Fort precinct and Jaffna
city core who may be affected by heritage restoration, public-space upgrading, access improvements, or
temporary disturbances during works.
     • Informal vendors operating in the outer moat area.
     • Learner drivers and heavy-vehicle operators using the moat road for parking or training.
     • Tourists and visitors affected by movement restrictions or temporary site closures.
     • Heritage custodians and institutional actors, including the Department of Archaeology, UDA, and
         the Jaffna Municipal Council.
     • Lagoon-dependent small-scale fishers using informal landing points and resting areas near the
         proposed Lagoon Park.
     • Parents, children, and staff of the community preschool located within or adjacent to the planned
         Lagoon Park area.
     • Prison authorities and prison visitors who currently use parking spaces and visitor huts within the
         Lagoon Park area.

Gurunagar Coastal Fisheries Town (Jaffna District)
Gurunagar serves as the largest artisanal fish-landing center in Jaffna, sustaining nearly 3,400 families. The
proposed jetty rehabilitation and coastal-road upgrades will directly involve:
   •    Fishing households, boat owners, and crew members operating Outboard Boat Motor (OBM)/
       fiberglass-reinforced plastic (FRP) crafts and using the beach-landing and jetty areas.
   • Vendors, auctioneers, and traders working within the fish market, auction space, and associated
       cold-storage facilities.
   • Dry fish producers
   • Residents and business owners located along the 1.8 km coastal road who may experience
       temporary access restrictions, noise, or service interruptions during road widening.
   • Church priests and congregations of the two coastal churches that may lose portions of land or
       frontage due to realignment or coastal-road improvements.
   • Transport users, buyers, fish carriers, and supply-chain operators dependent on uninterrupted
       access to the coastal link.
   • Fisheries cooperative societies, the Gurunagar Fishers’ Society, and The Department of Fisheries
       and Aquatic Resources (DFAR) field offices engaged in daily fisheries management.
   • Households and community members who will benefit from safer coastal access, improved
       circulation, and better waste-management conditions once works are completed.

Cod Bay Fisheries Harbour (Trincomalee District)
As one of the largest harbors in the Eastern Province, this PIA will affect multiple actors along the fisheries
value chain.
    • Vessel owners and boat operators using existing berthing and unloading zones.
    • Crew members and unloading/loading workers dependent on continuous pier access.
    • Fish buyers and auction participants requiring timely handling and circulation.
    • Ice suppliers and ice-plant operators dependent on uninterrupted ice delivery routes.
    • OceanPick aquaculture staff relying on harbour circulation and cold-chain logistics.
    • CFHC operational staff responsible for harbour operations, berthing, fuel access, and internal
        movement.
    • Fuel station workers and fuel-logistics operators affected by consolidation of fuel facilities.
    • Boat-repair and slipway/dry-dock workers whose workspaces are within the project footprint.
    • Internal harbour transporters (three-wheelers and mini-trucks) engaged in fish, ice, and supply
        movement.

Passikudah Tourism Zone (Batticaloa District)
Key stakeholders include local tourism workers, businesses, and community groups dependent on the
beachfront and tourism supply chain.
    • Local residents and fishing families using the accessible community beachfront and the Kannakuda
        fish-landing vicinity.
    • Regulated beachfront vendors in the vehicle park area.
    • Hotel, guesthouse, café, and restaurant operators whose access, signage, or customer circulation
        may be temporarily affected during road and sanitation upgrades.
    • Small traders, transport providers, and food-sector micro-entrepreneurs linked to increased visitor
        flows and dependent on upgraded public amenities.
    • Households and roadside business operators along the SLTDA/local authority road sections where
        pedestrian, drainage, and sanitation improvements will occur.
    • Beachfront tourism workers (lifeguards, beach-cleaning staff, informal guides) affected by
        temporary changes in beach movement patterns.

3.2 Analysis of Project-Affected Parties
The analysis of Project-Affected Parties (PAPs) under REVIVE SOP 1 examines how different groups —
communities, enterprises, and institutions — will experience the project’s potential benefits and risks.
Interventions are concentrated in five Priority Investment Areas (PIAs) across the Northern and Eastern
Provinces, mainly under Components 1 and 2, while two additional sites are under preparatory
assessment.

Jaffna Fort and City Center Area (Jaffna District)

Improvements to the Fort precinct, and the proposed Lagoon Park will affect residents, vendors, and
lagoon users within the urban core. Informal vendors and small traders around the outer moat may face
temporary relocation or access restrictions. Learner drivers and heavy vehicles using the moat area for
parking or training will experience disruptions. Lagoon-dependent small-scale fishers and the nearby
community preschool in the proposed Lagoon Park area will be directly affected by reconfiguration of open
spaces. Construction may restrict movement and generate noise, but long-term benefits include improved
drainage, safer public spaces, and expanded livelihood opportunities linked to tourism. The presence of
the prison and associated visitor huts also requires careful coordination to maintain access and security.

Gurunagar Coastal Fisheries Town (Jaffna District)

Gurunagar’s fish-landing, market, and coastal-road improvements will directly affect fishing households,
crew members, vendors, auctioneers, ice suppliers, and traders dependent on daily fisheries operations.
Temporary disruptions to landing, unloading, and market functions may affect incomes, particularly for
women involved in sorting and cleaning fish. Coastal-road widening will impact shops and residences along
the corridor, as well as two churches that may lose small portions of land. In the medium term, improved
landing facilities, road safety, drainage, and waste management will strengthen livelihoods and reduce
operational inefficiencies.

Cod Bay Fisheries Harbour (Trincomalee District)

Cod Bay’s interventions—jetty rehabilitation, pier upgrading, slipway improvements, and consolidation of
fuel and waste-management facilities—will directly affect harbour users whose livelihoods depend on
uninterrupted marine operations. Vessel owners, crew members, unloading workers, fish buyers, ice
suppliers, internal transport operators, and processing workers may experience temporary constraints on
movement, berthing, and handling operations during construction. Harbour staff responsible for daily
operations will also be impacted by temporary reconfiguration of access routes and service points. Since
no households, shops, or residents are located inside the harbour footprint, impacts are limited to
fisheries-value-chain actors operating within the facility.

Passikudah Tourism Zone (Batticaloa District)

Upgrading of visitor facilities, the creation of a night market, beachfront improvements, and road-side
mobility enhancements will directly affect regulated beachfront vendors, small traders, and local
enterprises operating along the access roads. Guesthouses, cafés, and restaurants located along the
improvement corridors may experience temporary reductions in frontage access or foot traffic. Beach
users and fishers accessing the small community beach may be temporarily affected by pathway or
sanitation upgrades. In the longer term, improved visitor amenities, waste management, and signage will
enhance tourism-linked incomes and strengthen community–tourism linkages.

3.4 Analysis of Disadvantaged and Vulnerable Groups
Women-Headed Households
Women-headed households often face socio-economic disadvantages due to cultural norms, limited
access to resources, and reduced income opportunities. These households may have less access to land,
credit, and business development services, making it harder for them to benefit from infrastructure
projects or economic opportunities. Additionally, women are more likely to be responsible for caregiving
and household chores, which can limit their ability to participate fully in the workforce or business
development initiatives. Ensuring that women-headed households have access to the benefits of the
project, such as through targeted financial support, training programs, and employment opportunities,
will be essential for fostering gender equality and inclusive growth.

Youth and Unemployed Persons

The youth, particularly those in rural and underserved areas, often face challenges in accessing
employment opportunities due to a lack of skills, education, or vocational training. Unemployment rates
among youth can be high, and they may not have the resources or connections to start their own
businesses or engage in income-generating activities. This group could benefit from targeted initiatives
under the project, such as skills training, business development services, and access to finance. Providing
youth with these opportunities will help address issues of unemployment and underemployment, thus
supporting economic stability and social inclusion.

Elderly Residents

Elderly residents, particularly those in remote areas such as islands, may face mobility challenges, lack of
access to healthcare, and limited participation in economic activities. They are often more vulnerable to
the adverse effects of infrastructure development, such as displacement or disruptions to community
services. The elderly population in these areas may also have fewer opportunities to benefit from new
employment or tourism-related activities. Targeted social protection measures, such as healthcare
services, transport facilities, and inclusion in community decision-making processes, can help mitigate the
impacts on this group.

Persons with Disabilities

Persons with disabilities (PWDs) face significant barriers in accessing public services, transportation, and
employment. The project must ensure that infrastructure developments, such as roads, water supply, and
tourism facilities, are designed with accessibility in mind. Furthermore, PWDs may face additional
challenges in participating in the workforce or entrepreneurship programs due to societal stigma or lack
of suitable resources. Implementing accessibility standards, offering inclusive job opportunities, and
ensuring PWDs can access benefits from social and business development programs are essential steps
toward fostering inclusion.

Small-Scale Artisanal Fisherfolk

Small-scale artisanal fisherfolk rely on local marine ecosystems for their livelihood. These communities are
particularly vulnerable to disruptions caused by infrastructure projects, changes in fishing areas, or shifts
in water quality. As coastal development proceeds, artisanal fisherfolk may lose access to traditional
fishing grounds or experience a decline in fish stocks due to environmental changes. To address this, the
project should ensure that the concerns of artisanal fishers are heard and considered during the planning
process. Measures such as compensation for lost livelihoods, access to alternative fishing zones, and
sustainable fisheries management will help protect their interests.

Indigenous Communities (if identified during implementation)

While there may not be any indigenous communities identified in the Northern and Eastern Provinces at
present, should any be identified during the project implementation, special attention will be given to
ensuring that these communities' rights are protected. Indigenous peoples often face marginalization and
may have unique cultural, social, and economic needs that must be addressed. The project will need to
follow national and international guidelines for engaging with indigenous communities, ensuring their
participation in decision-making, and safeguarding their land rights and traditional livelihoods.

4.1.1 Methodology for Consultation
The consultations conducted during the preparation of the project took place in March and April 2025.
These consultations aimed to gather comprehensive input from a broad spectrum of stakeholders,
including government authorities, private sector representatives, civil society organizations (CSOs), and
community groups, particularly focusing on the Northern and Eastern Provinces of Sri Lanka. The primary
goal was to assess local needs, opportunities, barriers, and to prioritize potential interventions for regional
development.

Key activities and steps taken during the consultation process included:

    •    Consultation Sessions: A total of 16 consultation sessions were organized with key stakeholders
         from various sectors. These sessions provided an opportunity for in-depth discussions on regional
         challenges, aspirations, and potential solutions.

    •    Site Visits: 14 site visits to potential project locations were conducted, offering firsthand insight
         into the infrastructure, land use, and community needs. These visits helped contextualize the
         feedback received during consultations by relating it to the actual conditions of the proposed
         project areas.

    •    Stakeholder Engagement: Direct engagement was carried out with over 35 government
         stakeholders, including provincial authorities, district officials, and representatives from various
         national ministries. In addition, consultations were held with more than 80 private sector
         stakeholders and 30+ civil society organizations (CSOs), including women’s and youth groups.
         These meetings ensured that a wide range of voices were heard and contributed to the project
         planning process.

    •    Field Visits and Consultations: The consultation process involved extensive field visits and
         consultations with a broad array of stakeholders. These included provincial authorities, district
         officials, local industries, and community representatives. Field visits were essential for
         understanding the region’s physical and social landscape, providing a direct connection to local
         needs and challenges, and enabling the team to collect authentic input from the communities
         affected by the project.

    •    Stakeholder Engagement: Meetings with government officials, provincial departments, and local
         industries focused on validating project ideas and ensuring alignment with regional development
         priorities. Discussions also aimed to identify opportunities for collaboration with development
         partners, maximizing synergies and ensuring that the project aligns with local, regional, and
         national development goals.

    •    Consultation Approach: The consultations were structured to engage stakeholders on a range of
         key development sectors, including infrastructure, tourism, agriculture, and social inclusion. At the
         same time, discussions were held on the challenges faced by the provinces, including the lingering

4.1.3 Key Topics Discussed During Consultations, Main Feedback and Issues Raised

The consultations held in March and April 2025 covered a wide range of topics, highlighting key challenges
and opportunities for development in the Northern and Eastern Provinces. These discussions focused on
the socio-economic issues affecting various sectors, with a particular emphasis on barriers to business
growth, infrastructure development, and social inclusion. Below are the main topics, feedback, and issues
raised during the consultations:

    •    SME Owners’ Challenges: Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) highlighted significant challenges
         related to access to raw materials, finance, production, and market access. The key issues included
         high transportation costs, the impact of bureaucratic delays, high interest rates on loans, and the
         lack of adequate production space. Stakeholders expressed the need for better infrastructure to
         support SME growth and for more accessible financing options.

    •    Tourism Stakeholders’ Challenges: Tourism sector representatives emphasized the need for
         improvements in infrastructure, such as better roads, improved connectivity (jetty/ferry services),
         sanitation facilities, and clearer signage. Long approval processes for tourism projects were also
         identified as a barrier. The sector also highlighted the need for better branding, marketing, and
         staff training to develop practical skills for tourism management.

    •    Banking Sector and Access to Finance: A major issue raised during the consultations was the
         difficulty in accessing finance, particularly for women and youth entrepreneurs. High-interest
         rates, restrictive loan conditions, and short repayment periods were identified as significant
         barriers. Discussions also focused on improving financial literacy and relaxing collateral
         requirements to make financing more accessible. The exploitation of rural communities by micro-
         finance institutions, leading to a cycle of debt, was another major concern.

    •    Skills Building and Training Needs: There was a strong demand for training programs in areas such
         as financial management, business development, marketing, product development, quality
         control, and market linkages. The consultations highlighted the mismatch between the skills of
         available labor and the needs of local industries, along with the poor work ethics attributed to
         insufficient training and lack of motivation.

    •    Infrastructure and Connectivity Needs: The Northern Province’s underdeveloped road network
         was identified as a major issue, with large sections of roads requiring rehabilitation. Stakeholders
         also emphasized the need for better connectivity, particularly for the tourism sector, with the
         Kurikadduvan Jetty cited as a priority for improvement. There were concerns about overcrowded
         boats and inadequate safety measures, particularly for women and children.

    •    Gender and Social Inclusion: There was a clear focus on empowering women and youth, especially
         in terms of access to finance and business opportunities. Women entrepreneurs faced additional
         challenges due to societal expectations of balancing domestic responsibilities with business
         management. The consultations also highlighted the lack of shelters for survivors of gender-based
         violence (GBV) and limited mental health services, which were identified as critical gaps in support
         systems for women and youth.

    •    Legacy Issues from Conflict: Stakeholders discussed the ongoing challenges related to mental
         health, substance abuse, and the social impacts of the civil conflict in the Northern Province. These
         issues continue to affect community well-being, especially for women and youth, and were
         identified as key areas requiring targeted interventions.

    •    Fisheries and Aquaculture Development: Issues related to the fisheries sector, such as the lack of
         processing facilities and the need for scaling up production (e.g., sea cucumber farming), were
         raised. Stakeholders saw potential for the sector's growth but highlighted the need for better
         infrastructure and market access.

    •    Social and Economic Inclusion: A recurring theme was the need for greater participation of
         women and youth in economic activities and political decision-making. Stakeholders called for
         support mechanisms such as better access to finance, childcare facilities, and digital marketing
         platforms to assist women entrepreneurs in balancing domestic and business responsibilities.

    •    Environmental and Land Use Conflicts: Conflicts between government departments, such as the
         Forest Department and the Department of Wildlife, over land ownership and the reclassification
         of lands as reserves, were identified as barriers to development. These issues were particularly
         challenging in areas with prior agricultural or residential use, where land is now being designated
         for conservation purposes.

    •    Provincial Development Challenges: Discussions on the Northern Province’s post-conflict
         development focused on low-income levels, inadequate infrastructure, and the urgent need for
         interventions to address socio-economic disparities. Stakeholders emphasized the importance of
         investing in infrastructure improvements, particularly roads, jetties, and transportation networks,
         to support economic growth and tourism development.

These consultations provided valuable insights into the key challenges facing stakeholders in the Northern
and Eastern Provinces, with a focus on addressing the barriers to economic participation, supporting
vulnerable groups, and identifying opportunities for growth in sectors like SMEs, tourism, and fisheries.
The feedback highlighted the need for targeted interventions to improve access to finance, skills
development, and infrastructure, as well as the importance of promoting social inclusion and gender
equality.

4.1.5 How Stakeholder Feedback was Incorporated into Project Design
Stakeholder feedback was integral to shaping the project design, ensuring that the needs and concerns of
local communities, government bodies, and private sector representatives were fully considered. Key
recommendations from consultations were incorporated into the design process, such as prioritizing
infrastructure improvements, enhancing educational and IT resources, and fostering sustainable economic
opportunities for MSMEs and local industries. Additionally, the feedback highlighted the need for a
gender-sensitive approach, with a focus on empowering women and youth. Development initiatives, such
as enhancing transport infrastructure (e.g., jetties and boat services), were also prioritized, aligning with
stakeholders' requests for better connectivity. The insights gathered ensured that the project design was
both practical and responsive to regional challenges, fostering a more inclusive and sustainable
development approach.

4.4 Proposed Stakeholder Engagement Strategy

The Proposed Stakeholder Engagement Strategy for this project outlines a clear and actionable
commitment to engage with all relevant stakeholders at the national, provincial, and local levels
throughout the project’s lifecycle. The strategy is designed to ensure that all stakeholders, particularly
project-affected parties, vulnerable and disadvantaged groups, and other interested stakeholders, are
continuously consulted, with their views and feedback integrated into project planning, implementation,
and monitoring.

4.5 Strategies for engaging vulnerable and disadvantaged groups
Given the diverse geographical and socio-economic contexts of the project’s intervention areas, the
project recognizes the importance of proactively identifying and meaningfully engaging vulnerable and
disadvantaged groups throughout the project lifecycle. These include, but are not limited to, low-income
households, female-headed households, persons with disabilities, elderly persons, remote island
communities, small-scale fisherfolk, and marginalized ethnic or religious minorities.

Special efforts will be made to overcome barriers that typically exclude these groups from participating in
development projects. This includes:

    •    Early Identification and Mapping: Vulnerable groups will be identified at the subproject level
         through baseline assessments, community consultations, and coordination with Divisional
         Secretariats, Grama Niladharis, and social development officers.

    •    Targeted Outreach and Communication: Project information, consultation invitations, and
         grievance redress procedures will be shared in accessible formats and languages, using local
         channels such as community leaders, women's organizations, religious institutions, and village-
         level meetings. Where literacy is low, pictorial communication or verbal briefings will be used.

    •    Tailored Consultation Methods: Focus Group Discussions (FGDs), separate community meetings
         (e.g., women-only or differently-abled group sessions), mobile consultations in island or remote
         areas, and door-to-door visits will be used where appropriate to encourage active participation.

    •    Timing and Location Considerations: Meetings and engagements will be scheduled at times and
         locations convenient to target groups—e.g., after work hours for daily wage earners, or in easily
         accessible venues for elderly and persons with disabilities.

    •    Representation in Project Structures: Representation of vulnerable groups in project-level
         stakeholder forums, monitoring committees, and beneficiary selection panels will be ensured to
         promote inclusivity in decision-making.

    •    Capacity Support and Social Mobilization: Where needed, community mobilizers and facilitators
         will support vulnerable groups to understand the project, articulate their needs, and engage with
         project processes meaningfully.

    •    Monitoring and Feedback: The participation of vulnerable groups will be tracked through
         disaggregated indicators in the project’s monitoring system. Regular feedback will be sought
         through satisfaction surveys, mobile tools, and consultations to address any gaps.

Through these strategies, the project commits to ensuring that vulnerable and disadvantaged groups are
not only informed but also empowered to contribute to and benefit from the project on equitable terms.

4.9 The Proposed Multi-Stakeholder Platform
Under Component 3 of the project, the proposed Multi-Stakeholder Platform (MSP) will form a
cornerstone of the stakeholder engagement strategy by establishing a participatory, transparent, and
inclusive mechanism for engaging a broad spectrum of actors throughout the project lifecycle. The MSP
aims to strengthen community representation and inter-agency coordination, ensuring that development
initiatives are grounded in the priorities of local populations while remaining aligned with broader regional
economic goals. According to the Terms of Reference (ToR) currently being prepared for the operation of
an inclusive MSP for regional development, this platform is envisioned as an innovative tool to enhance
stakeholder engagement and drive more responsive, collaborative planning and implementation
processes.

1. Embedding Participation in Project Planning and Implementation

The MSP will be directly integrated into the project’s stakeholder engagement framework as a formal
forum for dialogue, consultation, and decision-making. Through participatory planning processes
facilitated by the MSP, the platform will ensure that the perspectives and priorities of local communities
— including vulnerable and marginalized groups — are captured in the design and selection of sub-
projects. Community-level plans will be aggregated from the Grama Niladhari and Divisional levels up to
the district level, with community needs and proposals feeding into district and regional investment
planning.

District-level workshops conducted under the MSP during both project preparation and implementation
phases will be key venues for gathering stakeholder feedback, identifying and prioritizing investments,
addressing concerns, and refining the project’s strategic direction. This will facilitate inclusive decision-
making and early identification of potential risks or barriers to implementation.

2. Bridging Government Tiers and Community Structures

The MSP uniquely links devolved Local Government Authorities (LGAs) — including Pradeshiya Sabhas,
Urban Councils, and Municipal Councils — with the deconcentrated national government administration
operating at District, Divisional, and GN levels. By providing a structured interface between these
governance systems and community-level actors (such as women’s rural development societies, youth
groups, and other CBOs), the platform will strengthen coordination and ensure local development efforts
are mutually reinforcing across administrative boundaries.

This integration is particularly valuable for facilitating cross-sectoral alignment of policies and
interventions — a critical enabler of sustainable regional development.

3. Enhancing Two-Way Communication and Social Accountability

The MSP’s activities are designed to operationalize two-way communication between project
implementers and stakeholders. Quarterly stakeholder consultations, monitoring discussions, and
problem-solving forums will support continuous feedback loops. These interactions will allow the project
team to:
   • Identify and respond to evolving stakeholder concerns.
   • Monitor the effectiveness of engagement strategies.
   • Provide clarity on roles, responsibilities, and progress.
   • Reinforce transparency and build trust in the decision-making process.

Importantly, the platform will also serve as a mechanism for grievance redressal by offering structured

4. Capacity Building, Empowerment, and Ownership

The MSP will play a pivotal role in enhancing the capabilities of local government institutions and civil

continuous facilitation, training, and operational guidance provided by the platform’s operational
committees. As a result, the platform is expected to help institutionalize participatory governance
practices and cultivate long-term community ownership of project outcomes.

Moreover, the inclusion of producer cooperatives, SMEs, and private sector actors ensures that the
platform facilitates multi-sectoral dialogue and economic development, thereby promoting inclusive
growth in target regions.

5. Supporting Transparency through Communication and Outreach

The MSP is committed to documenting its deliberations and communicating key recommendations widely
through local government websites, social media, and community-based outreach. This transparency
initiative is critical for building stakeholder confidence and ensuring that the platform’s inputs and outputs
are seen as legitimate, inclusive, and actionable.

Furthermore, by using a recommendation framework that emphasizes clarity of intent over prescriptive
solutions, the platform ensures that its inputs remain relevant and adaptable to future policy and
implementation environments.

5. Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM)
The Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) for the REVIVE Project is designed to provide a fair, transparent,
inclusive, and accessible platform for receiving and resolving concerns related to the project’s
environmental and social performance. It will serve project-affected persons (PAPs), workers, and other
stakeholders across all phases of the project—from preparation through implementation to closure—at
no cost and without fear of retaliation.

While the GRM serves as the primary project-level mechanism for addressing grievances, it does not
replace Sri Lanka’s judicial system. Stakeholders retain the right to seek redress through formal legal
channels. Where appropriate, the project may offer facilitated dialogue or mediation as an alternative to
legal proceedings, provided the complainant agrees. The GRM is a vital tool for risk management,
stakeholder engagement, and enhancing transparency and accountability. By offering a user-friendly,
responsive mechanism for raising and resolving grievances, it helps minimize conflict, ensures timely
corrective actions, and fosters trust and collaboration among all parties involved in the REVIVE Project.

5.1 Principles of the Grievance Redress Mechanism

The GRM for the REVIVE Project is based on principles that ensure accessibility, transparency, fairness, and
protection for all stakeholders, especially vulnerable groups.

Key Principles:

    •    Accessibility: Open to all, with multiple culturally appropriate channels (in-person, digital, written)
         and support for vulnerable groups.

    •    Transparency: Clear procedures, timelines, and acknowledgment of grievances; anonymous
         complaints accepted.

    •    Fairness: Impartial, respectful, and confidential handling; time-bound resolution with appeal
         options.

    •    Non-Retaliation: Zero tolerance for intimidation or discrimination; confidentiality ensured.

    •    Responsiveness: Defined timeframes for resolution; urgent issues prioritized.

    •    Cost-Free: No charges for grievance submission; mediation offered before legal action.

    •    Monitoring: All grievances tracked in a centralized system; data analyzed to improve project
         performance.

By applying these principles, the GRM serves as a tool for resolving complaints and promoting inclusion,
accountability, and social sustainability.

5.3 Grievance Resolution Process and Timelines

The REVIVE Project’s Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) follows a structured, time-bound process to
ensure that complaints are handled promptly, fairly, and transparently. The process includes the following
core steps, applicable across all GRM levels:

Step 1: Receipt and Registration of Grievance: The process begins with the receipt and registration of the
grievance. Complaints can be submitted through multiple channels, including verbal or written formats,
physical drop boxes, or digital platforms such as email, SMS, or WhatsApp. Once received, every
complaint—whether anonymous or not—is logged in a centralized grievance register and assigned a
unique tracking number. The complainant receives an acknowledgment of the complaint within three
working days, confirming that the issue has been received and recorded.

Step 2: Grievance Assessment and Categorization: Following registration, the grievance is subject to an
initial assessment and categorization. The grievance focal point reviews the content of the complaint to
confirm whether it falls within the scope of the project’s GRM. It is then categorized based on its severity,
urgency, and complexity. In this stage, sensitive or high-risk complaints—such as those involving gender-
based violence (GBV), threats to personal safety, or corruption—are flagged for immediate escalation or
referral to specialized handling mechanisms. All complaints are treated with confidentiality, especially
those requiring survivor-centered or rights-based responses.

Step 3: Resolution at the Appropriate Level: Once categorized, the grievance proceeds to the resolution
stage, which prioritizes resolution at the community or subproject level, unless the issue requires
escalation. There are four levels at which grievances may be resolved, depending on their nature and
complexity. At the GN or subproject level, the community GRM committee attempts to resolve the
complaint within three working days. If unresolved, the grievance is escalated to the Divisional GRM
Committee, which works to find a solution within seven working days. Complex or unresolved grievances
at the divisional level are forwarded to the Provincial GRM Committee, with a resolution target of fourteen
working days. The final level is the National GRM Committee at the PMU, which addresses the most serious
or escalated grievances within twenty-one working days. If delays are expected at any level, the
complainant will be informed of the reasons and the revised timeline.

discussions undertaken, and the final decision or corrective action agreed upon. If the complainant is
satisfied with the outcome, the case is formally closed in the grievance register. If the complainant
disagrees with the decision, they may choose to escalate the issue to the next level of the GRM, where
the process is repeated with fresh oversight.

Step 5: Grievance Closure: The closure of a grievance is documented when either the complainant has
confirmed satisfaction with the outcome, or the grievance has been reviewed and resolved through all
available GRM levels. For anonymous complaints, closure occurs when appropriate corrective action has
been taken and logged.

Special Handling Provisions:

Certain types of complaints may require special handling. Grievances that pose urgent risks to health, life,
or property are fast-tracked for resolution within forty-eight hours. Complaints involving GBV, sexual
exploitation and abuse (SEA), or sexual harassment (SH) are managed under a confidential, survivor-
centered protocol aligned with World Bank and national guidelines. In parallel, complainants are always
free to seek legal redress through the national judicial system if they choose, and the project will assist
with information and referrals where appropriate.

The GRM’s structured timeline and transparent approach help ensure that community concerns are
addressed early and constructively, contributing to improved stakeholder trust and project outcomes.

5.4 Responding to SEA/SH Related Complaints

The REVIVE Project adopts a survivor-centered, rights-based approach to address grievances related to
Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (SEA) and Sexual Harassment (SH). Recognizing the sensitivity and gravity
of such incidents, SEA/SH complaints will be handled through distinct protocols that prioritize
confidentiality, safety, informed consent, and access to services over investigation or redress through
conventional grievance channels.

SEA/SH grievances can be reported through designated focal points at various levels of project
implementation, including the PMU, PIUs, and partner institutions. These focal points will undergo
mandatory training to ensure they are well-equipped to handle complaints with discretion and empathy.
No complaint mechanism under the REVIVE Project may reject a SEA/SH-related grievance. Survivors also
retain the option of directly reporting to trained GBV service providers without using the project GRM.

SEA/SH-related grievances will not be publicly recorded or tracked in standard grievance logs to avoid
exposing survivors to stigma or reprisals. Instead, only minimal, non-identifiable information may be
recorded, such as:
     • The nature of the complaint, in the survivor’s own words;
     • Whether the alleged perpetrator is linked to the project;
     • Age and sex of the survivor, if shared voluntarily;
     • Whether the survivor was referred to support services.
All survivors will be referred to established qualified GBV service providers, which may include:
     • Women Development Officers, Social Services Officers, and trained Counselors at the Divisional
         Secretariat level;

    •    The National Women’s Helpline (1938) managed by the Ministry of Women, Child Affairs and
         Social Empowerment;
    •    Mithuru Piyasa units available in most state hospitals, operated under the Ministry of Health;
    •    Women In Need (WIN) and other local NGOs with a strong GBV response track record;
    •    The Women’s Unit of the Sri Lanka Police at relevant police stations.

Information on these service providers and SEA/SH grievance channels will be publicly displayed in Sinhala,
Tamil, and English at key locations, including GN offices, Divisional Secretariats, project offices,
construction sites, and on the Ministry website.

To ensure confidentiality and consent, no information regarding a SEA/SH complaint will be disclosed to
any third party without the explicit informed consent of the survivor. Survivors may withdraw this consent
at any time, and any follow-up will cease immediately upon such request. In cases involving children,
informed consent must be obtained from both the child (if of appropriate age) and their legal guardian.

In line with WHO’s ethical and safety guidelines, the REVIVE Project will adhere to best practices in
documenting and responding to SEA/SH cases. Survivors will never be pressured to pursue any action they
are not comfortable with. Services will be offered even if the survivor chooses not to file a formal complaint
or if the incident is ultimately found to be unrelated to the project.

In instances where SEA/SH allegations involve project workers, the Project Management Unit (PMU) Social
Specialist, together with the GRM focal point of the respective partner agency, will assess whether the
case is likely to be project-related. If confirmed, the employer of the alleged perpetrator will be responsible
for taking appropriate disciplinary action in accordance with Sri Lankan labor law and contractual
obligations. The PMT will follow up to ensure that the survivor was referred to services and will report
back to the survivor (with their consent) on any non-identifying outcomes of the response.

By establishing a dedicated, safe, and confidential mechanism for handling SEA/SH-related complaints, the
REVIVE Project reinforces its commitment to zero tolerance for SEA/SH, safeguarding the dignity and
wellbeing of all stakeholders, especially women, children, and other vulnerable groups.

Reporting to the World Bank:

In alignment with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Incident Response Toolkit (ESIRT), the REVIVE
Project’s Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) will ensure timely and confidential reporting of SEA/SH
incidents to the World Bank. Upon receiving a SEA/SH-related grievance, the GRM focal point must
immediately inform the Project Management Unit (PMU) Social Specialist. The PMU Social Specialist, in
coordination with the Project Implementation Unit (PIU), is responsible for notifying the Task Team Leader
(TTL) within 24 hours of becoming aware of the incident.

The TTL will then report the incident to the World Bank’s senior management, including the Practice
Manager, Sector Director, and Country Management Unit (CMU), by submitting a first draft of the ESIRT
report within 24 hours of receiving relevant information. For serious allegations such as sexual assault,
rape, or human trafficking, the Vice President of the South Asia Region (SAR) must also be informed
promptly.

6. Monitoring and Reporting

6.1 Monitoring of SEP
Monitoring the implementation of the Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) for the REVIVE Project is a
multi-tiered process involving coordination between national, provincial, and district-level actors. The
objective is to ensure that stakeholder engagement is consistent, inclusive, and aligned with the project's
principles of transparency, accountability, and responsiveness.

The Project Management Unit (PMU) established under the Ministry of Transport Highway and Urban
Development (MOTHUD), the Executing Agency, will have overall responsibility for monitoring SEP
implementation. Day-to-day monitoring will be led by the PMU and the PIUs under the respective
Implementing Agencies—MOTHUD, and the Northern and Eastern Provincial Councils.



Institutional Arrangements for Monitoring

    •    National Level (PMU): The PMU will consolidate monitoring data received from the provincial PIUs
         and subproject agencies, generate quarterly monitoring reports, and ensure that engagement
         activities are progressing as planned. It will also coordinate periodic reviews and serve as the focal
         point for World Bank reporting.

    •    Provincial Level (PIUs): The PIUs will be responsible for tracking stakeholder engagement activities
         implemented in their respective provinces, compiling district-level reports, and liaising with the
         PMU. They will ensure that sectoral departments (e.g., water, urban development, agriculture) are
         actively involved in monitoring their respective subprojects.

    •    District and Subproject Level: Local offices of relevant departments will participate in monitoring
         SEP activities on the ground. This includes facilitating community meetings, recording issues
         raised, and ensuring follow-up through the project’s GRM or technical teams.

Community-Based Monitoring Structures

    •    Social Audit Committees (SACs): SACs will be formed at the GN or village level in subproject
         locations. These community-based committees will consist of local residents, including
         representatives from women’s groups, youth, and marginalized communities. They will serve as
         grassroots-level monitors of project activities, verify information dissemination, and track the
         responsiveness of the grievance redress mechanism.

    •    Stakeholder Engagement (SE) Platform: The multi-stakeholder SE Platform established under the
         project will play a dual role in both facilitating information sharing and monitoring. It will convene
         periodic review meetings (e.g., semi-annually or quarterly), bringing together key stakeholders—
         including local authorities, civil society, sector departments, and community representatives—to
         assess engagement progress, review grievances, and make recommendations for corrective
         actions.

Annex 1: Summary of Previous Stakeholder Engagement Activities

This annex presents a summary of the stakeholder consultations conducted as part of the project
Identification Mission for the Regional Empowerment through Vibrant, Inclusive, and Viable Economies
(REVIVE), Sri Lanka, held from March 25 to April 9, 2025. The consultations were carried out across
selected districts in the Northern and Eastern Provinces to engage with provincial, district, and divisional
authorities, community representatives, and sectoral agencies. The primary objective was to gather initial
feedback on development needs, institutional capacities, stakeholder priorities, and potential
environmental and social considerations to inform the early design of the REVIVE.



1. SME Sector: Challenges and Support

SME owners in Sri Lanka’s Northern and Eastern Provinces operate in a landscape marked by economic
uncertainty, post-conflict vulnerabilities, and persistent structural barriers. These conditions have led to
heightened risk aversion, with most business owners preferring to maintain small-scale operations due to
doubts about the viability of expansion and innovation. The lack of confidence in recouping investments
or scaling up has resulted in a cautious approach.

A critical challenge for many entrepreneurs is the procurement of raw materials. SME owners often travel
long distances, sometimes all the way to Colombo, to obtain essential inputs. This journey is costly and
time-consuming, with transport expenses between Special Economic Zones and Colombo often negating
the advantage of lower provincial rents. Poor road connectivity and unreliable transport, especially during
the rainy season, further isolate communities and disrupt supply chains.

Financing remains a major hurdle. Commercial banks typically reserve loans for well-established
businesses with high creditworthiness, while new start-ups face stringent requirements such as business
registration and multiple guarantors. Some SMEs report spending significant sums on registration just to
access loans that are only marginally larger. Micro-credit institutions, while more accessible, charge very
high interest rates—sometimes up to 25 percent—leading to indebtedness. Many SME owners are
burdened by loans from multiple institutions and often lack the capacity to repay, especially when loans
are used for purposes other than business investment. The Regional Development Bank has limited
branches in remote areas, restricting access for entrepreneurs, especially women-led households.

Production challenges are significant and multifaceted. Many SMEs export raw products with little or no
value addition, reflecting a limited understanding of export market requirements. Micro-enterprises often
lack production space and express a need for common production centers. The adoption of technology
remains low, and access to testing laboratories and quality control facilities is limited and costly. Issues
with packaging and labeling further hinder competitiveness, compounded by poor access to modern
equipment and limited knowledge of advanced production practices.

Market access and branding are underdeveloped. Micro and small enterprises typically rely on local
exhibitions and word-of-mouth, with limited use of social media and collective marketing platforms. Many
SMEs lack the skills and resources to develop effective branding strategies.

Infrastructure barriers further impede business operations. Inadequate water supply, poor road
connectivity, and unreliable electricity increase operational costs and limit access to markets and essential
services.

Training and capacity building needs are acute. Many SME owners lack financial literacy and are more
comfortable with cash transactions than formal banking systems. There is a need for targeted training in
business development, technical skills, and digital literacy.

Skills mismatches and poor work ethics among staff are frequently cited concerns. Young employees often
lack basic office skills and a strong work ethic, attributed to poor training and a culture of materialism.
Employers report difficulty finding candidates for higher-skilled occupations.

Regulatory barriers add another layer of difficulty. Licensing and registration processes are often delayed
and fragmented, requiring navigation of multiple institutions with unclear procedures and fee structures.
One SME in Batticaloa reported waiting over three years for approval after investing more than Rs. 350
million.

To address these challenges, stakeholders recommended establishing shared production facilities and
common spaces for SMEs, particularly in large industrial areas. Such collective infrastructure would
facilitate access to testing, quality control, labeling, and packaging services. Marketing support, including
linking SMEs with buyers and exporters, training in social media marketing, and creating dedicated sales
outlets, was also highlighted. Organizational models such as federations, cooperatives, or SME associations
can pool resources, negotiate better terms, and facilitate peer learning. Financial literacy and management
programs, streamlined business registration, and technical assistance from idea to execution were
identified as vital for SME resilience and growth.

Consultations with the Commercial Bank of Ceylon revealed a robust SME portfolio, with women
constituting a significant share of customers. Banks face challenges in background checks and collateral
requirements, but have adopted solutions such as site visits, relaxed collateral requirements, and lower
interest rates for women. Further support includes fast-tracking business registration, raising awareness
about formalization, and providing training in business development and financial management.



2. Tourism Sector: Challenges and Support

Tourism stakeholders identified several infrastructure barriers hindering sector growth, including poor
road connectivity, inadequate water and sanitation facilities, insufficient storm water management, and a
lack of streetlights. The absence of clear signages and lengthy approval processes—especially for hotel
licenses—further complicate tourism development. There is also ambiguity regarding government
responsibilities and limited support from departments such as archaeology, coastal, and forestry
authorities.

Despite these challenges, significant opportunities exist to drive activity-based tourism by leveraging local
assets. The region’s forts, Indian cultural centers, and vibrant street food scenes can be developed into
attractive destinations for both domestic and international tourists. Innovative concepts such as boat
houses, island hopping, and themed restaurants can diversify the tourism offering. Wellness tourism,
including traditional Siddha medicine and eco-friendly experiences, is another area with strong potential.

Community-based tourism presents a unique opportunity for women and youth to participate actively in
regional development. By promoting citizen engagement and social integration, such models can foster
inclusive growth and empower marginalized groups. Training and support for women and youth are crucial
to enable them to play leadership roles in tourism enterprises and benefit from sector expansion.

Maximizing the use of underutilized spaces, such as Mamthali beach and sand dunes, can further enhance
the tourism experience. Addressing religious and spiritual tourism needs, while catering to younger
generations’ interests in entertainment and activities, can help broaden the region’s appeal. Improving
transport options, such as introducing flights between Colombo and Jaffna, would reduce travel times and
make the region more accessible.



3. Challenges Faced by Women

Women-led enterprises face additional challenges due to societal expectations to fulfill domestic
responsibilities. The prevalence of women-headed households, a legacy of the civil war, has placed the
responsibility on women to act as primary breadwinners while maintaining domestic duties. Female
entrepreneurs often have to rush home between shifts to prepare food for their families and are expected
to take time off from work to care for children when they are unwell or when adequate childcare options
are unavailable. These dual responsibilities limit their time and resources, making it challenging to scale
their enterprises or access opportunities for growth.

Women also face barriers in accessing land, assets, and formal finance, which further restricts their ability
to expand their businesses. Several women-headed households, particularly widow-headed households,
face severe financial vulnerability with limited social security and need for additional services and/or
support to maintain basic household functioning. Access to elder or child care is either unavailable or
unaffordable to most women interviewed, and several confirmed that community-level options for care—
if subsidized—would be useful to enable women’s expanded economic engagement.

Women consistently flagged challenges in accessing finance to start or expand businesses. While loans
and grants are available, amounts offered are often insufficient to cover the full cost of starting or scaling
a business. Larger scale loans through commercial banks are often inaccessible due to unfavorable terms.
Recommendations include removing guarantor requirements, offering more flexible financial products,
and ensuring longer grace periods and flexible repayment terms.

Predatory microlending poses a significant challenge, with many microloans offered with unclear terms
and exorbitant interest rates. Borrowers frequently find themselves trapped in a cycle of debt, leading to

financial instability and increased mental health issues. Improving regulation, financial literacy, and
transparency in lending practices are recommended to protect vulnerable borrowers.

Women also highlighted the need for improved, accessible, and safe transportation and infrastructure to
enable market access. Safety concerns and restricted mobility make it difficult for women to sell products
or engage in marketing activities, leading to calls for local market spaces or collection centers.



4. Challenges Faced by Youth

Youth in the Northern and Eastern Provinces face a range of challenges, including skills mismatches, poor
work ethics, and limited access to economic opportunities. Employers report that young employees often
lack basic office skills and a strong work ethic, attributed to poor training and a culture of materialism.
High turnover rates and difficulty finding candidates for higher-skilled occupations are common.

Youth entrepreneurs flagged challenges in accessing finance, technical training, and business development
support. While some have received training from NGOs or government offices, the extent of follow-on
refresher trainings and business development support varies significantly. There is clear demand for
training in financial management, accounting, market linkages, and product development, as well as
quality control and standardization.

Career guidance is needed to connect youth to opportunities in tourism, SMEs, agriculture, and other
sectors. Peer support, mentoring, and job placement support are current gaps. Youth interested in
agricultural modernization should be made aware of opportunities beyond traditional jobs, such as
entrepreneurship and innovation. Community consultations with opinion leaders can address myths and
misinformation. Reactivating youth clubs to promote cultural activities, address issues of youth
emigration, and encourage youth to seek opportunities for business and employment in their communities
is vital.

Promoting socio-economic inclusion of youth is essential. Support for youth in accessing finance, digital
marketing, and childcare facilities, as well as encouraging participation in economic development and
political decision-making, will help ensure a more inclusive and equitable society.



5. Challenges Faced by People with Disabilities

People with disabilities face significant barriers to economic participation. Consultations highlighted the
need for inclusive infrastructure, such as wheelchair-accessible transport and gender-inclusive washroom
facilities, to enable market access for differently-abled entrepreneurs. Transportation options should
include ramps and wheelchair access for buses, reliable transit times, seating facilities, lighting, and
gender-inclusive washrooms.

Youth with disabilities should be provided with free courses and mentoring support, helping them obtain
certifications. Women and women with disabilities face marketing challenges and need places to showcase

Addressing legacy social issues—such as mental health, substance abuse, and gender-based violence—is

Coordination among government bodies and service providers is essential for effective delivery of social
support services.




Annex : Summary of Field Consultations Conducted in Jaffna, Trincomalee, Batticaloa and Ampara
(During the E&S screening process: November 2025)

This annex summarises the stakeholder consultations carried out across the Priority Investment Areas
(PIAs) of Jaffna Fort, Gurunagar, Cod Bay, Passikudah, and Arugam Bay. Discussions were held with
community groups, fisher societies, women vendors, traders, religious institutions, local authorities, and
sector agencies to identify needs, risks, and expectations associated with proposed interventions.

1. Urban Livelihoods, Public Space Users, and Informal Vendors (Jaffna Fort & Lagoon Park)

Consultations in Jaffna Fort revealed that informal vendors, particularly elderly persons, women-headed
households, and one vendor with a disability, depend heavily on vending near the outer moat for daily
income. These vendors lack shade, sanitation, and secure storage, making them vulnerable to disruptions
during construction. Street vendors along AB16/Pannai Circular Road, including fruit, snack, clothing, and
lottery sellers, operate entirely within the project footprint and rely on tourist traffic and passing vehicles.
Driving instructors and heavy-vehicle operators use the moat area for daily practice and parking and
expressed concerns about losing operational space. Along the lagoon edge, small-scale lagoon fishers
highlighted the importance of maintaining haul-out areas and requested designated landing points. Within
the Lagoon Park, parents asked for a new preschool building. Residents also flagged incidents of youth
misbehavior in secluded areas, reinforcing the need for open visibility, lighting, and safe pedestrian
circulation. Community members displayed strong interest in opportunities for local commercial kiosks,
requesting priority allocation for vulnerable families. Prison visitors who use the existing visitor hut and
vehicle park also emphasized maintaining shaded waiting areas and safe footpaths.

2. Fisheries Value Chain and Coastal Livelihoods (Gurunagar & Cod Bay)

In Gurunagar, fisher societies stressed the need to maintain continuous operational access to the jetty
during rehabilitation, noting that even short interruptions reduce freshness and market prices. They
requested micro-phasing, stepped or sloped access for boat haul-out, and simple bays at intervals along
the improved coastal edge. Resting huts used for shade, net-mending and equipment storage are essential
and must be relocated nearby. Sanitation and drainage weaknesses were raised as longstanding issues
needing integration into the design. In Cod Bay, the Harbour Master and Harbour Development Society
confirmed that while there are no residents or vendors inside the harbour, the site is extremely
operationally sensitive. Vessel owners, crews, unloading labourers, cold-chain operators, and ice-plant
workers rely on uninterrupted circulation. Any pier or slipway works must be sequenced to ensure at least
one active face for berthing and unloading at all times. Boat operators also highlighted the cultural
significance of neem trees within the harbour.

3. Tourism-Linked Livelihoods and Visitor-Area Operators (Passikudah & Arugam Bay)

At Passikudah, consultations revealed that 18 regulated temporary vendors operate from structurally weak
stalls with no electricity or proper sanitation, many of whom are women-headed households. They
requested permanent kiosks, shade, seating, and integration into the proposed night-market area. Tourism
boat operators expressed the need for improved passenger-handling points, while lifeguard units reported
inadequate           facilities        and         requested          more           safety         posts.
Fishers from Kalkudah and Kannakuda underscored the need for improved basic facilities at landing points
and       protection       of    their     access      routes      during    tourism         development.
In Arugam Bay, consultations with roadside traders showed that many permanent shops have informal
extensions built over drains, while several fruit and vegetable vendors (many from vulnerable
backgrounds) operate informally along the corridor. Fishers behind the vehicle park stressed the
importance of maintaining anchorage and launching paths, especially if vehicle facilities are moved.
