                         C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities

                         The proposed project aims to foster sustainable tourism in selected destination supporting the creation of local jobs,
                         prioritizing environmental stewardship, and strengthening community resilience. By developing unique tourism
                         offerings that respect natural assets and empower local communities, the initiative seeks to drive inclusive economic
                         growth, thereby creating conditions for sustained private sector participation.

                         D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings

                         The proposed Project focuses on selected demonstration destinations across Jammu and Kashmir (J&K), reflecting a
                         diverse range of ecological, topographical, and social contexts. Investment locations will be guided by a carrying
                         capacity-based access approach, which categorizes sites into three tiers according to ecological sensitivity, cultural
                         significance, and economic potential. Tier 1 comprises high-access urban heritage sites and commercial tourism hubs,
                         where most infrastructure investments will be concentrated. Tier 2 covers semi-natural landscapes, cultural villages,
                         and buffer areas near protected zones, where visitor flows will be managed through controlled access, guided
                         experiences, and community-led initiatives. Tier 3 consists of highly sensitive ecosystems such as alpine habitats, tribal
                         lands, and biodiversity hotspots, where activities will be strictly limited to permit-based or community-managed
                         ecotourism and conservation programs.

                         The region is home to approximately 3,560 plant species and over 570 vertebrate species, with about 20 percent of its
                         territory designated as protected areas, including national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, conservation reserves, and
                         RAMSAR sites. Many tourist sites are subject to seasonal climatic variations such as snowfall and monsoon rains with
                         risks of landslides. The region is highly prone to multiple hazards, being located in seismic Zones IV and V and facing
                         frequent floods, landslides, avalanches, and cloudbursts.

                         The population of J&K is predominantly rural, with about 75 percent of inhabitants living in rural areas. Scheduled Tribes
                         comprise approximately 13 percent of the population, with the principal tribal groups (Gujjars and Bakarwals) practicing




For Official Use Only
                         nomadic pastoralism. The economy of J&K comprises a mix of agrarian and service-sector activities, with substantial
                         reliance on natural resources. Horticulture and agriculture are the primary sources of rural household income. Tourism
                         and related services have become essential components of the regional economy. The sector accounts for 7 percent of
                         J&K’s Gross Domestic Product and provides job opportunities for locals in tourism-related activities in hospitality
                         (hotels, guesthouses), transportation (taxis, shikaras, pony treks), tour guiding, and the handicrafts sector, often
                         through employment in micro, small, and medium-sized enterprises (MSMEs). Tourism has significant potential to
                         generate further livelihood opportunities in J&K where unemployment stands at 6.7 percent, and youth unemployment
                         at 17.4 percent. It can serve as an important entry point for youth and women into the labor market, making it one of
                         J&K’s most promising sectors for economic diversification and job creation.

                         Access to essential services is inconsistent across the region’s rugged landscape. While rural road connectivity has seen
                         improvement in recent years, many villages, especially those at higher altitudes, continue to lack all-weather road
                         access and become isolated during the winter months. Electricity coverage reaches most villages in principle; however,
                         supply remains unreliable in remote areas. Springs and streams in hill regions and piped supply schemes in lowland
                         areas are the main sources of domestic water supply as the region moves towards universal access to tap water.
                         Healthcare infrastructure is uneven, with several smaller settlements in higher terrains lacking fully functional services.
                         Similarly, access to education in remote locations is hindered by single-teacher primary schools and high student
                         absenteeism and irregular attendance due to seasonal migration.

                         The environmental risk for the proposed Project is rated as Substantial. This rating reflects the type, scale, and
                         location of proposed activities, the sensitivity of the environmental setting, the envisioned types of investments
                         based on tourism planning studies, and the current capacity of the implementing entities. The proposed Project
                         follows a framework approach. None of the destinations is foreseen to be located in critical habitat areas, and future
                         investments will be guided by planning studies and the tiered approach to accessibility. Planning studies will
                         specifically include strategic environmental and social assessments (SESA) and suggest procedures to be adopted
                         during implementation. The no-regret investments identified for immediate implementation mainly include small-
                         scale works. Key environmental risks include the inadequate integration of environmental carrying capacity into
                         planning, which could lead to the risk of development in sensitive or disaster-prone areas, as well as over-tourism and
                         strain on local resources such as water and energy. Tourism-related activities may increase pressure on forests,
                         wetlands, and high-biodiversity zones, potentially disturbing habitats and wildlife. Climate change further heightens
                         risks by exposing infrastructure and communities to floods, landslides, and extreme weather events. In addition,
                         construction activities under the Project may generate air and noise emissions, soil erosion, slope destabilization, loss
                         of vegetation, and occupational health and safety concerns. Improper management of wastewater, drainage, and
                         solid waste during both construction and operation could lead to the pollution of rivers and wetlands, while
                         unmanaged waste from tourism activities may further degrade fragile ecosystems. While these impacts may not be
                         significant individually, the uncertainties about the cumulative environmental pressures from tourism activity across
                         multiple sites have been factored into the overall risk rating.

                          Some of the tourism sites are known for their rich historical and architectural heritage, including historic temples,
                          mosques, forts, Mughal gardens, sacred natural features, and traditional crafts. Any project activities near these sites
                          will have both positive and negative impacts. Positive impacts may include improved facilities for tourists, enhanced
                          visitor management, and better conservation outcomes. However, potential risks include vibration, excavation, or
                          ground disturbance near sensitive structures; visual or noise intrusion; crowding and overuse of historic areas;
                          commercialization or misrepresentation of intangible cultural heritage; temporary access restrictions during works;
                          and the possibility of chance finds. To address these issues, a Cultural Heritage Management Framework (CHMF) has
                          been developed in accordance with ESS8 and relevant legislation. The CHMF outlines principles, screening
                          procedures, and tools to identify, assess, and manage cultural heritage risks across subprojects. For activities in
                          sensitive areas, site-specific Cultural Heritage Management Plans (CHMPs) will be developed in consultation with
                          custodians and local communities. The CHMF also includes guidance on detailed design, construction, and
                          operational controls to safeguard both tangible and intangible heritage. These include heritage-sensitive design
                          guidelines, buffer zones, chance-find procedures, construction-stage monitoring, and the co-design of visitor
                          experiences with communities and custodians. A heritage-sensitive grievance mechanism will also be established to
                          ensure the timely resolution of concerns related to access, damage, or misuse of cultural assets.

                         C. Overview of Required Environmental and Social Risk Management Activities

                         C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or
                         required by implementation?


                         To manage E&S risks and impacts and meet the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) requirements,
                         a framework approach has been adopted to enable early-stage risk management while allowing for flexibility during
                         implementation. The following Environmental and Social standards are relevant to the Project: ESS1, ESS2, ESS3, ESS4,
                         ESS5, ESS6, ESS7, ESS8 and ESS10.

                         Based on that, the following instruments have been prepared and disclosed:

                         (i) Environment and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP): to agree on actions, timelines, and responsibilities;

                         (ii) Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP): to guide how meaningful and inclusive engagement can be undertaken with
                         stakeholders throughout the project lifecycle. It includes inputs on a functioning GRM, which will be accessible to all
                         stakeholders, including communities, workers, and vulnerable groups, including Grievance Redressal Mechanisms;

                         (iii) Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF): to cover environmental and social screening, risk
                         classification, mitigation, and monitoring, as well as institutional roles and responsibilities. It includes:
