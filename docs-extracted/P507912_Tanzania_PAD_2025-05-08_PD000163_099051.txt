 I. COUNTRY CONTEXT AND OPERATION SUMMARY
Operation Summary
1.        The proposed stand-alone Catastrophe Deferred Drawdown Option Development Policy Financing is an
International Development Association (IDA) credit of US$300 million (DPF of US$100 million and Cat DDO of US$200
million). The Tanzania DPF with Cat DDO introduces a comprehensive framework to (a) strengthen Tanzania’s disaster and
health-related emergency preparedness and response (EP&R) systems, (b) integrate resilience and risk prevention across
critical sectors, and (c) enhance economic resilience and establish a foundation for accessing global carbon markets. The
Program Development Objective (PDO) is to strengthen economic resilience and the technical, financial, and institutional
capacity to manage climate, fiscal, and disaster risks within the United Republic of Tanzania (URT). Prioritized by the
Government of Tanzania and backed by robust analytical work, the program will address ongoing climate and disaster
risks through reforms under three pillars. Pillar 1: Strengthening Institutional and Financial Capacity for Disaster
Preparedness and Response aims to strengthen the organized disaster response capabilities of the health and DRM
sectors. This pillar will increase human capacity within government systems for effective response; promote coordination
between the health and DRM sectors in areas of preparedness and response; and operationalize response teams,
enhancing readiness for both natural and health-related disasters. The pillar also promotes the establishment of flood
early warning systems (FEWS) and decision support systems in key flood-prone basins to safeguard at-risk populations,
improving disaster preparedness. The pillar will also support allocation of additional dedicated resources to the
Contingency Fund. Pillar 2: Mainstreaming Disaster Risk Management in Key Sectors aims to incorporate resilience and
risk prevention principles into the planning, decision-making, and management of essential infrastructure and services.
This ensures that current and future infrastructure investments in key sectors are risk-informed, contributing to long-term
resilience against climate-related and natural hazards. Specifically, the pillar will support efforts to reduce risk-prone urban
expansion through risk-informed urban planning and bolster the resilience of Zanzibar’s water and energy sectors. It will
do this by enhancing the reliability of power infrastructure and supporting sustainable management of the island’s
groundwater resources informed by aquifer monitoring and preventing overextraction and contamination, and mitigating
risks of saltwater intrusion. Pillar 3: Enhancing Economic Resilience and Establishing Foundations for Carbon Market Access
aims to enhance Tanzania’s economic resilience by strengthening institutional, financial, and regulatory frameworks, and
enabling access to global climate finance through carbon markets. The pillar promotes fiscal transparency, sustainable
investment, and accountability while integrating climate risk considerations into financial institutions’ decision-making to
support adaptation, mitigation, and long-term resilience to climate-related disasters.
Country Context
2.       The URT is the largest country in East Africa with approximately 945,087 km2 of area covering both the
territories of Mainland Tanzania and Zanzibar. Zanzibar is an archipelago located off the eastern coast of Africa, consisting
of two main islands—Unguja (the largest) and Pemba—and several smaller islands. Zanzibar is an autonomous region
within the URT with its own president, legislature, and legal system. There are currently 22 union matters under the
jurisdiction of the URT that apply to both Mainland Tanzania and Zanzibar. Nonetheless, Zanzibar has significant autonomy
on non-union matters, which include areas that are primarily internal to Zanzibar such as water resources management,
energy, environmental management, health, and DRM.

3.     Ranked 47th most vulnerable and 43rd least ready to adapt to climate change (out of 192 countries, 2022),1
Tanzania faces increasing exposure to disasters due to its growing population and economy. Impressive economic
growth in the URT since 2000 has propelled it into lower-middle-income status in 2020. At its current growth rate,

Tanzania’s population of 62 million people is expected to reach 118 million by 20502. However, Tanzania remains
vulnerable to risks. Over the past two decades, the country has experienced over 65 major events, with floods and
droughts accounting for 70 percent of natural disasters and causing annual economic losses of US$170 million3. Recent
cyclones, such as Kenneth (2019) and Jobo (2021), along with El-Niño rains and Tropical Cyclone Hidaya (2023–2024),
caused 155 deaths, affected over 200,000 people, and resulted in US$368 million in damages.4 In 2018, floods in Dar es
Salaam alone cost US$100 million (approximately 2 percent of gross domestic product [GDP]).5 The number of people
affected by drought, though wide-ranging, is significantly larger than those affected by floods, with some average annual
estimates reaching 4.8 million directly-affected people.6 Looking forward, estimates for Tanzania highlight that investing
in adaptation could reduce climate-related economic losses by 25 percent by 2050. However, available data indicate a
funding gap of more than US$12 billion for implementing the country’s 2021 updated Nationally Determined Contribution
(NDC). Carbon markets can contribute to achieving NDC targets as they can offer an important source of non-debt
financing for climate mitigation projects by providing an additional revenue stream.

4.       Tanzania is disproportionally exposed to flood risks due to increased seasonal variation in rainfall, with nearly
all urban municipalities affected by frequent and prolonged floods during rainy seasons. Further, climate change is
expected to cause an increase in the flood hazard levels.7 The urban population is increasingly exposed to disaster risks
due to poor settlement planning and inadequate development control mechanisms, compounded by a lack of adequate
drainage infrastructure and the encroachment of built areas on riverbanks and wetlands. Estimates suggest that between
1985 and 2015, settlement areas exposed to high flood risks grew by approximately 120 percent due to poor planning and
inadequate enforcement mechanisms.8 Tanzania’s major cities, such as Dar es Salaam and Zanzibar City, are low-lying
cities and extremely vulnerable to flooding. The impact in these cities is compounded by a sizeable percentage of residents
living in unplanned settlements—80 percent in Dar es Salaam, occupying on average 70 percent of land in urban areas.9
Rapidly growing secondary cities (for example, Mwanza, Ilemela, and Bukoba) face fluvial flooding risks. Low-lying coastal
cities including Mtwara, Lindi, Tanga, and Bagamoyo face pluvial flooding risks and impacts from sea level rise, which has
increased by 150 mm between 2000 and 2018. Under all climate futures, the number of people affected by different flood
intensities in these cities increases over time.10 Developing risk-informed plans for well-planned cities will avoid the
creation of risks as cities develop. Equally important, advancing toward the implementation of an FEWS will help urban
areas to reduce disaster risk and adapt to a changing climate.

5.      Climate change disproportionately affects Tanzania’s vulnerable populations, potentially pushing up to 1.17
million more people into poverty by 2050. The future poverty rate will depend on Tanzania’s economic path and climate
actions. Without such action, the World Bank projects a poverty rate of 4.2 percent by 2050, which could rise to 5.1
percent under severe dry and hot conditions, underscoring the intensified risks for vulnerable groups.11 Per the Tanzania
Country Climate and Development report, 27.7 percent of the population and 29.4 percent of the vulnerable population

are exposed to at least one climate risk. Tanzania’s major cities are extremely vulnerable to flooding, especially its low-
lying cities. Approximately 463 square kilometers of urban area are at high risk of flooding from rivers, rain, and sea level
rise. Annual damages across the transport infrastructure currently cost $108–109 million and could rise to $198–233

faces growing risks from flooding and sea level rise.

6.        Strengthening Tanzania’s economic resilience is a crucial priority for addressing climate-related risks. Investing
in resilient infrastructure could mitigate these risks but requires enhanced revenue mobilization to sustainably fund these
investments, which is a key policy priority. The Government also aims to strengthen economic resilience by maintaining
fiscal and debt sustainability by improving institutional, financial, and regulatory frameworks and increasing fiscal
transparency. Making the financial sector’s prudential framework more climate-informed is another priority area aimed
at facilitating efficient resource allocation and risk mitigation by the private sector.
7.      The significant disparities in water and electricity access and essential services compound the development
challenge. Poor access to water, sanitation, and hygiene alone costs approximately US$1.4 billion annually in lost
productivity, health care expenses, and premature deaths. Although access to electricity has improved in the country,
with households connected to the grid rising from 38 percent in 2009 to 57 percent in 2019/20,14 it remains a critical issue
in Zanzibar. Zanzibar’s energy sector, dependent on a single power line from the Mainland is highly vulnerable to the
impacts of climate change, which brings more frequent and severe storms, disrupting power distribution. Similarly,
Zanzibar’s water supply, which largely depends on seasonal rains and groundwater aquifers, faces increasing risks from
changing rainfall patterns and saline intrusion due to rising sea levels. Sustainable groundwater management requires
informed and systematic monitoring, which is essential to preserve this vital resource and ensure the resilience of
Zanzibar’s water supply system.
8.      The country’s capacity to prepare for and respond to disasters is outpaced by their increasing frequency and
intensity. The Prime Minister’s Office - Disaster Management Department (PMO-DMD) is the key institution for disaster
management in Mainland Tanzania. The PMO-DMD is responsible for formulating policies, coordinating disaster
management activities, and implementing disaster response strategies across Mainland Tanzania. Similarly, the
Commission for Disaster Management under the Second Vice President’s Office is the institution that is responsible for
comparable functions in Zanzibar. The DRM system is structured in both jurisdictions around national, regional, district,
and local (ward and village) levels of governance and response. The roles and responsibilities at each level are designed
to work in tandem to prevent, prepare for, respond to, and recover from disasters. These institutions are limited in
financial and technical capacity relative to the increasing frequency of disaster risk in Tanzania. The governance structures
and systems for disaster management in Mainland Tanzania are provided for in the 2022 Disaster Management Act.
However, subnational institutionalization of these arrangements is yet to be fully implemented. While Tanzania has
advanced its climate and DRM policy framework, gaps remain, which are to be addressed through reforms supported by
the DPF with Cat DDO.

9.       The macroeconomic framework of Tanzania is assessed as adequate for the proposed operation. While global
and climate risks remain substantial, Tanzania is appropriately balancing prudent macroeconomic policies, including
constraining non-concessional borrowing, and a pro-growth investment agenda, while continuing to make strides in
structural reforms. Ongoing implementation of the Blueprint, Tanzania’s business environment agenda, will continue to
drive the expansion of Tanzania’s private sector. Headline inflation is expected to remain within the target of 5 percent
whereas current account deficit (CAD) has declined and benefits from the increasing use of exchange rate flexibility for its
management. Strengthened revenue mobilization efforts, anchored in an on-track program with the International
Monetary Fund (IMF), combined with entrenched spending discipline, are expected to keep fiscal balances sustainable.
The Government is also committed to strengthening macroeconomic resilience through policy reforms that address the
risks that emanate from climate-related disasters. The economy’s and the Government’s ability to absorb external shocks,
including through debt are expected to retain some space to absorb shocks throughout the projection period, mitigates
residual risks.

10.     Tanzania has successfully navigated recent external and weather-related shocks, including the COVID-19 crisis,
tightening of global financing conditions, protracted droughts, and more frequent floods. Sound macroeconomic
fundamentals have underpinned the robust post-pandemic rebound. Tanzania’s economy is estimated to have grown at
a rate of 5.5 percent in 2024, picking up during the end of the year. The growth was primarily driven by industry and
services. The industry sector gradually regained momentum at 5.2 percent in 2024, mainly supported by the
manufacturing, electricity, and construction subsectors. Double-digit increases in tourism, coupled with buoyant
economic activities in trade, transport, and financial sectors, propelled the services sector to a growth of 6.7 percent.
Growth in agriculture was more modest at 4.1 percent in 2024.

11.     Helped by tight macroeconomic policies and an easing of food prices, inflation remains low. Headline inflation
has softened from a high of 4.3 percent in 2022 to 3.1 percent in 2024, well below the ceiling of 5 percent of the Bank of
Tanzania (BoT). Subdued food prices - emanating from cooled global commodity prices and bumper crop production
during the last harvest season - helped contain the inflationary pressure and significantly improved food security. The
monetary policy stance has also been prudent. Despite low inflation, the BoT raised its benchmark rate by 50 basis points
to 6 percent in April 2024 and has held it there since, in part to alleviate the pressures in the forex market. Consequently,
domestic liquidity in the financial system - dominated by two large and partially state-owned banks15 - tightened, and
private sector credit growth slowed to a still healthy 13 percent year-on-year. The banking sector operates well within
prudential guidelines, with nonperforming loans at 3.6 percent in February 2025, and is well-capitalized and highly
profitable, with returns on equity at 24.1 percent.

12.       Tanzania’s terms of trade have strengthened, and the narrowing CAD is increasingly financed by foreign direct
investments (FDIs). The CAD narrowed by half to 3.8 percent of GDP in 2023 and is estimated to have stood at a modest
2.3 percent of GDP in 2024. The deficit was funded by external borrowing and increasing FDI. Terms of trade have
improved due to higher prices for Tanzania’s commodities exports, including a surge in gold prices (about 22 percent of
exports), while the costs of many imports, especially petroleum products (about 19 percent of imports) and chemical
fertilizers (about 2 percent of imports), remained at moderate levels. At the same time, tourist arrivals (26 percent of
exports), which have exceeded their pre-pandemic levels since 2023, continued to expand in 2024, boosting service export
receipts. Combined with tight monetary and fiscal policies and strong foreign currency inflows, the increased exchange

18.      Tanzania’s most recent Joint IMF-World Bank Debt Sustainability Analysis (DSA) (June 2024) indicates that its
risk of external and public debt distress remains moderate, with some space to absorb shocks. By end-FY2022/23, total
debt held by the public sector stood at 46.7 percent of GDP (of which 45.7 percentage points were central government
debt and 1 percentage point domestic arrears), up from 43.6 percent of GDP in FY2021/22. While domestic debt has
increased in recent years, it stood at 17.2 percent of GDP at the end of FY2022/23. Meanwhile, external, including non-
concessional borrowing, has increased to finance the public infrastructure agenda. While projections for external debt
solvency and liquidity indicators remain below the policy-determined thresholds under the baseline scenario, the debt
service-to-export ratio and the present value of the debt-to-exports ratio surpassed their thresholds in the export-shock
stress test. Nonetheless, the present value of the public debt-to-GDP ratio and the debt service-to-revenue ratio are
projected to remain comfortably below Tanzania’s debt-carrying capacity benchmark. Public debt sustainability is most
exposed to a simulated one-time materialization of contingent liabilities and a major natural disaster. The authorities are
addressing these risks through coordinated policy reforms.16
19.      The macroeconomic outlook is broadly positive, but there are downside risks. Continued uncertainty around
tariffs and international trade and weakening global demand may adversely affect Tanzania’s growth prospects. Tanzania’s
increased spending ambitions against the background of still weak domestic revenue mobilization is a potential policy risk.
While double digit increases in planned expenditures in the next two years provides an opportunity to enhance service
delivery and avoid underinvestment in human capital, they also necessitate careful prioritization, particularly in public
investment, and improvements in revenues to ensure fiscal and macroeconomic sustainability. On the upside, more
decisive policy measures to improve the business environment could accelerate growth beyond current projections.
20.     Climate change and climate-related disasters remain a major risk for Tanzania. The WBG Tanzania CCDR finds
output losses, in some cases exceeding 4 percent, due to decreased labor productivity, impacts on livestock production
and infrastructure, and the effects of flooding and erosion, compared to a hypothetical scenario with no climate change
impacts over the coming 25 years. The effects on crop productivity depend on how climate change influences local rainfall
patterns. Climate action in line with Tanzania’s NDCs and structural transformation aligned with the country’s
development ambitions are found to leave Tanzanians better off across climate scenarios. The most recent DSA also finds
that increased investment in resilient infrastructure and greater revenue mobilization could abate climate-related
macroeconomic risks through debt channels.

 C. IMF Relations
21.     On April 17th, 2025, the IMF and Tanzanian authorities concluded Article IV discussions and reached staff-level
agreement on the Fifth Review of the Extended Credit Facility (ECF) and the Second Review of the Resilience and
Sustainability Facility (RSF). Tanzania is the second country to benefit from the IMF-World Bank enhanced cooperation
framework for scaled-up climate action. RSF reforms have been closely coordinated with World Bank programming,
leveraging World Bank analytics such as the CCDR and complementing ongoing and planned operations, including this DPF
with the Cat DDO. The US$790 million RSF aims to support the Government’s efforts to advance structural reforms and
investments in adaptation and mitigation to address climate change risks. Successful reviews in December 2024 made an
additional disbursement of US$205 million available to the authorities, bringing the cumulative disbursement under both
ECF and RSF arrangements to US$810 million. Near-term policy priorities under the US$1,046 million ECF program include
the implementation of fiscal consolidation as envisaged in the budget, while preserving priority social spending,
strengthening monetary operations, and continuing to allow exchange rate flexibility.

29.       This pillar aims to enhance Tanzania’s disaster and EP&R capacity by developing coordinated, multisectoral
systems to effectively manage climate-related events and health crises. Recognizing the increasing frequency of such
events, the focus includes establishing operational disaster response teams (staffed, trained, and equipped) across priority
regions. Within government systems, this pillar also strengthens health surveillance and response capacity through an
enhanced community health worker (CHW) program. Improved local health surveillance and response capacity will enable
early warning and timely action, reducing the impact of health crises on vulnerable populations. A critical innovation under
this pillar is the establishment of a fluvial FEWS in the high-risk Wami Ruvu river basin, complete with protocols for issuing
regular bulletins and warnings. This proactive approach ensures that communities receive timely information about
potential flood risks, enabling them to take precautions to protect lives, property, and livelihoods. The pillar will also
support the allocation of additional dedicated resources to the Contingency Fund, specifically earmarked for emergency
repairs and rehabilitation of road infrastructure following climate-related disaster events. By integrating these fiscal

30.     Rationale. The steady growth of climate and disaster risk exacerbated by climate change, including the increased
exposure of people and assets, combined with the lessons learned from previous disasters, indicate the need to further
strengthen disaster and EP&R. In July 2022, the Capacity for Disaster Reduction Initiative (CADRI) Partnership assisted in
conducting a comprehensive analysis of disaster and climate risk management capacities following a request from the
URT’s Government. The analysis, which covered both Mainland Tanzania and Zanzibar, identified several opportunities for
strengthening capacities within four priorities.19 Better coordinated, staffed, trained, and equipped disaster response
teams remain a critical gap at the regional level. Unlike Dar es Salaam City, which has a multi-agency emergency response
team, other highly exposed regions across Mainland Tanzania are yet to benefit from established, formalized, and
operational disaster response teams. The recently approved 2025 National Disaster Management Policy for Mainland
Tanzania notes that one of the shortcomings that led to the revision and update of the 2004 National Disaster
Management Policy was the lack of guidance for establishing and developing disaster response teams in line with
international standards. Further, it identifies challenges around training in disaster response skills.
31.     Policy content. The 2025 Disaster Management Policy for Mainland Tanzania formally integrates disaster
response teams into the disaster management institutional framework to support the functioning of the Disaster
Management Committees at the national, regional, district, and ward (community) levels. These teams are responsible

Pillar 2: Mainstreaming Disaster Risk Management in Key Sectors
43.      This pillar emphasizes the integration of DRM and climate change principles across urban planning, energy, and
water sectors in Tanzania, to incorporate resilience and risk prevention principles into the planning, decision-making, and
management of essential infrastructure and services. Given the rapid urbanization in high-risk areas in Tanzania, risk-
informed urban structure and master plans are needed to help regulate growth and reduce exposure to natural hazards.

change consideration, conducting proper consultation, using new technologies, and applying international best practices
are lacking. This pillar addresses this gap in Mainland Tanzania, in addition to key gaps in the water and energy sectors in
Zanzibar. Zanzibar, like many island states, is particularly vulnerable to disasters and climate change, and the water and

spelling out the governance, strategy, risk management, metrics, and targets they use to manage climate and sustainability
risks. The ISSB standards require the disclosure of information on sustainability and climate-related risks and opportunities
that could reasonably be expected to affect an entity’s cash flows, access to finance, or cost of capital over the short,
medium, or long term.
56.      Expected results. Enforcement of the guidelines will prioritize domestic systematically important banks. By 2028,
it is expected that commercial banks with a combined share of at least 70 percent of the total assets of Tanzania’s banking
sector will be in full compliance with the guidelines by fully incorporating risk management and disclosure practices and
thus strengthening the resilience of the financial sector while helping them leverage the opportunities.

58.      Policy content. The Carbon Trading Regulations establish the rules and procedures for the approval and
authorization of projects and programs to participate in carbon markets to mobilize additional climate finance for priority
climate projects. Adopting the Carbon Trading Regulations will allow Tanzania to establish the systems and requirements
needed to generate and monetize emission reduction credits through voluntary carbon markets. In Zanzibar, the 2023
amendment to the Zanzibar Environmental Management Act, No. 3 of 2015, aimed to advance the institutional framework
to enable access to international carbon markets and to include provisions for regulating carbon trade management and
other issues related to the environment. The amendment adds new measures on climate change, requiring ministries and
independent departments to put in place climate change strategies and review and approve international, domestic, and
private proposals to address climate change, including those related to land, water, forest, and ecosystems within
Zanzibar, while also coordinating carbon trading activities. Part 6 of the Zanzibar Carbon Trading Regulations includes
provisions for offences with criminal sanctions, which are not endorsed by the World Bank and could pose a reputational
risk if deemed excessive or wrongly applied. The World Bank’s interest in these regulations is limited to unlocking
Zanzibar’s potential for carbon trading, clarifying responsibilities for coordination and management, and providing clear
guidance to project proponents on government roles, and not the prosecution of sanctionable practices.

creating an enabling environment and guiding framework for participation in international carbon markets. The
regulations set out clear rules and procedures for participation in carbon markets, offering greater clarity for investors
seeking to participate in low-carbon projects. Furthermore, the regulations outline the decision-making process for
approving projects, which can improve transparency and build investor confidence. Support through technical assistance
will advance the development of guidance and procedures for participating in international carbon markets and the
establishment of a national carbon market registry to improve information flows by transparently tracking and recording
carbon market transactions. Progress will be measured through the number of carbon projects approved to generate
credits under the provisions of the carbon trading regulations (Baseline 2/Target 4).

 IV. OTHER DESIGN AND APPRAISAL ISSUES

 A. Poverty and Social Impacts
67.     Overall, the PAs are expected to reduce poverty by decreasing household vulnerabilities and mitigating the
effects of future climate-related disasters. The proposed policy actions aim to reduce poverty by addressing structural
inequalities and enhancing resilience to climate-related disasters. PAs 1–4 enhance local and national disaster response
capacities in the DRM, water, and health sectors, empowering local governments with improved EWSs and DRM
integration. This focus on preparedness and rapid response capabilities will better protect vulnerable groups, particularly


                                                                                                                     Page 23
       The World Bank
       United Republic of Tanzania - Development Policy Financing with a Catastrophe Deferred Drawdown Option
       (P507912)


those in informal urban areas, from the impacts of climate-related crises. PA3 may have short-term negative effects on
the poor but presents long-term benefits. The increase in the Railway Development Levy from 1.5 to 2.0 percent on
imports may contribute to inflationary pressures. However, the redirection of 50 percent of the levy proceeds to the
Contingency Fund for road infrastructure maintenance presents an opportunity for crucial long-term benefit by improving
connectivity and facilitating trade. PA5 mitigates risks associated with unplanned urban growth, where most of the urban
poor live, by promoting climate risk-informed urban planning practices. It effectively reduces disaster risks, contributing
to the long-term resilience of communities. Urban planning guidelines are an upstream instrument intended to provide
guidance for the development of master plans. They include provisions to minimize relocation, ensuring it is considered
only as a last resort. However, risk-informed land use planning, following these guidelines, may potentially contribute to
social risks related to preventive relocation from high-risk areas to safer locations, which could include loss of land, assets,
and livelihoods if poorly managed. To mitigate potential risks, the ongoing TACTIC Project (P171189) Investment Project
Financing (IPF) is financing the development of the first generation of master plans under these new guidelines, ensuring
alignment with Environmental Social Framework (ESF) policies and incorporating appropriate social assessments and
robust consultations with key stakeholders, including any affected social groups. PA6 is expected to improve water
monitoring, conservation, and access to clean and safe water, with the poorest households in Zanzibar benefiting the
most, thereby reducing their vulnerability to erratic rainfall patterns and water scarcity. By improving reliability, reducing
service interruption, and making energy more accessible to underserved populations, PA7 policies have the potential to
narrow the energy divide, enabling inclusive economic growth. By enabling access to carbon markets under PA9, Tanzania
can invest in sustainable agricultural practices, renewable energy projects, and essential infrastructure that directly reduce
the climate-related vulnerabilities faced by rural communities. See annex 3 for additional details.

 B. Environmental, Forests, and other Natural Resources Aspects

68.      The proposed policy actions are likely to have positive environmental impacts by reducing Tanzania’s
vulnerability to climate change and promoting low-carbon development. Pillar 1 focuses on strengthening institutional
capacity for disaster management, allowing timely responses to disasters that could damage natural habitats. Pillar 2
supports increased resilience in physical infrastructure, particularly in energy, urban, and water sectors, promoting
efficient pollution prevention and ecosystem conservation. Pillar 3 aims to enhance economic resilience and carbon
market access and climate finance, fostering low-carbon development and protecting biodiversity and ecosystems.
Overall, the reforms will help Tanzania better cope with the adverse effects of climate change.

 C. PFM, Disbursement, and Auditing aspects

69.       Tanzania’s public financial management (PFM) system has shown improvement, as evidenced by the 2022 PEFA
report compared to the 2017 PEFA report. Key advancements have been made in internal and external audit processes,
accounting, and financial reporting, supported by the introduction of the MUSE (Government Payment System) integrated
financial management system and the D-Fund system for managing donor-funded operations. Notable progress has also
been achieved in debt management, payroll controls, and procurement management, which are critical for efficient
service delivery. Additionally, the general government budget is now accessible on the MoF’s website. However, the PEFA
report highlights several areas requiring improvement, including the format and timeliness of budget documentation,
fiscal information, and in-year budget reports. Other challenges include enhancing public investment management, debt
transparency, medium-term expenditure budgeting, budget credibility, cash management, commitment controls, and the
monitoring of arrears and accounts payable. The World Bank-funded Public Finance Management and Procurement
Systems for Service Delivery Program (P180722) is effective and aims to address these challenges. The proposed operation
seeks to strengthen PFM and procurement systems by supporting the Government’s implementation of the PFMRP VII

strategy. It will specifically target gaps identified in the 2022 PEFA report, including improvements in budget
documentation, public investment management, and auditing processes.
70.      At present, public procurement in Mainland Tanzania is governed by Public Procurement Act (PPA), Cap 410
and its Regulations of 2024. The legal framework is based on the United Nations Commission on International Trade Law
(UNCITRAL) model. The legal framework is quite robust and covers all aspects of public procurement at all levels of
government and is internationally acceptable. Under the PPA 2023, the procurement function is decentralized to
procuring entities while the Public Procurement Regulatory Authority (PPRA) provides oversight functions. The public
procurement institutional setup comprises policy and monitoring functions and the implementation of the procurement
law. The PPA and its regulations provide for both competitive and less competitive methods of procurement; however,
competitive tendering is the default procurement method. Participation of state-owned enterprises is allowed under
certain conditions specified by the legislation. Standard Bidding Documents (SBDs) are developed by the PPRA and
available through NeST to all users; their level of detail varies according to the nature and complexity of the procurement.
The legal framework allows the use of price and non-price attributes and/or the consideration of life cycle cost, as
appropriate, to ensure objective and value-for-money decisions.
71.      The PPA and its regulations include provisions for emergency procurement, whereby the accounting officers
may determine that it is in the public interest to procure goods, works, or services as a matter of emergency. Under the
legislation, emergency procurement must meet one of the following criteria: (a) there is a compelling emergency that
poses a threat to life, health, welfare, or safety of the public due to a major natural disaster, epidemic, riot, war, fire, or
other similar events; (b) there is a situation whereby, without emergency procurement, the continued functioning of the
Government or organization would suffer irreparable loss or the preservation or protection of public property or the
health or safety of public will be threatened; and (c) there is an urgent need of health commodities in health care centers.
72.     The IMF safeguards assessment of the BoT (July 2022) found that the BoT has implemented relatively strong
safeguards, including financial reporting and internal audit. To strengthen the legal framework and ensure independent
oversight, the BoT will prepare draft amendments to the BoT Act, in consultation with IMF, to strengthen governance
arrangements as well as personal and financial autonomy, including addressing the composition of the Board to have a
clear majority of non-executive (independent) members, in line with leading practices.
73.     Disbursement mechanism, flow of funds, accounting, and audit arrangements. Disbursement of the proposed
credit will follow the ‘General Conditions for IDA Financing, Development Policy Financing’, dated December 14, 2018.
Once the Board approves the credit and it becomes effective, upon satisfaction of the trigger clause and at the request of
the borrower through a withdrawal application, the proceeds of the credit will be deposited by the DA into a designated
general budget support account at the BoT, forming part of the country’s foreign exchanges reserves. The borrower shall
ensure that, upon deposit into the said account, an equivalent amount in Tanzania shillings is credited to the borrower’s
consolidated fund account at the official exchange rate and captured as Development Revenue. This amount will then be
distributed to different votes based on budget allocations. The borrower will report to the World Bank on the amounts
deposited in the foreign currency account and credited to the Consolidated Fund account within 30 days after
disbursement. The proceeds of this credit allocated for disaster recovery will be ring-fenced for that purpose and be readily
available for responding to calamities with maximum urgency. The administration of this credit will be the responsibility
of the MoF. The proposed credit finances the Consolidated Fund, which is subject to audit by the CAG. The CAG is
mandated by law to present the audit report on the public accounts to Parliament within nine months of the financial
year-end.

 V. SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION
76.     The overall risk rating for the operation is Moderate. The ratings for all risks categories are provided in table 5,
with two risks to achieving the development objective of this DPF with Cat DDO rated substantial. These are institutional
capacity for implementation and sustainability and environment and social risk categories.
77.      Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability risk category is rated Substantial. The major risks
for the implementation of the reform program are associated with (a) limited institutional capacity, particularly around
the climate finance and carbon market agenda; (b) resource allocation across sectors for implementation; (c) potential
challenges in maintaining strong coordination between stakeholders and development partners; and (d) continued
financing for operation and maintenance of water monitoring systems and training and equipping of personnel. To
mitigate these risks, the policy reform program will be complemented by parallel technical assistance programs (described
in paragraph 28). Technical assistance will also be provided to address challenges during implementation and promote
strong coordination with development partners, ensuring alignment around reform priorities. Given the multisectoral
nature of this DPF with Cat DDO, the task team will also ensure close engagement with the MoF, which has a central
leadership role in coordinating program implementation.
78.     Environmental and Social Risks are rated Substantial. Integrating sustainable urban planning can reduce urban
expansion into risk-prone areas, but implementation could pose risks of land and livelihood losses, particularly in informal
settlements. Mitigation measures adopted include participatory land regularization processes and clear procedures to
ensure adherence to national laws and international standards when relocation is unavoidable. This operation also
emphasizes proactive urban expansion in secondary cities, where new growth is anticipated (rather than retroactive
interventions), minimizing displacement risks and ensuring social safeguards such as mandatory Social Impact
Assessments and grievance mechanisms are in place. In addition to applying the guidelines to the development of the first
generation of master plans under the TACTIC IPF building capacity and institutionalizing the best practices laid out in the
guideline, technical assistance will be provided to national and local authorities during implementation of the Cat DDO, to
support effective application of these guidelines and prevent unexpected adverse impacts.

Poverty and Social Impact Analysis
1.       The proposed policy actions are expected to reduce poverty by decreasing household vulnerabilities and
mitigating the effects of future climate-related disasters. A recent analysis conducted for Tanzania’s CCDR highlights that
the poorest households are disproportionately vulnerable to climate shocks. These households, often located in North-
Western and Southern Tanzania, face significant challenges such as lower levels of education, limited access to health care,
and heavy reliance on agriculture for their livelihoods. They also lack essential public services such as improved drinking
water, sanitation, and electricity, and often live in inadequate housing with mud walls, earth floors, and grass roofs. Given
this heightened vulnerability, the actions in this operation are likely to reduce poverty in the medium term by addressing
the structural inequalities that leave these communities most exposed to climate shocks.
2.      PA1 on improving prevention and response capacities at both the national and local levels is set to protect
vulnerable populations from the impact of disasters by enhancing local government capabilities, advancing EWSs, and
integrating DRM into broader development plans. Strengthened local governments will be instrumental in mitigating risks
faced by diverse vulnerable groups, from farmers to residents of informal urban settlements. By empowering local
authorities—who are frontline responders and primary points of contact for at-risk populations, PA1 will facilitate swift
and effective crisis management. These enhanced local capacities are expected to improve disaster preparedness, enabling
more timely and targeted responses.
3.      PA2 marks an important step toward addressing health inequalities and enhancing resilience for the poor.
Vulnerable populations in Tanzania face barriers to accessing health care, with unexpected medical expenses often pushing
households into financial distress, depleting savings, and accumulating debt. The fifth wave of the National Panel Survey
reveals that only 6 percent of households who sought health care financed their treatment through insurance, with a
pronounced disparity across income groups: just 7.4 percent of those in the lowest consumption quintile relied on
insurance, compared to 45.0 percent among the wealthiest quintiles. The vulnerable remain highly exposed to financial

                                                                                                                                  43
       The World Bank
       United Republic of Tanzania - Development Policy Financing with a Catastrophe Deferred Drawdown Option (Cat
       DDO) (P507912)


shocks from health emergencies. By enhancing coordination and response capacity, PA2 is likely to reduce the burden on
low-income households by streamlining and improving access to health resources during crises, thereby helping mitigate
the risk of catastrophic health expenses that can trap families in cycles of poverty.
4.      PA3, aimed at strengthening crisis preparedness and fiscal resilience, may have short-term negative effects on
the poor but presents long-term benefits. The increase in the Railway Development Levy from 1.5 to 2.0 percent on
imports may contribute to inflationary pressures, depending on the extent to which importers pass the additional costs to
consumers, what proportion of consumption is from imported goods, and the elasticity of substitution. If a significant
portion is transmitted to final prices and there are no alternatives, the poorest households could see a reduction in their
real consumption, potentially affecting their welfare. However, the redirection of 50 percent of the levy proceeds to the
Contingency Fund for road infrastructure maintenance presents an opportunity for crucial long-term benefit. Poor road
conditions disproportionately affect low-income and rural communities by limiting market access and raising transport
costs. By improving connectivity and facilitating trade, better road networks can enhance economic opportunities,
ultimately supporting rural development and poverty reduction, helping offset some of the short-term risks associated with
price increases. PA4, meanwhile, is expected to safeguard agricultural livelihoods by facilitating timely evacuations and
crop-saving interventions, mitigating poverty from property and harvest losses.
5.       PA4 and PA6, which focus on water and sanitation, address a critical vulnerability intensified by climate change.
Households with limited access to water are disproportionately vulnerable, with only 13 percent of those in the bottom
quintile of income distribution having access to piped water, compared to 29 percent in the richest quintile. Additionally,
65 percent of households rely on rivers for irrigation, and many depend on rivers and boreholes for livestock, making water
access essential to their livelihoods. This is especially true for farmers and pastoralists, who are highly vulnerable to shifts
in water availability. Therefore, conservation efforts must carefully balance environmental sustainability with the water
needs of these communities to support their resilience. Implementing mitigation measures, such as promoting water-
efficient technologies like drip irrigation, will be essential to ensuring agricultural productivity while safeguarding long-term
sustainability. Moreover, involving local communities in water management decisions will help create balanced outcomes
that address both conservation and livelihood needs. PA4 is expected to improve the riverine EWS, which will be important
to protect vulnerable parts of the community that are usually affected the most by natural disasters. PA6 is expected to
improve water monitoring, conservation, and access to clean and safe water, with the poorest households benefiting the
most. Since the poor are more likely to derive their livelihoods from agriculture and are highly dependent on water
availability, these efforts will directly reduce their vulnerability to erratic rainfall patterns and water scarcity. By enhancing
their resilience to climate shocks, PA4 and PA6 will likely contribute to long-term poverty reduction.
6.       PA5, which focuses on guiding urban development and controlling informal settlements, will help vulnerable
households better cope with climate impacts. Currently, 70 percent of the urban population lives in unplanned,
unsurveyed areas, making them highly susceptible to floods, landslides, and other climate-related hazards. Additionally,
67.1 percent of buildings in Tanzania are constructed on unplanned land, with 33.3 percent lacking formal ownership
documentation, highlighting the critical role of secure land tenure in urban resilience. However, the implementation of
resilient urban planning measures could risk displacing vulnerable populations living on unregulated land, as nearly 47
percent of Tanzanians ages 15 and older own land without legal documentation. To mitigate these risks, participatory land
regularization processes that involve affected communities in urban planning decisions will be crucial. Additionally, urban
plans that will be financed by the World Bank’s TACTIC Project (P171189) will be subject to ESF standards on resettlement
and compensation that will guide implementation while also building the capacity and experience of local governments.
7.     PA7, which focuses on enhancing energy access and resilience, is likely to disproportionately benefit the poor by
addressing critical vulnerabilities that impede poverty reduction. Currently, only about 5 percent of households in the
bottom income quintile use electricity for lighting, compared to 40 percent in the richest quintile. This stark disparity
underscores the inequities in energy access, which particularly affect rural and low-income households. Limited access to

energy not only hinders the expansion of non-agricultural economic activities but also restricts access to essential services
such as health care and education, crucial for human capital development. By improving energy infrastructure and making
it more accessible to underserved populations, PA7 policies have the potential to narrow the energy divide, enabling
inclusive economic growth and contributing significantly to long-term poverty alleviation, especially among the poorest
communities.
8.      PA8 and PA9 are expected to contribute to Tanzania’s financial stability and expand access to climate finance
and carbon markets. While these measures lay a foundation for sustainable growth, particularly in rural areas where
agriculture remains the primary livelihood source, their direct poverty and social impacts are likely to be small.
Strengthening the resilience of the banking system and unlocking climate finance could facilitate investments in climate-
smart agriculture, renewable energy, and infrastructure, potentially reducing climate-related vulnerabilities for rural
communities. However, financial sector reforms may also introduce unintended effects, such as more cautious lending
practices if climate risks are perceived as high. Recognizing these complexities, the overall impact on poverty will depend
on how effectively these reforms translate into increased access to finance for vulnerable households and productive
investments in key sectors.
9.      PA10, which focuses on enhancing accountability and transparency in public debt management, is an important
step toward prudent fiscal management. Improved reporting can strengthen oversight and investor confidence, which
may help maintain sustainable debt levels and create fiscal space for essential public investments. However, the impact on
low-income households is likely to be modest and indirect, as benefits will materialize through broader fiscal stability and
more predictable public service delivery.


Summary of Complementarities between IMF and WBG-Supported Programs (Pilot for Enhanced Framework for IMG-
WBG Collaboration on Climate Only)
1.       The Government of Tanzania is implementing a comprehensive reform agenda to enhance macroeconomic
resilience and strengthen its capacity to manage climate-related risks. The proposed World Bank DPF with Cat DDO
complements ongoing programs supported by the IMF and WBG, reinforcing a coordinated approach to fiscal sustainability
and climate resilience.

2.       The IMF’s ECF and RSF support the macro-fiscal foundation for Tanzania’s structural and climate-focused
reforms. The ECF supports growth-friendly fiscal consolidation, revenue mobilization, and expenditure efficiency, while the
RSF anchors the Government’s climate reform agenda. Specifically, the RSF enables policy and regulatory enhancements
that (a) strengthen the institutional and legal framework for climate change management, (b) enhance DRM and social
protection, (c) promote climate-resilient PFM, (d) encourage climate-resilient sectoral policies, and (e) mainstream climate
risk management in the financial sector. This DPF with Cat DDO reinforces and builds upon these policy advances across all
IMF reform areas while being sufficiently distinct from the RSF reform measures to ensure additionality. For example, while
the RSF enhances governance of climate change through strengthening of institutional frameworks and development of
various vulnerability maps, the DPF with Cat DDO enhances governance and coordination of DRM and will make use of
some of the vulnerability maps. Similarly, while the IMF program focuses on a broad set of disaster risk financing measures,
mainstreaming DRM into sectors, and strengthening emergency preparedness, the DPF with Cat DDO focuses on
strengthening specific basin-wide EWSs, improving city- and community-level disaster response teams, improving
coordination between health emergency and climate-related disaster responses at village level, and mainstreaming DRM
in the water and energy sectors.
3.     In parallel, the World Bank’s Enhanced Cooperation Framework for Climate Action, under which Tanzania is the
second beneficiary country, ensures complementary sectoral interventions. Joint program planning meetings were held

       The World Bank
       United Republic of Tanzania - Development Policy Financing with a Catastrophe Deferred Drawdown Option (Cat
       DDO) (P507912)


between the World Bank and IMF, resulting in a complementary matrix of World Bank support and IMF reform measures.
In total, 14 IMF reform measures build on World Bank programming, including the operation (P507912), recent
Development Policy Financing (P507912), an ongoing PFM-focused Program-for-Results (P180722), and sectoral
investment projects such as the Second Tanzania Water Sector Support Project (P150361).
4.      The IMF’s macroeconomic surveillance and fiscal policy measures provide a stable foundation for the World
Bank’s development policy engagements, ensuring that climate-related expenditures remain fiscally and externally
sustainable. Moreover, the joint IMF-WBG technical support enables a coherent strategy for scaling up private sector
financing for climate adaptation, ensuring that Tanzania can leverage concessional financing and market-based instruments
to address climate vulnerabilities.
5.      The combined support of the IMF and the WBG creates a synergistic policy framework where fiscal sustainability,
climate resilience, and disaster preparedness are mutually reinforcing. The World Bank’s Cat DDO provides the
Government with a contingent financing mechanism, while the IMF’s ECF and RSF secure macroeconomic stability and
long-term climate policy reforms. This coordinated approach ensures that Tanzania is better equipped to respond to
external shocks while maintaining a sustainable growth trajectory.
