    I.   STRATEGIC CONTEXT
    A. Country Context
    1. Bangladesh has achieved rapid economic and social progress in recent decades, reaching lower middle-income
    status in 2015. Stable macroeconomic conditions underpinned an average annual real Gross Domestic Product (GDP)
    growth of 6.3 percent between 2011 and 2024, with notable reductions in poverty. However, the pace of GDP growth
    and poverty reduction slowed down over the past few years. An interim government was sworn in August 8, 2024, after
    the former Prime Minister resigned amid a mass uprising.
    2. Real GDP growth remained relatively resilient in Fiscal Year (FY) 25, declining marginally to 4.0 percent from 4.2
    percent in FY24, despite a FY25 first-quarter slump linked to the student-led mass uprising. External pressures subsided
    and exports rebounded after a sharp contraction in FY24. Inflation averaged 10 percent in FY25 and marginally eased to
    average 8.4 percent in the first quarterquarterQ1 of FY26. Monetary policy has remained tight to rein in inflation.
    Investment activities continue to be subdued amid political uncertainty, elevated raw material and borrowing costs, and
    lending restrictions at some banks. The financial sector remains vulnerable to high nonperforming loans and weak
    governance, prompting corrective actions by the government.
    3. The current account balance has improved, while the fiscal deficit widened. The current account moved from deficit
    to a surplus in FY25, driven by a sharp increase in remittances and exports. The exchange rate has remained stable in
    recent months, and there has been a slow reversal of the depletion of foreign exchange reserves, which stood at US$26.5
    billion (3.5 months of prospective import coverage) as of September 2025, up from US$19.9 a year earlier. In contrast,
    the fiscal deficit is estimated to have widened to 4.7 percent of GDP in FY25, up from 3.9 percent in FY24, reflecting
    moderate tax revenue growth alongside higher current expenditures.
    4. Real GDP growth is projected to rise to 4.8 percent in FY26, driven by rising private consumption aided by moderating
    inflation. Investment is expected to improve relative to FY25 but remain sluggish, reflecting election-related political
    uncertainty and vulnerabilities in the banking sector. Export growth is expected to stay robust despite global tariff
    uncertainty. From FY27, growth is projected to accelerate as investment picks up. The current account balance is likely
    to revert to a deficit as imports normalize, while the fiscal deficit is expected to remain below 5 percent of GDP. Reform
    priorities include improving the business environment to create more and better jobs, increasing domestic resource
    mobilization, stabilizing and deepening the financial sector, diversifying exports, and investing in human capital.
    5. Bangladesh remains highly vulnerable to climate change due to its low-lying geography, dense population, and high
    dependence on water resources. The country faces increasing risks from floods, sea level rise, cyclones, droughts, and
    salinity intrusion, particularly in coastal and riverine areas. In Dhaka, climate impacts are compounded by severe water
    pollution in the city’s rivers, resulting from inadequate sanitation systems and poor domestic and industrial waste
    management. These factors heighten public health risks disproportionately affecting the poorest communities. These
    combined threats posed by climate hazards and water pollution underscore the urgent need for effective adaptation,
    improved water management, and resilience measures.
    B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
    6. Dhaka, the economic engine of Bangladesh, generates half of the country's formal employment, contributes 35
    percent to national GDP, and is home to 80 percent of export-oriented garment factories that have been instrumental
    to poverty reduction. But rapid urbanization and industrial growth have outpaced the city's capacity to manage water
    demand, wastewater, and pollution. The result is a severe decline in water quality, with profound implications for public
    health, environmental sustainability, and economic growth. The Dhaka metropolitan region1 (Metro Dhaka) now

    source of domestic supply, is also contaminated by effluent infiltration, leaking sewers, and unsewered sanitation
    systems.3 Consequently, water-borne diseases remain prevalent, and the combined environmental, health, and
    economic costs of river pollution in Dhaka are estimated at US$2.83 billion annually.4
    7. Dhaka’s municipal wastewater infrastructure is grossly inadequate. The city’s piped sewerage system covers only 20
    percent of the population, and an additional 2 percent have access to functional fecal sludge management (FSM) systems.
    This means that over 80 percent of wastewater and sewage—about 1,500 MLD—flows untreated into rivers, often via
    unauthorized sewer connections to stormwater canals. Existing sewage treatment plants (STPs) operate below capacity
    due to insufficient sewage networks and household connections, allowing more contaminated water to reach the rivers.
    Sanitation coverage is even lower in neighboring cities like Gazipur, Narayanganj, and Savar, further intensifying pollutant
    loads discharged into Dhaka’s interconnected waterways.
    8. Industrial wastewater is a major contributor to Dhaka’s pollution crisis. Over 7,000 factories—including textiles,
    dyeing, tanneries, and pharmaceuticals—discharge an estimated 2,400 MLD of untreated effluent into rivers and canals,
    often in violation of environmental regulations. The lack of compliance with effluent treatment standards is due to weak
    enforcement mechanisms and the perception that the rivers and canals are a low-cost waste disposal option. While all
    industries are required by law to install and operate effluent treatment plants (ETP), most do not operate them at all,
    while others do so intermittently. Industrial wastewater pollution is linked to a range of health issues, including skin
    infections, neurological conditions5, and diarrheal diseases, and undermines both public and environmental health.
    9. Solid waste management (SWM) systems are weak and inadequate in Dhaka, leading to dumping and littering of
    waste. Between 50 and 70 percent of the city’s 10,000 tons of daily municipal solid waste is dumped directly in rivers
    and canals or in adjacent dumpsites, exacerbating water pollution. Waste collection is uneven, largely managed by local
    private operators with little accountability to the City Corporations (CCs) and weak coverage of poorer neighborhoods
    where operations are less profitable. Existing disposal systems are saturated and unsafe, requiring urgent capacity
    upgrades and technological improvements. Efforts to treat, recycle, and recover waste are grossly inadequate. CCs have
    initiated several new projects to develop sanitary landfill, waste treatment facilities, but further dedicated efforts are
    required to improve collection, sorting, and diversion. In addition, integrated SWM system planning is needed to address
    the service needs linked to future urban expansion.
    10. The pollution crisis is compounded by poorly managed urban drainage and canal networks. Over half of Dhaka’s
    natural drainage channels have disappeared or are clogged due to unplanned urban growth, encroachment, solid waste
    dumping, and lack of maintenance. As a result, polluted water remains stagnant, with prolonged waterlogging in mostly
    low-income settlements, creating significant public health risks.
    11. At the national level, the institutional and governance framework for water pollution reduction and river
    restoration is fragmented, and inter-institutional coordination is weak. River restoration agenda requires strong
    coordination between multiple ministries and departments at the national level, as well as local agencies responsible for
    service delivery. The Department of Environment (DoE) is the primary regulatory authority for controlling water pollution.
    The Bangladesh Water Development Board (BWDB) oversees major river restoration and flood control, whereas the

    and waste management services appear to recover costs, but this masks the reality of underfunding and insufficient
    investment in effective service delivery. Both DWASA and CCs lack the creditworthiness to access private and commercial
    capital for critical infrastructure investments.
    14. Improving Dhaka’s water quality requires an integrated, multi-sectoral approach that combines policy,
    institutional, and regulatory reforms with a sequenced set of priority infrastructure investments. Key actions include
    accelerating access to safely managed sanitation and solid waste management, supporting policy reforms and regulatory
    capacity for pollution control, enhancing revenue generation and financial sustainability, and strengthening institutional
    capacity and coordination. This program supports the Government of Bangladesh (GoB) in initiating these critical
    foundational interventions that can be scaled through subsequent phases. The rationale for public investment is clear:
    the scale of market failures, externalities, and public health risks associated with water and solid waste pollution far
    exceeds what the private sector can address alone. Public investment is essential to de-risk and catalyze private capital,
    particularly through blended finance and enabling reforms that improve creditworthiness. The project will leverage public
    resources to crowd in private investment, support regulatory and institutional reforms, and lay the foundation for
    sustainable, scalable solutions to Dhaka's sanitation and pollution challenges.

    less complex activities on the ground that can start reducing pollution discharge into water bodies and undertaking the
    necessary preparatory activities including city-wide investment plans, feasibility studies, surveys, detailed engineering
    designs and planning for larger infrastructure projects (such as sewage treatment plants, sanitary landfills, common
    effluent treatment plants (CETP) etc.). This also includes: (i) establishing a geographic information system (GIS)-based
    database of the major polluters and point pollution sources; (ii) setting up a comprehensive water quality index for
    Dhaka’s rivers and digital real-time pollution tracking and water quality monitoring systems to support evidence based
    decision making and regulatory enforcement; and (iii) strengthening the monitoring capacity through increased
    manpower, frequent inspections and sample investigations following a risk-based approach.
    20. Sub-program II – Water quality improvement. This will include activities related to: (i) sustainable planning and
    management of river pollution, ecological quality, and water flows; (ii) clean-up, excavation, and solid waste removal and
    debris management in priority catchments; and (iii) demarcation of the boundaries of the water bodies through surveys

    developing database and plans for restoring the water flow capacity of rivers and canals through clean-up and

 G. Lessons Learned and Reflected in Program Design
 34. A combination of results-based financing mechanisms and technical assistance is an effective instrument to
 support and incentivize local service delivery institutions to shift towards improved performance in an incremental
 manner. This is a long-term agenda, and it takes time to deliver impactful results, as seen in several World Bank funded
 local government and WSS programs in Africa, Asia and Latin America. PforR Programs have successfully delivered results
 in terms of capacity development, institutional performance and service delivery improvement in multiple cities, such as
 the Chennai City Partnership in India and Casablanca Municipal Support Program in Morocco. Implementation experience
 of the ongoing Dhaka Sanitation Improvement Project and Dhaka City Neighborhood Upgrading Project also show that
 infrastructure improvement without institutional strengthening is neither sufficient nor effective.
 35. Addressing water pollution in mega cities and metropolitan regions requires wider engagement and coordination
 amongst multiple agencies at national and local levels. Addressing inter-agency coordination issues is difficult but the
 World Bank programs can operationalize effective mechanisms and provide handholding support to incrementally
 address these issues through capacity building and targeted incentives for improved service performance. Engaging
 multiple agencies also fosters a positive competition to deliver results, creates an effective peer to peer learning
 environment, and provides flexibility to deliver overall results targeted under the Program by leveraging on the different
 paces of implementation by different agencies.
 36. Pollution reduction requires a comprehensive, integrated approach for the implementation of sustainable
 solutions. This includes waste management infrastructure and service delivery systems, strengthening policy
 implementation and regulatory enforcement, and widespread outreach and community engagement. The Bank has
 supported several river pollution and restoration programs in China, Brazil, Colombia, India and several other countries.
 The key learning is that pollution reduction is an incremental process and requires an integrated approach that addresses
 the fresh waste issues through better service delivery and treatment infrastructure, legacy waste issues through right
 technical solutions, strict regulatory enforcement and compliance monitoring backed by robust data systems and risk-
 based approaches, and awareness generation for behavior change and social accountability. Citizens, private sectors, and
 communities have a major role to play in ensuring that polluting activities are controlled and managed sustainably.

 IV. ASSESSMENTS SUMMARY
 A. Technical
 49. The Program is fully aligned with GoB’s policy priorities and is strategically relevant as it seeks to address Dhaka’s
 critical water pollution issues. The Program supports GoB’s Blue Network program, leverages strong government
 ownership at national level and responds to local priorities and demands of the participating agencies. In alignment with
 the GoB’s approach under the Blue Network program, the Program design seeks to address the major sources of river
 pollution and address critical capacity gaps while increasing preparedness for more complex pollution-control and river
 restoration measures in subsequent phases.
 50. The Program design is grounded in a technically sound and incremental strategy which focuses on sustainable
 solutions for combatting the key sources of river pollution. The design is informed by a series of detailed technical,
 institutional and financial assessments, including: (i) recommendations from the World Bank-supported UIP and Water
 Sector Assessment and Prioritization (WaterSAP); (ii) sector-specific deep dives on investment needs in the three cities;
 (iii) structured technical assessments and due-diligence, including site visits, for investment identification, screening,
 scoping, and prioritization based on readiness, alignment with program objectives, and potential impact; (iv) close
 engagement and dialogue with key implementing agencies as well as relevant sectoral ministries, Economic Relations
 Department (ERD) and Planning Commission; and (v) detailed financial analyses of DWASA and the CCs, using 3–5 years
 of financial data to evaluate their fiscal health and identify structural constraints to creditworthiness.
 51. Under a balanced and integrated approach, the Program prioritizes improved wastewater and solid waste
 management services to reduce the fresh waste being discharged by cities into water bodies. By prioritizing sanitation
 infrastructure and solid waste collection, authorities can reduce the volume of raw sewage and waste that flow untreated
 into rivers, delivering immediate improvements in water quality. The prioritized activities are focused more on improving
 service delivery in an incremental way instead of just focusing on infrastructure asset creation. The Program will support
 all the agencies in strengthening the systems for long-term integrated sector planning. These activities will be reinforced
 by awareness generation, community engagement and advocacy for behavior change to prevent wastewater and solid
 waste dumping into water bodies. All interventions will be implemented in coordinated manner along with canal and
 drainage improvement for demonstration impact in terms of reduced pollution and improved water flows.
 52. The Program design seeks to create a successful model for restoration of critical waterways through an integrated
 approach that targets reduction in pollution loads and enabling improvement in water quality over time. The Program
 incentivizes the agencies in Dhaka to coordinate for the design and implementation of interventions in targeted
 catchments to achieve visible results on the ground. The identified priority interventions will focus on upgrades to
 drainage networks, capacity enhancements, canal rehabilitation, pollution control and waste removal, and nature-based
 solutions like bio-swales, retention basins, and green corridors to enhance water absorption and improve water quality.

 B. Fiduciary
 73. An Integrated Fiduciary System Assessment (IFSA) was conducted to assess the adequacy of the fiduciary system
 (FM, Procurement and GAC). The objective was to assess if the existing systems provide reasonable assurance that funds
 will be used for their intended purposes, based on the principles of economy, efficiency, integrity, effectiveness,
 transparency, and accountability. The assessment has identified the Program's fiduciary risk as ‘Substantial’ in
 accordance with the World Bank’s PforR Financing Bank Guidance (January 9, 2024). The fiduciary and anticorruption
 systems within the relevant Program boundary were evaluated for the LGD, DWASA, DNCC, DSCC, NCC, WARPO and
 BWDB. Lessons from recently implemented Bank operations with some of these agencies, the Bangladesh PEFA report
 published in 2023, Assessment of Bangladesh Public Procurement System 2020, the PFM reform action plan 2024-28,
 and other recent fiduciary systems assessments by the World Bank for three other PforR Operations23 were taken into
 consideration. Moreover, the assessment has identified fraud and corruption risks and effective mitigation measures.
 74. Fiduciary Risk and Mitigation Measures: The IFSA identified risks related to capacity, institutional governance and
 oversight, and budgetary factors, and developed ex-ante and ex-post mitigation measures in various forms, such as
 conditions for disbursement, Program Action Plan (PAP) actions, dated covenants, and DLRs as well as capacity support
 under the IPF component. The key risks include limited fiduciary capacity for FM, procurement and contract
 management, delays in procurement activities, insufficient budget allocation and fund release, inadequate financial
 report disclosure, audit delays, weak internal controls, inadequate use of e-GP, delays in bid evaluation and contract
 award, weak contract management, and ineffective internal audit processes. The recommended mitigation measures
 comprise comprehensive training in fiduciary responsibilities (such as procurement, contract management, financial
 management, governance, and anti-corruption), creating Procurement Unit at the agency and the ministry as per Public
 Procurement Rules 2025, nominating evaluation committees with at least 2 out of 5 members from outside the agency,
 conducting annual procurement post review by an independent consultant supervised by Bangladesh Public Procurement
 Authority (BPPA), strengthening risk-based internal audits, semi-annual disclosure of program financial statements,
 mapping program expenditures against DLI costs, prompt resolution of audit observations, integrating iBAS++ into
 financial management operations in CCs and DWASA, and building institutional capacity for financial management and
 accounting. There will be a legal covenant that any procurement (other than the IPF Component) conducted outside e-
 GP shall not be eligible expenditure for the Program.

 83. The E&S risks of the PforR Program are rated as Substantial, primarily due to the nature of the activities to be
 implemented by CCs and DWASA, and the weak institutional capacities and experience of the agencies in managing

 such projects and NCC does not have such experience. The E&S rating of the IPF component is rated as high considering
 the potential downstream risks related to the implementation of the activities in the IPF component – specifically related
 to preparation of frameworks, plans, studies and detailed reports for investments to be funded in subsequent phases.
