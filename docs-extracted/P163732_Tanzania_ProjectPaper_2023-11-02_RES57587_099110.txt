    I. PROGRAM STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING

A          Program Status

1.      The Tanzania Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Program (SRWSSP) is a US$655 million Program-
for-Results (PforR) operation. The Program was approved on June 26, 2018, with an initial IDA credit of SDR 243.4 million
(US$350 million equivalent) and became effective on July 15, 2019. Additional Financing (AF) of SDR 234.4 million (US$300
million equivalent) and a grant of US$4.931 million from the Energy Sector Management Assistance Program (ESMAP)
were approved on December 14, 2022. Both were declared effective on April 17, 2023. The Program closing date is July
31, 2025. As of October 30, 2023, the Program has disbursed 69.44 percent (US$449.18 million equivalent).

    2.     The Program Development Objectives (PDO) of SRWSSP is to increase access to rural water supply and
    sanitation services in participating districts and strengthen the capacity of selected sector institutions to sustain service
    delivery. The Program is in its fifth year of implementation, and both progress towards achieving the PDO and overall
    implementation progress are rated Satisfactory (S). Currently, the Program covers 137 rural districts in 25 regions. The
    Program has three Results Areas and an IPF component. Each result area and associated DLIs are summarized as below:
            (i) Results Area 1- Sustainable access to improved water services in rural areas, providing increased access to
                  well-designed, properly constructed, and well-managed rural water supply services (both new and
                  rehabilitated) and sustaining existing water points to ensure they remain functional and provide adequate
                  service levels (DLI #1, 2 and 10).
            (ii) Results Area 2- Increased access to improved sanitation services in rural areas, achieved through promotion
                  of hygiene practices and improved sanitation at household level; construction and rehabilitation of
                  institutional water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) facilities at primary schools and health care facilities; and
                  Community-Wide Sanitation (DLI #3, 4, 5 and 9).
            (iii) Results Area 3 - Strengthening of the capacity of sector institutions to sustain service delivery in rural areas,
                  through better data management for reporting and decision-making; strengthening of technical and financial
                  capacities of Community-based Water Supply Organizations (CBWSOs); and establishment and
                  operationalization of the Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Authority (RUWASA) (DLI #6, 7, and 8).
            (iv) Investment Project Financing (IPF) component (US$20 million) to provide technical support to address
                  capacity gaps and institutional deficiencies.

3.       More than 4.75 million people have gained access to improved water supply with adequate pressure and closer
to their houses, over three years of verified results under Results Area 1 (Sustainable access to improved water services
in rural areas). Results of the sustainably functioning public water points (WPs) and household connections as of June 30,
2023, are 41,440 and 21,283, respectively. During the third year of verification, the number of LGAs that met the 80
percent threshold were 61 out of 86.

4.     Results Area 2 (Sustainable access to improved sanitation services in rural areas), 6.61 million people have
gained access to improved sanitation facilities.1 Cumulatively, there are totally 552 villages that have achieved
community-wide sanitation (CWS),2 and 165 of them have sustained the status. It is slightly below the yearly target of 650.

5.        Result Area 3 (Strengthen the capacity of sector institutions to sustain service delivery in rural areas) has
continued to progress well. Cumulatively, 7,283 villages were under the management of registered CBWSOs with
improved operation and maintenance (O&M) capacity for water supply services. RUWASA was established in June 2019
with established and operational audit unit and quality assurance and quality control (QA&QC) mechanisms. As of June
30, 2022, 954 out of 2,278 CBWSOs (or 41.8 percent) have technical backstopping arrangements with an urban water
utility, a CBWSO trust, RUWASA or a private service provider. There has been improvement in data collection and

6.       Verified results in year three for DLI 6 exceeded the amount allocated to the DLR. There are two levels for DLI 6
-- Level I and II, and criteria for achieving each level are as follows. To achieve level I, the following applies: i)the scheme
must be managed by a registered CBWSO/best modal with an active management team; ii) At least one of the CBWSO
governance body positions must be held by a woman; iii) Participate in at least one of the bi-annual district-level
community of practice meetings and; iv) have a cost-reflective tariff (Operating Cost Ratio ≤ 1) and a well-maintained cash
book. To achieve level II the following applies: i) achieved Level I; ii) participate in at least two bi-annual district-level
community of practice meetings; iii) the water scheme has a functioning chlorination system (handpumps excluded); iv)
has a Backstopping Mechanism for operation and maintenance in the form of a written agreement for O&M services
established by CBWSOs individually or in clusters with RUWASA or private sector service providers; v) an urban utility; or
a water trust that can provide technical and managerial support to each CBWSO and; vi) has passed an annual capacity
assessment, as defined in Project Operations Manual. In year three alone, additional 3,808 villages were verified for DLI
6, which include 128 villages achieved Level I and 3,680 villages achieved both Level I and II. This resulted in the eligible
disbursement of SDR 78,448,640 with available funds being SDR 50,824,119. Therefore, results equivalent of SDR
27,625,521 where achieved in year 3 but no funds remain for this disbursement.

B    Rationale for Restructuring

7.       The proposed Level II restructuring was requested by GoT through an official letter dated September 18, 2023.
The requested restructuring is based on the year 3 verified results whereby DLR 6 does not have enough funds available
to fully disburse up to the results achieved. Therefore, it is proposed to reallocate funds from DLR 1 & 2 to provide the
required funding for DLR 6 up to the results achieved in year 3 and those expected in year 4. In addition, the government
has request to increase the advance amount allocation to 20 percent.

8.       This restructuring paper seeks approval of the reallocation of budget from DLR 1 & 2 to DLR 6 and the revision
of unit cost and targets for DLI 1, 2 and 6 for future disbursement. The reallocation request will add funds to DLR 6, to
ensure the verified year results are dispersed and there are funds available for expected year 4 results. The revision of
unit costs and targets for DLI 1, 2 and 6 is to align better with available funds per DLI and based on implementation
experience over the last 3 verification cycles. DLI 6 has been overachieved so it has been agreed that the DLI criteria should
be refined to make it more stringent, as well as reduce the unit rate. It was also agreed that the original 17 Regions will
not be eligible for level I whilst 8 new regions will be eligible for all levels (Level I and II). It should be noted that three
disbursements for three consecutive years have already been made to 17 regions. For 8 new regions, the disbursement

 II. DESCRIPTION OF PROPOSED CHANGES


9.      The following change to the Program is proposed:
     a) Reallocation of funds from DLI 1 and DLI 2 to cover for year 3 disbursement for DLI 6. The proposed restructuring
        will reallocate budget across the DLRs. Funds from DLR 2 which has not been fully utilized under credit number
        6295-TZ and credit number 7218-TZ and some of the fund from DRL 1 under credit 7218-TZ will be reallocated to
        DLR 6 to cover the deficit for year 3 disbursement for DLI 6. Based on the calculation from Table 2 above, the
        expected disbursement under DLR 6 for year 3 is SDR 78,448,640. The available disbursement funds for DLR 6 for
        year 3 under credit number 6295-TZ and credit number 7218-TZ is SDR 50,823,119, therefore, there is a deficit
        of SDR 27,625,521. To cover for this deficit, reallocation must be made from the funds available under DLR 1 and
        DLR 2. The amount equal to SDR 36,367,433.93 will be reallocated from the disbursement fund available from
        DLR 1 under credit 7218-TZ and additional disbursement funds equal to SDR 14,972,497.2 under credit number
        6295-TZ and credit number 7218-TZ will be reallocated from DLR 2 this makes a total of SDR 51,339,931.13
        available to cover for the deficit of year 3 disbursement for DLI 6 (SDR 27,625,521), and the excess SDR
        23,714,410.13 will be available for disbursement for the verified results of DLR 6 in year 4.

     b) There will be revised unit costs for DLI 1, 2 and 6 for the remaining funds to be able to meet the year 4 and
        onwards disbursements. To facilitate the disbursement of DLRs for the remaining implementation years, it has
        been agreed by the IAs that the unit cost for DLI 1 will be reduced from SDR 15.63 to SDR 11.2 and for DLI 2 from
        SDR 128.14 to SDR 85.1. In addition, the targets for the DLR 6 will be increased. The unit cost for DLI 6 will be
        reduced from SDR 7,813.23 to SDR 5,225 for level I and from SDR 10,451 to SDR 15,626.46 for Level II while
        maintaining the same unit cost (SDR 3,906.62) for sustaining the CBWSOs at Level II. The targets for the DLR 1
        will be reduced from 10,137,353 to 8,852,712 people based on allocated resources, while that of DLR 6 will be
        increased to 9,035 from 8,127 CBWSOs. These proposed changes were requested by the client to ensure
        adequate funds continue to be allocated to DLI 6 to continue to prioritize the strengthening of the capacity of
        CBWSOs to provide sustainable services under this Program. DLI 1 continues to be significantly supported by the
        government’s counterpart funds, and thus the original target (and more) will be met by the closing date of the
        Program. In the case of DLI 2, the unit rate was further analyzed, and client proposed a reduction in unit cost
        whilst committing the required additional resources from their own budget. While the target for DLR 1 may be
        fully achieved in year 4 and year 5, the target for DLR 6 may be fully achieved in year 4, therefore any results
        achieved in year 5 onwards will be covered by the counterpart funds. The GoT is committed to provide the
        counterpart funds to cover for any achievements that exceed the revised targets. Table 2 below summaries the
        changes made to DLRs.

     c) Increase of the advance withdrawal amount to 20 percent. The initial agreement with the GoT was that the
        recipient may withdraw an amount not exceeding SDR 23,500,000 to be disbursed to additional eight regions
        only. This amount is equivalent to 10 percent of the total amount for the AF (SDR 234,400,000). Given the needs
        of the new eight regions to implement the program, the client is requesting an increased advance withdrawal
