1. INTRODUCTION AND COUNTRY CONTEXT
  1. This program document proposes the first operation in the amount of US$800 million of a programmatic series
  of two development policy loans (DPLs) to support energy transition and climate resilience reforms in the
  Philippines. The Program Development Objective (PDO) of the operation is to support Government of the Philippines
  (GOP) reforms to (1) scale up adoption of clean energy technologies; (2) increase the security, flexibility, and
  competition of the electricity markets; and (3) improve water management across water uses; represented in three
  pillars. Pillar 1 aims to strengthen the policies and regulations for scaling up renewable energy (RE), energy efficiency
  (EE) and adoption of electric vehicles (EVs). Pillar 2 will help establish electricity market mechanisms for accelerating
  grid integration of variable renewable energy (VRE) and improving competition in wholesale and retail electricity
  markets, keeping a downward pressure on electricity cost. Pillar 3 will support the creation of an enabling
  environment for water financing and management that promote the incentives and governance framework in the
  national and local governments for climate resilient water supply and sanitation (WSS) services. The three pillars
  integrate to facilitate a robust policy foundation for building a low-carbon and climate-resilient economy, continuing
  the development trajectory envisioned in the Philippine Development Plan 2023-2028 (PDP 2023-28).
  2. Climate change poses major development challenges in the Philippines. It requires development policy
  reforms to address the new or elevated threats to inclusive growth and prosperity aspirations. The Philippines has
  been one of the most dynamic economies in the East Asia Pacific (EAP) region, with GNI per capita more than tripling,
  poverty declining sharply from 33.5 to 15.5 percent and income distribution improving from 2000 to 2023. While the
  economy has rebounded from the disruptions of the COVID-19 pandemic, there remain considerable risks and
  challenges to long-term growth and prosperity, prominent among them: dependency on imported fossil fuels, high
  cost of electricity, and climate-induced stresses and disasters.
  3. The positive growth outlook of the Philippines entails steadily increasing demand for energy and heightens
  energy security and carbon footprint concerns. Fueled by robust domestic consumption, and strong performance of
  its services and industry sectors GDP growth is expected to average 6.0 percent between 2024-2027. The government
  expects such growth rate to sustain beyond 2040 and to drive up energy demand substantially. The Philippine Energy
  Plan 2023 – 2050 (PEP 2023-50) projects electricity demand to more than quadruple between 2023 and 2050,
  requiring a similar expansion in supplies. The Philippines has substantial RE resources, particularly solar and wind,
  but they are underdeveloped and underutilized. Out of the 29.23 GW total installed generation capacity in 2023,
  hydro, geothermal, biomass, and solar/wind accounted for 3.87, 2.0, 0.65, and 2.41 GW, respectively, with a
  combined share of 30 percent. There is total untapped hydro power of 13.1 GW in the country, while the estimated
  geothermal potential is 4.4 GW. Wind and solar have the greatest potential but so far are the least developed. The
  total onshore and offshore wind technical potential is estimated at 76 GW and 178 GW, respectively. The abundant
  solar insolation translates into potential power generating capacity of 4.5–5.5 kWh per square meter per day. Despite
  having introduced the Renewable Energy Act in 2008, the country lags behind regional leaders such as Viet Nam and
  Thailand in installed solar and wind capacity by a significant margin. Without deliberate efforts to accelerate the
  deployment of RE, particularly solar and wind, much of the surging demand in electricity will be met by fossil-fuel-
  based generation, locking in carbon-intensive technologies, and increasing energy security risks.
  4. The high cost of electricity in the Philippines is a persistent concern for domestic affordability and
  international competitiveness. The increased dependence on imported oil and coal over the last twenty years
  alongside limited progress in reforms to increase competition in the electricity market has stymied the government’s
  efforts to reduce the cost of electricity. Based on July 2024 data, the Philippines had the second highest average
  electricity tariff in the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) after Singapore. For example, residential and
  industrial electricity tariffs in the Philippines were US¢21 and 13/kWh, respectively, significantly higher than US¢9

and 6/kWh in Indonesia and US¢12 and 10/kWh in Viet Nam. While subsidies (or lack thereof) explain much of the
differences, studies also point to factors such as limited competition in power generation, lack of competition at the
retail level, domestic taxation and red tape (e.g., lengthy permitting process). They also suggest that the sustained
high cost of electricity in the Philippines may have suppressed the growth of industries for which electricity is an
important production factor.
5. Energy transition - away from fossil fuels towards indigenous RE, EE, and other clean energy sources - can help
the country lower electricity generation costs while increasing energy security and lowering emissions.
Decarbonizing the power and transport sectors through large-scale deployment of cost-competitive RE is essential
for the Philippines’ transition to a prosperous low carbon economy. In fact, solar and onshore wind power generation
is already competitive against coal-fired power. While greater grid integration of solar and wind will require additional
investment in energy storage systems, grid expansion and upgrades, the long-term cost of RE (including cost of grid
integration) is expected to be competitive against fossil-based electricity. Modeling work undertaken by the World
Bank indicates that the levelized cost of electricity of the Philippine power system would decline as more solar and
wind are integrated into the system. Power generation and transport together account for 90 percent of fossil fuel
consumption and 80 percent of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in the Philippines. Coal-fired power plants (CCFPs)
contributed to 58 percent of power generation in 2023 up from 34 percent in 2010. Transport, which almost entirely
relies on imported oil, is the largest energy end-user in the country. Reducing fossil fuel consumption in power
generation and electrifying urban transport, would reduce ambient air pollution in urban areas, improving public
health. A transition will also help the country fulfil its Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) and commitments
under the Paris Agreement.
6. To be successful in energy transition, the Philippines needs to overcome the triple challenge of meeting fast
growing energy demand, transforming its fossil-fuel-based energy infrastructure, and keeping energy affordable.
The required investments for energy transition are massive: according to PEP 2023-50, achieving the high-RE-growth
pathway (RE share reaching 70 percent of total power generation by 2050), the power sector would need over PHP
15 trillion (equivalent to about US$265 billion) capital investments in generation at 2022 prices. Policy and regulatory
reforms are needed to create the enabling conditions for substantially increasing private sector investments in RE. At
the same time, continued reforms in the electricity markets are also needed to facilitate faster pace of grid integration
of RE supplies and increase competition to lower the cost of electricity to end users.
7. In addition to securing the needed RE generation, investments in grid expansion and modernization also need
to happen in tandem, and often time, ahead of generation investments. Transmission development has become a
bottleneck to the government’s RE ambition. The existing grid infrastructure is already strained to ensure reliable
supply of electricity and has limited spare capacity to absorb the expected large increase in RE generation in the
coming years. Delays in grid expansion projects have already caused stranding of new RE generation assets. The
criticality of timely investments in transmission capacity is well recognized. But the existing arrangements for
transmission development planning and investments have not demonstrated effectiveness in addressing the
expansion needs.
8. Climate resilience through adaptation and mitigation in water is critical for the Philippines. The Philippines is
one of the countries most vulnerable to climate change and extreme weather events. Its archipelagic nature is an
important factor that also shapes its water management challenges. The impact of climate change is already felt
through changes in rainfall patterns that affect water availability and quality and increase the risks of floods and
droughts. As recognized in the World Bank Water Security and Climate Adaptation Global Challenge Program,
efficient and sustainable water resource management across water uses is central to climate resilience, and WSS
delivery is a key building block to this resilience. In the Philippines, while the National Climate Change Action Plan
(NCCAP) 2011-2028 targets water sufficiency as Action Item 2, including strategic actions on restructuring water
governance and ensuring access to sustainable WSS, important policy and institutional challenges remain to achieve

      those objectives from the national to the local levels. At the national level, with more than 30 institutions involved
      and with overlaps and gaps on their mandates, governance on water resource management needs clarity. This clarity
      will enable planning of water resources that ensures efficient and sustainable long-term use and promote climate
      resilience to floods and droughts. The responsibility of providing WSS services lies with local government units (LGUs).
      But most LGUs are operating with workforce limitations and water tariffs that do not reflect the real cost of providing
      water services. These result in low funding, poor maintenance, neglection of sanitation, and inefficiencies in
      operations as reflected in huge volumes of non-revenue water (NRW) or water lost in distribution systems. These
      water losses limit the improvement and expansion of critical water services – only around 48 percent of the
      population in the Philippines have access to safely managed water services, most of them in rural and poor areas.
      The lack of sanitation services contributes to pollution of water sources. It is important that water resources and
      service delivery are managed sustainably for climate resilience, especially for poor and vulnerable areas which have
      lower socio-economic resilience. In the Philippines, natural disasters are a cause of poverty and poverty magnifies
      the impact of climate stresses and disasters by making people more vulnerable and less resilient. 1 Poverty and climate
      vulnerability rank high for sectors that have agriculture-dependent livelihoods such as farmers and fisherfolk, who
      are exposed to floods, droughts and sea level rise.
      9. This DPL series is an important part of the continuing effort in operationalizing the Philippines Country Climate
      and Development Report (CCDR). The CCDR has provided ample evidence on why climate actions, particularly in
      accelerating energy transition and ensuring water security, are essential to the Philippines’ economic development
      and climate agenda, identified key constraints and challenges, and recommended priority actions. The proposed
      program is underpinned by rigorous analytics carried out through the CCDR work and continued engagements with
      counterparts, including a multi-year advisory and analytics program in energy transition and two consecutive advisory
      and analytics activities in water management.
 2. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK
     10. The macroeconomic policy framework is adequate for the proposed operation. The growth outlook is positive,
     anchored on robust domestic demand, more accommodative monetary policy, and an improving external
     environment. Structural reforms will increase competitiveness and support growth. The financial sector remains
     stable, while asset quality continues to improve, even with the removal of most pandemic-related forbearance
     measures. Potential liabilities from the expansion of Public Private Partnerships (PPP) pose marginal risks to the
     country’s fiscal position and are managed by a contingency allocation in the budget. It is also managed by the
     realization of sellable asset value in the event of default, and the decreasing risk to projects in operation and those
     that are reaching completion within the portfolio. The credibility of both monetary policy and fiscal policy remains
     strong. The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) remains committed to its inflation-targeting objective (2-4 percent) and
     is responsive to global interest rate movements to the extent that they affect the inflation outlook. Gradually
     declining inflation and associated monetary easing is expected over the medium term. The government is targeting
     a steadily declining fiscal deficit until 2028. Fiscal consolidation is led by a decline in current expenditures while
     additional revenues will be generated through tax policy and administration reforms. The credibility of the medium-
     term fiscal program is supported by a solid track record of fiscal prudence and a legislative pipeline supportive of
     fiscal consolidation. The combination of fiscal consolidation and the strong growth will keep debt levels sustainable.

      its growth momentum, expanding by 5.6 percent year-on-year in 2024. Services remained the main growth engine,
      as healthy domestic demand fueled the growth of wholesale and retail trade, financial activities, and business

      12. Headline inflation returned to the central bank target range after peaking in 2023. Headline inflation remained

      normalization. Rice price inflation has been steadily declining, turning negative (-2.3 percent) in January 2025, after

      in full-year 2024 and 2.9 percent in January 2025, within the 2–4-percent inflation target range, as expected. Core

      cut its key policy rate by 25 bps, noting inflation has remained on a target consistent path (3.3 percent for 2025 and

      13. The current account deficit widened to 3.9 percent of GDP in Q1-Q3 2024 (from 3.5 percent in Q1-Q3 2023).
      This is explained by a wider trade deficit in goods and a moderation in services trade receipts. The goods trade deficit
      widened largely due to declining global demand that led to 6.7 percent y-o-y contraction in electronics exports, the
      country’s main export commodity, in 2024. In addition, net services trade receipts moderated by 1.3 percent of GDP

3.    GOVERNMENT PROGRAM
     24. The GOP is committed to achieving the medium-term development objectives outlined in the PDP 2023-28.
     The PDP’s strategic framework is guided by AmBisyon Natin 2040, the long-term development aspirations of the
     country according to three objectives: (i) develop and protect capabilities of individuals and families; (ii) transform
     production sectors to generate more quality jobs and produce competitive products; and (iii) foster an enabling
     environment encompassing institutions, the physical and natural environment. Underlying these priorities is a
     commitment to expand and upgrade infrastructure, accelerate climate action and strengthen disaster resilience
     among others.
     25. The GOP is embarking on a substantial energy transition agenda, highlighted by the strong push to scale up RE,
     particularly solar and wind, and increased efforts in promoting demand-side EE and electrification of transport. The
     government’s energy transition program is broadly guided by the PEP 2023-50, which sets RE target at 35 percent of
     electricity generation by 2030 and 50 percent by 2040, compared with 24 percent in 2023. The PEP in its clean energy
     scenario also sets the national EE target of achieving 10 percent savings in 2040, as well as a target for EV penetration
     at 50 percent of motor vehicle fleet in 2040. The government’s energy transition program is underpinned by a series
     of recent and forthcoming policy and regulatory reforms aimed at: (i) accelerating RE deployment and grid integration
     by augmenting demand, streamlining permitting process, boosting foreign direct investments, addressing grid
     constraints and enhancing role and functions of electricity markets; (ii) intensifying efforts on EE by formulating the
     first national EE and conservation plan, implementing public sector EE mandates and tightening minimum energy
     performance standards for buildings and a range of end-use equipment; and (iii) laying the foundation for
     electrification of transport by promulgating the necessary laws, regulations and standards governing the EV sector
     development, as well as policies to incentivize early adoptions of EVs and value chain participation. The government
     is in the process of amending the Electric Power Industry Reform Act (EPIRA) which liberalized the power sector but
     has failed to achieve one of its main objectives of lowering the cost of electricity. There is a renewed effort to increase
     the competition in electricity markets and strengthen the role of the Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC) to promote
     competition and protect consumer interests.
     26. The GOP is committed to improving water management across water uses and enhancing climate resilience.
     The strategy is anchored on sustainable water resources management; addressing impacts of floods, droughts, and
     sea level rise; and ensuring access to safe WSS services. The GOP, through the Water Resources Management Office
     (WRMO) created in 2023, is harmonizing government policies, plans, and programs to address the needs for
     irrigation, water supply, flood control and hydropower; promoting integrated water resources management; and
     addressing the constraints of a decentralized WSS service delivery model. The WRMO is a transitional agency which
     will shepherd the creation of a new Department of Water Resources (DWR), with a new law that is being deliberated
     in the Senate. The sector will require more funding and financing from public and private sources, as the GOP and
     LGUs expand their WSS programs.

4. PROPOSED OPERATION

4.1. LINK TO GOVERNMENT PROGRAM AND OPERATION DESCRIPTION
   27. The proposed operation will address some of the binding constraints to achieving the government’s goals in
   accelerating energy transition and improving climate resilience. The binding constraints to energy transition
   addressed by this operation include (i) uncertainty in the deployment and grid integration of RE at the scale needed
   to achieve the government’s RE targets; (ii) weak drivers for scaling of energy efficiency due to underdeveloped
   financing and delivery mechanisms and outdated energy performance standards; (iii) the slow adoption of EVs due
   to the lack of catalysts for early adoption and investments in charging infrastructure and supply chain development;
   and (iv) prospects of rising cost of electricity due to large scale grid integration of RE. The binding constraints to
   increasing climate resilience in the water sector, which this operation aims to address are: (i) inefficient and
   ineffective sector management at the national level; and (ii) financial instability of WSS providers at local level.
   28. The Program Development Objective of the operation is to support Government of the Philippines reforms to
   (1) scale up adoption of clean energy technologies; (2) increase the security, flexibility, and competition of the
   electricity markets; and (3) improve water management across water uses. The proposed operation is the first in a
   programmatic DPL series of two operations. It is structured around three pillars corresponding to the development
   objective:
      •   Pillar 1 will support reforms to accelerate private sector investments and delivery of RE projects, as well as
          promote EE market transformation and electrification of transport.
      •   Pillar 2 will support reforms to address critical gaps in the electricity markets that restrict greater integration
          of RE or weaken competition among electricity suppliers.
      •   Pillar 3 will support reforms to improve water management across uses especially in poor and vulnerable
          areas. The initial set of reforms include measures in water resources management and WSS service delivery.
   29. Reforms supported by the proposed operation are expected to accelerate the energy transition, lower the
   cost of transition, reduce GHG emissions, and strengthen resilience to climate change impacts on electricity and
   water. Without action, climate change imposes substantial economic and human costs, affecting the poorest
   households the most. Prior actions (PAs) 1- 7 (Pillars 1 and 2) are expected to contribute to mitigation. PAs 8-10 (Pillar
   3) are expected to contribute to adaptation. The team will work closely with the counterparts and the EAP climate
   team to maximize the impact of the reforms on mitigation and adaptation.
   30. This DPL series aligns with the Sustainable Recovery (SR) DPL series in supporting key reforms in the RE and
   EV sectors. The SR DPL series (approved in FY2023 and FY2024) facilitated foundational reforms by the government
   to support the energy transition, including: (i) increasing the mandated market share of RE through a significant
   update of the Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS); (ii) lifting the foreign equity cap on solar and wind energy to boost
   foreign direct investment; (iii) establishing the policy and administrative framework for offshore wind (OSW)
   development; (iv) issuing the implementing rules and regulations (IRRs) of the Electric Vehicle Industrial Development
   Act (EVIDA) to foster the development and use of EVs; and (v) creating a streamlined permitting system and setting
   standards for EV charging stations. The Energy Transition and Climate Resilience (ETCR) DPL series adopts a
   comprehensive approach to the reforms necessary for supporting the energy transition in the Philippines. It will
   support downstream actions related to RE policy reforms initiated under the SR DPL series, such as RPS compliance
   and de-risking OSW investments, while also advancing new initiatives, such as the operationalization of the reserves
   market and enhancing retail competition in electricity markets. Additionally, the ETCR DPL series will bolster EV policy
   reform implementation by providing catalysts and incentives for EV adoption, attracting segments of the EV value
   chain and setting adoption mandates for government agencies to stimulate demand within the local EV ecosystem.
   The framework of policy reform support provided by these consecutive DPL series is detailed in Annex 8.

     31. Energy transition is a multi-faceted agenda which requires numerous additional efforts beyond the actions
     supported by the two consecutive DPL series. Given the private-sector-driven nature of the Philippine energy
     markets and the reliance on private capital for nearly all investments in the power system—from generation to
     transmission and distribution—proper risk allocation and necessary government derisking interventions will be key
     for mobilizing the hundreds of billions of USD needed for financing the transition. The bankability of private sector
     RE projects is critically linked to the mitigation of risks in offtake, including cost recovery and the necessary physical
     infrastructure, among others. For instance, addressing the bottleneck in transmission is a critical derisking
     intervention to ensure the achievement of the RE generation target. This requires not only proper planning and
     adequate financing but also timely delivery of transmission projects, which involves resolving the long-standing issue
     of delays caused by permitting and acquisition of the right of way.
     32. The third pillar recognizes water as the main vector of climate impacts and thus water security as a building
     block of climate resilience through improved water resources management and enhanced sector capacity for WSS
     services improvement and expansion in harder to reach areas and population. Climate change will result in more
     frequent and more intense droughts and floods – with heightened risks in archipelagic and island contexts. Existing
     inefficiency in water management and gaps in WSS services will be further exacerbated by climate risks, as availability
     and quality of water will be affected. These risks impact the ability of the poor and vulnerable in the Philippines to
     recover from income shocks and to rebuild their assets. The average annual wellbeing losses due to disasters in the
     Philippines is estimated at US$3.9 billion per year, more than double the asset losses of US$1.4 billion. Strengthening
     climate resilience should thus include strengthening ecological, physical, financial, and social and institutional
     resilience. In the water sector, these can be through sustainable water resources management and efficient and
     expanded water services that prioritize the poor. A major constraint is the highly fragmented institutional
     arrangements in the water sector at the national and local levels. This constraint hinders integration, alignment, and
     implementation of plans and programs of national and local governments necessary for effectively managing water
     sources and services. This DPL series supports key policy reforms that help provide the enabling environment to
     address this constraint. Specifically, the reforms will clarify and strengthen multi-level governance structures for
     water management. At the national level, it supports (i) the creation of the DWR and the Water Regulatory
     Commission; (ii) the Unified Resource Allocation Framework which clarifies funding support for WSS and prioritizes
     poor and lagging areas; and (iii) the Program Convergence Budget for Water which aligns national agency programs
     toward more efficient use of water resources. At the local level, it promotes the enabling environment for sustainable
     management of water resources and expansion of WSS services by the creation of local water economic enterprises.

Pillar 2: Increasing the security, flexibility and competition of electricity markets
51. The efficient operation of electricity markets is essential for a successful energy transition in the Philippines.
It will be important to ensure the following: (i) adequate competition in the market to ensure lower generation costs
translate into lower electricity tariffs for customers; (ii) appropriate incentives for provision of ancillary services,
reserves and storage to increase the flexibility of the electricity system and increase VRE integration and climate
resilience; (iii) mechanisms to ensure stable revenue streams for RE investors to reduce investment risks; (iv)
mechanisms to integrate distributed energy resources such as rooftop PV, EV charging stations and flexible demand;
and (iv) framework for valuing local and global environmental externalities in accordance with government policy.
52. The institutional and regulatory framework underpinning electricity markets in Philippines needs to be
enhanced to support the energy transition. The current electricity market framework in Philippines is based on the
EPIRA of 2001. The legislation, which was adopted in response to a series of power sector crisis in the late 1990s,
unbundled a vertically integrated sector into generation, transmission, distribution, and retail functions, and
established the Wholesale Electricity Spot Market (WESM). However, incomplete, or weak implementation of the
reforms have meant that the full potential of these reforms has not been realized. The government and the regulator
have not fully advanced reforms to (i) lower the demand threshold for Retail Competition and Open Access at which
customers would be able to contract directly with generators and (ii) avoid cross ownership in different segments of
the electricity sector by large private sector conglomerates. The Philippines has also not yet put in place
complementary mechanisms to enable the electricity market to integrate significantly higher shares of VRE, such as

     service delivery of LGUs. In addition, the proposed policy reform will provide for dedicated staff requirements of WSS

74. ISSUE: Scaling up and rationalization of investments in the water sector at the national and local levels of
government will help address the challenge of water security and climate adaptation. Investments of the national
government in the water sector have been focused largely on single-purpose flood management projects. For
example, the current flagship projects of the GoP have an allocation of 7 percent to the water sector (around US $13
billion), of which 80 percent is on flood management, 9 percent is on irrigation and only 3 percent is on water supply.
Investments needed for universal WSS are substantial at US$1.8 billion per year but only about 15 percent of this
amount is funded annually. At the local level, LGUs and other public water service providers, especially those with
high poverty and low capabilities, need support from the national government to improve and expand their WSS
services. But national government support for WSS has been limited to grants through small programs and ad hoc
funding from congressional allocations. There is also no clear performance targets set for the support provided. There
is a need to rationalize and increase investments in the sector to ensure efficient use of limited fiscal resources while
ensuring the sustainable management of water resources and promoting adaptation to too much or too little water.
75. SUBSTANCE OF THE REFORM: PA 9 supports a Program Convergence Budget (PCB) for Water. The PCB
approach to budgeting was introduced in 2012 through DBM National Budget Memorandum No. 114. Its objective is
to focus the available fiscal space on identified priority programs and ensure that key agencies participating in and
contributing to the programs coordinate their targets and activities to facilitate program execution. The PCB is a
strategy for effective planning and implementation of government programs and projects with a long-term goal
within a specific timeframe, as supported by a masterplan. The DBM favorably considers PCB programs as it ensures
an integrated approach in identifying key needs and priorities resulting in a well-planned program proposal for the
sector. Several PCBs have continuously received government budgets through many years which include, among
others, the Agricultural Development Program, Tourism Development Program, and Risk Resiliency Program. This
experience has shown that PCBs help not only with budget allocation but more importantly, on institutional
alignment. In the same manner, the PCB for Water will promote the adequacy and efficiency of national government
resources going into water initiatives. Through the PCB, there is an opportunity for multipurpose water facilities, for
example, to introduce water supply functions in flood control and irrigation projects which often get budget support.
It provides a forum to plan and develop water storage facilities that directly mitigate the impact of floods and
droughts. The PCB for Water will have the DENR-WRMO as the lead agency and will require the collaboration of
participating national agencies: the NEDA, DILG, Department of Public Works and Highways (DPWH), Local Water
Utilities Administration (LWUA), and the Department of Health (DOH). It will support the implementation of an
integrated water resource management approach and the PWSSMP and will be implemented following the URAF
principles in PA 10 below. The PCB for Water already has a budget in the Fiscal Year 2025 budget proposal submitted
to Congress. Its guidelines have also been approved by the NEDA Cabinet Committee. To continue with the PCB
implementation and ensure that the programs are directed towards clear and measurable sector targets, the
proposed trigger is a policy to be supported by a NEDA Board Resolution that details the annual targets on coverage
of water supply and sanitation, irrigation services and flood management. It will also support a monitoring and
evaluation system to be followed by all the agencies in the PCB.

enhance climate resilience of the local communities. Triggers in this pillar will directly contribute to enhance climate
resilience in water. PA 10 would likely generate short-term negative environmental impacts during the
implementation of investments in WSS infrastructures and facilities, wastewater treatment facilities, and
replacement and disposal of inefficient equipment and machinery. This impact could be significant depending on
the scale, locations, and sizes of investments. EIA or EPP are required for these water investment projects, which
include measures to mitigate the potential adverse effects. Management of waste will be addressed through
execution of provisions on waste management under the PEISS.
5.3. PFM, DISBURSEMENT AND AUDITING ASPECTS
90. PFM System: The GoP has significantly improved its PFM at the national level over the past decade. The 2016
Public Expenditure and Financial Accountability (PEFA) assessment showed improvement in three out of the seven
PFM pillars (transparency of public finances, policy-based budgeting and asset and liability management) compared
to the 2010 assessment. Reforms in the management of budget resources continue to be undertaken by the GoP
under the PFM reform program. The Second and Third Philippines DPL to Foster More Inclusive Growth (P126580,
P147803) supported the formulation, adoption, and use of Unified Accounts Code Structure in budgeting, accounting,
and reporting. A Treasury Single Account has been implemented and the Bureau of Treasury is expanding its
coverage. The DPL for Improving Fiscal Management (P167651) included actions to strengthen budget preparation,
cash management, and adoption of the Budget and Treasury Management System (BTMS) as the basis for a single
national government financial information system. The national government budget is made public on the DBM
website. Public procurement systems have strengthened over the last two decades following the adoption of
Republic Act (RA) No. 9184, otherwise known as the Government Procurement Reform Act, in January 2003. An
assessment of the country’s public procurement using the Methodology for Assessing Procurement Systems (MAPS)
was completed in 2021 and the recommendations are guiding the GoP to strengthen its public procurement system.
The PhilGEPS is undergoing modernization with the virtual store and merchants’ registry already in-place. The
electronic bidding feature, which includes an online platform for the creation of the annual procurement plan,
                                                                                                                Page 27
     The World Bank
     Philippines First Energy Transition and Climate Resilience Development Policy Loan (P181389)


purchase requests, e-bulletin, e-bid submission, online conduct of post-qualification, online purchase order/contract
management, and online filing of requests for reconsideration and protest, is under implementation with pilot
agencies. The e-Marketplace was piloted in 2024.
91. Foreign Exchange (FOREX) Control Environment: The BSP 2020 to 2022 audited financial statements have
unqualified audit opinions and do not raise any major concerns about the FOREX control environment. The audit
opinion for 2022 BSP’s financial statements contains an “Emphasis of Matter” paragraph related to the BSP’s position
in the formulation and conduct of effective monetary policy, where the institution found it appropriate to differ, in
some aspects, from the adoption of the relevant Philippine Financial Reporting Standards (PFRS) and Philippine
Accounting Standards (PAS). The opinion of the auditor was not modified (qualified) in respect to this matter.
92. Fiduciary Risk: Based on the assessment of the PFM Systems and FOREX control environment, fiduciary risk is
moderate. There were no major qualifications on the DOF annual financial statements in recent years, and most
matters raised in previous years’ audit reports were fully or partly addressed.
93. Disbursement and Auditing Arrangements: Upon withdrawal, the proceeds of the DPL will be deposited in US
dollars in a dedicated deposit account at the BSP, forming part of the country’s official FOREX reserves. The GoP will
transfer funds from the US dollar account to the treasury single account in local currency (pesos) that will be used to
pay government’s budget expenditure. Within 30 days of the disbursement of the DPL amount, the GoP will confirm
receipt of these funds to the Bank. Disbursements of the loan will not be linked to any specific purchases, and no
procurement requirements have to be satisfied. The DPL should not be used for excluded expenditure or connected
in engagements in corrupt, fraudulent, collusive, or coercive practices, as per the General Conditions for IBRD
Financing for Development Policy Financing that became effective on July 15, 2023. Should this be the case, the Bank
will seek for a refund from the GoP, which will subsequently be cancelled from the loan. Given the moderate fiduciary
risk, there will be no requirement for the audit of the DPL.
6.     SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION
94. The overall risk assessment is moderate.
95. The institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability risk is substantial. The reforms require inter-
agency coordination, making their success dependent on institutional capacity. PAs under Pillars 1 and 2 require close
co-ordination and collaboration between four electricity sector institutions – DOE, ERC, IEMOP and the National
Transmission Corporation. The reforms supported by PA 4 will also require coordination with the Department of
Transportation and DOF. While the risks are mitigated to an extent by the ongoing working relationship between
these agencies, significant residual risks remain given the complexity of reforms required to support the energy
transition. Under Pillar 3, all PAs will require several agencies, the DENR, DILG, NEDA, DBM, DOF, NIA, LWUA, and
others, to work together. For PA8, LGUs will be the primary implementor of the reform. The pending creation of the
DWR and WRC may reduce the inter-agency collaboration that will be required. The Bank is providing technical
assistance to mitigate capacity risks through (1) on-going joint WBG support for offshore wind development and an
multi-year recipient-executed technical assistance program on energy transition capacity buildings among key
stakeholders (under preparation); and (2) engagements with key WSS stakeholders through the preparation and
implementation of the proposed Philippine Water Supply and Sanitation Project (P504257). Nonetheless, under all
three pillars, residual risks remain as substantial.
96. Stakeholder risk is substantial. Reforms under Pillar 1 and 2 to increase share of RE and increase competition
may face resistance from incumbents that would gain from preserving the status quo, for example, by seeking to
maintain the dominance of fossil fuel generation or seeking to keep the current market arrangements where few
large companies can exercise significant market power. To mitigate these risks, it will be important that the central
government agencies, DOE and DOF, play a coordinating and advocacy role to keep the reforms on track and to
ensure that any potential adverse impacts of reforms are mitigated. The Bank will remain engaged on these reforms,
                                                                                                             Page 28
       The World Bank
       Philippines First Energy Transition and Climate Resilience Development Policy Loan (P181389)


   convene, and share knowledge with stakeholders who have shown an interest in reforming these sectors. PA8
   involves bringing the reforms down to the LGU level. Since LGUs have different capacities, complying with the reforms
   may be difficult for those with limited personnel and capabilities. The DILG will play a major role in facilitating the
   cooperation of the LGUs by assisting especially LGUs at the 4th to 6th deciles of the income distribution. The IPF on
   WSS that is currently under preparation will involve capacity building to be implemented by the DILG. In addition, the
   Bank is providing implementation assistance to the relevant agencies.

     ANNEX 7: PFM, DISBURSEMENT AND AUDITING ASPECTS

       1. PFM Environment: The PFM narrative has a strong underpinning that captures the strengths and weaknesses
       identified in the 2016 Public Expenditure and Financial Accountability (PEFA) assessment. The GOP has significantly
       improved its PFM at the national level over the past decade and continues to be a strong performer in the region. The
       PEFA indicated that three of the seven PFM pillars (transparency, policy-based budgeting, and asset and liability
       management) are strong and have improved since the 2010 assessment. The financial statements of government
       agencies are audited annually. There were no major qualifications on the Department of Finance (DOF) annual financial
       statements in the most recent years, and most matters raised in previous years’ audit reports were fully or partly
       addressed. The national government budget is made available to the public online through the DBM website, from
       the budget proposal stage (the National Expenditure Program) to enactment of the General Appropriations Act (GAA),
       to declaration of its effectivity through the signing by the President of the Philippines.
       2. The GOP continues to make strides in PFM reform. The WBG Second and Third Philippines Development Policy
       Loan to Foster More Inclusive Growth in 2014 (P126580, P147803) supported the formulation and adoption of a
       Unified Accounts Code Structure to be used across budgeting, accounting, and reporting. A Treasury Single Account
       has been implemented and the Bureau of Treasury (BTr) is working to expand its coverage. The DPL for Improving
       Fiscal Management (P167651) included actions to strengthen budget preparation, cash management and adoption of
       the Budget and Treasury Management System (BTMS) as the basis for a single national government financial
       information system 13. To improve budget predictability and execution, the GOP gradually tightened the validity period
       for obligations since 2019 when an annual cash budget was adopted. The adoption of cash-based budgeting effectively
       limited the validity of obligations to the year funds are appropriated. The Budget Reform Bill is one of the priority bills
       of the current administration and there is a renewed commitment and support to pass the bill.
       3. The GOP has made great strides during the last two decades towards strengthening the country’s public
       procurement system through the adoption of Republic Act (RA) No. 9184, otherwise known as the Government
       Procurement Reform Act, in January 2003. Several measures were taken under this law to address systemic
       procurement corruption, including inter alia the mandatory use of public bidding, periodic monitoring, and evaluation
       of the performance of the procurement system through the Agency Procurement Compliance and Performance
       Indicator system and the mandatory use of the Philippine Government Electronic Procurement System (PhilGEPS)
       feature of bid and contract award notification. These are monitored through the indicator on numbers of bid
       opportunities and subsequent procurement results. The estimated amount of contract versus the awarded prices,
       among others, are also available through PhilGEPS. It also established Government Procurement Policy Board (GPPB)
       as the public procurement regulatory and normative body, initiated the professionalization of public procurement
       practice, enhanced collaboration with Civil Society Organizations as procurement observers, and promoted
       sustainability through the introduction of the Green Public Procurement Roadmap in 2017. However, many challenges
       remain to be addressed in further reforms, especially with respect to eligibility and rules of participation, procurement
       approaches for optimal value for money and sustainable procurement, independent complaints review body, and
       modernization of PhilGEPS operational functionalities for increased efficiency. A comprehensive assessment of the
       Philippines public procurement system using the revised Methodology for Assessing Procurement Systems (MAPS) has
       been jointly conducted by the GPPB-TSO, the World Bank, and Asian Development Bank. The assessment’s

a cash budgeting system, the conduct of advance procurement has been strengthened and institutionalized through

implementation with pioneer agencies. The eMarketplace will be piloted this year.
5. The BSP foreign reserves control environment is based on domestic assessments. The Philippines does not have
an active IMF program to develop safeguard assessments. The BSP is also not subject to international audit. Its financial
statements are audited by the Commission on Audit (COA). The World Bank and the IMF have been relying on the
audited financial statements released by the COA. The auditor's opinions in the BSP-audited financial statements
related to calendar years 2020 to 2022 are unmodified (unqualified). The audit opinion on 2022 BSP’s financial
statements contains an “Emphasis of Matter” paragraph related to the BSP’s position that having considered its
responsibilities in the formulation and conduct of effective monetary policy, it is appropriate to differ, in some aspects,
from the adoption of the relevant Philippine Financial Reporting Standards (PFRS) and Philippine Accounting Standards
(PAS). The opinion of the auditor is not modified in respect to this matter.
6. The proceeds of the DPL will be deposited in US dollars in a dedicated deposit account at the BSP that forms
part of the FOREX reserves once the loan becomes effective and the World Bank is satisfied with i) the progress
achieved by the GOP in carrying out the program, ii) the adequacy of the GOP macroeconomic policy framework,
and iii) submission of withdrawal application in required format in US dollar. The GoP will transfer funds from the
US dollar dedicated account to the treasury single account in local currency (pesos) that will be used to pay
government’s budget expenditure. Immediately after disbursement of the loan, the GOP will ensure that the loan
amount is promptly accounted for in the GOP budget system in the General Fund, and thereby is available to finance
budget expenditures. The GOP will provide written confirmation to the World Bank within 30 days that this accounting
and transfer has been completed, including the exchange rate applied to convert the loan proceeds into Philippine
Peso, and the name and number of the government’s bank account in which the funds have been deposited and that
the exact amount of the funds received in the account. Disbursements of the loan will not be linked to any specific
purchases, and no procurement requirements have to be satisfied, except that the borrower is required to comply
with the standard negative list of excluded items that may not be financed with World Bank loan proceeds, as defined
in the General Conditions for IBRD Financing, Development Policy Financing, dated December 14, 2018 (Last revised
