                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The Project aims to increase access to drinking water supply and sanitation (WSS) services in underserved areas in the
                     city of Kinshasa, the capital city of the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC). The project intends to leverage financing
                     from IDA, as well as mobilize private capital for water supply production. The aim is to provide safely managed drinking
                     water services to previously unserved populations and to improve the quality of services from basic to safely managed
                     drinking water services in Kinshasa through safe and reliable water access through household connections. Moreover,
                     the project will improve access to safely-managed sanitation through the construction of fecal sludge treatment plants
                     (FSTP), and increasing access to water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) facilities in selected schools and health centers

                     within the city. Aligned with the regional Multi-phase Programmatic Approach (MPA) design, the proposed components
                     are the following: Component 1: Strengthening Governance and Reforms and Improving the Performance of Service
                     Providers. Subcomponent 1.1. on Governance and Reforms (mapped to pillars 1 and 2 of the MPA) finances technical
                     assistance, training and equipment to address governance bottlenecks and support the implementation of reform
                     measures including for the mobilization of private capital for WASH. This includes the development of regulatory

                     Improvement, which is mapped to pillar 3 of the MPA, finances works, equipment, technical assistance, training for the
                     national water utility REGIDESO. This covers support for the implementation of REGIDESO's Performance Improvement
                     Action Plan, including Non-Revenue Water, digitalization, and water quality management improvements and social
                     connection subsidies. Component 2: Leveraging Financing for Resilient Investments to Expand WASH Access (mapped to
                     pillar 4 of the MPA). Subcomponent 2.1 finances infrastructure investments in climate -resilient urban water production
                     and distribution systems for Kinshasa, including viability gap financing for the construction of new bulk water treatment
                     plant(s). A Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT) arrangement is proposed for these water production plant(s), blending public
                     and private funding to ensure an affordable tariff for REGIDESO as the off-taker, and to the households as end-
                     consumers. IDA financing would support the viability gap financing, as well as the completion of the rehabilitation and
                     expansion of the existing Ndjili water treatment plant, and expansion of REGIDESO’s water distribution network to
                     maximize impact on the population. MIGA de-risking instruments will be brought as appropriate. The IFC role would be
                     to provide debt and mobilize other lenders (either through co-development with value-for-money assurances or
                     through a competitive process). Subcomponent 2.2 finances construction of one or more fecal sludge treatment plants
                     (FSTPs) in Kinshasa, as well as technical assistance and support to strengthen the liquid sanitation collection,

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The project area is Kinshasa, a rapidly urbanizing city with a population exceeding 15 million, faces significant challenges
                     in water supply and sanitation provision, particularly in informal and peri-urban neighborhoods where infrastructure
                     and services are limited. The city’s high population density and mix of formal and informal settlements contribute to
                     complex environmental and social issues, including severe erosion and flooding resulting from inadequate drainage and
                     uncontrolled urban growth, as well as poor solid waste management due to limited landfill capacity and insufficient
                     municipal resources. Social challenges are compounded by difficulties in providing safe drinking water and sanitation in
                     areas with unreliable electricity and multiple water sources, such as the Congo and Ndjili rivers, leading to public health
                     risks—especially waterborne diseases that disproportionately affect vulnerable communities, especially informal
                     settlers and street vendors.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The project will be implemented under the leadership of the Cellule d’Exécution des Projets Eau (CEP-O), which
                     operates within the Ministry of Water Resources and Electricity (MRHE). CEP-O will be responsible for project

                     CEP-O has demonstrated capacity in managing World Bank-financed projects. Currently, CEP-O is co-implementing the

                     focus on decentralization and improved delivery, but limited expertise and financial constraints hinder progress.
                     Subcomponent 1.2 funds works, equipment, assistance, and training to support REGIDESO’s Performance Improvement
                     Action Plan, including water quality management and social subsidies. Private sector partners will design new bulk
                     water treatment plants under a Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT) arrangement, blending public and private funding for
                     affordable tariffs. Additional investments are needed in water monitoring, customer engagement, and grievance
                     redress. The Bank's due diligence will identify capacity needs for managing E&S risks, reflected in the ESCP, which must
                     specify staffing for environmental, social, and SEA/SH specialists.

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                             Substantial

                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                      Substantial
                     The Environmental Risk of the Project is assessed as Substantial at the Concept stage. The Project will finance
                     construction and rehabilitation of water supply and sanitation infrastructure, including water treatment plants and
                     fecal sludge treatment facilities, which will generate construction waste as well as hazardous and fecal sludge waste
                     during construction and operation. These activities will be implemented in an urban context characterized by weak
                     wastewater management systems and limited water quality monitoring capacity, which increase environmental risk if
                     not adequately managed. The Environmental Risk is not rated High at this stage because the locations, scale, and
                     technical designs of subprojects are not yet finalized, and potential impacts are expected to be largely site specific
                     and manageable through the application of proportionate mitigation measures. Substantial risk investments,
                     including treatment plants and sludge facilities, will be subject to project specific ESIAs, while lower risk activities will
                     be managed through E&S Screening and ESMPs, with explicit provisions for waste and sludge management in line
                     with GIIP. Private sector investment in the design and construction of WASH facilities through Build-Operate-Transfer
                     (BOT) arrangements will provide technical capacity to promote the sustainable use of resources and to minimize
                     adverse impacts on human health and the environment. Institutional capacity constraints, including the limited
                     capacity of REGIDESO in wastewater management, environmental monitoring, and operational oversight, are
                     recognized as a contributing risk factor. These risks are considered manageable through strengthened supervision,
                     clear contractual requirements, and targeted capacity building measures. The Environmental Risk rating will be
                     reassessed prior to or at Decision Meeting, once subproject details are confirmed.

                     The Social Risk of the Project is assessed as Substantial at the Concept stage. The Project will be implemented in
                     densely populated urban areas of Kinshasa, where civil works may give rise to social risks related to labor influx,
                     community health and safety, SAE/SH risks, traffic and public safety, and potential land acquisition, resettlement, and
                     economic displacement. Governance and institutional capacity constraints further contribute to the social risk
                     context, particularly with respect to oversight, coordination, and enforcement. The Social Risk is not rated High at this
                     stage because the scope, location, and footprint of subprojects are not yet fully defined, and the anticipated scale of
                     land acquisition and resettlement remains uncertain at Concept. Based on current information, impacts are expected
                     to be site specific and manageable through the application of proportionate risk management measures. While there
                     is asome risk heightened likelihood of incidents and fatalities associated with civil works in congested urban
                     environments, these risks are considered manageable through strengthened Occupational and Community Health
                     and Safety measures, clear contractor requirements, and enhanced supervision. The Project will apply a risk based
                     approach to social assessment and mitigation, including proportional instruments and targeted capacity building. The
                     Social Risk rating will be reassessed prior to or at Decision Meeting, once subproject details, land acquisition
                     requirements, and implementation arrangements are better defined.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                             Relevant

                      The project activities will entail significant use of energy, water, and raw materials, and are expected to generate

                      and management of construction and operational waste, control of air emissions (including dust and
                      vehicle/equipment exhaust), and prevention of water and soil pollution. Private sector investment in the design and
                      construction of WASH facilities through Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT) arrangements will provide technical capacity to
                      promote the sustainable use of resources and to minimize adverse impacts on human health and the environment.

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                             Relevant

                      The project will directly interface with densely populated urban communities, many of whom are highly vulnerable
                      due to poverty, rapid urbanization, and exposure to climate-related hazards. Key potential risks include community
                      exposure to construction hazards, increased traffic and road safety concerns, labor influx and associated social risks
                      (including gender-based violence and communicable disease transmission), disruption of access to essential services,
                      and heightened vulnerability to natural disasters. Community health and safety issues will be assessed and mitigated

                      ESS5 - Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement                                 Relevant

                      The planned rehabilitation and expansion of water treatment plants, construction of new water intakes, and
                      intensification of distribution networks in Kinshasa may require land acquisition that could result in both physical and
                      economic displacement of project-affected persons (PAPs). While the precise locations and scope of land impacts are
                      not yet fully defined at this stage, based on the nature of the investments and typical sector experience, there may be
                      impacts that include loss of residential or commercial land, temporary or permanent loss of access to assets or
                      livelihoods, including of vulnerable groups. The project documentation anticipates the need for Environmental and
                      Social Impact Assessments (ESIAs) and Resettlement Action Plans (RAPs), or livelihood restoration plans, as needed,
                      and allocates resources to anticipate for potential resettlement costs, which will be reflected in a land acquisition
                      financing memo.

                     C. Overview of Required Environmental and Social Risk Management Activities

                     C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or
                     required by Appraisal?
                     The Borrower will prepare, consult upon, and disclose an Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP) and a
                     Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) — including a project-level Grievance Mechanism — prior to appraisal. The ESCP
                     will specify minimum E&S staffing requirements, including dedicated environmental, social, and SAE/SH specialists, and
                     will define timeframes and responsibilities for the preparation of subsequent instruments during implementation. An
                     E&S Screening Tool fulfilling the requirements of ESS1, paragraph 52 is currently being prepared and will be disclosed
                     prior to appraisal.

                     Given that subproject locations and designs are not yet finalized, site-specific Environmental and Social Impact
                     Assessments (ESIAs) will be required for substantial-risk investments — including water treatment plants and fecal
                     sludge treatment facilities — with dedicated assessments of biodiversity impacts on the Congo River and its tributaries
                     in line with ESS6.

                     Lower-risk activities will be managed through Environmental and Social Management Plans (ESMPs). Labor
                     Management Procedures (LMP) and a SAE/SH Prevention and Response Action Plan will be prepared as disclosable
                     components of the Project Operations Manual. Where land acquisition results in displacement, Resettlement Action
                     Plans (RAPs) or Livelihood Restoration Plans will be prepared in accordance with ESS5.

                     The World Bank's due diligence will include an assessment of the institutional capacity of CEP-O and REGIDESO to
