                  IDA PROGRAM DOCUMENT FOR A PROPOSED CREDIT TO THE KYRGYZ REPUBLIC

 I. COUNTRY CONTEXT AND OPERATION SUMMARY
1.      This proposed Development Policy Operation (DPO) of US$101.5 million in IDA credit aims to support
the Government of the Kyrgyz Republic (GoKR) with the implementation of reforms and policy priorities
highlighted in the National Strategy for Development (NSD) for 2018-40. This DPO is the first in a series of two
operations that is central to the World Bank Group’s (WBG) engagement in the country, as outlined in the 2024-
2028 Country Partnership Framework (CPF). The DPO series will support structural reforms across three areas: (i)
ensuring sustainable and equitable energy supply, including financial viability, transparency and accountability in
the energy sector; (ii) enhancing social service delivery and climate resilience; and (iii) strengthening fiscal
sustainability and promoting a more competitive and private sector driven economy. The OPEC Fund is considering
providing parallel financing in support of this first DPO.
2.      Greater macroeconomic and political stability, combined with an ambitious reform and investment
program (including the Kambarata 1 Hydroelectric Power Plant, or K1 HPP, project currently under preparation
with World Bank support), have created an important opportunity to re-engage with the Kyrgyz Republic on
key elements of its structural reform agenda. The last DPO in the Kyrgyz Republic was approved in 2018, but
policy dialogue became more challenging thereafter with frequent changes within government and an unstable
government policy.1 Greater political and institutional stability since 2021 has enabled the GoKR to elaborate a
major new economic reform program and accelerate transformational economic investments such as the K1 HPP,
mobilizing significant commitments from a range of international financial institutions. In this context, the GoKR
has engaged the WBG to help shape a structural reform agenda that can anchor its transformational investments,
ensure their benefits are inclusive and sustainable, and support vibrant private sector growth.
3.       Despite a recent period of rapid growth, the Kyrgyz Republic still faces persistent barriers to private
sector investment and job creation. With a GNI of US$1,760 per capita in 2023 (Atlas method), the Kyrgyz
Republic is the second poorest country in the Europe and Centra Asia (ECA) region, even as its population is
growing at 2 percent a year. Following the break-up of the Soviet Union, the Kyrgyz Republic was one of the first
among the Commonwealth of Independent States to liberalize prices, open its economy to trade and foreign
investment, and to join the WTO. Based on these reforms, growth accelerated in the 2000s. However, since then
the Kyrgyz Republic has experienced a slowdown in the pace of reforms, thereby risking the sustainability of
economic growth, and hindering the creation of productive jobs. Although the Kyrgyz Republic has seen a surge
in economic activity in recent years, partly due to a large increase in trade-related economic activity, job creation
is hampered by the persistent constraints in the business environment, weak competition, high but inefficient
social spending, barriers to trade and poor infrastructure, including energy supply. Without reforms to raise the
economy’s supply potential, this growth acceleration may be short-lived.
4.      In this context, the GoKR is taking action to strengthen foundations for growth and job creation by
establishing affordable and reliable energy, more efficient social services targeting the vulnerable, and a more
attractive business environment. The proposed DPO series and the planned K1 HPP (currently under preparation
with possible Board presentation in H1 FY26) are two complementary pillars of the WBG support for this national
strategy. The K1 HPP is a transformational export-oriented energy project which, once completed, will bring

significant clean, reliable and sustainable energy supply and increased export revenues in the Kyrgyz Republic,
and contribute to the decarbonization of regional power grids in Central Asia (CA). The Kyrgyz Republic already
generates around 90 percent of its electricity from hydro-power but suffers from insufficient and unreliable
generation. Further investment in renewable energy generation will help meet fast-growing domestic and regional

improve water resources management, alleviate winter energy deficits, displace fossil-fuel generation, and
strengthen regional cooperation in Central Asia. The successful implementation of K1 HPP is fundamental to
private sector-led growth and job creation through the provision of reliable and green energy for firms and
citizens, while also enabling the country to lift living standards for the wider population through increased energy
export earnings. However, the full realization of the significant domestic and regional welfare benefits expected
from the K1 HPP will require complementary reforms to enable a rapid, inclusive and sustainable development
trajectory for the Kyrgyz Republic.
5.       The proposed DPO series will support reforms to advance the broad GoKR reform agenda, including to
maximize the benefits from the K1 HPP. First, the DPO series will support policies to improve financial viability,
transparency and accountability in the energy sector as well as to promote private sector investment in the energy
sector. Furthermore, it will support reforms to help establish effective systems through which future benefits of
K1 HPP and other large scale investments will benefit the population, including the most vulnerable. The Kyrgyz
Republic public spending on social sectors (health, education and social protection) is slightly higher, at 12.2
percent of gross domestic product (GDP) in 2022, than the lower middle-income country average of 11.8 percent.
Increased revenues from the K1 HPP and other large scale projects will need to be accompanied by improvements
in the efficiency and quality of social spending and their targeting for poor households and through inter-
budgetary transfers targeted to address local development priorities, which the DPO series will support.
Specifically, the reforms target improved efficiency and quality of social programming for poor households and to
enhance equity through inter-budgetary transfers targeted to address local development priories, as well as
reforms to strengthen climate resilience through the adoption of the National Green Taxonomy (NGT) and
improving water resource management. The DPO series will also support efforts to strengthen fiscal sustainability
and improve the business environment, including by strengthening investors’ legal rights in line with good
international practice.

 II. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK
6.       The Kyrgyz Republic’s macroeconomic policy framework is adequate for the proposed operation. The
Kyrgyz Republic has achieved rapid growth while achieving a fiscal surplus and lowering public debt. While a fiscal
deficit is expected to reemerge from 2025 onwards, fiscal sustainability risks are expected to remain limited.
Monetary policy has remained sufficiently prudent to bring inflation to the target in 2024, following a series of
shocks, although continued vigilance in the face of an overheating economy remains important. Historically, the
Kyrgyz Republic has been able to maintain a relatively large current account deficit and an open capital account
and is expected to do so in the future, financed with a combination of foreign direct investment (FDI), portfolio
inflows, external loans and domestic gold production. Although the country’s exchange rate has been moderately
overvalued in recent years, the Kyrgyz Republic operates a managed floating exchange rate regime and has
substantially increased its gross international reserves in the last year.2 The financial sector has demonstrated

 A. Recent Economic Developments
7.      In 2024, the economy posted the third year of very strong economic growth, driven by continued
acceleration in exports, consumption, and investment. Real GDP grew by 9 percent, maintaining the same rate
as in the past two years, prompting overheating of the economy as growth has exceeded estimated potential
output growth of 4.9 percent. Exports, private consumption, and investment all provided substantial contributions
to growth. Exports are estimated to have grown by 42.7 percent,3 driven by gold and re-exports. Domestic
consumption contributed 10 percentage points to GDP growth, owing to robust remittance inflows, primarily from
Russia, and strong credit growth to households. Total investment increased by 33.7 percent in 2024. On the
production side, economic growth was concentrated in services, which grew by 9.3 percent, led by trade, in
addition to hotel and restaurant services. Despite a contraction in gold production at Kumtor, the largest gold
mine, industry growth was 5.6 percent, while construction expanded by 18 percent. There was also strong output
performance in agriculture, which grew by 6.3 percent.
8.       Although inflation has fallen from its post-Covid peak, recently there has been a resurgence of
inflationary pressure as the economy is overheated. Consumer price index (CPI) inflation averaged 5.0 percent
in 2024, within the central bank’s target range of 5-7 percent and less than half the average of 10.8 percent in
2023, as the effects of external commodity price shocks dissipated. In response, the National Bank of the Kyrgyz
Republic (NBKR) cut the policy interest rate by 400 basis points to 9 percent between March and May 2024. Yet
there has been renewed inflationary pressure in recent months, with year-on-year (yoy) CPI inflation rising from
3.8 percent in August 2024 to 7.0 percent by February 2025, driven by food price inflation, which reached 7.6
percent in February 2025. The recent increases in electricity and heating tariffs also contributed to rising inflation.

9.      The latest Balance of Payments (BoP) data show that exports, in US dollar (US$) terms, increased by
49.8 percent, yoy, in the first three quarters of 2024, including strong growth of gold exports. Imports grew more
slowly at 11.6 percent, yoy. Accordingly, the trade deficit declined to 50.5 percent of GDP in Q1-Q3 2024, from
60.3 percent in the same period a year ago. This, and remittance inflows that increased by 10.5 percent in US$
terms, led the current account deficit (CAD) to decline to 38.7 percent of GDP from 52.8 percent in the same
period of 2023. The CAD was financed by net foreign investment inflows of 4.4 percent of GDP and external
borrowing of 4.9 percent of GDP. Rapid growth and inflows have supported a stable exchange rate and
strengthening gross international reserves (GIR). The last available external balance assessment conducted by the
IMF, and summarized in its 2025 Article IV Consultations Report, indicated that Kyrgyz Republic’s real effective
exchange rate (REER) was overvalued by approximately 10 percent in 2024. The country’s GIR increased from 2.1
months of imports (US$3.0 billion) at end 2023 to 3.2 months of imports (US$4.7 billion) at end 2024.

 B. Macroeconomic Outlook and Debt Sustainability
13.      Economic growth is expected to begin slowing in 2025 from elevated levels in the last three years.4
Growth is expected to moderate to 6.8 percent in 2025 as re-export activity stabilizes and this then reduces its
contribution to growth in the service sector. With real wage increases amid a tight urban labor market and
continued growth in remittances, private consumption is projected to grow by 5.8 percent in real terms.
Investment is expected to grow by 13 percent in real terms in 2025, driven by private investment in the
construction of residential and commercial properties, and public investment programs of roads and other
infrastructure. On the production side, real GDP growth is expected to be driven by services, followed by industry,
construction, and agriculture. Gold production is expected to grow, buoyed by a strong international market.
Growth of 5.3 percent is forecast in 2026 before a slight rebound to 5.8 percent in 2027 with the intensification
of capital work on the K1 HPP and other public projects, stabilizing to 5.3 percent in 2028.
14.      Recent strong demand is expected to lead to an increase in inflation in 2025, before the cooling of
economic activity and prudent monetary policy bring it down in future years. Average CPI inflation is projected
to rise to 7 percent in 2025 reflecting the continued overheating of the economy and expected increase in
electricity tariffs. Assuming the NBKR maintains an appropriately tight monetary stance, inflation is projected to
fall to 5 percent in the medium term, in line with moderating demand. This assumes that global food and fuel
prices, which have a significant bearing on domestic prices, will remain stable in the medium term.
15.    The CAD, adjusted for re-exports, is expected to decline over the forecast period, projected at 8.5
percent of GDP in 2025. Exports of goods are projected to grow by 39 percent in US$ terms in 2025, driven by
gold and increases in both production and export prices. Service exports are expected to increase, driven by

 III. PROPOSED OPERATION

 A. Link to Government Program, CPF, other WBG operations, and Corporate Priorities

20.      The operation is informed by the government’s priorities set out in the long-term National Strategy for
Development (NSD) for 2018-2040, the medium-term National Program for Development (NPD) for 2021-2026,
and the Green Economy Strategy for 2024-2028. The operation supports 4 of the 6 pillars in the government’s

energy generation; (iii) social development including economic inclusion programs; and (iv) special development

21.     This operation is aligned with the goals of the Paris Agreement including the targets of the Kyrgyz

 B. Prior Actions, Triggers, Expected Results and Analytical Underpinnings

Pillar I. Ensuring Sustainable and Equitable Energy Supply

25.    This first pillar supports reforms to ensure financial viability, transparency and accountability in the
energy sector as well as to promote private sector investment in the sector, as a foundation for the Kyrgyz
Republic’s sustainable and inclusive economic transformation.
26.     Rationale: Major reforms are needed to ensure a clean, reliable, and sustainable energy supply in the
Kyrgyz Republic. The design and implementation of energy reforms, including the ongoing tariff reform, are crucial
to attract investments into the sector, particularly in the K1 HPP. In addition to the need for investment in clean
energy, the Energy Sector Development Program 2035, approved by the GoKR in 2024,11 sets clear goals for
achieving financial sustainability, affordability, and improved governance in the sector. The country is facing a
growing energy deficit, particularly during the winter months.12 Yet the Kyrgyz Republic, with large hydro and
other renewable energy resources, has abundant potential to generate low-carbon and affordable energy and
ensure energy security for the country and the broader CA region. The energy sector is beleaguered by chronic
underinvestment and requires a structural overhaul to become more effective. Current tariffs are below cost
recovery, hindering investment, as tariffs fail to generate sufficient revenue. Residential electricity tariffs of
US¢1.3/kilowatt-hour were the sixth lowest globally and covered only around 70 percent of costs in 2023. Broad
subsidies to residential tariffs are regressive, with 80 percent of subsidies going to non-poor households. The

the MTTP, and adopt a new methodology for heating tariffs consistent with cost recovery principles.
Prior action #1: To promote financial sustainability of the energy sector, the Recipient has issued an Energy Regulator’s Order

Indicative trigger #1: To promote financial sustainability of the energy sector, the Recipient through its Cabinet of Ministers,

2030; (ii) enact tariff adjustments consistent with the adopted Medium-Term Tariff Policy to achieve full-cost recovery levels
by 2030 ; and (iii) adopt a new methodology to set heating tariffs consistent with the principle of full cost recovery.

Pillar II. Enhancing Social Service Delivery and Climate Resilience
33.      The second pillar supports key reforms that seek to broaden the benefits of the Kyrgyz Republic’s
economic transformation agenda. The reforms target improved efficiency and quality of social programming,
enhancement of equity through inter-budgetary transfers targeted to address local development priorities, as
well as reforms to strengthen climate resilience through the adoption of a green taxonomy and improvement of
water resource management. These reforms will help establish effective systems through which future benefits
of K1 HPP and other large-scale investments will benefit poor households and the vulnerable.
Improving efficiency of expenditures and outcomes in the social sectors
34.     Rationale: Despite the relatively high level of expenditure on education in the Kyrgyz Republic –
approximately 5 to 6 percent of GDP – the outcome in terms of quality of education is low. The Learning Adjusted
years of schooling data show that an equivalent of 4 years of learning is lost due to inefficient education practices
with learning poverty15 standing at 64 percent. Part of the reason for this large learning poverty is that children in
the Kyrgyz Republic start compulsory schooling at age 7, and thus schooling before this age is partial and lacks
robust learning standards. Children are disadvantaged by starting formal learning later, compared to their
counterparts in other countries.
35.     The GoKR’s program of Social Contracts (SC) aims to enable low-income households to create new,
sustainable jobs for themselves. The program aims to give the most disadvantaged the opportunity to generate
their own sustainable livelihoods through the availability of upfront lumpsum cash grants (of KGS 100,000 or
approximately US$1,150) and support services. The SC is a voluntary program that consists of an agreement
between a low-income family and the government, under which a family receives a one-off cash grant to start an
economic activity in exchange for giving up further entitlement to UBK benefits for a three-year period. The
program addresses gender disparities and promotes women's economic empowerment and currently over 90
percent of beneficiaries are women. There were considerable gender gaps in 2023 in the Kyrgyz labor markets,
with female participation at 53.1 percent compared to 78.6 percent for men.16 In 2021, women led only 30 percent
of small businesses, and 34.3 percent of female-headed firms experience credit constraints, compared to 25.0
percent of male-headed firms. Only 14.8 percent of women secure access to banks and credit lines, versus 23.3
percent for men, while women face a higher loan rejection rate of 10.2 percent compared to 4.2 percent for men.
The program specifically addresses credit constraints faced by women, whereby eligible families can receive

Equitable Regional Development

40.      Rationale: Spatial disparities in poverty and welfare outcomes in the Kyrgyz Republic are increasingly
shaped by regional inequalities, largely influenced by differences in local level development. Local self-
governing bodies (LSBs) are critical public services providers in the country, but they lack sufficient resources to
provide adequate services. LSBs are particularly important in terms of local infrastructure provision but implement
less than 10 percent of the State Budget. Targeted transfers from central government for capital investments are
pre-determined at the national level and do not leave room for local decision-making, often creating a mismatch
between resource allocation and local priorities for poverty reduction, climate resilience and service delivery.
More than 80 percent of LSBs rely on transfers from the central government, which are insufficient to redress
significant resourcing disparities between prosperous and deprived local areas. In 2024, the GoKR implemented a
nationwide administrative-territorial reform, consolidating the number of LSBs from 452 to 231 in order to
enhance LSB efficiency as well as laying the foundations for channelling increased funding to the local level. To
support this reform and strengthen inter-budgetary linkages with local priorities, a new standardized Socio-
economic Development Plan (SEDP) methodology was developed with World Bank support. The updated
approach utilizes improved local-level data to identify priorities for local development while aligning with local
budgets including resources from Regional Development Funds, with strategic, inclusive, and climate-resilient
development priorities, reinforcing accountable and evidence based regional planning.
41.      Program: In order to improve efficiency and equity of resource allocation, the GoKR has linked resources
to the local level with the priorities set out in the SEDPs. Article 88 of the Budget Code of the Kyrgyz Republic
states that the SEDP must form the basis of local budget planning. Thus, the newly introduced SEDP methodology
strengthens the alignment between local plans and budgets, linking funding to evidence-based, measurable
priorities focused on improving local opportunities and access to services, especially for vulnerable groups and
increasing climate resilience. Based on SEDP methodology, the GoKR approved the State Program for
Comprehensive Socio-Economic Development of the Regions as a key instrument for reducing regional disparities,
fostering sustainable growth hubs, promoting climate resilience, and enabling systematic local development. The
program introduced a program-based financing mechanism to enhance the effectiveness and transparency of
budget planning, ensuring resources are allocated in line with SEDP priorities and development outcomes.
Prior action #5: To improve the funding of local development priorities, the Recipient through its Cabinet of Ministers has
issued a Decree that establishes an integrated planning and fiscal framework to allocate national resources to the local level,
aligned with the inclusive and climate resilient development priorities outlined in the SEDPs developed using evidence-based,
standardized methodology.
Indicative trigger #5: To improve the funding of local development priorities, the Recipient through its Cabinet of Ministers
will issue a Decree approving inter-budgetary targeted regional transfers on a program basis for financing local development
priorities.

42.     Expected results: The Program will establish a foundation for enhancing linkages between planning and
budgeting at the LSB level and increase efficiency of inter-budgetary allocation. Results will be tracked with an
indicator that shows the number of locally initiated programs by LSBs that receive inter-budgetary targeted
transfers to address local priorities and close regional disparities in development with a target of all 40
rayons/districts, following this by end 2027.

Climate legislation and the National Green Taxonomy
43.      Rationale: The GoKR is at an early stage of establishing national regulatory frameworks to support its

Water management
46.     Rationale: The updated NDC identifies the water sector as the highest national adaptation priority.
Changes in climate impact on water resources. Poor performance and limited coverage of water infrastructure,
combined with the lack of pricing and weak corporate and financial governance in the sector, mean that the Kyrgyz
Republic is not meeting its water services demand for irrigation and water supply and sanitation (WSS) in a resilient
and sustainable manner. With the increasing intensity and duration of droughts, combined with depleted glaciers
and growing water demand (particularly during summers, which are becoming hotter) and uncontrolled
abstraction, water availability is compromised. Less than one-third of WSS service providers (municipal and
community-led) are fully operational,17 and most of them have challenges maintaining more than eight hours of

Pillar III. Strengthening Fiscal Sustainability and Improving the Business Environment
49.     The third pillar supports foundational reforms that unlock opportunities for private sector participation
in the Kyrgyz Republic’s fast-growing economy. Specifically, these reforms target fiscal sustainability, public
procurement policy and the investment framework.
Improving the business environment
50.      Rationale: To enable continued economic growth and job creation, the Kyrgyz Republic needs to tackle
key challenges in its business environment. As highlighted in the recently completed World Bank Country
Economic Memorandum, key challenges include the investment and competition framework. The stock of FDI in
the Kyrgyz Republic has declined from 77 percent of GDP in 2016 to 35 percent in 2022, and inflows have been
impeded by an outdated legislative investment framework that falls short of providing key investment protections
in line with modern standards. Moreover, a spike in state-business disputes in the Kyrgyz Republic as the
authorities pursue an aggressive anti-corruption campaign has been adversely affecting the business
environment. The current competition environment in the Kyrgyz Republic does not reward the most productive
firms, leading to allocative inefficiency, low productivity growth and low jobs creation, as revealed by firm-level
evidence.21 Enhancing market competition requires that the excess market power of, and preferential treatment
given to, SOEs22 is moderated so as to give space to private sector firms to grow and create more jobs. The country
lacks a state aid control framework to ensure that state support does not undermine the level playing field. More
broadly, it is important that investor rights are assured by providing core protection guarantees.
51.     Program: The government has submitted a new Investment Law to parliament for approval. The new
Law represents a significant advancement over the previous Law on Investments of 2003. First, it strengthens
investors’ legal rights by providing core protection guarantees in line with good international practices. This
includes protection against expropriation; a guarantee to treat foreign investors no less favourably than national
investors; and the right to freely transfer funds. Second, it provides investors free access to information on
regulations and court rulings that are relevant to investors, at the state and municipal levels. Third, the Law
provides the conditions for establishing a pre-trial investor dispute resolution mechanism at the National
Investment Agency (NIA). This mechanism aims to identify and resolve investor complaints before they evolve
into disputes. The Law also provides an open market access regime, defines the promotion of environmentally

Fiscal sustainability and debt management
53.     Rationale: In the context of external volatility and a scale up in fiscal spending in the coming years,
including for large investment projects, it is important to anchor the government’s strong commitment to longer
term fiscal sustainability. Despite historical periods of fiscal stress, the Kyrgyz Republic has established, and is
cementing, a reputation for strong fiscal control and prudence. As the authorities seek to invest while preserving
their hard-won fiscal credibility and minimize the cost of financing in the market, they will need to establish a clear
and credible longer-term framework for fiscal responsibility. A key priority of the government is to address chronic
underinvestment in public infrastructure while limiting the fiscal burden through effective use of PPP frameworks.
While the 2021 PPP Law incorporates many positive features, only four projects have reached financial closure,
with total investment of US$120 million. The lack of transparency and suitable investment protection mechanisms
have discouraged foreign investors to participate and finance large-scale PPP projects. Addressing these issues
could improve market acceptability and enhance value-for-money in PPP projects.

the 500 MW Scaling Solar program with a 150 MW pilot valued at US$115 million, four small hydroelectric
powerplants on the Chatkal River and a hydroelectric power plant on the Aral River. The amendments to the PPP

legal framework to help make these and other future PPPs a reality. Key changes include the introduction of

Public procurement
56.     Rationale: The public procurement system needs improvements to increase transparency and the use
of competitive methods of procurement, so as to create incentives for private firms to compete for business
and ensure value for money and quality goods and services for the public sector. Procurement of SOEs, which
includes public procurement for pharmaceuticals and textbooks, lacks a robust regulatory and monitoring
framework. The list of exceptional circumstances that justify the use of non-competitive procurement methods is
extensive. The widespread use of direct selection and the lack of a comprehensive electronic procurement
platform elevates risks of mis-procurement, undermines competition, and restricts the government's ability to
analyze procurement data. The World Bank has a long-standing partnership with the GoKR in promoting public
procurement reforms, including support for the Public Procurement Law (PPL) of 2015, establishment of the Public

Review Commission on procurement in 2018 and adoption of standard bidding documents for pharmaceuticals
and other medical products in 2018. Following the amendments of the PPL in 2022, which removed SOE
procurement from its scope, there is a need to enhance the legal framework for SOE procurement and make it
more transparent and competitive. Since the establishment of a dedicated SOE in 2022 for non-competitive
pharmaceutical procurement for the public sector, Kyrgyzpharmacia, which accounts for about 10 percent of total
public procurement in the country, there is a need to revisit internal procurement procedures and the charter of
this SOE to comply with horizontal regulation on SOE procurement and the application of non-competitive
procurement methods only in exceptional circumstances.

 IV. OTHER DESIGN AND APPRAISAL ISSUES
 A. Poverty and Social Impacts
61.     Prior actions supported by this operation are expected to have mostly positive or neutral impacts on
welfare, and no prior actions are anticipated to have significantly adverse poverty, social or distributional
impacts (Annex 3). Only the energy tariff reform (PA #1) could potentially have adverse effects, but its overall
impacts on the poor are estimated to be insignificant due to a combination of the lifeline tariff and households’
low energy expenditure shares. The tiered heating tariff increase, with a 25 percent rise for homes under 80
square meters along with the subsidized (lifeline) electricity tariff for residential consumption up to 700 KWH of
use, has helped mitigate the adverse impacts. The supported energy tariff increase is projected to have increased
the poverty rate by only 0.07 percentage points and reduced the purchasing power of poor households by 0.3
percent. These insignificant impacts align with the data on high frequency household perception surveys, which
show a declining trend in the share of people who reported that they were struggling with energy utility bills —
from 18 percent in March 2024 to 8 percent in November 2024. Further tariff increases are planned (under IT #1)
and their welfare impacts and any required mitigation, if necessary, will be assessed and included in DPO2.
62.      The expansion of the Social Contract (SC) program (part of PA #4) is designed to help households
improve their livelihoods. Expanded benefits will improve welfare of some poor households. This program targets
low-income families receiving UBK assistance or holding a social passport. Participation in the SC program is
voluntary hence UBK beneficiaries are not being forced off the UBK to participate in the SC program. Those who
have elected to participate in the SC program submit applications along with a viable business plan and are
selected by relevant commissions, based on evaluation criteria. The business plans are developed with initial
training and support of the employees of the relevant local LSBs. Grievance and appeal mechanisms are available
to the participants and others. Additionally, the program includes an opt-out mechanism, allowing participants to
return to the UBK if their business fails, along with provisions for returning funds and assets in cases where the
applicant cannot generate income.
63.      The new Water Code (PA #7) aims to improve water access, which is expected to improve child health
and reduce the time spent collecting water, allowing families to engage in productive activities. By waiving permit
requirements for households and priority public services, the reform is not expected to negatively impact low-
income households. The IT will establish a framework for a new water tariff structure for cost recovery while using
cross-subsidy mechanisms to ensure affordability for poor and vulnerable groups and the direct welfare impacts
will be assessed and appropriate mitigation measures taken once the tariff structure is in place under DPO-2.
64.     All other PAs are expected to have positive or neutral poverty and distributional impacts. Cash flow
management and audit of the energy sector is not expected to directly impact welfare (PA #2). Education reforms

 B. Environmental, Forests, and other Natural Resources Aspects
65.     Overall, the policy reforms and prior actions supported by this DPO are expected to have a positive
impact on the Kyrgyz Republic’s environment, forests, and other natural resources. PA #3, on promoting private
investments in the renewable energy sector is envisioned to lead to GHG emissions reduction, decrease in air and
water pollution and enhancing energy security, and thus have significantly positive effects on the country’s
environment. PA #7, on greater efficiency in the use and management of water resources, is expected to have a
positive impact on water resources. PA #8, on financing of climate related activities and a NGT, is also expected
to have positive environmental impacts. Collectively, these reforms are expected to encourage the growth of a
green economy, reduce air pollution, and provide new opportunities for private sector involvement and
investment in renewable energy. In the event negative environmental effects arise, the Kyrgyz Republic’s
environmental laws and regulations incorporate necessary mitigation measures. The 2010 Constitution provides
the basis for protecting and managing the environment and natural resource use. In addition, the requirements
and procedures in its environmental protection law (1999) and environmental impact assessment (2015), contain
principles of environmental impact assessment with regulations, policies, laws and practices, and enforcement
mechanisms that can mitigate adverse impacts to acceptable levels.

 C. PFM, Disbursement, and Auditing Aspects
66.      The fiduciary risk for this operation is moderate. The most recent Public Expenditure and Financial
Accountability (PEFA) assessment from 2020 highlights an improvement in performance since 2014, with 18 out
of 28 performance indicators rated "B" and above. The General Government Budget is publicly accessible,24
reflecting a commitment to transparency. The GoKR Public Financial Management (PFM) Reform Strategy is
focused on increasing efficiency, transparency, and accountability in managing public finances through improved
budget execution, integration of financial systems, and modernization of internal controls. Key priorities include
strengthening commitment controls, automating treasury operations, improving internal audit quality, and
aligning financial practices with international standards. The PEFA Assessment confirmed public availability of the
General Government budget that demonstrates the government's commitment to transparency and allows
citizens and stakeholders alike to scrutinize and understand how public funds are being allocated and spent.
67.     Overall, the PFM is assessed to be adequate. The PFM system is undergoing reforms aimed at further
improvement of budget processes and overall financial governance. These reforms are part of the country’s PFM
Development Strategy for 2017-2025, which has been largely implemented. The World Bank is supporting these
reforms in public audit through the Central Asia Supreme Audit Institutions Capacity Strengthening Project, aimed
at strengthening the capacity of the Kyrgyz Supreme Audit Institution in financial and performance audits.

 V. SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION
73.     The overall risk to this operation is substantial.27 The categories of political and governance, institutional
capacity for implementation and sustainability, sectoral strategies and policies and stakeholders risks are deemed
as posing substantial risks to the achievement of the PDO. Table 5 presents the residual risk ratings by category.
A description of these risks and their respective mitigation measures is provided below.
74.      Political and governance risks are substantial. Political risks stem from frequent changes in government,
which may lead to changes in policy in relation to areas of the supported government program. There have been
12 changes in government since 2011. Governance risks arise from political-business ties, weak institutions, and
deficiencies in the investment environment that may potentially derail timely implementation of the program,
including those that target the business environment and utility sector. Upcoming Parliamentary elections are
scheduled in 2026, to be followed by a Presidential election a year later. These risks are partially mitigated by the
fact that the reforms supported by this operation are broadly recognized to be highly important and are supported
by the private sector, civil society, and the general population.
75.     Risks related to the institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability are substantial. The
technical capacity of the implementing agencies is limited and subject to high staff turnover. This challenge is
compounded by the centralization of much of the GoKR reform implementation effort. Reforms will need
complementary capacity-building. These risks are mitigated by capacity support from the World Bank, as well as
from other development partners through improved donor coordination. Examples of coordinated donor efforts
in supporting counterpart capacity building include World Bank’s collaboration with the Asian Development Bank
on energy and SOE procurement reforms.
76.      Risks related to sectoral strategies and policies are deemed substantial. The government has well-
elaborated policies and strategies in the water and energy sectors. However, the strategic vision in other reform
areas, including public procurement and targeted social assistance, is less clear. By supporting the government’s
reform efforts in these sectors, the current operation will help to address these risks by assisting the authorities
to improve policy design and strategic vision.
77.      Stakeholders risks are also assessed as substantial given the country’s history of social unrests triggered
by utility tariff reforms and other reform initiatives. The ambitious reform program supported by this DPO has

ANNEX 3. Environment and Poverty/Social Analysis

Poverty and Social Impact Analysis of Electricity Tariff Increase

Context: The energy tariff reform included a uniform 10.8 percent increase in household electricity expenses, with tiered
heating cost increases: a 25 percent rise for homes under 80 square meters and a 290 percent rise for larger homes. This
annex elaborates on the results of the analysis of the distributional impacts of the tariff increases.


The methodology presented here evaluates the welfare impacts of energy tariff reforms in the Kyrgyz Republic using data
from the 2023 Kyrgyz Integrated Household Survey (KIHS). It establishes a welfare baseline based on per capita
consumption and determines poverty status by comparing consumption to the national poverty line. The impact of energy
tariffs is estimated using a compensation variation approach which measures the welfare impact of a price shock as the
amount of money sufficient to compensate households for them to achieve similar levels of welfare at pre-shock prices.
This is estimated as the sum-product of household budget shares (i.e., electricity and heating expenditure shares) and the
price change for each item (electricity and heating tariff changes). This gives the incremental household energy
expenditure burden, which is then subtracted from total household consumption to derive post-reform welfare levels and
estimate poverty impacts by comparing pre-reform and post-reform poverty rates. The analysis assumes constant
consumption despite price changes, static household incomes, and perfect tariff implementation. It does not account
behavioral adaptations, such as substitution between energy sources, thus it presents an upper-bound estimate of welfare
impacts.

Results: The tariff increase has minor short-term welfare impacts. According to simulations, the 2024 reforms would raise
annual household energy bills by 766 KGS for the poorest quintile and 1,003 KGS for the richest quintile, representing
about 0.3 percent of household expenditure. Consequently, the poverty rate is projected to marginally rise from 30.92
percent to 30.99 percent, affecting around 1,200 households in the Kyrgyz Republic. The small welfare losses are
attributed to the low energy shares in total households’ expenditures, with an average share of 2.6 percent nationally.
Rising energy tariffs have a similar impact across different regions of the country, affecting household consumption by
about 0.3 percent. Areas like Batken and Osh experience only slight welfare loss, likely due to their lower energy use. In
contrast, Bishkek, the capital, has a more significant welfare loss of around 0.5 percent, primarily due to its heavy reliance
on central heating.

 Annex 6: IMF Assessment Letter

                     Kyrgyz Republic—Assessment Letter for the World Bank May 12, 2025

Recent Developments, Outlook, and Risks

1.     The Kyrgyz Republic has performed remarkably well amid a highly uncertain external environment. The
economy grew by 9 percent annually since 2022, which picked up further to 13.1 percent in the first quarter of
2025 with trade, manufacturing and construction driving the expansion. Headline inflation rose to 6.9 percent
by March 2025 while core inflation declined to 6 percent. The current account deficit reached 45 percent of GDP
in 2023, while BoP errors and omissions exceeded 42 percent of GDP, largely reflecting unrecorded re-exports.
IMF staff estimates a CA deficit of 31 percent of GDP for 2024, which after accounting for re-exports narrows to
8.3 percent. Public debt declined to 36.6 percent of GDP in 2024 underpinned by strong GDP growth and a budget
surplus of 1.9 percent of GDP. The som appreciated by 6.0 percent in real effective terms in 2024 and the External
Sector Assessment suggests that it was overvalued by 9.9 percent. Reserves reached 4.3 months of imports
thanks to the purchases of domestic gold.

2.     Growth is projected to moderate to 6.8 percent in 2025 and converge to about 5¼percent in the
medium term. This reflects the expected normalization of re-exports trade and easing domestic demand. The
construction of the China-Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan railway and Kambarata-1 HPP is expected to provide a growth
boost of about ¼ percent in 2025 and 2027. Inflation is projected to remain broadly stable under the assumption
of prudent monetary policy, and the CA deficit to stabilize at about 6-7 percent of GDP over the medium term,
accounting for the gradual moderation of re-export trade and large infrastructure-related imports. These large-
scale public investments would widen the overall fiscal deficit, but public debt should remain contained under
42 percent of GDP thanks to robust GDP growth. Reserves are projected to decline to 2.7 months by 2030.

3.      The outlook remains highly uncertain and depends significantly on regional geopolitical developments.
A slowdown in activity in Russia and depreciation of the ruble would adversely impact Kyrgyz growth. This risk
would be compounded by a reversal in gold prices, an increase in oil prices, extreme weather events, and
cyberthreats. Conversely, the ending of the war in Ukraine could translate into higher demand for Kyrgyz workers
in Russia and tighter domestic labor markets but also higher remittances, wages and inflation pressures. The
direct impact of US tariffs is likely to be insignificant given limited direct exposure, but the indirect effects through
commodity prices, tighter global financial conditions, and higher imports from China could be larger.

Economic Policies and Structural Reforms

4.      The overall fiscal balance is projected to swing from a surplus of 1.9 percent of GDP in 2024 to a deficit
of 3.4 percent of GDP in 2025 and stay around 3 percent of GDP over the medium term. Tax revenue should
dip by about 1 percent of GDP in 2025 due to a moderation of taxes on international trade, CIT and VAT. High
gold prices, on the other hand, are projected to boost dividends from Kumtor Gold Mining Company. Current
spending is expected to increase by 0.2 percent of GDP in 2025 and capital expenditure by 1.6 percent of GDP. The
latter includes the outlays for the China-Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan railway and Kambarata-1 HPP, which are estimated to

cost US$4.9 billion and US$3.9 billion respectively. The recent upgrade of the Kyrgyz Republic’s sovereign credit rating
by S&P to B+ with a stable outlook would support the authorities’ plans to issue international bonds to finance smaller-
scale hydropower generation projects. Staff projects a US$500 million Eurobond in 2025 and US$400 million per year
in 2026-28. The Debt Sustainability Analysis finds that public debt is sustainable with a medium risk of debt distress,
which points to the need for additional fiscal space to improve resilience to shocks. To this end, tax revenue can be
increased by up to 4 percent of GDP in the medium term by streamlining VAT exemptions and special tax regimes;
increasing the progressivity of the PIT; and improving revenue administration. Channeling Kumtor’s full profits to the
Treasury can provide an additional 1 percent of GDP. Expenditure can be optimized by reducing energy subsidies and
the public wage bill, and improving the efficiency of public spending.

5.      Monetary policy should aim at keeping inflation within the NBKR’s target range of 5–7 percent. The
direct impact of US tariffs on inflation is likely to be negligible but consumer prices could be affected through global
food and energy prices or increased imports from China. Despite high interest rates, credit grew by 34 percent in 2024,
and consumer loans surged from 11 percent of total loans in 2020 to 27 percent by end-2024. While the credit gap is
negative, its upward trajectory and the shift towards debt-driven consumption suggest elevated vulnerabilities. Strong
GDP, credit and real wage growth in 2024 coupled with the projected fiscal stimulus point to persistent demand
pressures which could warrant tightening of the monetary policy stance if inflation pressures were to persist or rise.
While banks are well capitalized and liquid, NPLs remain elevated at over 10 percent of total loans, which call for
supervisory vigilance in view of heightened uncertainty.

6.      Strengthening the autonomy of the NBKR is critical to safeguard its ability to maintain price and
financial sector stability. The NBKR’s governance could be strengthened by implementing the remaining
recommendations of the IMF Safeguards Assessment which include an introduction of a non-executive majority to the
NBKR’s board and the audit committee. Moreover, the repeated transfers of NBKR profits to the budget even as its
capital remains below the statutory threshold weakens the NBKR’s credibility and institutional integrity, unduly
increases liquidity, and adds to inflation pressures.

7.       Structural reforms remain crucial to enhance inclusive growth and job creation. Improving Public Financial
Management, reforming SOE ownership and management, strengthening the AML/CFT framework, and enhancing
transparency and accountability of the public sector are important priorities to pursue. A comprehensive SOE ownership
strategy is essential to define the government's policy objectives for SOEs and guide its privatization decisions. The rule
of law, protection of property rights, avoiding state monopolies and fostering competition are crucial for building trust
in public institutions and improving the business climate to encourage private investment and innovation.

8.      The Fund maintains close engagement with the Kyrgyz Republic. The 2023 Article IV consultation was
concluded on February 5, 2024, and the 2025 Article IV consultation is scheduled. In addition to the ongoing technical
assistance on national accounts statistics, the public wage bill, and fiscal risks management, the Kyrgyz Republic would
benefit from the Fund’s technical assistance on external sector statistics, public finance and public investment
management, digital assets, and international reserve management.
