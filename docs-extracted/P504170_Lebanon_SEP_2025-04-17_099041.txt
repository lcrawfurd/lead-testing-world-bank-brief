1. INTRODUCTION AND PROJECT DESCRIPTION

The Greater Beirut Water Supply Project (GBWSP; Project ID: P103063) loan was approved on December
16, 2010 with an original Closing Date of June 30, 2016. The Loan Agreement between the Borrower and
the Bank was signed on February 8, 2012, and declared effective on December 4, 2012. The Project
Agreements between the two project implementing agencies was made as follows (i) the Bank and the
Beirut Mount Lebanon Water Establishment (BMLWE) and (ii) the Bank and the Council for Development
and Reconstruction (CDR) and these agreements were signed on the same date as the Loan Agreement.

The Closing Date of the GBWSP is April 30, 2025 while the construction works have not been fully
completed. The BMLWE and the CDR are currently preparing a second phase of the GBWSP, the Second
Greater Beirut Water Supply Project, SGBWSP (the Project), to be financed by the World Bank, with the
aim to complete the remaining construction works and bring the water supply system into operation
(details provided under section below: Project Components).

Under the first GBWSP, which is under the safeguards policies, the two implementing agencies prepared
and disclosed the following: 1) Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) for the ongoing GBWSP
project on August 6, 2010 and the original ESIA instrument is available at this link, the English Executive
Summary is available at this link, and the Arabic Executive Summary is available at this link; and, 2) the
Resettlement Action Plan (RAP) for the ongoing GBWSP project dated August 4, 2010 is available at this
link.

The SGBWSP falls under the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) which was launched
in October 2018. The ESSs which apply under this project include:

     -    ESS1: Assessment and management of environmental and social risks and impacts;
     -    ESS2: Labor and working conditions;
     -    ESS3: Resource efficiency and pollution prevention and management;
     -    ESS4: Community health and safety;
     -    ESS5: Land acquisition, restrictions on land use and involuntary resettlement;
     -    ESS6: Biodiversity conservation and sustainable management of living natural resources; and
     -    ESS10: Stakeholder engagement and information disclosure.

Under the SGBWSP, the CDR and BMLWE PMUs prepared the following E&S instruments which were
cleared and disclosed on the CDR and BMLWE project websites and will ensure their implementation
throughout the project life cycle:

     -    Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP);
     -    Stakeholders Engagement Plan (SEP);
     -    Addendum to the Resettlement Action Plan (RAP);
     -    Addendum to the Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA)
     -    Labor Management Plan (LMP)

Project Components

As per the Project Agreements, the CDR and BMLWE will undertake the implementation of the Project.
The proposed Project will support: (i) the provision of more reliable water services in selected areas of
Greater Beirut and Mount Lebanon through the completion of the bulk water infrastructure initiated
under the ongoing GBWSP; (ii) strengthening the resilience of the water treatment infrastructure to
changes in raw water quality; and (iii) improving the operational and financial performance of the BMLWE
by supporting key institutional reform activities. The Project will consist of six components, which are
summarized below:
1.      Component 1: Completing remaining bulk water infrastructure (US$148 million total cost, 100%
IBRD). This component will finance investments to complete the remaining bulk water infrastructure
started and significantly advanced under the GBWSP1 to ensure water is conveyed to the existing water
supply distribution network serving the GBML area, including the required construction supervision
services for all the works. It will also finance rehabilitation works of bulk water infrastructure that may
have been potentially damaged by the conflict in the Middle East.2 The component will have the following
subcomponents:
     (a) Subcomponent 1.1 – Remaining Tunnel and ancillary works (US$80 million): This subcomponent
         will finance the remaining works to complete the bulk water tunnels started and significantly
         advanced under the GBWSP. These include, among other: (i) the lining of 4 km of the bulk water
         tunnel conveying raw water from the intake point to the Wardanieh WTP; (ii) the lining of 20 km
         of the bulk water tunnel conveying treated water from the Wardanieh WTP to the twin
         transmission pipelines; (iii) the Joun Regulation Structure (JRS) to control the raw water flow to the
         WTP; (iv) an inverted syphon; and (v) a surge shaft.
     (b) Subcomponent 1.2 – Remaining Pipeline Works (US$18 million):3 This subcomponent will finance
         the works to complete the installation of the twin transmission pipelines conveying the treated
         water from the end of the bulk water tunnel at Khalde to the regional reservoirs. It will include,
         among other: (i) excavation and installation of remaining pipeline (about 1660 m of twin pipes), (ii)
         hydrostatic testing of newly installed pipelines, (iii) construction of piles and thrust blocks and, (iv)
         final testing and commissioning of the system.
     (c) Subcomponent 1.3 – Regional reservoirs and transmission pipeline (US$50 million): This
         subcomponent will finance the construction of the three regional reservoirs and an
         interconnecting pipeline. The reservoirs (Hadath 125, Hadath 90 and Hazmieh 904) will increase
         BMLWE’s storage capacity by about 120,000m3, and increase the overall resilience and efficiency

2.       Component 2: Strengthening treatment processes for improved water quality (US$56 million
total cost, 100% IBRD). This component will finance the completion of the construction works of the
original design of the Wardanieh WTP6 that could not be completed under the GBWSP, and adjustments
(add-ons) to the Wardanieh WTP treatment processes through an adaptive management approach. The
adoption of the adaptive management approach aims to balance the need to find the optimal
configuration of add-ons to enhance the resilience of the Wardanieh WTP to variable raw water quality
while maintaining operational costs within the financial capacity of the BMLWE. Following this approach,
the component will finance “no regrets” investments which include, among other: (i) the establishment
of daily raw water quality monitoring and an early warning system, to be installed as soon as the Project
becomes effective;7 and, (ii) the addition of a Powder Activated Carbon (PAC) system including powder
storage, feeder, and dosing system.8 Once water is available at the Wardanieh WTP (after completion of
the raw bulk water tunnel), this subcomponent will finance: (iii) the operation of the Wardanieh WTP in
an adaptative management mode, making it possible to respond to the fluctuations in raw water quality
and the response of the current configuration to different pollution loads, and to identify and manage
potential treatment challenges; and (iv) based on the results of (i) and (iii), the design and installation of
the optimal configuration of add-ons to ensure that the plant’s effluent meets WHO standards throughout
the year. This component will also finance the required construction supervision services for the needed
works. In addition, this component will finance activities to improve water quality management at the
BMWLE, including the rehabilitation and upgrade of BMLWE’s laboratory at the Dbayeh through the
expansion of existing facilities and the purchase of sufficient equipment, laboratory tools and chemicals,
and the reinforcement of chlorination practices to ensure residual chlorine in BMWLE’s distribution
network. To address the impact of the potential damages in the distribution network due to the conflict
in the Middle East, including in the Greater Beirut and Mount Lebanon area, this component will also
finance repairs and rehabilitation of key infrastructure as needed in the distribution network served by
the Wardanieh WTP (area A and C).

2. OBJECTIVES AND DESCRIPTION OF THE STAKEHOLDERS ENGAGEMENT PLAN

The World Bank Guidelines ESS10 Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and
Impacts requires the “Borrower” to develop and implement a Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP)
proportionate to the nature and scale of the project and its potential risks and impacts. The SEP aims to:

     -    Establish a systematic approach to stakeholder engagement that will help borrowers identify
          stakeholders and build and maintain a constructive relationship with them, in particular project-
          affected parties;
     -    Assess the level of stakeholder interest and support for the project and enable stakeholders’ views
          to be taken into account in project design and environmental and social performance;
     -    Promote and provide means for effective and inclusive engagement with project-affected parties
          throughout the project life cycle on issues that could potentially affect them;
     -    Ensure that appropriate project information on environmental and social risks and impacts is
          disclosed to stakeholders in a timely, understandable, accessible and appropriate manner and
          format; and
     -    Provide project-affected parties with accessible and inclusive means to raise issues and grievances
          and allow borrowers to respond to and manage such grievances.

4. STAKEHOLDER IDENTIFICATION AND ANALYSIS

4.1.      Methodology

This section outlines the key stakeholders who will be informed and consulted about the project.

To meet best practice approaches, the project will apply the following principles for stakeholder
engagement:

     -    Openness and life-cycle approach: Public consultations for the project will be arranged during
          the entire project life cycle, carried out in an open manner, free of external manipulation,
          interference, coercion or intimidation.
     -    Informed participation and feedback: Information will be provided to and widely distributed
          among stakeholders in a culturally appropriate format; opportunities are provided for
          communicating stakeholders’ feedback and for analyzing and addressing comments.
     -    Inclusiveness and sensitivity: Stakeholder identification is undertaken to support better
          communications and build effective relationships. The participation process for the project is
          inclusive. Stakeholders are encouraged to be involved in the consultation process, to the extent
          the current circumstances permit. Equal access to information is provided to all identified
          stakeholders. Sensitivity to stakeholders’ needs is the key principle underlying the selection of
          engagement methods. Attention is also given to vulnerable groups.

For the purposes of effective and tailored engagement, stakeholders of the proposed project can be
divided into the following core categories:

     -    Affected Parties – persons, groups and other entities within the Project Area of Influence that are
          directly influenced (actually or potentially) by the project and/or have been identified as most
          susceptible to change associated with the project, and who need to be closely engaged in
          identifying impacts and their significance, as well as in decision-making on mitigation and
          management measures.
     -    Other Interested Parties – individuals/groups/entities that may not experience direct impacts
          from the project but who consider or perceive their interests as being affected by the project
          and/or who could affect the project and the process of its implementation in some way.
     -    Vulnerable Groups – persons who may be disproportionately impacted or further disadvantaged
          by the project(s) as compared with any other groups due to their vulnerable status and that may
          require special engagement efforts to ensure their equal representation in the consultation and
          decision-making process associated with the project.

5. STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PROGRAM

5.1.      Purpose and Timing of Stakeholder Engagement Program

The main goal of the Stakeholders consultations and engagements is to ensure stakeholders, and
particularly local communities in the targeted geographical areas are aware of the planned project
activities, and understand the possible environmental and social implications, impacts, and justifications
of such activities.

The SEP aims to garner the opinions, comments and grievances of affected communities and stakeholders
in an inclusive manner to establish early alignment. It is a vital tool that is used to establish clear and
transparent communication channels with stakeholders. Based on the feedback provided by stakeholders,
the scope of the study can be modified to tackle issues that have been raised.

CDR is planning to hold a series of consultation meetings throughout the project and with a possibility of
adding relevant stakeholders according to the needs of the project. Additional identified stakeholders will
accordingly be reflected in an updated SEP.

The approved cleared SEP will be disclosed prior to project appraisal and following consultation with the
identified stakeholders. As the SEP is a living document, it will be updated periodically and as necessary
as agreed with the Bank following the provisions of the Environmental and Social Commitment Plan
(ESCP). The program proposed in Table 5-1 includes the proposed timing of the various engagement
activities. Precise dates cannot be confirmed until a schedule for the project activities is fixed.
Stakeholders will be made aware of these events through various means of information disclosure as
identified in Table 5-1. Local communities and vulnerable groups will be reached out too, through the
support of local NGOs.

5.2.      Proposed Strategy for Information Disclosure

This section describes the types of information that will be disclosed, the disclosure formats, as well as
the methods that will be used to communicate this information to each of the stakeholder groups during
consultations that will be organized in the project areas.

Consultations proceedings will include findings, the list of attendees, the presentations, and photos. They
will be shared with the stakeholders after the workshop completion. Where necessary, working sessions
materials may be shared prior to the workshop.

Besides the workshops, the stakeholders will be kept informed throughout the period of the project’s
development, specifically regarding the implementation of the stakeholder engagement plan and
Grievance Redress Mechanism via direct messages to consider their feedback and comments and address
them back through formal communication.

5.3.      Proposed Strategy for Consultation

 CDR and BMLWE PMUs will use the following methods for future consultations with each of the
 stakeholder groups. Methods used may vary according to target audience, for example:

     -    Interviews and meetings with stakeholders and relevant organizations;
     -    Surveys, polls, and questionnaires;
     -    Public meetings, workshops, and/or focus groups on specific topics via participatory methods;
     -    One on one interviews with each stakeholder;
     -    Other traditional mechanisms for consultation and decision making; and
     -    Use of traditional mass media and social media.

The PMUs will be documenting stakeholder engagement activities in consultation with the World Bank
and updating the SEP whenever required and ensuring the dissemination of the documented activities on
the CDR’s website. The documentation will take place by including the following:
     -    Date and location of each meeting, with a copy of the notification to stakeholders;
     -    The purpose of the engagement (for example, to inform stakeholders of an intended project or
          to gather their views on potential environmental and social impacts of an intended activity);
     -    The modality of engagement and consultation (for example, face-to-face meetings such as town
          halls or workshops, focus groups, written consultations, or online consultations);
     -    Number of participants and categories of participants;
     -    A list of relevant documentation disclosed to participants;
     -    Summary of main points and concerns raised by stakeholders;
     -    Summary of how stakeholder concerns were addressed and taken into account;
     -    Issues and activities that require follow-up actions, including clarifying how stakeholders are
          informed of decisions; and
     -    Specific measures adopted to reach out to vulnerable groups.

5.4.      Proposed Strategy to Incorporate the View of Vulnerable Groups

Considering the specific scope of this project, vulnerable/disadvantaged groups at this stage can be
identified to be persons with disabilities, elderly, women, those who do not know how to read or write,
and poor households. In order to ensure those groups are adequately involved in the project, specific
engagement methods will be applied. Since the mentioned groups are cross-cutting throughout all
different stakeholder groups, the envisioned methods are being applied throughout consultations with
any of the listed stakeholders. Those methods include:

     -    Women-only consultation sessions; and
     -    Dedicated consultations with identified informal community members; they will be identified
          through close coordination with the local authorities.

5.5.      Timelines

The timeline for the construction phase of the project is 48 months. During construction, operation and
maintenance stages, consultative meetings will be held with stakeholders and vulnerable groups covering
all activities associated with the project and in line with this SEP. Deadlines for comments will be two
weeks after the proposed consultation meetings.

The SEP will be published on the websites of CDR and BMLWE for the entire period of project development
and operation and both PMUs will continue to review the SEP and update it as needed. The
implementation of SEP will be reflected in the progress reports, through its various phases, to ensure
timely identification of any new stakeholders and interested parties and their involvement in the process
of collaboration with the project including any feedback received from them that support successful
project implementation. The methods of engagement will also be revised periodically and as needed to
maintain their effectiveness and relevance to the project’s evolving environment.

5.6.      Review of Comments

Communication and feedback from stakeholders will be taken into consideration at each stage of this
project as per Table 5-1. The stakeholders will be kept informed as the project develops, including
reporting on project environmental and social performance and implementation of the stakeholder
engagement plan and grievance mechanism. The PMUs at CDR and BMLWE plan to have several meetings
with stakeholders, during and post construction, to receive reviews and comments that will be reflected
in progress reports and the future updated SEP.

5.7.      Future Phases of the Project

All stakeholders will be kept informed as the project develops, including reporting and documenting
project environmental and social performance and implementation of the Stakeholder Engagement Plan
and Grievance Mechanism. Both PMUs will prepare quarterly reports regarding environmental and social
impacts, grievances and project achievement and will present these to stakeholders.

7. GRIEVANCE MECHANISM

This section summarizes the main steps and procedures of a formal and documented Grievance Redress
Mechanism to be followed throughout the project implementation.

7.1.      Objectives of the Grievance Mechanism

According to provisions and requirements of the WB’s ESS10 (Stakeholder Engagement and Information
Disclosure), CDR and BMLWE will put in place a functioning, accessible, and inclusive Grievance
Mechanism (GM) that is proportionate to the potential social risks and impacts of the project.
Nevertheless, the key purpose of this GM is to provide an avenue for complaints and suggestions
regarding project’s components.

The main GM uptake channels will be physical visits, phone calls, and emails to CDR and/or BMLWE (see
section 7.4 below). Other channels will be through a QR code placed on mobile signs near the work sites
as well as through Municipalities or union of municipalities where the Project activities will be executed.
The project will respond to all grievances within 30 calendar days of submission, using 6 steps, handled
by the ESHS specialists of the contractors and/or the ESHS specialists of the PMUs at CDR and BMLWE (1
environmental/health & safety specialist and 1 social specialist for both PMUs).

The implementation of this procedure will result in the reduction of environmental and social risks
associated with the project, generate systemic change to reduce the volume of grievances and enhance
the quality of the relationship between the project and its stakeholders.

7.2.      Summary of the GM under of the first GBWSP

Seven grievances were received during the execution of the first GBWSP notably in the period extending
from 19 June 2023 to 06 December 2023. These were received by the ESHS Specialist of the contractor,
registered in a specialized log and attended to. The log was shared with CDR Project Manager since these
grievances were related to the works overseen by CDR. There are no pending complaints. Also, BMLWE
did not receive complaints/grievances during the ongoing execution of the first GBWSP. Details of the
received grievances are detailed under Appendix C.



7.3.      Raising Awareness About the Grievance Mechanism

The existence of the grievance mechanism will be consistently publicized during stakeholder engagement
activities verbally during meetings and focus group discussions. It will also be posted on the websites of

CDR and BMLWE as well as on the concerned municipalities boards or social media accounts (in case they
exist). Mobile signs that are visible to passersby will also be displayed along the work sites, these will
include the contact details (phone number dedicated for the project, e-mail, bar code) for raising a
complaint in both Arabic and English languages.

7.4.      Specification

The GM will be integrated within the existing complaining system(s) at the CDR and BMLWE, and will use
available resources and assets, to the extent possible. But will, at the same time, ensure that the system
has the following key features in place, as a minimum:

     -    Multiple-channel: CDR and BMLWE will activate different channels for submitting a complaint.
          This will include installing complaint boxes in prominent places in all project sites, physical visits,
          phone calls, emails, and QR codes.

     -    Standardized: CDR and BMLWE will put in place a standardized process for receiving
          grievances/appeals through assigning clear responsibilities to the PMUs that have been assigned
          to the project from the existing staff. The existing staff will be trained on the SEP and GM. In case
          of receiving any SEA/SH complaints, they should be carefully handled following the key principles
          of confidentiality and survivor’s consent, and reported to the WB within 48 hours.

     -    Culture-sensitive: CDR and BMLWE will handle grievances in a culturally appropriate manner,
          being objective, sensitive, and responsive to the PAPs. The GM will allow for anonymous
          complaints to be raised and addressed. The mechanism will also facilitate lodging complaints in
          a simplified format and language, suitable for local cultures. Moreover, to manage the risks
          related to labor and/or SEA/SH, CDR and BMLWE will assign female staff (in case the ESHS
          specialists are not women) for receiving and processing grievances from women, where
          separation between men and women is the norm in the beneficiary community and as women
          are more comfortable talking to other women. Provision to ensure confidentiality as well as
          protocols for referring cases to specialized agencies will be included (e.g., cases related to gender-
          based violence, child neglect/abuse, etc), following the consent of the victim/complainant.

     -    Vulnerability-responsive: CDR and BMLWE will put in place all measures that would facilitate
          grievances lodging from vulnerable groups, including, people with disabilities, women, and elderly
          and people that have been impacted by any of the project’s activities. These measures will
          consider the special needs, protect privacy, and provide safe and secure channels. By so doing,
          the GM will build on existing grievance structures, coordinate with social service providers, and
          provide SEA/SH complainants with all possible means of protection starting from point of
          complaining until resolution. The GM will also provide adequate advice/ procedural assistance for
          referral to traditional justice mechanisms, whenever needed.

     -    Outreach and Communication: CDR and BMLWE will designate necessary staff to receive,
          acknowledge, sort, refer, and report on grievances/appeals. Communication material will be
          developed, which will include brochures and social media channels. The material will not only

          practices and coordinated between the ESHS specialists at PMUs (1 environmental/health &
          safety specialist and 1 social specialist for both PMUs). The level of satisfaction will also be
          surveyed and measured annually. In addition, the GM will allow complainants who were not
          satisfied with responses, to resort to a second appellate level.

7.6.      The Grievance Mechanism Process

The project undertakes to respond to all grievances within 30 calendar days of submission (see the detail

Grievance Log will be maintained by ESHS specialists. A summary of the feedback received, date that it

the issue is under consideration and/or has been escalated, whether the feedback is anonymous (this

non-anonymous complainant , whether the issue is pending and if so, whether it has been escalated to
the PMU GM director.

     c. Acknowledgment and Follow Up
If the identity of the complainant is known, then receipt of the feedback will be acknowledged within 3-6
business days. At the 20 business-day mark, if a complaint/question is still pending, the ESHS specialists
will provide an update to the non-anonymous complainant, inform them if there will be delays in resolving
their case, and provide the date for which they will be able to provide a response. The Grievance should
be classified as follows:

High-Level Feedback

Feedback received to be categorized as ‘high’ level instances will include issues that meet the following
criteria:

     -    Incidents that caused or may potentially cause significant or great harm to the environment,
          workers, communities, or natural resources, including issues of gender-based violence and
          SEA/SH;

     -    Incidents which entail failure to implement E&S measures with significant impacts or repeated
          non-compliance with E&S policies;

     -    Incidents for which failure to address may potentially cause significant impacts that are complex
          and/or costly to reverse; and

     -    Incidents that may result in fatality or some level of lasting damage or injury.

This type of feedback will be acknowledged, and an investigation will be launched by the PMUs and any
other relevant stakeholders within 24 hours during workdays and within 48 hours if the feedback was
received over the weekend. It should be noted that all incidents, including accidents and fatalities and
SEA/SH incidents need to be reported to the World Bank within 48 hours. This guidance is provided in the
Environment & Social Incident Response Procedures.

Standard- Level Feedback

If the identity of the aggrieved person is known and the grievance is classified as ‘standard’, the
acknowledgement of grievance will be within 3 business days. At the 20 business-day mark, if a
complaint/question is still pending, the ESHS specialists will provide an update to the non-anonymous
complainant, inform them if there will be delays in resolving their case, and provide the date for which
they will be able to provide a response.

Second Greater Beirut Water Supply Project (SGBWSP)                               Stakeholder Engagement Plan




     d. Verification, Investigation, and Action
Verification and investigation involve gathering information about the complaint to determine its validity
and to generate a clear picture of the circumstances surrounding the issue under consideration. This
process normally includes site visits, document reviews, a meeting with the complainant (if known and
willing to engage), liaising with the PMU safeguards specialist(s) and meetings with other individuals
and/or entities who can assist with resolving the issue. Complaints related to the misuse of funds are not
categorized as ESHS risks, but will be reported to the team.

Potential actions include responding to a query or comment, providing complainants with a status update
by the 20-working day mark. Feedback that cannot be resolved at the PMUs level should be referred to
the GM director for further investigation per the timelines and standards established for the GM.

     e. Providing Responses to Complainants
This step entails informing complainants and the public at large about the results of the investigation and
the actions taken. It is integral to enhance the GM’s visibility among beneficiaries and increase users’ trust
in the system (making it more likely that they will provide feedback). The ESHS specialists of the PMUs can
provide feedback by contacting the complainant directly (if his or her identity is known) within a 30-day
period of receipt. Information included in the written response should include: setting out/summary of
the feedback, detailing how the investigation was conducted, summarizing the facts, outlining the findings
appropriate resolution along with the rationale for such resolution. Resolutions may include an
explanation, apology, reconsideration, reimbursement, restitution– all are possible remedies.

     f. Closing and Reporting
The overall accountability concerning the management of the Grievance Mechanism Procedure lies with
the PMUs. The ESHS Specialists and the PMU Director (one director for BMLWE and one director CDR) are
responsible for overseeing the implementation, monitoring and treatment of the grievances. They will
determine if the grievance has been closed to the satisfaction of the claimant. Once it has been closed,
the information will be entered in the grievances register and the grievance can be closed.

ESHS specialists will report to the PMU directors on a monthly basis. Continuous communication should
be maintained between the ESHS specialists at the PMUs (1 environmental specialist and 1 social specialist
for both PMUs), document and follow up the resolution of all grievances. The ESHS specialists will make
consolidated reports available to the World Bank on a quarterly basis and on request. The reports should
provide an overview of feedback received that is related to project implementation. Additional
information should include:

     -    Nature of the feedback;

     -    Aggregate information on complainants (including demographic – student, parent, gender, etc.);

     -    Information on where the feedback was received and in what format;

     -    Information on the status of complaints and queries (resolved, under review, etc.);

     -    Information on how complaints and queries were resolved;

                                                                                                                37
Second Greater Beirut Water Supply Project (SGBWSP)                              Stakeholder Engagement Plan




     -    Information on unresolved complaints/queries and why they are not yet resolved; and

     -    Recommendations for improving the GM. These recommendations should be based on the
          monitoring of the GM, specifically the extent of the GM’s functionality and the types of feedback
          that have emerged.

     g. Procedure for Appeal
Aggrieved people who are dissatisfied with the outcome of their complaint can appeal the decision by
resubmitting their complaint to the ESHS specialists within 30 working-days of receiving a response to the
original submitted grievance. Subsequently, the ESHS specialists have 30 working-days to investigate and
address the issue. Additionally, the ESHS specialists has 10 working-days to prepare a comprehensive
response, including the findings of the investigation and the rationale of the determination. Accordingly,
within a maximum of 40 working days, the appeal case should be closed.

Lastly, if the aggrieved person is still not satisfied with the solution provided, they have the option to go
to court.
