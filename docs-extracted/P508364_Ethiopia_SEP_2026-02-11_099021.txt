1. Introduction

1.1 Background

The Ethiopia Ministry of Irrigation and Lowlands (MILLs) in collaboration with other line ministries and
regional governments, is preparing the Climate Resilient Irrigation for Sustainable Production (CRISP)
Project (P506818). The Project is to improve access to resilient irrigation services for sustainable
production in Ethiopia, which should be achieved through the following components: Component 1:
Irrigation Rehabilitation and Modernization; Component 2: Small-Scale Irrigation Development;
Component 3: Irrigation Service Improvement; and Component 4: Project Management. The proposed
World Bank-financed Project will support the preparation and implementation of the National Irrigation
Plan (NIP), transitioning the sector from the project-based approach to a more comprehensive, program-
based model. It is expected that the NIP will be developed and launched within the first years of CRISP
implementation, and then further implemented and operationalized during the remaining
implementation period, ensuring that the NIP will serve as a practical, implementable program, rather
than a high-level strategy.

A new ministry, the Ministry of Irrigation and Lowlands (MILLs), was established in 2021 to lead irrigation
development in Ethiopia. Previously, the Ministry of Water, Irrigation and Energy (MoWIE) oversaw
irrigation development, while the Ministry of Agriculture (MoA) managed small-scale irrigation
development. MILLs is mandated to develop and implement irrigation policies, strategies, and laws;
design and implement irrigation projects; and to promote private sector participation. MILLs is responsible
for constructing, operating and managing federal-level large- and medium-scale irrigation schemes, as
well as providing technical support and capacity building to the regions for regional-level medium- and
small-scale irrigation project. The Ministry also owns and operates key irrigation dams. As a newly
established ministry, MILLs requires significant capacity building to effectively lead and manage irrigation
development in Ethiopia.

The World Bank’s Environmental and Social Standard (ESS) 10 recognizes the importance of open and
transparent engagement with all project stakeholders, based on the recognition that effective stakeholder
engagement can improve the Environmental and Social (E&S) sustainability of project activities, enhance
project acceptance and implementation, and allow stakeholders to contribute to project design. The key
objectives of stakeholder engagement include an assessment of the level of interest and support of the
project by stakeholders, to promote effective and inclusive engagement with all project-affected parties,
and to ensure that project information on E&S risks and impacts is disclosed in a timely and
understandable way.

This SEP has been prepared to anchor all CRISP Project stakeholder engagement in a systematic way. It
lays out legal and policy requirements in regard to stakeholder engagements, lists engagements that have
been undertaken already as part of Project preparations, provides a stakeholder analysis of all relevant
project-affected parties and lays out the means of dissemination of information to different parties as
well as means and ways to continue to consult different stakeholder groups throughout the project cycle.
Furthermore, it contains a monitoring plan to assess the implementation of SEP.

1.2 Project Development Objective and Project Components

Project Development Objective (PDO): To improve access to resilient irrigation in selected areas in
Ethiopia.

Component 1: Irrigation Rehabilitation and Modernization.

Component 1 will support the rehabilitation and modernization of existing federal-level large- and
medium-scale irrigation schemes1, as well as improving their Operational & Maintenance (O&M) and
performance. It will also support the development of the Investment Plan by financing the necessary
studies and designs for pipeline projects. In addition, Component 1 will support institutional strengthening
and capacity building for dam safety management and pilot water security and salinity management
initiatives. Component 1 will be implemented mainly by the federal government.

Subcomponent 1.1: Large- and Medium-Scale Irrigation Rehabilitation and Modernization: Three
schemes—Gode, Gidabo, and Ada’a Becho—have been selected as the first batch to be supported under
CRISP. These schemes have high potential and readiness. During project implementation, if the security
situation improves and resources permit, additional high-potential schemes from subsequent batches
may also be considered for support under CRISP. The CRISP will focus on improving the performance of
existing schemes, and will not construct new large- and medium-scale irrigation schemes at this stage.

Subcomponent 1.2: O&M and Agricultural Production for Large- and Medium-Scale Irrigation Schemes:
CRISP focuses on improving irrigation services and performance,. Ethiopia’s irrigation sector has long been
trapped in an unsustainable “build–neglect–rehabilitate” cycle which limits drought resilience and
increases flood vulnerability of neglected infrastructure. To address this, CRISP highlights the need to
integrate all irrigation infrastructure interventions with O&M and performance improvement activities.
CRISP also adopts a value chain approach to irrigation development, linking irrigation with improved
agricultural production and value chain development.

Subcomponent 1.3: Dam Safety and Water Security: CRISP will strengthen coordination between the
irrigation and water sectors to ensure water security for irrigation, and enhance the country’s capacity for
dam safety management and irrigation water resources management. This subcomponent will be jointly
implemented by MILLs and MoWE.

Component 2: Small-Scale Irrigation (SSI) Development.

Component 2 will support the rehabilitation and modernization of existing SSIs and the development of
new ones, as well as improving their O&M and performance. Regional and local governments are
responsible for the development of SSIs (<500 ha). CRISP will prioritize support to SSIs, given their strong
linkages to rural livelihoods, food security, and employment. Similarly, this component will also finance
the preparation of pipeline SSI projects under the Investment Plan. Component 2 will be implemented by
the regional governments across all 13 regions of Ethiopia.

Subcomponent 2.1: SSI Development: This subcomponent will fund the rehabilitation and modernization
of existing Small-Scale Irrigation (SSIs), development of new SSIs, small water storage, investment plan
preparation for SSIs.

Subcomponent 2.2: O&M and Agricultural Production for SSIs: CRISP will systematically integrate all SSIs
with O&M strengthening and agricultural production support to enhance sustainability and productivity.

Component 3: Irrigation Service Improvement.

Component 3 will strengthen the enabling environment for improving irrigation services, and promote
private sector engagement in both irrigation and agricultural sectors.

Subcomponent 3.1: Enabling Environment for Irrigation Service Improvement: CRISP will support the
formulation of policy and regulatory instruments to enable sustainable O&M and private sector
engagement. The activities will include the preparation of a National Irrigation O&M Directive, the
development of cost recovery mechanisms and tariff structures, the updating of the IWUA Proclamation,
the development of a private sector participation strategy for the irrigation sector, and the preparation
of strategies on sustainable water use for FLID and micro-irrigation. In addition, targeted technical
assistance will support a comprehensive assessment of irrigation-related PIR issues and identify gaps for
future policy and regulatory support.

Subcomponent 3.2: Private Sector Participation: CRISP will enhance private sector participation in both
irrigation and agriculture. It will pilot innovative models and create stronger market linkages to improve
sustainability, efficiency, and farmer incomes.

Component 4: Project Management and Capacity Building

Component 4 will provide institutional support to improve overall project management and coordination
activities.

Component 5: Contingency Emergency Response Component (CERC):

This component will enable the rapid reallocation of project funds to support an eligible response in the
event of a crisis or emergency.



1.3 Project Beneficiaries and Geographic Locations

CRISP is a national project implemented jointly by three ministries and covering all 13 regions of Ethiopia.
The Project will benefit a wide range of stakeholders:

    •   Direct Beneficiaries: The irrigation investments will span 13 regions and 155 woredas. A map of
        the project’s investment areas is provided in Annex 3. Approximately 319,651 people (including
        97,555 women and 124,007 youth in 44,818 households) will directly benefit from improved
        irrigation services and enhanced resilience to climate risks. Smallholder farmers, farmer
        cooperatives, IWUAs, and private enterprises will gain from strengthened O&M capacity,


                                                                                                          7
       improved water use, increased agricultural productivity, and enhanced access to markets,
       finance, and agribusiness opportunities.
   •   Indirect Beneficiaries: It is estimated that 266,000 people will benefit from improved food and
       nutrition security as a result of increased agricultural production in project-supported schemes.
       The Project is expected to generate jobs, directly related to irrigation construction, O&M, and
       agricultural production, and indirectly created through improved value chains, input supply,
       equipment services, logistics, and agro-processing.
   •   Institutional Strengthening: The Project will enhance the institutional capacity of MILLs, MoA,
       and MoWE, as well as regional irrigation agencies, to plan, manage, and deliver sustainable
       irrigation and O&M services. Capacity building, digital tools, financing solutions, and
       performance-based management approaches will also benefit extension services, local
       governments, research institutions, and private sectors.

The Project is prepared under the World Bank’s Environment and Social Framework (ESF), including ESS10
on Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure.



2. Objectives of the Stakeholder Engagement Plan
The main objective of this SEP is to define a program for stakeholder engagement, including public
information disclosure and consultation throughout the entire project cycle and to improve relationship,
communication and build trust between the Project and the stakeholders. The SEP seeks to ensure that
stakeholders are given sufficient opportunity to voice their opinions and concerns, and that these
concerns influence project decisions.

The specific objectives of the planned stakeholder engagement are:

   ➢ Ensures inclusive and transparent process of engagement modalities through effective
     communication, consultations and disclosure
   ➢ Provides the approach to stakeholder engagement, showing how this will be integrated
     throughout project implementation
   ➢ Provide stakeholders with opportunities to communicate their views, concerns, and raise
     comments throughout the project period.
   ➢ Build relationships through open dialogue, engagement thus maintaining a productive
     relationship between the Project team and stakeholders.
   ➢ Manage expectation by ensuring the Project does not create, or allow, unrealistic expectations
     to develop amongst stakeholders.
   ➢ Provide the opportunity for grievances to be handled in a fair, timely and transparent manner in
     line with ESS10 as well as allowing monitoring of effectiveness of the mechanism
   ➢ Provide the stakeholders with accessible and inclusive means to raise issues and grievances,

3. Stakeholder Identification and Analysis

3.1. Stakeholder Mapping and Analysis

Stakeholder engagement is an inclusive process that will be conducted throughout the project lifecycle.
Stakeholder engagement is most effective when initiated at an early stage of the project development
process and when it becomes an integral part of early project decisions and the assessment, management,
and monitoring of the project’s environmental and social risks and impacts. The process involves the
identification of key stakeholders and the influence they have on the proposed project, an assessment of
their interests and the way in which these interests are likely to affect the planning process, its riskiness
and viability.

The stakeholder mapping for the CRISP Project followed the definitions of ESS10, which recognizes two
broad categories of stakeholders: 1) those likely to be affected by the project because of actual impacts
or potential risks to their physical environment, health, security, cultural practices, well-being, or
livelihoods (project affected persons - PAPs), and 2) other interested parties. Directly affected Persons,
groups, and other entities within the Project area are those impacted or are likely to be impacted directly
or indirectly, positively, or adversely, by the project. Such stakeholders are directly influenced by the
project and/or have been identified as most susceptible to change associated with the project. The Project
will engage them closely in identifying risk and impacts and their significance, as well as in decision-making
on mitigation and management measures. The identified stakeholder groups of the CRIPS Project include
farmers, agro-pastoralists, pastoralists village heads, religious leaders, clan leaders, women, youth and
children, persons living with disabilities, female-headed households, Local Government Authorities
(Woreda/City and Kebele Administration and Kebele level structures - Keble Development Committees
(KDC)), and community-level organizations, such as: members of the Community Project Management
Committees (CPMC), Grievance Redress Committees (GRC), Water and Sanitation Committees, Irrigation
Water Users Association, Watershed Committee, etc). Furthermore, it includes federal, regional and
woreda implementing sectors, and development agencies.

Other interested parties include the environmental protection officers, Woreda Office of Justice and
regular court, Women and Youth Affairs Office, academia, media etc. The below Table identifies these
stakeholders with their key characteristics, description of their interests, degree of interest and their
expected contributions to the CRISP Project.

In addition to institutional and administrative stakeholders, the Project identifies PAPs disaggregated by
the type and nature of impact associated with specific project activities. These include individuals and
groups who may experience physical or economic displacement, temporary or permanent loss of land,
and loss of access to natural resources and ecosystem services, such as grazing areas, livestock corridors,
and watering points. While detailed identification and engagement of PAPs affected by land acquisition
will be undertaken through site-specific Resettlement Action Plans (RAPs) in accordance with ESS5, such
groups are recognized at the framework level as key stakeholders and will be engaged throughout
subproject preparation and implementation.

General categories of vulnerable groups include women, youth, female-headed households, children,

3.3 Sub-Saharan Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities

ESS7 on Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities
(IP/SSAHUTLC) aims to ensure that the development process fosters full respect for the human rights,
dignity, aspirations, identity, culture, and natural resource-based livelihoods of IP/SSAHUTLC. The ESS
applies to the CRISP Project, as Ethiopia has groups that can be considered under the definition of
SSHUTLCs in this ESS. As Project locations are not all known yet, it is not clear yet where and if this ESS
will apply. The definition of SSAHUTLCs are laid out in the below table.

SSAHUTLC are present in several CRISP intervention regions (e.g., Afar (Afar – pastoralist underserved
traditional community; Somali and Argobba agro-pastoralists or pastoralists), Somali (Somali pastroalists
and Tomal), Benishangul‑Gumuz (Berta, Gumuz, Shinasha, Mao, Komo), Gambella (Nuer, Angewak,
Majangir, Komo, Opo), and lowland Oromia (Borana Oromo, Guji Oromo, and other sub groups). The
Project will implement culturally appropriate engagement measures—local language and oral methods;
facilitators from customary institutions; women‑only consultations; mobile outreach adapted to pastoral
mobility—to ensure inclusive participation and informed decision‑making. Where subprojects may affect
customary land/natural resources, cause relocation, or significantly affect cultural heritage material to
identity, the project will carry out and document FPIC prior to project implementation. One of the main
objectives of ESS7 is to improve project design and promote local support by establishing and maintaining
an ongoing relationship based on meaningful consultation with the SSAHUTLC affected by a project
throughout the project’s life cycle; and to obtain the Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) of affected
SSAHUTLC, where applicable.

The Project will mandate E&S screening for each sub-project, which will include questions about the
presence of SSAHULC in the sub-project area. Where such groups are present, a Social Assessment has to
be prepared. On the basis of the Social Assessment, the Project will ensure the benefits of underserved
and vulnerable communities by appropriately informing and disseminating information by means of
conducting Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC).

3.4 FPIC Protocol


Applicability and triggers:

The Project will screen for ESS7 communities for FPIC triggers: (i) adverse impacts on land/natural
resources under customary use; (ii) relocation from such land/resources; (iii) significant impacts on
cultural heritage central to identity. Where triggered, FPIC is required before final decisions on those
project aspects.

Steps:
    • Early social assessment to identify customary land/resource use, cultural heritage, and
       differentiated impacts. During E&S screening and scoping, identify SSAHUTLC and determine if
       ESS7 FPIC conditions apply (adverse impacts on land and natural resources under customary use,
       relocation, or significant impacts on cultural heritage).
    • If identified, begin culturally appropriate, inclusive engagement early, allow time for community
       decision-making, and carry FPIC through impact assessment and design via good-faith
       negotiations, documenting a mutually accepted process and evidence of agreement (collective
       support), noting that unanimity is not required.
    • The respective E&S instrument should explicitly record the FPIC process and outcomes before
       decisions that commits to land acquisition, relocation, or affect critical cultural heritage.
    • Incorporate FPIC agreements into the design of the respective E&S instruments; and verify that
       FPIC and instrument commitments are fulfilled prior to any site access or civil works that trigger
       impacts.
    • Third-party facilitation/observation for credibility (e.g., local academia/CSO).
    • Disclosure of outcomes (respecting confidentiality requests).

The mutually accepted process should involve agreement on how to conduct consultations, who are the
representatives, how the decision-making of the community is carried out, and what the timing of the
consultation is.

FPIC concluded and documented before finalizing land decisions and any compensation for the affected
ESS7 community; conditions are integrated into the E&S instruments and contracts.
The project will establish and maintain a culturally appropriate GRM accessible to SSAHUTLC, allowing
oral submissions, local-language intake, confidential survivor-centered SEA/SH channels with referrals,
with no-cost.




4. STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN

4 .1 Stakeholder Engagement Activities During Project Preparation

As part of the preparatory and planning process, MILLS has been conducting consultations with various
stakeholders in line with the stakeholder engagement requirements. MILLS has conducted consultations
at the national level, in November 2025, which included staff of the Ministry of Agriculture, Ministry of



                                                                                                      25
Water and Energy, the Agricultural Transformation Institute, and the Ministry of Irrigation and Lowlands.
The participants welcomed the Project, especially as SSI structures are important to ensure improved
livelihood conditions for communities. It further conducted consultation in the Project regions. MILLS
further conducted regional consultation in each of the 13 regions that will be covered by the Project.
Consultations were conducted in November – December 2025. Participants included staff and members
of regional and local institutions and Ministries relevant for Project implementation. For detailed results
of the regional consultations, see Annex 3.

In the national consultations, stakeholders identified the following key challenges as part of their current
situation: Maintenance issues of irrigation systems lead to high costs; there is a general lack of community
engagement and consultation or upstream and downstream communities; there is an absence of modern
irrigation technologies; and there is weak water management, saving of water and project administration
and agronomic practices due to lack of detailed design. Communities are usually consulted around land
distribution activities, irrigation scheme site selection, conflict management and to a limited extend they
are engaged in participatory watershed management activities. The Project team responded that there
will be increased community engagement under the Project on a variety of issues.

The participants further identified environmental risks in relation to the project activities, including: risks
associated with quarry management; gaps between the design and implementation phases can lead to
several risks; poor Environmental Management Plans and insufficient monitoring of environmental risks;
and water logging and soil salinity due to poor irrigation water management practices. The Project team
has taken note of these environmental concerns and has integrated the respective mitigation and
monitoring measures as part of the ESMF.

The FPIU will continue to do regular consultations with beneficiaries and communities throughout
subproject implementation, following this SEP. Furthermore, all stakeholders participating in the
consultation will be informed about the GRM and where/how complaints can be made, and be provide
with regular updates on the project and specifically sub-projects. Throughout the project, consultation
summaries are prepared, including who was consulted, where and when, what concerns were expressed,
and how these concerns were addressed.

4.3 Proposed Strategy for Information Disclosure

Information disclosure to the above mapped stakeholders will rely on the following key methods: posting
of information on the Ministerial websites, radio broadcasting, community meetings in coordination with
local authorities, phone communication (SMS), and notices at the community/kebele level. Information
will be disclosed in Amharic or the respective local languages, where appropriate. Local authorities, such
as kebele administrators and other authorities will be requested to inform communities in community
meetings and through disclosure of information at project locations.

The Project will assume special measures to disclose information to the vulnerable groups listed above.
Information disclosure in these cases will be designed to ensure accessibility, inclusiveness and cultural
appropriateness. Information will be disclosed at community level using local languages and non-technical
formats, including oral briefings, community meetings, visual materials, and engagement through existing
local structures such as development agents, IWUAs, women’s groups and customary leaders. Disclosure
methods will adapt to the specific needs of specific groups, including targeted outreach to women and
girls, persons with disabilities, elderly people, pastoralists, ethnic minorities and people with low or no
formal education. Where relevant information will be shared through mobile or seasonal outreach
approaches to reach pastoral and mobile populations, and through household-level engagements for
individuals with mobility or time constraints. Particular attention will be given to ensuring that information
on land requirements, access to water resources potential impacts , mitigation measures and GRM are
communicated in a timely, understandable and culturally appropriate manner, enabling informed
participation throughout project preparation and implementation.

4.5 Proposed Strategy to Include Vulnerable Groups

The CRISP Project will carry out targeted consultations with the identified vulnerable and SSAHULTCs
groups to understand concerns/needs in terms of accessing information throughout the entire life of the
project cycle. These consultations will be based on direct community meetings to ensure persons with
disabilities, elderly people, youth, women and girls, ethnic minorities, pastoralists, people with low or no
formal education, female-headed households, migrants and refugees women, are participating effectively
and meaningfully in consultative processes and that their voices are not ignored. These meetings will be
conducted in local languages, where feasible, in the case of pastoralists, to accommodate their potential
patterns of movement. Meeting times will be selected to accommodate different needs of these
stakeholder groups; and for women child care needs will be accommodated.

In view of promoting gender equality, it is most important to engage women’s groups on an ongoing basis
throughout the lifetime of the project. Women voicing their concerns and contributing in the decision-
making process on issues such as community infrastructure should be encouraged, especially in various
forums that predominantly consist of men. The project will adopt a gender-responsive stakeholder
engagement strategy to ensure that women, including women from vulnerable households, are
meaningfully informed, consulted, and able to participate throughout project and subproject preparation
and implementation. Engagement approaches will include targeted outreach through women’s groups,
health and agricultural extension workers, and development agents; the use of local languages and non-
technical communication methods; and the organization of separate or women-only consultations where
culturally appropriate. Consultations will be scheduled at times and locations convenient for women, and
female facilitators will be engaged where feasible. Particular attention will be given to ensuring that
women receive information on project activities that may affect access to land, water, and livelihoods, as
well as on available grievance redress mechanisms, so that their perspectives are reflected in subproject
design, mitigation measures, and benefit-sharing opportunities.

Similarly, A feedback mechanism will be established through the GRM for stakeholders to provide
feedback and contribute to the ongoing development and improvement of the information disclosure
process. The GRM will be designed in such a way that it is accessible for the groups that are identified as
vulnerable to submit their grievances and receive feedback as prescribed. Especially vulnerable groups
will receive awareness training on how to access the GRM.


5. Responsibilities for Implementing Stakeholder Engagement
At the federal level, the MILLs will serve as the lead implementing agency for CRISP and will host the
Federal Project Implementation Unit (FPIU). The FPIU will be responsible for day-to-day coordination,
oversight of implementation, and consolidation of project reporting and monitoring and evaluation
(M&E). It will work closely with focal persons from the MoA and MoWE to implement activities within
their respective mandates. A Federal Steering Committee (FSC), to be established during project
preparation and maintained throughout implementation, will provide strategic guidance and ensure
effective multi-ministry and federal–regional coordination. The FSC will be chaired by the Minister of
MILLs, and the Committee will comprise ministers/state ministers from the MoF, MoA, and MoWE, along
with representatives from regional governments and development partners.




                                                                                                         36
At the regional level, CRISP will adopt a hybrid implementation approach, leverage the existing capacity
of Bank-financed projects while establishing dedicated structures where needed. Regional Project
Implementation Units (RPIUs) will be established under the regional Bureaus of Irrigation to manage
project activities, particularly under Component 2. Mirroring the federal structure, Regional Steering
Committees will be formed in each region to coordinate across the irrigation, agriculture, and water
sectors. The implementation structures of the MILLs-led Lowlands Livelihood Resilience Project II (LLRP-
II, P180076), and the MoA-led Food Systems Resilience Program for Eastern and Southern Africa (FSRP,
P178566), were assessed during project preparation. Both projects have proven experience in
implementing SSI investments and community-based interventions. CRISP will build on these institutional
arrangements to avoid duplication and maximize efficiency. CRISP will operate in all 13 regions, whereas
LLRP-II is active in 8 regions and FSRP in 11. In the larger regions, CRISP will establish dedicated RPIUs
under the Bureaus of Irrigation to oversee SSI and related activities. In four smaller regions with limited
activities (Benishangul-Gumuz, Gambella, Harari, and Dire Dawa), the Project will leverage existing RPIUs
under LLRP-II or FSRP to optimize resources, strengthen coordination, and progressively build the capacity
of these units. The RPIUs will include, or already include, Social Specialists, which will be mainly
responsible for the implementation of the SEP at the regional level.

The FPIU will include a Social and an Environmental Specialist. The Social Specialist will be mainly
responsible for the successful implementation of this SEP. The RPIUs also include Social Specialists, one
per region. At the regional level, these Social Specialists will be the main responsible staff for the
implementation of the SEP. The FPIU, MoWE, MoA and RPIUs staff implementing different sub-
components of the Project will gather all comments and inputs from community meetings, GRM
outcomes, and surveys and share them with the Environmental and Social Specialists in the FPIU and
RPIUs. They will collect participant sheets of meetings and take photographic evidence where appropriate.
In consultation meetings, the respective staff will prepare minutes of the meetings. The appropriate staff
will respond to comments and inputs from stakeholders, and the FPIU and RPIUs will ensure the
availability of a feedback line to the communities, as well as kebele and woreda level stakeholders. The
Social Specialists in the FPIU and RPIUs will review all inputs received during the consultation exercises
and will ensure that inputs are included into the Project modalities where applicable.



6. Grievance Redress Mechanism
Under the World Bank ESS10, Bank-supported projects are required to facilitate mechanisms that address
concerns and grievances that arise in connection with a project. One of the key objectives of ESS10 is ‘to
provide project-affected parties with accessible and inclusive means to raise issues and grievances and
allow borrowers to respond and manage such grievances’. This Project GRM should facilitate the project
to respond to concerns and grievances of the project-affected parties related to the environmental and
social performance of the project. The Project will provide mechanisms to receive and facilitate
resolutions to such concerns. This section lays out the grievance redressal mechanisms (GRM) for the
Project.

The goal of the GRM is to strengthen accountability to beneficiaries and to provide channels for project
stakeholders to provide feedback and/or express grievances related to project supported activities. By
increasing transparency and accountability, the GRM aims to reduce the risk of the project inadvertently
affecting citizens/beneficiaries and serves as an important feedback and learning mechanism that can
help improve the project impacts.



                                                                                                        37
The GRM will be operated in addition to a specific workers’ GRM, which is laid out in the Labor
Management Procedures (LMP).

The GRM is designed to ensure that grievances and perceived injustices are handled by the project, and
that the project helps mitigate general conflict stresses by channeling grievances that occur between
people, beenficiaires, social groups, communities, government actors, beneficiaries, project staff and
workers, contractors or primary suppliers, etc... Aggrieved parties need to be able to refer to institutions,
instruments, methods and processes by which a resolution to a grievance is sought and provided. The
GRM therefore provides an effective avenue for expressing concerns, providing redress, and allowing for
general feedback from community members.

The GRM aims to address project-related concerns in a timely and transparent manner and effectively.
Information on the GRM will be readily available to all project-affected parties. The GRM is designed in a
culturally appropriate way and is able to respond to all needs and concerns of project-affected parties.
The availability of the GRM does not prevent recourse to judicial and administrative resolution
mechanisms.

The project GRM provides for multiple channels through which complaints can be registered in a safe and
confidential manner can be enabled. The complaint should be related to the project activities and/or to
its implementation and management. Any complaint not directly related to the project will be referred to
the responsible government body.

This GRM sets out the steps to be taken to resolve grievances, the role of different staff members involved
and timeframes to reach a decision on grievances. The GRM management will be the FPIU’s and RPIU
responsibility and should ensure implementation with support from the MoWE, MoA, and RPIUs, as well
as woreda and kebele levels.


6.1 Principles of Grievance Redress Mechanism

The GRM is proposed to address any complaints and grievances including in regard to Sexual Exploitation
and Abuse (SEA) / Sexual Harassment (SH) arising during the course of implementing the project and its
sub-projects. Community members may perceive risks to themselves or their property or have concerns
about the environmental performance of the project. Any concerns or grievances should be addressed
quickly and transparently, and without retribution.

A primary principle is that all complaints and grievances are resolved as quickly as possible. The resolution
of complaints and grievances will therefore be addressed at the lowest possible level for resolution. All
minor land or property-related complaints that can be resolved, should be resolved immediately at the
sub-project level. The focus of the GRM is to resolve issues in an appropriate fashion at community level
and record details of the complaint, the complainant and the resolution. Moreover, the GRM is critical to
ensure inclusion, transparency and accountability of the sub-project financial disbursements. For this
reason, the CRISP Project will put in place information and communication campaigns aimed at deepening
public engagement and visibility of the activities and their funding. The general principles that guide the
GRM procedure are:




                                                                                                          38
Fairness: Its procedures should be widely perceived as fair, especially in terms of access to information
and opportunities for meaningful participation in the final decision. The GRM operates independently of
all interested parties in order to guarantee fair, objective, and impartial treatment to each case. GRM
officials have adequate means and powers to investigate grievances (e.g., interview witnesses, access
records).

Accessibility: It should be accessible to everybody who would like to submit a complaint and should
provide assistance to those who face barriers related to language, literacy, awareness, cost, or fear of
reprisal. Procedures to file grievances and seek action are simple enough that project beneficiaries can
easily understand them. Project beneficiaries have a range of contact options including, at a minimum, a
telephone number (preferably toll-free), an e-mail address, a project staff, and a postal address. The GRM
is accessible to all stakeholders, irrespective of the remoteness of the area they live in, the language they
speak, and their level of education or income. The GRM does not use complex processes that create
confusion or anxiety (such as only accepting grievances on official-looking standard forms or through
grievance boxes in government offices).

Predictability: It should offer a clear procedure with time frames for each stage and clarity on the types
of results it can and cannot deliver.

Compatibility: Its outcomes should be consistent with applicable national and international standards and
should not restrict access to other redress mechanisms.

Transparency: Its procedures and outcomes should be transparent enough to meet the public interest
concerns at stake.

Capability: It should have the necessary technical, human and financial resources to deal with the issues
at stake.

Gender responsive: It should ensure equitable benefits of women and men;

Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (SEA) and Sexual Harassment (SH) responsive: It should provide survivor-
centered responses to GBV cases/incidents.

Feedback: It should serve as a means to channel citizen feedback to improve project outcomes for the
people.

Responsiveness and efficiency: It is designed to be responsive to the needs of all complainants.
Accordingly, officials handling grievances are trained to take effective action upon, and respond quickly
to, grievances and suggestions.

Speed and proportionality: All grievances, simple or complex, are addressed and resolved as quickly as
possible. The action taken on the grievance or suggestion is swift, decisive, and constructive.

Participatory and social inclusion: A wide range of beneficiaries, community members, members of
vulnerable groups, project implementers, and other stakeholders are encouraged to bring grievances and
comments to the attention of project authorities. Special attention is given to ensure that vulnerable
groups, including those with special needs, are able to access the GRM.



                                                                                                          39
6.2 Grievance Handling Mechanisms

The Social Specialist at the FPIU will be assigned to oversee the GRM in general. The Social Specialist at
the FPIU and the Social Specialists at the MoWe, MOA and in the RPIUs follow up on complaints related
to the Project. The complaint, to be filed, should be related to the project components and/or to its
implementation and management. The grievance resolution process involves the following main steps:
      ▪ Receipt of grievances: anyone can submit a grievance (written, verbal, telephone, etc. as
          appropriate for the complainant).
      ▪ Registering the complaint: the focal point who receives the complaint will use the GRM
          logbook for registering.
      ▪ Referral and examination of complaints: a GRM Committee will be established at each sub-
          project site (comprising of members from the community, elders, a representative from
          Women and Children Affairs office, etc.) who will examine the complaint, resolve, or refer.
      ▪ Notifying the complainant: the decision/solution/action by the GRM Committee shall be
          communicated to the complainant as per the stipulated timeline.
      ▪ Closing the complaint: where the decision/solution of the complaint is accepted by the
          complainant, or complaint that is not related to the project or any of its components, or a
          complaint that is being heard by the judiciary will be closed following the appropriate
          procedure.

 All complaints received will be entered into an online database system managed by the FPIU that will
 allow complaints to be tracked and monitored, but that can guarantee confidentiality for aggrieved
 people. The system will include a database showing:

     ▪   No of complaints were received.
     ▪   No and % of complaints that have reached agreement.
     ▪   No and % of complaints that have been resolved.
     ▪   No and % of complaints that have gone to mediation
     ▪   No and % of complaints that have not reached agreement

The FPIU, MoWE, MoA or the RPIUs and other stakeholders have the responsibility of addressing
complaints related to the project on the basis of the GRM mechanisms (see the complaint handling chart
in the Table below).

The grievance redress mechanism will be free of charge, accessible without fear of retaliation, and
responsive to the needs of vulnerable groups, including historically underserved traditional local

6.3 Addressing SEA/SH Cases

Given the sensitive nature of SEA/SH complaints, the GRM provides different ways to submit grievances.
All grievance uptake channels can be used to report on SEA/SH-related grievances. No grievance uptake
mechanism can reject such grievances, and all personnel directly receiving grievances will be trained in
the handling and processing of SEA/SH-related grievances.



                                                                                                     45
The SEA/SH survivor has the freedom and right to report an incident to anyone: community member,
project staff, SEA/SH case manager, local authorities. All recipients of the report should – with the
survivor’s informed consent – report the case to one of the Project’s formal GRM. Furthermore, a survivor
can ask someone else to act as a survivor advocate and report on her/his behalf.

The grievance recipient will be responsible for recording and registration of the complaint. A GRM
operator cannot reject a SEA/SH complaint. At the same time, however, the project can only respond to
a SEA/SH complaint if it is directed into the designated GRM channels.

Confidentiality: All grievance recipients and anyone handling the SEA/SH-related grievances must
maintain absolute confidentiality in regard to the case. Maintaining confidentiality means not disclosing
any information at any time to any party without the informed consent of the person concerned. There
are exceptions under distinct circumstances, for example a) if the survivor is an adult who threatens his
or her own life or who is directly threatening the safety of others, in which case referrals to lifesaving
services should be sought; b) if the survivor is a child and there are concerns for the child’s health and
safety. The survivors need to be informed about these exceptions.

Informed Consent: The survivor can only give approval to the processing of a case when he or she has
been fully informed about all relevant facts. The survivor must fully understand the consequences of
actions when providing informed consent for a case to be taken up. Asking for consent means asking the
permission of the survivor to share information about him/her with others (for instance, with referral
services and/or FPIU/RPIU), and/or to undertake any action (for instance investigation of the case). Under
no circumstances should the survivor be pressured to consent to any conversation, assessment,
investigation or other intervention with which she does not feel comfortable. A survivor can also at any
time decide to stop consenting. If a survivor does not consent to sharing information, then only non-
identifying information can be released or reported on. In the case of children, informed consent is
normally requested from a parent or legal guardian of the children.

6.4 WB Grievance Redress Service (GRS)

World Bank Grievance Redress Service (GRS): Anyone is eligible to file a grievance directly with the World
Bank’s Grievance Redress System (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed
in order to address project-related concerns. Project affected communities and individuals may submit
their complaint to the WB’s independent Inspection Panel which determines whether harm occurred, or
could occur, as a result of WB non-compliance with its policies and procedures. Complaints may be
submitted at any time after concerns have been brought directly to the World Bank's attention, and Bank
Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For information on how to submit complaints to
the     World     Bank’s     corporate    Grievance      Redress     Service     (GRS),     please    visit
http://www.worldbank.org/en/projects-operations/products-and-services/grievance-redress-service.
For information on how to submit complaints to the World Bank Inspection Panel, please visit
www.inspectionpanel.org.
