                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings




                     The Irrigation Water Security, Resilience, and Value-Addition Project is characterized by its strong rural emphasis,
                     focusing on the northwestern governorates of Tunisia, primarily Jendouba regarding irrigation infrastructure
                     improvements, with interventions also planned in Béja, Bizerte, and Siliana aiming at water governance improvements,
                     feeder roads network rehabilitation and agricultural value chain integration. This region's landscape is dominated by
                     agriculture that is underpinning the local economy and shaping the the four governorates socio-economic profile.

                     Despite the agriculture sector’s promising potential , rural populations are facing entrenched social challenges, including
                     persistent poverty: 22.5% compared to the national rate 16.6%, high rates of unemployment, especially among young
                     people and women (Regional unemployment profiles point to the North West as highest (≈33 percent in 2021, with
                     national unemployment ≈16 percent, women ≈22 percent, youth ≈40 percent, source: WB Poverty & Equity Brief); Rural
                     and interior areas generally face worse outcome, and ongoing issues related to land tenure insecurity and unclear land
                     ownership among family members for medium and large farms, and small landholders in addition women facing limited
                     access to finance, agriculture support services and markets.

                     During the 2010–2011 Tunisian Revolution, Jendouba was a center of unrest driven by concerns over unemployment
                     and marginalization. These issues continue to shape perceptions today especially among youth. Targeted project




Public Disclosure
                     activities will be implemented to uplift marginalized populations and promote inclusive value-adding opportunities such
                     as food processing and agri-business development. The Irrigation Water Security, Resilience, and Value-Addition Project
                     represents a holistic approach to rural development in northwestern Tunisia. By combining irrigation infrastructure
                     modernization with water governance improvements and targeted agriculture value chains, the project seeks not only
                     to improve water security and agricultural productivity but also to promote equitable growth and ensure social inclusion
                     of those left behind.

                     From an environmental perspective, the project areas are located within transformed agricultural landscapes that
                     nonetheless include environmentally sensitive features. The only large irrigation perimeter targeted for infrastructure
                     rehabilitation is BouHertma (Jendouba), where interventions will take place within an existing irrigated scheme
                     comprising primary and secondary canals, drainage infrastructure, pumping stations, and buried pipelines. The
                     environmental setting of BouHertma includes riparian vegetation along drainage canals, erosion-prone soils,
                     hydromorphic depressions, and natural drainage outlets, which increase sensitivity to earthworks, sediment
                     mobilization, and water-quality disturbance during construction activities.

                     In Beja, Bizerte, and Siliana, the project does not intervene in irrigation infrastructure, as they are already rehabilitated.
                     Instead, it focuses on rehabilitation of feeder roads that provide access to agricultural production areas and connect
                     farmers to markets. These rural roads frequently cross small wadis, drainage ditches, steep slopes, and cultivated plains.
                     The environmental issues in these three governorates are mainly linked to (i) erosion and gullying along degraded rural
                     roads, (ii) poor drainage and runoff concentration, (iii) localized vegetation clearing, (iv) temporary obstruction of access
                     during works, (v) risks of sediment mobilization into nearby watercourses, and potential disturbance of forest edges and
                     olive groves.

                     Across all governorates, the project area exhibits a combination of alluvial soils, colluvial slopes, and forest patches,
                     producing varied sensitivities. While the project footprint is almost entirely within already transformed agricultural land,
                     some environmental receptors require attention: wadi crossings, fragile slopes, riparian corridors, and wooded areas.
                     No protected areas or critical habitats lie within the planned works, but these intersections with natural features will
                     require site-specific management of earthworks, drainage, borrow pits, and waste.
                     The environmental setting also reflects broader regional pressures: recurrent multi-year droughts, reduced dam inflows,
                     salinity risks in low-lying plots, and soil degradation from intensive cultivation. Infrastructure rehabilitation is therefore
                     expected to improve water efficiency and climate resilience, but construction activities must be carefully aligned with
                     local hydrological functioning, slope stability, and biodiversity interactions.

                     Overall, the project operates in a heavily agricultural yet environmentally sensitive context where the main challenges
                     relate to soil and water protection, erosion and drainage management, safe execution of civil works, and proper
                     oversight of contractors especially in the BouHertma irrigation perimeter, where interactions with natural drainage and
                     sensitive soils are most pronounced.

                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                   Substantial
                     The environmental risk rating is Substantial, due to the scale, geographic dispersion, and technical complexity of the
                     planned interventions. The project will modernize approximately 14,400 ha of irrigation infrastructure in BouHertma,
                     rehabilitate the main pipeline in Mateur, and upgrade feeder and service roads across Testour, Medjez El Bab (Béja),
                     Gaafour, and Laaroussa (Siliana). These interventions are distributed across four governorates and involve
                     simultaneous civil works in multiple environmentally sensitive rural settings. Civil works will involve excavation and
                     trenching, replacement of pipelines, rehabilitation of pumping stations, canal and reservoir improvements, and
                     feeder-road rehabilitation, including earthworks and drainage structures. These activities are sensitive to soil stability,
                     drainage patterns, and vegetation disturbance, particularly where works intersect wadis, erosion-prone slopes,
                     agricultural plains, and forest edges. Key construction-phase environmental risks include soil erosion, increased runoff
                     and sedimentation in drainage canals and wadis, temporary disruption of natural drainage, dust and noise generation,
                     and improper handling of excavated materials and construction waste. Additional risks relate to accidental spills of
                     fuels, oils, and concrete residues, particularly near watercourses. The potential presence of asbestos-cement pipes in
                     older irrigation networks represents a high-risk aspect requiring strict occupational health and safety measures and
                     precautionary management. Environmental sensitivity is heightened by the climate-stressed hydrological context of
                     the Medjerda basin, where recurrent droughts and reduced storage increase vulnerability to sedimentation, water
                     losses, and reduced system performance. While impacts are expected to be temporary and site-specific, risks are
                     amplified by the large geographic footprint, concurrent works across multiple sites, and known erosion and drainage
                     challenges. Occupational health and safety risks are significant due to the extensive use of heavy machinery, deep
                     excavations, electrical installations at pumping stations, confined spaces, and construction traffic along narrow rural
                     roads used by local communities. Component 3 introduces additional environmental risks related to the generation of

                     B.3 Other Salient Features

                       Use of Borrower Framework                                                                                         In Part
                       The project will adopt a hybrid approach regarding the use of the Borrower’s Environmental and Social (E&S)
                       Framework, opting for partial rather than full reliance to meet the requirements of the relevant Environmental and
                       Social Standards (ESSs).

                        While Tunisia’s national E&S framework is grounded in robust legislation, the comprehensive gap analysis conducted
                       during project preparation revealed several critical shortcomings. Notably, challenges persist in the effective
                       enforcement of existing laws, due to limited institutional capacity and inconsistent application across regions.
                       Furthermore, weaknesses in institutional coordination, particularly between national, regional, and sectoral bodies,
                       have resulted in fragmented oversight and reduced efficiency in addressing E&S risks. Additionally, the current system
                       lacks comprehensive and systematic monitoring mechanisms for E&S risks, which impedes timely identification and
                       mitigation of potential adverse impacts.

                       Given these findings, the project team determined that full reliance on the Borrower’s framework would not
                       adequately ensure compliance with the objectives and requirements of all relevant ESSs. Instead, partial reliance will



Public Disclosure
                       be considered for specific elements of ESS2, Labor and Working Conditions, where the national framework
                       demonstrates substantial alignment with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF). However, the
                       gap analysis indicated that enforcement of labor standards in rural and remote areas is often inconsistent, and
                       mechanisms for addressing labor grievances are not always accessible or effective for vulnerable groups. To address
                       these disparities, the project will supplement national systems with enhanced monitoring, transparent grievance
                       redress mechanisms, and regular audits to ensure compliance with ESS2 requirements.

                       For environmental standards (primarily ESS1, ESS3, and ESS6), full reliance on the national system is not feasible.
                       While Tunisia’s environmental legislation, including the EIA Decree, Water Code, Forest Code, and waste management
                       laws, provides a solid regulatory basis, implementation capacity remains limited at CRDA and local levels. Certain
                       components of ESS3, especially those related to solid and hazardous waste management and pollution control, show
                       partial alignment with national regulations and can be relied upon to some extent. However, national systems do not
                       address several core ESS3 requirements such as ESF-aligned environmental screening, cumulative impact assessment,
                       GIIP/EHS Guideline application, structured pollution monitoring, nor safe in-situ management protocols for asbestos-
                       cement pipes. Enforcement is uneven, and contractor oversight is weak. Therefore, only partially aligned aspects of
                       ESS3 will rely on national regulations, while the remaining ESS1/ESS3/ESS6 requirements will be fully addressed
                       through Bank instruments (ESMF, ESMPs, LMP, SEP, RAP/LRPs) and strengthened monitoring and supervision
                       procedures.

                       To bridge the identified gaps in enforcement, institutional coordination, and E&S risk monitoring, the project will
                       implement targeted capacity-building activities and institutional strengthening measures. Specific areas for
