 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

1.     This Project Appraisal Document (PAD) covers phase IV of the Accelerating Access to Water, Sanitation and
Hygiene Services in the Eastern and Southern Africa Region Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) (P512763), with
an overall IDA/IBRD financing envelope of US$1,580 million, approved by the World Bank’s Executive Directors on
September 30, 2025.

 A. Country Context

2.        The Union of the Comoros, a small island developing state located in the Indian Ocean between Madagascar
and the East African coast, faces severe structural challenges stemming from its small market size, geographic isolation,
institutional fragility, and high vulnerability to climate change. With an estimated population of about 870,000
inhabitants distributed across all islands, the country’s development trajectory is constrained by limited natural resources,
fiscal fragility, and frequent climate shocks. Nearly 90 percent of the population live in climate-vulnerable low-lying coastal
areas exposed to cyclones, flooding, erosion, and saline intrusion. Cyclone Kenneth of 2019 caused damages equivalent
to nearly 25 percent of Gross Domestic Product (GDP), revealing systemic weaknesses in resilience of infrastructure and
delivery of public services. Growth has been persistently subdued. Accordingly, the country has an opportunity to
strengthen its resilience and responsiveness to shocks by establishing pre-positioned funding mechanisms that will
enhance future crisis response efforts. Since independence in 1975, Comoros has averaged just 2.6 percent annual GDP
growth (1980–2023), with per capita income hovering at about US$1,530.1 Growth accelerated to 3 percent during 2020–
2023. Low economic growth has resulted in high poverty rates at 38.2 percent of the population in 2023 (using the lower
middle-income poverty rate threshold) (KMF 41,495 per capita per month). However, Comoros’s economic growth rose
to 3.4 percent in 2024, fueled by private consumption and the services sector, as tourism related activities grew by 4.5
percent and is expected to average 3.8 percent over 2025–2027.

3.      Climate change poses significant risk to the Comorian economy that depends heavily on remittances,
subsistence agriculture, and public expenditure, with limited fiscal space for capital investment. Comoros remains
exposed to high risk of coastal floods, extreme heat and cyclone hazards as well as rising temperatures and sea levels.
Coupled with cyclones and more extreme precipitation events, this will lead to increased sea level surges and a higher risk
of coastal inundation.2 Agriculture in Comoros is the largest employer and second largest sector and accounted for 31.6%
of GDP between 2011 and 20173. Hence, addressing climate vulnerabilities along with systemic service and governance
weaknesses in the water and sanitation sector is critical for strengthening national resilience, protecting public health,
and enabling sustainable development.

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
4.      Water insecurity continues to define the landscape throughout the territory of the Comoros. The islands
experience water insecurity differently with each island experiencing distinct yet interlinked challenges that are
increasingly exacerbated by climate change. In Grande Comores, the largest island with an estimated population of 379
367 inhabitants, the absence of permanent rivers makes communities almost entirely dependent on shallow aquifers and
rainwater harvesting. Frequent pump failures, saline intrusion driven by sea level rise, and limited storage capacity

translate into daily struggles for thousands of families. Despite having the nation’s richest network of rivers and streams,
estimated at 47000 m3/day4, Anjouan, the second largest island with a population of 327 382 inhabitants, suffers from
dilapidated network and inadequate and poor water supply to its inhabitants. Moheli, the smallest island with a population
of 51 567 inhabitants5, derives its main sources of drinking water from small river catchments and springs located in upland
zones. Decades of limited maintenance and deforestation in catchment areas have severely reduced the reliability of these
systems while rising temperatures and increasingly erratic precipitation patterns threaten water quality and supply. Across
all islands, power outages from mostly diesel-fuel small thermal generators in addition to some hydropower generation
in Anjouan, limited maintenance capacity, and uncoordinated institutional responsibilities compound these problems.6

5.       While the physical and hydrogeological characteristics of the islands differ, the underlying patterns are
consistent. Dwindling water sources, unreliable access, undersized water distribution systems, and inadequate
management structures leave households, especially women and children, bearing the brunt of water insecurity that is
increasingly exacerbated by climate change. This insecurity directly affects livelihoods and social stability, with women
spending hours each day in the search for water, sacrificing time for education, childcare, and income-generating
activities. Farmers report crop losses during dry seasons due to inadequate irrigation, while households spend significant
resources on water trucking, delivered by privately-owned water trucks.

6.       While an estimated 80 percent of households have access to basic drinking water, service reliability and quality
remain low. In urban centers, intermittent supply and high non-revenue water (NRW) constrain service continuity. In rural
areas, many community water systems are non-functional due to poor operation and maintenance. Sanitation access
remains limited—only 36 percent of households have access to basic sanitation, and the country lacks any wastewater or
fecal sludge treatment facility, with reports of raw sewage being directly discharged to local streams and to the ocean.
These deficiencies contribute to high rates of waterborne diseases, especially affecting women and children. Climate
change is expected to exacerbate Operations and Maintenance (O&M) as well as public health challenges by increasing
rainfall variability, saltwater intrusion, and storm intensity.

7.      In rural areas, water supply schemes currently operate through a mix of community-based management and
limited utility involvement, with municipalities nominally owning assets and community committees handling day-to-day
operations and fee collection. Capital investments have largely been financed by donor projects and community/diaspora
contributions, whilst O&M is funded through locally set tariffs - typically per-container charges in community-run systems
that are insufficient for full cost recovery, resulting in chronic underfunding and uneven service quality.

8.       Recent Water Security Diagnostics undertaken for Comoros found that, while women remain the primary
managers of household water, bearing the greatest burden of scarcity, their representation in formal water governance
remains extremely limited, with fewer than 15 percent of local committee members being women. The diagnostics
further found that, in several communities, women have begun organizing water-user groups to advocate for repairs and
equitable distribution.7 Strengthening gender inclusion in governance and identifying targeted measures to enhance
women’s participation and leadership in decision-making is therefore a critical component of water security reform.

9.      The national water utility, (Société Nationale d'Exploitation et de Distribution des Eaux, (SONEDE8), established
in 2018 with the mandate of managing the state’s water resources, employs over 500 staff across the national territory.
As a relatively young utility operating in a fragmented governance environment, SONEDE faces ongoing institutional
capacity challenges related to internal governance, professional development systems, and technical expertise required
to sustainably deliver climate-resilient services. Within this context, gender imbalances are pronounced: although women
make up 26 percent of the workforce, they are largely concentrated in customer service and human resources, with very
limited representation in technical and managerial functions9. This structural underrepresentation underscores the
importance of strengthening institutional systems to support inclusive, merit-based professional advancement and sector
sustainability.

10.     Support of the World Bank for the water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) sector in Comoros is critical in
promoting provision of climate resilient WASH services in the country. The Comoros National Water Resilience Project
(CNWRP, P504691, US$20 million, approved in June 2024) is financing climate-resilient infrastructure for the production,
storage and distribution of water in the capital city. The project provides significant technical assistance to the national
water utility, as well as the consolidation of the currently fragmented institutional mandate for WASH into one entity, the
Direction Générale pour l'Eau, l'Assainissement et l'Hygiène (General Directorate for Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene
(DGEAH)).

11.      While the Water Code of 2021 establishes the legal foundation for an Integrated Water Resources Management
(IWRM) framework, implementation remains partial, with overlapping responsibilities, fragmented authority, and
weak enforcement mechanisms. As a result, water governance operates through a mosaic of systems, national utility
services, municipal oversight, and community committees, each functioning largely in isolation. This fragmented water
governance creates gaps in operations, uneven coverage, undermines equity and accountability, and weakens system
resilience to shocks and climate variability.

12.     The expected establishment of the DGEAH within the Ministère de l'Energie, de l'Eau et des Hydrocarbures
(Ministry of Energy, Water and Hydrocarbons (MEEH)), as the national entity responsible for policy formulation,
coordination, and planning, will for the first time provide a dedicated government counterpart for sector leadership.
Though a decree for its establishment has been prepared, its approval and ratification remain delayed. Enhanced
momentum and traction under the newly appointed sector leadership is expected to hasten the process. SONEDE, on the
other hand, will benefit from a dedicated performance improvement program under the CNWRP, aimed at enhancing
operational efficiency, reducing NRW, modernizing billing and collection systems, and improving financial transparency.
The program includes capacity building in asset management, preventive maintenance, and customer relations, laying the
groundwork for sustained improvements in institutional performance.

13.     In parallel, a set of strategic studies and technical analyses, financed under the sister project, the Regional
Climate Resilience Project (RCRP – P180171) have been launched. These include detailed engineering studies for
alternative water sources for the capital city, Moroni, additional water storage capacity, replacement of dilapidated
distribution infrastructure, and solarization of pumping stations. These studies will provide a robust analytical base for
investments and policy decisions to strengthen climate resilience while also improving mitigation outcomes.

rehabilitate and expand production, storage, and distribution systems of the capital city, Moroni. These are aimed at

fecal sludge treatment plant to prepare for a sanitation investment in the future.

15.       While these efforts represent important progress, significant challenges remain. The regulatory framework for
the water sector in Comoros has historically been characterized by fragmented institutional arrangements and limited
oversight capacity. Water supply and sanitation access and service provision remain unequal and unregulated throughout
the islands. In rural areas, many water schemes suffer from recurrent breakdowns due to the absence of professionalized
management and insufficient funding for maintenance. Tariff setting and performance monitoring of the national water
utility, SONEDE, have been managed directly by ministerial decree, with limited mechanisms for independent oversight or
consumer protection. This institutional gap has contributed to persistent challenges in service delivery, including
widespread dissatisfaction among users regarding water quality, reliability, and affordability.

16.     Recognizing these challenges, the government in June 2025 signed a decree establishing a new regulatory
agency—Agence de Regulation des Secteurs de l’Energie et de l’Eau (ARSEE)—an independent administrative authority
with a cross-sectoral mandate covering energy, water, and sanitation. ARSEE is tasked with strengthening sector
governance by developing and enforcing a coherent and transparent regulatory framework—spanning licensing and
authorizations, tariff setting and review, performance and quality of service standards, and consumer protection—while
coordinating with relevant ministries and utilities to ensure consistent application of sector laws. This nascent agency has
the potential of bringing about a significant improvement in sector governance, including a more coherent and transparent
framework for regulation, tariff setting, and quality assurance. The agency faces considerable capacity constraints and will
require concerted support to effectively fulfill its oversight functions, foster accountability, and ensure that sector reforms
translate into tangible improvements for consumers.

 iii.    Key Results of the Program

26.      The Program Development Objective (PrDO) is to increase access to climate-resilient drinking water supply,
sanitation and hygiene services in participating countries in the Eastern and Southern Africa (AFE) Region. The project
directly contributes to the PrDO of the MPA. The project is fully aligned with the four pillars under the WASH AFE MPA:
the Program Pillar 1- National Platforms to Accelerate Access to WASH, maps with Project Component 1; the Program
Pillar 2—Addressing Sector Governance aligns with Project Component 2; Program Pillar 3—Service Provider Performance
Improvement and Pillar 4—Leveraging Finance to Increase WASH Access directly maps to Project Components 3 and 4.

27.     The Project Development Objective (PDO) is to increase access to climate-resilient drinking water supply,
sanitation and hygiene services in Comoros, and strengthen sector governance.

28.     Progress towards achievement of the PrDO will be measured through three PrDO-indicators, that are also PDO-
indicators at the project level. A fourth project specific indicator has been added to measure progress towards improved
water service delivery.

 •      PrDO/PDO Indicator 1: Number of people with access to water supply services. Disaggregated by gender, youth,
        and sub sector (percentage basic and safely managed).
 •      PrDO/PDO Indicator 2: Number of people with access to sanitation services. Disaggregated by gender, youth, and
        sub sector (rural-urban) and level of service (percentage basic and safely managed).
 •      PrDO/PDO Indicator 3: Number of beneficiaries with enhanced resilience to climate risks, disaggregated by gender,
        youth, of which people benefiting from climate-resilient infrastructure.
 •      PDO Indicator 4: Strengthened sector governance measured through operationalization of the new water regulator
        and preparation, adoption and publication of new tariff strategy

29.     The Program Framework delivers a systematic and climate change informed approach to WSS access expansion
in selected countries in the AFE region. The regional and country-level WASH Platforms will assist each country to
implement their national roadmaps towards achieving universal access. Countries will join the MPA based on expressed
interest and readiness.

 II. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

 A. Project Description

 30.     Building on the foundations created under the CNWRP, the new operation will expand its geographical scope
 beyond the capital city of Moroni to reach additional communities across the country (mapped to Pillar 4 of the MPA,
 Financing Climate-Resilient WSS Infrastructure). This expansion aims at addressing persistent challenges by providing
 targeted support for resilient water and sanitation services in both urban and rural settings. In light of ongoing
 challenges related to the reliability and adequacy of water supply for critical public infrastructure, the project will
 include targeted investments to strengthen water supply systems serving priority facilities, including the new El-
 Maarouf National Hospital, with the objective of ensuring continuous, safe, and climate-resilient water services. Outside
 of the capital city, the project will prioritize investments in climate resilient community-managed rural water supply
 schemes. It will also support capacity building for rural water operators and village water committees, equipping them
 with the technical, managerial, and financial skills needed to sustain and improve service delivery.

 31.      WASH sector governance fragmentation remains a hindrance to sector progress. Building on progress initiated
 under CNWRP, the Project will support the establishment of a government-led National WASH Platform11 that builds
 upon the institutional foundations laid under the Comoros National Water Resilience Project and adapt lessons from
 Ethiopia’s One WASH approach12. The platform will formalize coordination among line ministries with some level of
 responsibility for WASH (water, health, education, and finance ministries) through a National WASH Steering Committee
 and a National WASH Technical Team to align policy, planning, and implementation. Establishment activities will
 include: (i) constituting committees at the national level with approved TORs; (ii) preparing a National WASH Investment
 Plan (covering rural, urban, and institutional WASH) and citywide inclusive sanitation plans to guide prioritized, climate-
 resilient investments and (iii) instituting a joint sector review calendar and a partner coordination forum to align
 technical assistance and implementation support. The activity will systematize and scale governance progress initiated
 under CNWRP by anchoring the platform under DGEAH and using the Steering and Technical Committees created under
 CNWRP as the basis for the platform’s governance structure. It will further build on sector strategies and policies, and
 the Grand Moroni master plan to be developed under CNWRP, to expand into a national investment plan and citywide
 inclusive sanitation planning.

 32.      The project will support the operationalization of the new water regulator. The newly formed Agence de
 Regulation des Secteurs de l’Energie et de l’Eau is a laudable step towards governance reforms and more accountable,
 transparent and effective oversight of utilities, particularly in setting and approving tariffs and establishing service
 standards, which are currently managed through ad hoc ministerial orders. The project will support the
 operationalization and capacity building for the agency, by financing essential start-up activities that directly address
 stakeholder grievances and governance gaps identified in the Light Water Security Diagnostics. Beyond the founding
 legal instrument, the nascent agency will be in need of a governing body as well as minimum operating budget. Activities
 will include: (i) tariff-setting strategy with methodologies balancing cost recovery and affordability; (ii) service quality
 standards (continuity, pressure, water quality); (iii) a public-facing consumer feedback and grievance redress
 mechanism; and (iv) performance reporting and enforcement protocols, including compliance monitoring against
 standards; (v) the development and promulgation of regulatory instruments. Further, the Agency will benefit from
 exposure to well-functioning regulators in the region under the regional knowledge pillar of the MPA.

 33.      The Project includes Performance-Based Conditions (PBCs) to incentivize improvements in the governance
 framework, to encourage a result-based culture, and emphasize sustainability. PBCs will be introduced with
 disbursements to be made upon their achievement and against eligible expenditures to ensure attainment of the
 institutional milestones that underpin the Project's governance and sustainability objectives. Specifically, the PBCs will
 target (i) Operationalization of ARSEE and the development, adoption and publication of a Tariff Strategy and (ii)
 establishment and operationalization of DGEAH. Verification will be based on documentation submitted by the
 Recipient in accordance with the Verification Protocol (Annex 1).

 Provision Performance). To help address persistent skills gaps in the water and sanitation (WASH) sector, the project
 will design, develop, and implement competency-based vocational courses for plumbers, masons, electricians,
 technicians, and local councils, with a strong focus on constructing and managing climate-resilient infrastructure.

 institutional capacity development for key stakeholders on policy implementation, climate risk management, gender,
 and inclusion in WASH. The project will also facilitate South-South knowledge exchange, enabling participating countries

45.      The project benefits four principal groups. These are (a) residents that benefit from investments in water

 D. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners

46.      This is the second water operation in Comoros financed by the World Bank in the last two years. The World
Bank is uniquely positioned to leverage the necessary technical and institutional reform expertise to support
improvements in WSS in Comoros, and to bring together other development partners supporting the sector to align
financing. The project will build on the achievements of the ongoing project and continue to strengthen the utility’s and
government’s institutional capacity to manage WSS investments and service delivery. In addition to solid experience with
water sector operations, the World Bank has an extended engagement in countries across the region and across sectors
(including Disaster Risk Management, Social Protection, Environment) and is well placed to incorporate the lessons and
experience from past engagements in the design of the project.

47.      Several development partners are already engaged and investing in the water and sanitation sector in Comoros,
principally outside of Grand Moroni. The French Development Agency (Agence française de développement, AFD) is
currently the most active donor in the water sector in Comoros, supporting three projects, focusing on water supply
studies on Moheli and Grande Comore; and a governance-focused project that is financing technical assistance and
capacity building of the MEEH. The United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), in a project mapped to the Ministry
of Agriculture, Fishing, Environment, Tourism and Artisans, is also investing in small town and rural water supply across all
islands. The MPA will leverage analytical work done under the AFD and UNDP projects, particularly around institutional
strengthening, to operationalize the new structures being created. The Saudi Fund for Development is rehabilitating and
re-equipping the three water sources and two of the three existing reservoirs in Moroni. For sanitation, the United Nations
Children’s Fund (UNICEF) is active and is spearheading support for ending open defecation through Community-Led Total
Sanitation approaches that have had varying degrees of success across the country. UNICEF is also in the process of
finalizing a WASH bottleneck analysis (WASHBat), which is expected to highlight critical institutional actions that are
needed to support the sector.

 E. Lessons Learned and Progress on Learning Agenda

49.     Project implementation will draw upon experience from other sectors in Comoros and from water supply
projects across Africa and Asia, including in other fragile contexts. The project builds upon and complements other
regional and national programs financed both by the World Bank and other development partners supporting investments
in the Comoros. Specifically, the project will be informed by diagnostics and technical and environmental studies on water
supply and the Comoros National Water Resilience Project (P504691).

50.     The following key lessons informed the project design:

        o   Building the institutional capacity of service providers and strengthening sector coordination are critical for
            the sustainable delivery and scaling of WASH services. The design draws on Ethiopia’s One WASH National
            Program - a government-led, sector-wide approach that aligns water, health, education, and finance actors
            under one plan, one budget, and one reporting system, supported by a pooled financing mechanism.
            Experience from Ethiopia shows that embedding targeted technical assistance linked to clear performance
            metrics, while anchoring it in a national coordination platform and joint planning cycle, accelerates on-the-job
            learning, improves operational and financial performance of utilities and service units, and enhances
            accountability for results.

        o   Prioritizing and sequencing interventions are key to addressing the short and medium-term priorities of the
            water sector, while working towards long-term goals. The project will address sector priorities related to
            critical water supply infrastructure needs as well as capacity building the institutional foundations to enhance
            and sustain service delivery across the islands in Comoros. Strengthening the sector’s institutional capacity,
            including the skills and the efficiency of SONEDE and its coordination with the Ministry of Energy, Water and
            Hydrocarbons, will be key to addressing the sector’s short-term priorities, while building stronger governance
            towards the achievement of long-term goals. Prioritizing and sequencing interventions maximizes efficiency
            and development impacts.

        o   Reinforcing coordination and synergies across sectors is essential to optimize investment impact. The
            project will seek collaboration with the World Bank’s Education team on technical and vocational training
            support, as well as building on the Urban team’s institutional and policy assessment for Solid Waste
            Management. The need for collaboration in the sector has also been highlighted by interactions with all major
            WSS donors active in Comoros. The project will ensure continued collaboration across sectors and across the
            portfolio in Comoros. The project PIU will continue to collaborate closely with institutions involved, including
            urban development and disaster risk management. The project will further support the strengthening of the
            government’s role in coordinating the WASH sector.

56.     To sustainably address the nation’s water supply challenges across Grande Comore, Anjouan, and Mohéli, the

to ensure reliable, climate-resilient services. Consistent with global good practice, the approach integrates institutional,
regulatory, and technical dimensions to break the cycle of weak management and unsustainable investments.

57.     Building on preparation advanced under ongoing projects and programs, the project will carry forward and

to expressed needs.

D. Environmental and Social

82.       Environmental risk is rated as "Moderate." Overall, the project will bring positive development impacts with
respect to people’s access to water improvement. Potential environmental risks and impacts are expected under
Components 4, they are linked to the rehabilitation and the construction of water supply infrastructure for Grande
Comores, Moheli, and Anjouan. Key anticipated direct, indirect, and cumulative risks and impacts associated with project
activities will entail disruption of traffic in the project area due to trench excavation work to install drinking water
distribution pipes, OHS risks for workers, community health and safety risks for communities around works sites, waste
generation including reservoir sludge, risks related to chemical water treatment, risks of pollution, disturbance of aquatic
habitats at water intake sites, inadequate wastewater management that can promote the proliferation of insects, risk of
waterborne diseases if water distributed to communities is not well treated and stored appropriately in reservoirs, road
safety concerns due to construction activities, waste generation including reservoir sludge, risks related to chemical water
treatment, risks of pollution, inadequate wastewater management that can promote the proliferation of insects, risk of
waterborne diseases if water distributed to communities is not well treated and stored appropriately in reservoirs. Risks
to biodiversity and habitats are expected to be limited. The social risk rating has been classified as moderate, and the
initial Sexual and Exploitation and Abuse and Sexual Harassmen SEA/SH risk screening as moderate. Project activities will
have positive social impacts by improving people’s access to water. Potential adverse risks and impacts on the
communities and population are likely to be temporary, site-specific, reversible, and could be mitigated. The expected
social risks and impacts of the Project are mainly related to community health and safety risks associated with the
expected civil works, including traffic accidents and other road safety risks, and the transmission of diseases such as STDs;
labor risks, particularly as they relate to the potential discrimination of workers, especially of migrant workers, as well as
the risk of child labor and unfair working conditions; risks associated with the intensification of SEA/SH, particularly
considering that some interventions may require the engagement of foreign companies / workers with specializations not
available at the local labor market; potential land acquisition and access restrictions, which could lead to economic
displacement and disruption to livelihoods, with probably physical displacement even considered limited; and risk of elite
capture of project benefits. Activities under components 1, 2 and 3 are not expected to lead to significant adverse risks
and impacts. The risk rating takes into account the capacity of stakeholders involved in project, especially the Ministry
responsible for the water sector, SONEDE and Comorian Environmental authorities to manage potential environmental
and social risks and impacts and to implement ESF. The project will be managed with existing PIU who already have
Environmental specialist, and Social specialist to manage E&S risk management of the project. The World Bank team will
provide training on the standards of the World Bank’s new Environment and Social Framework during implementation to
strengthen the Borrowers capacity to develop and implement environmental and social (E&S) measures. The Borrower
has prepared appropriate assessments to manage the environmental and social risks. The Project’s Stakeholder
Engagement Plan (SEP) was disclosed in-country on February 3, 2026 and on the World Bank’s website on March 6, 2026,
while the Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP) and the Labour Management Procedures (LMP) were
disclosed in-country and on the World Bank’s website on March 13, 2026. The outcome of the E&S assessments will be
considered in the design and implementation of project activities, and no activities or bidding processes will commence
before the appropriate assessments and mitigation measures are in place. The project will use the existing Labor
Management Procedures and Resettlement Framework developed under the CNWRP. As the nature of activities are
similar it is proposed that the project will update and use also the existing SEAH action plan developed under the CNWRP.

84.      Gender. Institutional patterns within SONEDE show limited female representation in technical and higher-level
positions, indicating constraints in upward mobility. There is equally limited training and mobility pipeline for women,
with the absence of core gender-supportive policies and mentorship programs constraining retention and advancement.30
Promotion data show a persistent imbalance: women accounted for only 10 of 32 promotions in 2024 and 8 of 27
promotions in 2025 (approximately 30 percent). National data on women’s representation in leadership positions in
Comoros are scarce. Regional comparators indicate low female representation, with women accounting for about 29
percent of senior and middle management positions in Madagascar and 27 percent in Tanzania. In Comoros, the only
available proxy – women holding 16.7 percent of parliamentary seats – likewise suggests structural constraints in women’s
access to leadership, reinforcing the need to address advancement barriers within SONEDE. Institutional data from
SONEDE indicate that this imbalance is driven by: (a) absence of structured and targeted training pathways for women;
(b) lack of formally documented, merit-based promotion procedures; and (c) advancement and length of service are tied
to professional cadre status, which requires formal qualifications and certifications that women have had limited access
to. To address these drivers, the project will support: (a) formal documentation and publication of transparent, merit-
based promotion criteria; (b) establishment of a structured review mechanism to ensure promotion decisions are
consistent with documented criteria and principles of equity; (c) annual sex-disaggregated tracking and reporting of
promotion outcomes; (d) review and clarification of the skills, qualifications, and certification requirements for
advancement and cadre mobility; (e) dedicated access for qualified female staff to certification and training required for
higher-grade positions and cadre progression; and (f) development and adoption of a SONEDE Gender Strategy that
institutionalizes equitable career progression and monitoring mechanisms.

85.     Citizen engagement. The project will build on existing mechanisms to strengthen two-way communication
between SONEDE and service users, with the objective of improving service delivery and institutional accountability. Rural
water committees will be formalized and strengthened to act as community liaison platforms, facilitate feedback on
service performance, and support referral of grievances through established channels. Women’s meaningful participation
and leadership in these committees will be promoted to ensure inclusive local governance. At the utility level, complaint
management systems will be strengthened through improved tracking, standard operating procedures, and quarterly
monitoring of performance against the 72-hour resolution target.

86.      Climate Co-benefits. The project is rooted in the need to address climate-related risks through a multiscale
approach, by integrating the need to adapt to increasing climate change impacts across the project’s components and
subcomponents. The project integrates activities that will contribute to Comoros’ adaptation to various climate change
vulnerabilities (particularly floods, drought, and sea-level rise), and thus climate co-benefits will result from, among others:
(i) drought and flood resilience measures through water source protection and increased storage solutions to help cope
with more frequent and intense climate extremes; and (ii) strengthened institutional level capacity to plan and respond
to increasing climate shocks.

87.     Grievance Redress. Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by a project
supported by the World Bank may submit complaints to existing project-level grievance mechanisms or the Bank’s
Grievance Redress Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed to address project-
related concerns. Project affected communities and individuals may submit their complaint to the Bank’s independent
Accountability Mechanism (AM). The AM houses the Inspection Panel, which determines whether harm occurred, or could
occur, as a result of Bank non-compliance with its policies and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution Service, which
provides communities and borrowers with the opportunity to address complaints through dispute resolution. Complaints
may be submitted to the AM at any time after concerns have been brought directly to the attention of Bank Management
and after Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For information on how to submit complaints to the
Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS), visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to submit
complaints to the Bank’s Accountability Mechanism, visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.

 VI. KEY RISKS

88.      The overall risk of the proposed project is Substantial. The most significant risks to the project are linked to the
institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability of project investments, Political and Governance and
Stakeholder risks due to the operating context, data protection under unclear governance, and fiduciary aspects. These
risks are expected to decline with mitigation measures currently ongoing under CNWRP.

89.      Institutional capacity for Implementation and sustainability risk is Substantial. Given the still fragmented nature
of the WASH sector pending the creation and operationalization of DGEAH; a new regulatory agency that is yet to be fully
functional and the ongoing financial and operational challenges faced by SONEDE, there are substantial risks for both
implementation and sustainability of investments. In the long term, however, technical assistance to the various
institutions will strengthen the long-term sustainability of the associated project investments for improved service
delivery. These measures are expected to strengthen institutional performance and improve service delivery.

90.      Political and Governance risk is rated Substantial, reflecting the country's fragile, small island state context, which
is characterized by fragmented sector governance, weak institutional capacity, limited private sector presence, and
potential political resistance to tariff and regulatory reform. To mitigate these risks, Performance-Based Conditions (PBCs)
will incentivize critical institutional milestones — including the operationalization of the national water directorate and
the adoption of a national tariff strategy — while dedicated support to the regulatory agency (ARSEE) will strengthen
sector oversight.

91.      Stakeholder risk is rated Substantial, driven by community-level risks that include elite capture in the
management of rural water systems and the underrepresentation of women in formal water governance roles. To address
these risks, multi-level grievance redress mechanisms and structured annual community engagement sessions will ensure
inclusive participation and accessible recourse, with targeted measures to promote women's leadership in water
governance throughout project implementation.

92.     Fiduciary risk is Substantial. Potential risks relate to weak FM capacity, lack of transparency and accountability in
the use of public funds and low performance monitoring system. The project will use the existing PIU under the CNWRP.
Though the PIU is already established, training on implementation of World Bank-financed projects remains ongoing.
