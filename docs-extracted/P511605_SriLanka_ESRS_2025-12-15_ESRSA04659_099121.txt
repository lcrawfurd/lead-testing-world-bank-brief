                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     Sri Lanka faces significant spatial disparities that hinder equitable and balanced growth opportunities, particularly in the
                     Northern and Eastern provinces. These provinces struggle with low GDP contributions (only 4.1% and 5.1%,
                     respectively) and high poverty rates (23.8% and 18.6%) compared to the national average of 14.3%. Unemployment
                     rates in districts like Jaffna, Kilinochchi, Ampara, and Batticaloa exceed 7%, well above the national average of 4.7%.
                     Despite the strong potential of key productive sectors such as tourism, fisheries, and agriculture, resources and assets
                     remain largely underutilized. This is compounded by critical gaps in enabling infrastructure—including weak
                     connectivity, inadequate water supply, poor solid waste management, and limited productive facilities—as well as the
                     absence of comprehensive regional development strategies and insufficient support for priority economic value chains.

                     To address these challenges, the proposed operation adopts a programmatic and phased approach, with an emphasis
                     on spatially targeted investments in selected clusters. Project interventions comprise a set of inter-linked components
                     and activities, aimed at: (i) improving regional economic planning, while building institutional capacity for sustained
                     development; (ii) financing catalytic public infrastructure investments with a view to attract private investments in the
                     selected clusters; and (iii) supporting local enterprises in the selected clusters through business development and skills
                     enhancement to strengthen the value chains and stimulate private sector. The project comprises two main
                     components: Component 1 will establish the strategic foundation for long-term regional development by financing
                     technical assistance, consultancy services, goods, and equipment to strengthen regional planning capacity and guide
                     long-term economic development in the Northern and Eastern Provinces. The component will enhance the institutional
                     capacity of national and provincial entities to manage integrated, climate-resilient, and private-sector-responsive
                     development. Component 2 will finance civil works, consultancy services, feasibility studies, detailed designs,
                     management and supervision of works for infrastructure investments in five priority clusters, with the aim of activating
                     the private sector and promoting economic growth. Investments will improve tourism and fisheries infrastructure,
                     enhance basic urban services, and upgrade public spaces to strengthen the competitiveness and livability of key
                     economic areas. The five priority clusters supported under Component 2 are: (i) Jaffna Fort Revival, focusing on
                     heritage-led urban regeneration through site restoration, public space upgrades, and complementary tourism
                     amenities; (ii) Guru Nagar Fisheries Town & Culinary Hub, enhancing landing, market, and waste management facilities
                     while creating new tourism and culinary livelihood opportunities; (iii) Cod Bay Harbor, upgrading onshore fisheries
                     infrastructure within the existing harbor footprint to improve quality standards, reduce post-harvest losses, and
                     increase value addition; (iv) Passikudah Tourism Development, improving beach access, visitor facilities, waste
                     management, and local retail spaces to support sustainable tourism and extend visitor stays; and (v) Arugam Bay

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The Project Development Objective is to improve access to enabling infrastructure in project supported clusters in Sri
                     Lanka’s Northern and Eastern Provinces to enhance regional economic development and as such, activities will be
                     restricted to those two provinces. Project typologies include technical assistance, procurement of goods, civil works and
                     renovations and capacity building. Additional detail is included in Section C. Associated Facilities will be identified during
                     through finalization of the E&S screening process. If applicable, the Borrower will apply (or cause a third party to apply)
                     the ESSs to all associated facilities works and operations.

                     All activities will be implemented within the Northern and Eastern Provinces which have a tropical climate with distinct
                     wet and dry seasons with a wide range of ecosystems including lagoons, mangroves, coral reefs, and sand dunes. Fauna
                     includes the Asian elephant, leopards, sloth bears, otters, and a wide range of birds, reptiles, amphibians, and fish. Coral
                     reefs and seagrass beds provide habitat for dugongs, dolphins, and a diversity of fish and invertebrates. The IUCN Red
                     List notes various mammals, birds, and marine and coastal species in the two Provinces. Both Provinces are rich in
                     cultural heritage, particularly relating to religious practices and temples, and a dense network of colonial-era forts and
                     port.

                     Agriculture and fisheries are currently the backbone of the local economy in the Northern and Eastern Provinces,
                     sustaining around 30 percent of the workforce of two provinces and serving as the primary source of livelihoods for

                     around 65 percent of their population. Due to the lack of adequate onshore infrastructure including cold storage and
                     hygienic fish handling facilities—post-harvest losses are substantial, with up to 30% of the fish catch. Value addition in
                     fish and other marine products is limited due to lack of processing facilities. Poor last-mile and rural-urban connectivity
                     restrict market access. These challenges are lowering producers’ incomes and private sector interest in investing in
                     these provinces, thereby limiting local economic development and employment opportunities.

                     Tourism, a critical growth sector nationally, remains underexploited in the Northern and Eastern Provinces. With both
                     the provinces accounting for more than 60% of the coastline, tourism is emerging as a promising sector.

                     Gaps in connectivity, basic infrastructure, and productive infrastructure are key challenges for advancing economic
                     growth and attracting private sector investments. Limited air, rail and road connectivity lead to long travel times from
                     Colombo and reduced competitiveness. Inadequate water supply is a major bottleneck, hindering industrial growth and
                     limiting tourism potential: only 19 percent of households have access to safely managed water supply in the Northern
                     Province, significantly lower than the national average of 60 percent, and in the Eastern Province, water provision is
                     intermittent due to limited supply. Jaffna and key tourist destinations suffer from poor waste segregation, open
                     dumping and unsafe solid waste disposal, and untreated effluent affecting the town’s tourism appeal, causing ground
                     water contamination, and health risks for residents. In addition, infrastructure provision in the past has been through
                     sectoral investments, without systematically connecting these investments to sustainable economic growth and
                     employment opportunities.

                     Sri Lanka faces multiple climate-related risks including flooding, cyclones, wildfires, tsunamis, and extreme heat, which

                     are projected to cause annual GDP losses of up to 1.2% by 2050. These hazards disproportionately affect the poorest
                     populations, especially those in agriculture and coastal regions, potentially increasing poverty rates by up to 1.8
                     percentage points.

                     Coastal areas in Northern and Eastern Provinces have higher population densities and economic activity due to fishing,
                     trade, and tourism, while inland regions remain sparsely populated and agriculture focused. Increased urbanization of
                     coastal hubs like Jaffna, Batticaloa, and Kalmunai increase exposure to climate risks, highlighting the urgent need for
                     resilient infrastructure. Flood-prone zones overlap with urban centers, disrupting infrastructure and key economic
                     activities. Extreme heat affects communities in the northern province, where rising temperatures are expected to drive
                     declines in living standards, with incomes projected to fall by up to 10% by 2050.

                     Lagoon Park sustains small-scale fisheries with 74 fishers and 30–35 non-mechanized boats using scattered landing
                     points, especially during the monsoon.

                     Guru Nagar Harbor Town. Guru Nagar is a fishing community in Jaffna, adjacent to the Jaffna Lagoon. The coast is
                     heavily modified but the wider lagoon is a critical habitat for birds, supports fish, prawns, crabs, molluscs, seagrass beds

                     Arugam Bay Tourist Town. Arugum Bay is located on Sri Lanka’s southeast coast surrounded by dunes, lagoons and

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Ministry of Transport, Highways and Urban Development (MTHUD) will serve as the Project Implementation
                     Ministry, responsible for overall project execution. Implementation will be coordinated through a Project Management
                     Unit (PMU) housed at MTHUD, comprising staff from MTHUD and the Urban Development Authority (UDA).

                      ESS8 - Cultural Heritage                                                                                        Relevant

                      ESS8 is considered relevant to the REVIVE Project because several interventions will occur in areas with
                      archaeological and culturally significant assets, requiring careful planning to avoid adverse impacts and ensure
                      culturally sensitive development. The outer islands of Jaffna—such as Delft, Kayts, and Nainativu—are notable for
                      their unique cultural heritage and ecological richness, featuring historic sites like Delft Fort and Nainativu Temple
                      alongside diverse marine ecosystems. Improving connectivity and tourism facilities in these areas offers opportunities



Public Disclosure
                      to promote eco- and heritage tourism and strengthen cultural identity; however, these activities also pose risks to
                      heritage structures. For example, works at Jaffna Fort will affect important elements such as the Queen’s Palace and
                      the historic weapon market. Similarly, in Cod Bay Harbor, sacred neem trees near the piers hold ritual significance for
                      fishers and are integral to traditional practices, yet construction activities—including pier extensions—could cause
                      physical damage, restrict access, or even lead to permanent loss of cultural practices if these trees are harmed. At
                      Gurunagar Fishery, religious and cultural structures such as statues and sections of church walls may need to be
                      rebuilt or relocated, and if this process is not handled appropriately, it could be perceived as disrespectful and result
                      in cultural loss. Furthermore, due to the prolonged civil conflict, certain buildings and structures may hold memorial
                      significance for local communities, making their removal or redesign a matter that must be approached with
                      sensitivity and meaningful consultation. To manage these risks, the project will undertake cultural heritage
                      assessments during detailed design and integrate Cultural Heritage Management Plans (CHMPs) for identified sites.
                      For sensitive elements such as neem trees, in-situ protection will be prioritized; if relocation becomes unavoidable, it
                      will be carried out carefully in consultation with local communities and cultural experts to maintain ritual continuity.
                      In addition, project activities such as urban regeneration in Jaffna City and infrastructure development in tourism and
                      fisheries clusters may involve excavations, demolition, earth movement, or other physical changes that could impact
                      unknown archaeological, cultural, and religious assets. To address this, Chance Find Procedures will be applied during
                      excavation and incorporated into Environmental and Social Management Plans (ESMPs) where necessary. The project
                      will engage contractors with appropriate expertise and provide them with additional training. All renovation works
                      will be carried out under the strict supervision of the Department of Archaeology and other cultural heritage
                      regulators, ensuring that improvements follow approved specifications and are overseen by the designated
                      authorities and relevant experts. In addition, ongoing engagement with local communities and relevant interest

                     C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or

                     The following instruments have been prepared, agreed, and disclosed prior to Project appraisal: the Environmental and

                     Framework (ESMF), the Resettlement Planning Framework (RPF) and the GBV Action Plan.

                     stakeholders throughout the project cycle and also documents the extensive consultations conducted during the

                     studies, capacity building, and training) is completed in accordance with the ESSs. The Bank will review TORs and the
                     associated outputs to confirm compliance prior to finalization and will consider the downstream impacts of
                     implementation such as for the regional development plans and feasibility studies.

                     displacement occurs. The entitlement matrix will be finalized once the staffing is in place and will be re-disclosed.
