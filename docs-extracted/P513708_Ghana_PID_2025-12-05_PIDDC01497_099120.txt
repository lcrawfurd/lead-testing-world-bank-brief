    B. Introduction and Context

  Country Context
1. Despite setbacks from the 2022 macroeconomic crisis, Ghana’s economy rebounded with 5.7 percent GDP growth
   in 2024. Over 2009–2019, growth averaged 6.8 percent, outpacing regional peers, but poverty reduction has stalled,
   rising from 39.0 percent in 2017 to 39.6% in 2024 (at the $3.00/day line). High inflation - especially food prices - and
   fiscal imbalances eroded household welfare1, prompting a US$3 billion IMF program (2023) and US$360 million World
   Bank budget support (2025). Still, structural and fiscal constraints persist, though growth is projected to moderate to
   4.3 percent in 2025 and 4.6 percent in 2026 with continued reforms and stronger domestic revenue mobilization.
2. Agriculture accounts for about 20.94 percent of GDP2 (2023) and employs nearly half of Ghana’s labor force. While
   vital for food security and rural livelihoods, its role has declined in the south and center due to urbanization and
   informal job growth. In productive regions, poor road conditions drive high post-harvest losses, lower farm incomes,
   and restrict market access, pushing farmers toward subsistence rather than commercial production leading to job
   migration to low-productivity informal services which limit the poverty-reducing impact of structural transformation,
   particularly for youth and women.
3. Ghana key agricultural value chains are cocoa, maize, palm oil, cassava and rice. Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire collectively
   account for nearly 60 percent of global cocoa production, with Ghana producing approximately 700,000 tons in 2024.
   Ghana is also the fourth largest cassava producer in the world producing about 22 million tons in 2020.3 Cassava is the
   most important root crop in Ghana engaging about 70 percent of the farming households and accounting for about 22
   percent of the agricultural GDP.4 Yam production follows closely cassava with an estimated 8.5 million in 2020 making
   Ghana the second producer of yams in the world. These highly productive crops including cereals like maize and rice 5
   are critical to Ghana’s economic growth and job creation making their easy transportation to markets key.
4. The government launched the Big Push Initiative in 2024 and the “24-Hour Economy” Program in July 2025 to boost
   productivity, focusing on agriculture, jobs, and logistics among other priorities.6 They promote continuous operations

5. Success depends on efficient road and logistics networks that enable farm produce, raw materials, and finished
   goods to move seamlessly at all hours. It requires investments in feeder and trunk roads, logistics hubs, reliable
   transport services, stronger power and digital infrastructure. This is crucial for agricultural value change production,
   particularly for export-oriented ones and those focusing on food security. By reducing the infrastructure gap, the
   government aims to improve farmers’ market access, reduce transportation costs, and minimize post-harvest losses.
   Strengthening these networks will create jobs, boost productivity, competitiveness, and inclusive growth, while better
   linking isolated regions in Ghana.
  Sectoral and Institutional Context
6. Road transport dominates Ghana’s mobility, carrying over 95 percent of passenger and freight traffic. The country’s
    94,000-kilometer road network is only 27 percent paved. More than 50 percent of the network, including feeders
    which comprise more than 50 percent of the road network and secondary roads, are in fair to impassable condition.7
    Even though Ghana’s road density is above the regional average8, distribution is uneven with the north of the country
    being poorly served, while the southern half has greater road density supporting agricultural and livestock production,
    but often at a poor service level resulting in increased travel distances and costs to markets and opportunities.
7. Ghana’s road network has experienced significant deterioration because of chronic underfunding and insufficient
   maintenance. The National Road Fund has historically met only approximately half of the nation’s road maintenance
   requirements, with revenues from fuel levies and vehicle fees remaining largely stagnant and frequently reallocated
   for purposes other than road upkeep. In July 2025, the Road Maintenance Trust Fund was passed by parliament to
   replace the existing framework to promote greater equity, accountability, and efficiency, while also de-capping
   revenues to ensure that all funds generated are dedicated exclusively to maintenance activities. The current 2025
   national budget law designates GH₵2.81 billion (USD 234 million) for road maintenance9. Through various initiatives
   and programs—including the World Bank–financed Transport Sector Improvement Project (TSIP - P151026) which
   concluded in June 2025—the Ministry of Roads and Highways initiated a comprehensive improvement of Ghana’s road
   asset management performance by establishing a dedicated unit and tool to provide improved road asset management
   practices.
8. Ghana’s road sector involves various actors. The Ministry of Roads and Highways (MRH) is the lead ministry overseeing
   the road sector through its three departments: Department of Feeder Roads (DFR), Department of Urban Roads (DUR)
   and Ghana Highway Authority (GHA) who are responsible respectively for feeder, urban and trunk roads.
  Relationship to CPF
9. The proposed project is aligned with the World Bank’s Country Partnership Framework FY22-26 (CPF) and twin goals
   of ending extreme poverty and promoting shared prosperity in a livable planet. The project supports two objectives
   under the CPF including objective 1.4: Improved connectivity for access to productive opportunities, through feeder
   roads improvement and maintenance to enhance sustainable connectivity within areas in the country with high
   potential for job creation through agriculture and improve access to services and opportunities. The improved feeder
   road will reduce post-harvest losses and transport costs for farmers to encourage scaling up from subsistence farming

  C. Proposed Development Objective(s)
 11. The Project Development Objective (PDO) is to: (i) enhance connectivity in selected areas of Ghana to support key
     agricultural value chain development; and (ii) establish an efficient and sustainable national-level road
     maintenance mechanism.


 12. The following PDO indicators will be considered and refined during project preparation:
        •     Direct users that benefit from improved access to sustainable transport infrastructures and services (number,
              gender-disaggregated)
        •     Inferred beneficiaries of improved access to sustainable transport infrastructure and services (Number of
              people, gender-disaggregated)
        •     Numbers of farmers with improved connection from AgriConnect targets (to be defined)
        •     Agricultural production volumes transported through key selected feeder roads (tons)
        •     Percentage of roads maintained each year from the annual plan

 D. Concept Description
 Project Financing and Components:
13. The proposed project aims to (i) rehabilitate feeder roads in selected regions, (ii) support maintenance improvement
    and (iii) enhance private sector mobilization in the sector. The selection of feeder roads will be done through a
    framework approach11 considering multiple criteria including impact on accessibility to agricultural key value chains,
    maximize AgriConnect targets, job creation, reduced impact on the environment, poverty reduction and, access to
    basic services and markets. Analytical tools such as FlowMax, the Rural Access Index (RAI), and critical link (climate
    resilience) models can be used to guide these prioritization decisions. The proposed project consists of three
    components as detailed below:
      Component 1: Feeder road rehabilitation program and small agriculture facilities

14. Sub-component 1.1 - Rehabilitation of feeder roads: The sub-component will finance the rehabilitation of
    approximately 1000 km of feeder roads comprising of 300 km bitumen paved, and 700 km gravel-surfaced roads within
    some selected regions in Ghana. The selection of roads to be paved or unpaved will be determined using the SPADE
    PLUS12, a two-stage approach that considers an initial multicriteria assessment based on social, environmental,
    engineering and other benefits to make a determination to pave or not. The second stage will further justify the
    economic viability of roads designated to be paved roads through a cost-benefit analysis using Roads Economic Decision
    (RED) or Cost-effectiveness Analysis (CEA) depending on the traffic volume. The Department of Feeder Roads has pre-
    selected 5,000 km of feeder roads. The framework approach will be implemented through this preselection (cf. figure
    1). This approach was successfully used in other operations.13 It is expected to have a package of roads ready for the
    first year of implementation to enable commencement of works as soon as possible. The project preparation will also
    ensure synergy with ongoing WB operations carried out by the Water and Agriculture Global Practices (GP). The design
    of the feeder roads will be informed by climate vulnerability assessment studies that will propose innovative solutions
    for climate-smart and resilient infrastructure as well as solutions for the operations and maintenance of these
    infrastructures. Maintenance will be critical to ensure sustainability of these roads. To ensure effective maintenance,
    the project includes a component 2 dedicated to this purpose.
15. All 1,000km rehabilitated roads will integrate safety audits, speed management, and design measures to protect
    vulnerable users. Especially pedestrians, cyclists, and motorcyclists who account for over 70 percent of road fatalities
    in rural areas where appropriate. Based on the design, the project will finance low-cost, high-impact interventions such
    as pedestrian crossings, rumble strips, and improved signage in towns along the corridors. Major bridges and drainage
    structures along the selected roads and within the project sites will be constructed to improve resilience of the road
    and districts to climate hazards like floods, precipitation and temperature. All road works will incorporate universal
    accessibility standards, safe pedestrian walkways, signage, and drainage to protect vulnerable users
16. Sub-component 1.2 – Agricultural market support: This sub-component will finance the development of small-scale
    agricultural logistics infrastructure to (i) strengthen value-chain connectivity, (ii) reduce post-harvest losses in project
    areas and (iii) enhance jobs creation. Investments will include the construction and equipping of rural aggregation and
    storage centers, drying platforms and loading bays along the rehabilitated feeder-road network, as well as the
    upgrading of selected rural markets located at key junctions. Facilities will integrate climate-smart design features—
    such as solar-powered cold storage, rainwater harvesting, and energy-efficient lighting—to ensure year-round
    operation and minimize greenhouse-gas emissions. The centers will serve as local nodes for aggregation and primary
    processing. The sub-component will also promote digital market linkages through partnerships with local agritech
    platforms, enabling real-time price information and logistics coordination. Collectively, these interventions will
    enhance productivity, reduce losses, and stimulate inclusive rural enterprise development around the newly connected
    feeder-road corridors, hence creating jobs at these locations.

    Component 2: Road maintenance and sustainability
17. Sub-component 2.1 -Road Fund reform support : this subcomponent will finance technical assistance to support the
    ongoing road fund reform already started by the GoG through the new maintenance trust fund bill enacted in July
    2025. Disbursement of this sub-component will be linked to a Performance Based Condition (PBC) to guarantee
    effective implementation of the reform.
18. Sub-component 2.2 – Improved Road Maintenance and Road Asset Management: This subcomponent will support
    the financing of the annual national road maintenance plan from MRH and strengthen efficient road asset management
    practices by aligning maintenance planning with revenues from the road fund and other sources through a
    performance-based condition. Building on the road asset management tool developed under the TSIP project, the
    proposed initiative will leverage this tool to enhance maintenance planning and budgeting, ensuring optimal use of
    mobilized funds. Additionally, the component will support traffic data collection and a comprehensive inventory of the
    national road network to inform maintenance prioritization. The maintenance program which comprises of both
    routine and periodic maintenance on feeder, urban and trunk roads will potentially create jobs for the local labor force
    including women and the youth and improve maintenance practices through increased financing both from the Road
    Fund and the project. Disbursement to maintenance will be tied to resources mobilization by the road fund and annual
    performance of the plan measured by an annual target to be determined during project preparation.

    Component 3: Enhancing private sector mobilization and project management
19. This component will through Technical Assistance, support efforts to mobilize private sector financing for some
    selected road corridors under the government’s infrastructure development program dabbed “the Big Push”. This
    component will finance feasibility studies for selected corridors through Public-Private Partnerships and assess
    opportunities for asset recycling (Accra-Tema Road corridor for example). Other activities to enhance the enabling
    environment can be supported.
20. The component will also support the project implementation by key implementing agencies including consultancy
    services, implementation of GRM, and capacity-building on contract management and other targeted technical
    assistance to improve project delivery.
    Component 4: Contingent Emergency Response Component (US$0.0 million)
21. The component will finance immediate and effective interventions including reconstruction of damaged
    infrastructure in response to GoG’s request to reallocate project funds in the event of an eligible crisis or emergency.
    The Borrower may request the Bank to re-allocate project funds to support emergency response and reconstruction.
    This component would draw from the uncommitted credit resources under the project from other project components
    to cover emergency response.

  Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts



22. The overall Environmental and Social Risk Classification, which at this concept level is rated “Substantial” as activities
    involve civil works in rural regions of the country. The project involves several civil works (rehabilitation of 1000km
    feeder roads, construction of market hubs, and energy infrastructure) across multiple sectors, with potential for
    significant environmental and social impacts. The risk rating reflects the scale, complexity, and context of the
    interventions, including the likelihood of land acquisition, community health and safety concerns, and cumulative
    impacts from multiple infrastructure upgrades.


Key Environmental Risks and Impacts
23. Key environmental risks and impacts include construction Impacts (i) Dust, noise, and vibration from roadworks and
    logistics hub construction, (ii) Soil erosion, loss of vegetation, and potential impacts on water resources, (iii)
    Generation of solid and hazardous waste, including e-waste from digital infrastructure upgrades, (iv) Pollution risks
    from fuel storage, machinery, and construction runoff, (v) Biodiversity: (potential disturbance to biodiversity and

24. Key social risks and impacts anticipated include: a) land acquisition or restrictions on land use, with potential for
    physical or economic displacement, especially for squatters and encroachers on feeder roads and market
    rehabilitation sites; b) impacts on livelihoods, particularly for communities trading along the road and dependent on
    right-of-way corridors; c) minor labor influx and associated risks, including the potential to upset local dynamics and
    the risk of sexual exploitation and abuse; d) risks to tangible cultural heritage, should project activities intersect with
    sites of historical or cultural significance; and e) disproportionate adverse impacts on vulnerable groups, requiring
    targeted identification and mitigation measures to ensure equitable project benefits and avoid disproportionate harm,
    especially for interventions related to road fund reform and private sector mobilization. To facilitate the activation of
    the proposed zero Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC), the CERC manual will detail the
    environmental and social aspects of needs assessments considered adequate, as well as potential activities (positive
    list) to be financed under the CERC and within an acceptable quantum of risk mitigation.
