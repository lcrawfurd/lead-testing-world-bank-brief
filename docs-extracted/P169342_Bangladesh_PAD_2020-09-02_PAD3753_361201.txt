    I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

    A. Country Context

   1.      Bangladesh has made rapid social and economic progress in recent decades, reaching lower-
   middle-income status by 2015. Gross domestic product (GDP) growth averaged close to 6 percent
   annually since 2000 and, according to official estimates, accelerated to over 8 percent in FY19. Strong
   labor market gains contributed to a sharp decline in poverty, with the national poverty rate falling from
   48.9 percent to 24.3 percent between 2000 and 2016, while extreme poverty declined from 34.3 percent
   to 12.9 percent. 1 However, the pace of poverty reduction slowed in recent years even as growth
   accelerated, particularly in urban areas and in the west of the country. Similarly, the progress on shared
   prosperity slowed between 2010 and 2016, after a decade of improvements, with annual consumption
   growth of the bottom 40 percent trailing that of the overall population (1.2 percent versus 1.6 percent).
   Bangladesh entered the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) crisis with a relatively strong
   macroeconomic position. Garment exports and remittances narrowed the external deficit in recent years
   and international reserves were adequate at the end of April 2020 at US$32.9 billion, equivalent to six
   months of imports. While tax collections are among the lowest in the world, under-execution of the
   budget has contained the fiscal deficit, which has been below 5 percent of GDP since FY01. As a result,
   public debt is low and stood at 33.7 percent of GDP at the end of FY19. A key economic vulnerability is in
   the banking sector where the nonperforming loan ratio is high at 9.3 percent of outstanding loans in
   December 2019 and is underestimated considering significant under-provisioning, regulatory
   forbearance, and gaps in the legal framework.

   2.      Growth declined sharply as the COVID-19 pandemic brought about major disruptions to
   economic activity. In the first half of FY20 (July to December), growth decelerated as slower global trade
   and deteriorating external competitiveness lowered exports and tighter access to finance constrained
   private investment growth. With declining ready-made garment (RMG) orders, exports declined by 5.8
   percent (year-over-year) during this period. A sharp contraction in capital goods imports (3.4 percent,
   year-over-year) suggests private investment also declined. Growth during the first half of the year was
   primarily supported by remittance-fueled private consumption. The initial phase of the pandemic in early
   2020 disrupted the supply of intermediate goods from China, reducing manufacturing output. As the
   pandemic intensified abroad, export orders from Europe and the United States declined precipitously and
   an estimated US$3.2 billion of RMG orders were cancelled or suspended.2 The Government implemented
   a national shutdown from March 26 to May 30 to control an accelerating domestic outbreak of the virus.
   Control measures resulted in a sudden stop of many components of the service and industrial sectors.
   Remittance inflows declined by 23.6 percent year-over-year in April 2020 and exports declined by 82.9
   percent in the same period. In FY19, inflation remained modest at an average of 5.5 percent, primarily
   driven by a rise in nonfood prices. Demand for food surged with precautionary purchases ahead of the
   national lockdown but has eased more recently as government food distribution programs were
   implemented. Overall inflation reached 5.4 percent year-over-year by the end of May 2020.

   3.       COVID-19 has darkened the economic outlook through domestic economic disruptions,
   declining exports and remittances, and rising stress in the financial sector. FY20 GDP growth is projected
   in a range between 1.6 percent and a downside scenario of 1.0 percent. The downside forecast is based
   on a situation in which (a) lockdown measures are extended and mobility remains significantly constrained
   and (b) the global outlook deteriorates further. In FY21, growth is projected between 1.0 percent and 3.0
   percent. In the downside scenario, a second round of infections and a prolonged global recession would
   result in the realization of some contingent liabilities, especially from the financial sector. The extended
   national shutdown is likely to depress economic activity across all sectors in the last quarter of FY20, and
   varying levels of control measures are likely to continue in FY21. Private consumption, the main engine of
   growth, is expected to slow, and declining remittance inflows reduce household income. The
   unprecedented uncertainties related to COVID-19 are likely to further dampen private investment. The
   decline in exports is expected to persist, as developed market recessions depress demand for RMGs,
   Bangladesh’s primary export. A shortage of intermediate inputs is expected to lower industrial production,
   while labor shortages could have an adverse impact on all sectors. Transportation disruptions are
   expected to dampen agricultural growth, particularly production of perishable products such as dairy,
   poultry, and vegetables. The recovery is expected to be very gradual, with ongoing economic disruptions
   and increasing fragilities in the banking system. In the medium term, a gradual recovery in growth is
   expected, with some increase in export demand and higher public spending.

   4.      Bangladesh is extremely vulnerable to the effects of climate change. The Global Climate Risk
   Index ranks Bangladesh as the world’s seventh most-affected country over 1999–2018. 3 Rising
   temperatures leading to more intense and unpredictable rainfalls during the monsoon season and a
   higher probability of catastrophic cyclones are expected to result in increased tidal inundation. It is
   estimated that a 1 m rise in sea levels would submerge 18 percent of arable land in coastal areas.4 Recent
   studies estimate that by 2050, Bangladesh could have 13.3 million internal climate migrants.5 Additional
   rural-urban migration would have significant consequences for air and water pollution and unsustainable
   consumption of natural resources, while putting additional pressure on urban labor markets. Addressing
   climate risks is increasingly becoming urgent to ensure sustainable economic development of the country.

    B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

   5.      As Bangladesh aspires to meet its target of eliminating poverty by 2030 and attaining upper-
   middle-income status by 2031, significant human capital development challenges remain, which may
   become larger as the country grapples with the consequences of COVID-19. In 2017, the Human Capital
   Index of 0.48 for Bangladesh surpassed the South Asian average and the lower-middle-income country
   average in all criteria except for stunting. Bangladesh’s performance against the Millennium Development
   Goals (MDGs) was also impressive relative to the South Asia Region average for most of the indicators.
   However, the learning-adjusted years of schooling is just 6.5 years due to the poor quality of education,
   36 percent of children are afflicted by chronic malnutrition, and nearly half of the children under five in
   the poorest wealth quintile are stunted, placing significant limits on human capital development. With
   current education and health conditions, a child born today in Bangladesh is expected to be on average
   52 percent less productive than they could be. In addition, there is great concern over COVID-19’s long-

   6.      Progress toward human capital development can be enhanced partly through coordinated
   multisectoral approaches that effectively address the underlying determinants of nutritional status. For
   example, reductions in stunting are more likely to materialize when the multiple contributing factors—
   such as access to health care; childcare practices; and access to water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH)—
   are adequately addressed for a child. Recognizing the multifactorial character of malnutrition, the
   Bangladesh Health, Nutrition and Population Strategic Investment Plan (2016–2021) advocates
   multisectoral approaches, which include closely linking WASH interventions with interventions to improve
   dietary diversity, food quality, and safety. In this context, this project takes a convergence approach,
   where the proposed project and the World Bank’s ongoing investments in health and income support for
   child nutrition would be coordinated so that maximum human capital development benefits could be
   achieved in some of the poorest parts of rural Bangladesh (see paragraph 27 and annex 1).

   7.       WASH plays a critical role in human capital development, the key to achieving the country’s
   ambition of becoming an upper-middle-income country by 2021. The World Bank’s Bangladesh WASH
   Poverty Diagnostics highlighted the implications of WASH access and quality on the country’s human
   capital development and poverty reduction.6 It points out that with more than one-third of children under
   five stunted and their ability to grow and learn subsequently limited, Bangladesh is not achieving its fullest
   potential. This is because children’s inability to grow to their full physical and cognitive potential may have
   long-lasting effects on their future schooling and earnings, in turn reducing productivity through the
   associated costs of health care and time loss placed on the individual and their households. The poorest,
   especially women and girls, feel the greatest burdens of inadequate WASH because of its negative
   synergies with other life deprivations such as little income, poor access to health services, food insecurity,
   and low levels of education. In particular, menstrual hygiene practices can have a significant impact on
   girls’ health and human capital because some girls do not attend school during the menstruation period.
   Over time, evidence has suggested that successful WASH interventions may improve overall health and
   reduce stunting in children under five years of age.7 This occurs through multiple pathways that include
   (a) fewer episodes of diarrheal disease, (b) improved gut health, (c) reductions in protozoa and helminth
   infection, and (d) reductions in anemia. Furthermore, quality WASH services are an essential part of
   preventing disease and pandemic preparedness to protect human health during the current and future
   infectious disease outbreaks. Easy access to clean and reliable water supply is needed for handwashing
   and cleaning and disinfecting surfaces where germs and viruses settle. Handwashing with soap is one of
   the most important things to slow infectious diseases, such as diarrhea, cholera, typhoid, and COVID-19.
   Improving access to WASH services also helps mitigate some of the secondary impacts of the crisis, which
   may include increased mortality risk from more routine childhood diseases (for example, diarrheal
   disease) due to not seeking care for fear of COVID-19 infection, inability to seek care because of lockdown,
   or overwhelmed health care facilities.

   8.        Access to WASH services in rural Bangladesh improved much over time, but significant
   challenges remain for the Government to achieve the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goal
   (SDG) 6 on clean water and sanitation. In 2017, about 97 percent of the rural population had access to at
   least ‘basic’ water supply.8 This achievement was largely due to the expansion of tube wells in rural areas,
   with close to 95 percent of rural people using them. 9 Meanwhile, the community-led total sanitation
   approach enabled a vast majority of the rural population to end open defecation and use sanitation
   facilities. As a result, access to ‘improved’ sanitation increased dramatically from 30 percent to 65 percent
   in rural areas from 2000 to 2017.10 Still, significant challenges remain in rural WASH to meet the SDG 6
   service standards, which are recommended for maximizing human capital outcomes. The challenges
   include the following:

             (a)    Water supply. The proliferation of tube wells has given rise to concerns about (i)
                    sustainability, where excessive withdrawal may threaten water security in the long term,
                    and (ii) water quality, as there are few practical solutions to regulating and monitoring tube
                    wells against arsenic and fecal contamination. Indeed, the rural population using ‘safely
                    managed’11 water supply barely increased from 59 percent in 2000 to 61 percent in 2017.12
                    Furthermore, the job of fetching water almost always falls to the girls and women of a
                    household—in 2013, females had 90 percent of the share of water collection responsibility.
                    Treated piped water may address these issues, but household piped water connections in
                    rural areas are rare, with a coverage of just 3 percent in 2017. Existing schemes have faced
                    significant operation and maintenance (O&M) challenges due to weak technical
                    backstopping mechanisms, small customer base, and a combination of low willingness-to-
                    pay and low willingness to charge for water.13 Out of about 1,900 rural piped water schemes
                    that are currently functional in the nation, none are being metered, and the users pay a flat
                    tariff per household. In addition, climate change is exacerbating water security and quality,
                    especially in parts of southern Bangladesh, where traditional rain patterns are disrupted and
                    sea-level rise is causing saltwater intrusion.

             (b)    Sanitation. While low-cost options have significantly increased access to ‘improved’
                    sanitation, in 2017, only 32 percent of the rural population had access to a ‘safely managed’
                    sanitation service, and 35 percent still were using ‘unimproved’ sanitation facilities. 14

            (d)   WASH in public places, including health care facilities. Scattered evidence suggests that
                  inadequate WASH access in places outside the home is a significant barrier in people’s daily
                  lives. Women and girls find it especially difficult to use public toilets, as few are deemed
                  gender-appropriate and secure. WASH in health care facilities is of paramount importance
                  due to its implications for health and safety of health care workers and patrons alike.
                  However, while 86 percent of rural health care facilities have access to an improved water
                  source, only 42 percent have it on the premises.18 Of the 78 percent of rural health care
                  facilities that have access to an improved sanitation facility for clients, only 12 percent have
                  designated toilets for women.19

                  many repeatedly use cloths that are not adequately washed and dried between uses. This is

   9.       The current administration’s vision23 to enhance the WASH service standards in rural areas to

   supervision, asset management, tariff setting, sustainability, climate resiliency, water quality monitoring,
   citizen engagement, monitoring and evaluation (M&E), and collaboration; (b) a road map for national

   18.     The World Bank Group engagement in Bangladesh has been guided by the FY2016–20 CPF,

   19.      COVID-19 pandemic has severely disrupted economic activity and created an unprecedented
   crisis that is likely to worsen poverty in the short term. As the pandemic intensified globally, Bangladesh
   exports to Europe and the United States declined precipitously. Growth in GDP is projected to range in
   FY20 between 1.6 and 1.0 percent and in FY21 between 1.0 and –3.0 percent. The country’s achievement
   in reducing poverty between 2000 and 2016—the national poverty rate fell from 48.9 to 24.5 percent and
   extreme poverty from 34.3 to 13.0 percent—will likely be reversed. Slower GDP growth, the income losses
   of informal workers throughout the economy, and lower international remittances may add 8 to 12
   percentage points to what the 2020 poverty rate would have been without COVID-19.

    F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

   33.     Long-term sustainability is at the center of rural water project design. The current
   administration’s vision on rural WASH translates into providing piped water services, but a successful
   sustainable delivery model has not yet taken root in Bangladesh (see box 1). There is a need to apply the
   lessons from the World Bank’s worldwide experience with rural water and promote a scalable model that
   meets the sustainability and service delivery objectives set forth in the Government’s new National
   Strategy for Water Supply and Sanitation. The key lessons of the World Bank’s experience are described
   in paragraphs 34–36.

   34.      Private sector participation proves effective in rural piped water schemes. There is an increasing
   global realization that the community management model has reached the limits of what can be achieved
   through voluntarism.33 Water schemes involving private operation continue to show promise particularly
   when critical elements—such as strong private partners, capacitated local partners, and clear regulatory
   regimes—are in place. Globally, various PPP and hybrid models have emerged and are now moving to
   scale, particularly in countries such as Senegal and Benin, where assets are owned by central or local
   government entities and supervised by community groups but operated and maintained by private
   contractors. A wide spectrum of private sector models has been tested also in Bangladesh over the last
   decade. The main lessons include the following: (a) private operators can only deliver rural piped water
   services with some form of subsidies for the capital investment, and in remote sparsely populated areas,
   the traditional PPP model with equity investment is unlikely to become profitable; (b) while private sector
   engagement is deemed cost-effective for delivering quality services to the poor, the private operator
   model often fails in small schemes with low revenue collection; and (c) effective private sector
   participation for sustainable O&M largely depends on the reliability and technical expertise of the private
   sector, which may be lacking in rural Bangladesh.

   35.     Clustered DBO or BO contracting is a promising alternative to traditional PPPs in the rural
   Bangladesh context. DBO or BO contracting is less ambitious than the traditional PPPs as it involves a
   lower risk, but it is more likely to attract and sustain private sector participation in rural water supply
   operations.34 To achieve economies of scale and attract larger and more professional engineering firms,
   rural water schemes in this project will be clustered together to form much larger tenders and contracts.
   DBO or BO contracting is fairly common in Bangladesh, and clustering can mitigate many of the risks of
   the private sector models relating to scale, expertise, and revenue generation.

   36.     The private sector model is more likely to succeed when participation from the UPs and
   communities remain strong. For private participation to be sustainable, governments must maintain
   some functions to support and supervise the private sector involvement. Furthermore, lessons indicate
   that continuation of community management principles should not be neglected when outsourcing key
   service provision tasks to private operators. Hence, the project will establish clear roles and

    IV. PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY

    A. Technical Analysis

   48.      Continuously pressurized water supply from deep groundwater aquifers prioritizes drinking
   water safety and security. The project will source water that is compliant with the Bangladesh Drinking
   Water Quality Standards with a focus on water safety (primarily microbial, arsenic, and manganese
   contamination) and user acceptability (primarily iron and salinity). Support extended for the piped water
   schemes includes the drilling and casing of wells in the deep aquifers, 41 water quality and drawdown
   testing,42 and equipping and fitting of submersible electric pumps and overhead tanks of a sufficient size
   to ensure the continuous availability of safe water. Given the microbial contamination risks associated
   with intermittent schemes, the piped water schemes will deliver a continuously pressurized service to the
   household connections. Chlorination would be incorporated in BO and DBO contracts as needed to
   prevent contamination throughout the system. If beneficiary households of piped water schemes receive
   MFI water loans through the project (that is, Subcomponent 1.3), quality of the plumbing installed by LEs
   within the household will be checked to maintain the safety of drinking water through to the point of
   consumption.

   49.     Households in project areas who will not get piped water connection can access MFI water loans
   to convert their hand pumps into micro-electric piped water systems for handwashing, bathing, and
   flushing latrines. This would involve the installation of an electric suction pump in parallel with a hand
   pump on a shallow tube well or the replacement of the hand pump with a submersible electric pump in a
   deep tube well to establish a micro-piped water network within the household. These investments help
   ensure that water is always available in the kitchen, bathroom, and toilet so that the households can
   practice good hygiene behaviors. The compliance of micro-piped hand pump conversion systems to water

   50.      The twin alternating offset pit latrines are designed to facilitate compliance with the ‘safely
   managed’ sanitation service standard. Most of the population in Bangladesh uses direct pit latrines that
   are often unhygienic and extremely difficult to empty. Offset pit latrines are hygienic, increase the
   convenience (for example, enabling the commode to be situated within the house), and facilitate easier
   emptying. When offset pit latrines have two alternating pits, the pit that is offline can neutralize the
   pathogens given sufficient time, enabling the safe removal of the fecal sludge following WHO guidelines.43
   Adherence to the twin alternating offset pit latrine standard, along with the provision of training to
   households and LEs on the correct procedures for O&M and safe disposal of fecal sludge, is considered to
   facilitate compliance to SDG 6.2 ‘safely managed’ sanitation service standard.

   51.      The WASH infrastructure investments and WASH sector strengthening will incorporate climate-
   resilient features in support of climate change adaptation and mitigation. Overall, increased access to
   safely managed WASH through the construction of piped water schemes and twin pit latrines in the
   targeted areas will help reduce the vulnerability of beneficiary communities against extreme weather and
   climate change-related events, such as floods, droughts, and heat waves. For Subcomponents 1.1 and 1.2,
   (a) the assessments of groundwater sources will consider water resource availability in view of climate
   vulnerability and extreme weather conditions; (b) to promote energy efficiency, solar-powered
   photovoltaic (PV) pumping systems with elevated platforms above the flood level would be considered
   where feasible; and (c) water metering will be introduced, which will contribute to reduced water
   consumption in the face of drought and ensure cost recovery, helping the piped water schemes’ financial
   sustainability and leading to more funding being channeled to infrastructure maintenance that would
   reduce the chance of water supply systems or pumps being damaged or overwhelmed during a flood. For
   Subcomponents 2.1 and 2.2, (a) offset pit latrines will be used, which are climate resilient by design and
   able to function even when the pits are under water; (b) the latrine superstructures will be built on raised
   platforms above the flood level to enhance resilience against heavy rain and flooding; and (c) advice to
   households and pit emptiers on the safe use of dry fecal sludge as a soil conditioner would facilitate the
   productive reuse of this sludge and reduce the use of synthetic fertilizers, which are greenhouse gas (GHG)
   intensive.44 Under Subcomponent 2.3, LEs will be offered training on climate-smart design, installation,
   and maintenance of WASH facilities, contributing to communities’ enhanced resilience to floods and
   droughts. Under Subcomponents 3.1 and 3.2, strengthening of WASH sector policy and capacity building
   will prominently feature topics on climate resiliency to deal with floods and droughts, such as low-carbon
   WASH technologies including climate-smart designs and renewable energy use, nonrevenue water
   management, and FSM.

    E. Environmental and Social

   63.      The project poses E&S challenges, but E&S benefits are expected to be significant as well. In
   general, the project interventions are small-scale in nature, so they are not expected to have any
   significant or unmitigable E&S impacts. Key environmental management issues include (a) the
   contamination of water and soil if the facilities supported are not properly designed and maintained and
   (b) the construction-related impacts on air, noise, and water if management plans regarding workplace
   and community health and safety are not followed. The project has several implications on the social side,
   including (a) gender (design, safety, and impact on women’s health); (b) inclusion (addressing the needs
   of indigenous, marginalized, and vulnerable communities; culturally sensitized design; easing access to
   information and finance; and assistance with repair and maintenance); (c) land use (common/private
   property, optimizing access through strategic location, and resettlement impacts if any); (d) community
   health and safety; and (e) the type of labor used and associated impacts. If these impacts are well
   managed, the project is expected to result in better E&S outcomes through improved access to ‘safely
   managed’ sanitation facilities, leading to better health. Especially for women, menstrual hygiene, privacy,
   security, access, and comfort will be greatly improved.

   64.     The proposed project follows the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF),
   which will apply to AIIB financing as well. Among 10 Environment and Social Standards (ESS), the relevant
   ESS are ESS1: Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts; ESS2: Labor
   and Working Conditions; ESS3: Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management; ESS4:
   Community Health and Safety; ESS5: Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary
   Resettlement; ESS7: Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local
   Communities; ESS8: Cultural Heritage; ESS9 Financial Intermediaries; and ESS10: Stakeholder Engagement
   and Information Disclosure. The required ESF documents—the Environmental and Social Commitment
   Plan (ESCP), Environmental and Social Management Framework, and Stakeholder Engagement Plan—
   were prepared and disclosed in May 2020 in-country and on the World Bank’s website. Hard copies of the
   documents will also be made available in all field offices for project implementation. The ESCP sets out
   the appropriate time frame for plans and actions required for the project to meet the ESF requirements.
   ESF documents that need to be prepared during implementation include specific Environment and Social
   Management Plans and a Labor Management Plan. Given that most of workers will be from local areas
   and based on other project conditions, the gender-based violence risk rating for the project is ‘low’. To
   support implementation of ESF documents, the project will hire qualified E&S specialists in the PMU,
   ensure adequate budget is allocated, and provide training.

   65.      For E&S due diligence, both the DPHE and PKSF will adopt a framework approach. The exact

   considerations incorporated in project design include the following:

           (a)   Improving access to water at home significantly reduces the burden on women for carting
                 water for handwashing, bathing, MHM, and the flushing of latrines.

           (b)   The project places women at home (rather than men in the marketplace) as the primary
                 decision-maker on household WASH investments. The provision of a ‘turnkey’ latrine
                 installation service and information sessions within credit groups on WASH loans will enable
                 the paradigm shift.

           (c)   The design of toilets takes account of female preferences, such as lockable doors and
                 adequate space for menstrual hygiene.

           (d)   The extension of MFI loans to an estimated 150 women entrepreneurs will help them sell
                 sanitary napkins at the doorsteps, improving the menstrual hygiene especially among
                 adolescent girls who are shy to purchase them from the market. Support for this industry
                 will also help economic empowerment of women.

           (e)   The project will promote women’s representation and leadership in water management
                 committees at the community level. Women will be encouraged to voice their concerns and
                 ideas about WASH services in regular water management committee meetings. Training on
                 the importance of participating and voicing their opinions in these water management
                 committee meetings will be done through the credit group sessions to maximize
                 dissemination.

    VI.      KEY RISKS

   70.      The overall risk rating for the project is Moderate. The project will be implemented by two
   agencies that have ample experience working with the World Bank. Project design incorporates lessons
   learned from experience and builds in sufficient mitigation measures against key project risks as discussed
   in the following paragraphs.

   71.      Political and governance risk is Substantial. Bangladesh ranks low on transparency,55 and there
   is a substantial likelihood that political and governance factors could have a significant impact on the
   project achieving the PDO. With a significant amount of the project financing to be spent on civil works,
   there is a substantial risk of rent seeking that would take advantage of weak governance. The Government
   has taken steps to improve transparency, accountability, and participation, but their impact has been slow
   to realize. This risk will be partially addressed in the project by ensuring strong fiduciary measures (see
   paragraph 73) and building capacity to allow greater planning, implementation, and monitoring roles in
   UPs and communities, especially through water management committees.

   72.      Institutional capacity risk for implementation and sustainability is Substantial. The weak
   institutional capacity at the central and local levels is a major challenge, which subsequently affects the
   project sustainability. To enhance institutional capacity, a series of tailored training workshops, both on
   technical subjects and soft skills such as leadership and collaboration, will be offered to targeted central
   and local government officials and community health workers over multiple years. The project will also
   set up two PSCs at the secretary level to foster effective coordination among different agencies (see
   paragraph 41). Various measures to enhance sustainability have been embedded in the project design,
   including plans to build capacity of the local private sector (see paragraph 47).

   73.     Fiduciary risk is Substantial. Fiduciary risks have a probability of affecting the PDO in a
   substantially adverse way, especially on the FM aspect where many of the PKSF partner MFIs need to be
   mobilized and prudently controlled. Overall fiduciary environment also has some weaknesses, including

                 ANNEX 1: Convergence Approach to Maximize Human Capital Development

                                      COUNTRY: Bangladesh
          BD Rural Water, Sanitation and Hygiene for Human Capital Development Project

 1.       The project will converge with the two ongoing World Bank projects, seeking to leverage the
 human capital interventions in WASH, health, and nutrition. The two ongoing projects in Bangladesh
 that will converge with the project are (a) the HSSP (P160846, US$500 million, July 2017–December 2022),
 which seeks to strengthen the HNP sector's core management systems and delivery of essential HNP
 services, and (b) the ISPPP (P146520, US$250 million, March 2015–June 2022), which seeks to provide
 income support to the poorest mothers in selected Upazilas while increasing the mothers’ use of CNCD
 services and enhancing local-level government capacity to deliver safety nets. While both the HSSP and
 ISPPP are currently expected to close within the first two years of this project, synergies between the
 three projects are built on government systems that will continue to be in place, specifically the targeting
 platform and community health workers. Even after the close of both projects, this project will leverage
 their investments and strengthened capacity to deliver on its objectives.

 2.      The convergence approach among the three World Bank projects can be summarized as having
 four key converging features: a geographical overlap of project locations, the use of common delivery
 platforms including beneficiary targeting, a coordinated communication strategy, and leveraging of each
 other’s results indicators. With at least two projects implemented in project locations, the convergence
 approach is expected to leverage the multisectoral interventions and resources to address human
 development challenges faced by vulnerable households in some of the poorest regions of the country.

    Box 2.1. Lessons from the BOT Model
    •    The World Bank’s BRWSSP, implemented in 2005–10, piloted promoting a BOT model that involved a 30
         percent equity share from private operators under a 15-year concession contract.
    •    An evaluation of experiences with private sector delivery under the BRWSSP found that of the 21 schemes
         set up, 10 are nonfunctioning and only 4 schemes have any likelihood of recovering the private investment
         by the end of their contract terms. The evaluation highlights a number of lessons, including (a) the higher-
         than-expected costs for potential investors, (b) challenges with tariff collection, (c) unpredictable local
         politics, and (d) lack of professional and entrepreneurial capacity to successfully operate and manage
         schemes once built. Ensuring private sector participation, particularly by large investors in the rural piped
         water sector, was a challenge due to volatile community demand, user reluctance to pay tariffs, low
         profitability, liability of long-term investment, closed option for exit, and lack of trusted local partners to
         protect interest of the firms and companies. The model is deemed less profitable in rural poverty prone
         areas.
    •    In summary, with technically strong private operators and community water management committees that
         are actively involved in scheme implementation, rural piped water schemes may be successful, especially
         if packaged into a cluster of multiple schemes so that larger and more capable private sector contractors
         can be attracted.
