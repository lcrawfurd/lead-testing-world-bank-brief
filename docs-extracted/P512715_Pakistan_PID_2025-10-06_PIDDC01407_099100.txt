B. Introduction and Context

Country Context

   1. Amid an economic crisis at the start of fiscal year (FY) 2024, Pakistan undertook decisive measures to prevent
      a sovereign default, including sharp fiscal consolidation, exchange rate adjustment, and monetary tightening.
      An IMF-SBA arrangement helped to bridge financing gaps and restore confidence. Adjustment has been
      successful, with inflation now falling, the exchange rate stabilizing, and foreign exchange reserves at more
      comfortable levels, allowing for the easing of import controls. However, fiscal consolidation measures and tight
      monetary conditions continued to weigh on economic activity, with the economy growing by only 2.7 percent in
      FY25. The outlook has been further complicated by recent flooding, including of critical crop-growing areas of
      Punjab, with expected negative impacts on the fiscal balance and exports. Pakistan must now carefully navigate
      flood impacts while sustaining stabilization efforts to accelerate growth and reduce poverty from current high
      levels (around 25.6 percent using the national poverty line). This will require reforms to make the tax system more
      effective and equitable, enhance the efficiency of public spending, reduce the large role of the state in the
      economy, lower trade barriers, improve the business environment, and improve basic services to address human
      capital deficits. Reform efforts are underway in critical areas but remain at early stages of implementation. The
      outlook is predicated on the IMF Extended Fund Facility program remaining on track, continued fiscal restraint,
      and continued external financing inflows. Significant downside risks arise from heavy banking sector exposure to
      sovereign debt, geopolitical factors, and further natural disasters and climate change impacts.

Sectoral and Institutional Context

   2. Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (KP) is among the most flood-prone regions in Pakistan, facing recurrent threats from
      riverine floods, flash floods, and Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs). These events have intensified over time
      due to factors such as climate change, deforestation, land degradation, and increasing population density in flood-
      prone areas. The province has in the recent past experienced severe flood events—including in 2010, 2016, 2022,

    3. Khyber Pakhtunkhwa sustained significant damage and losses during the 2022 floods with estimated damages
       of US$935 million and losses of US$658 million. Recovery and reconstruction needs were assessed at
       approximately US$780 million1 while 309 people lost their lives.2 More recently, the 2025 monsoon triggered
       localized riverine, glacial, and flash flooding, causing 479 fatalities (as of August 29, 2025)3, alongside extensive
       infrastructure damage and additional livestock losses. This represents the highest death toll seen in the province
       in recent years surpassing the 2022 floods, underscoring the severity of the impact despite the localized nature
       of the events. The increasing intensity and frequency of such disasters in KP highlight the compounded risks of a
       changing climate, environmental degradation, and the encroachment of built-up areas into natural floodplains.

    4. KP is characterized by diverse topography, with nearly 70 percent of its area consisting of mountains and hills
       ranging from 200 meters in the southern plains to peaks above 7,000 meters in the north, while a large share
       of the population is concentrated in valleys and river basins along the Kabul, Swat, and Indus river systems,
       where flood and landslide risks are highest.4 The population is exposed to a mix of hazard types including flash
       and riverine floods, GLOFs, mudflows and landslides precipitated by glacial melting and heavy monsoon rainfall.
       Rising regional temperatures have accelerated glacial melt in north-western KP, resulting in the formation of
       more than 3,000 glacial lakes5. Further downstream, the province’s exposure to sustained monsoon rainfall is
       expected to intensify, increasing flood potential6. Of KP’s 35 districts, the Government currently designates 11 as
       high flood risk and 14 as medium flood risk7.

    5. Pakistan’s national and provincial policy frameworks prioritize disaster risk management (DRM) and climate
       resilience, reflecting the country’s global commitments and creating opportunities for strengthened
       implementation at the sub-national level. At the provincial level, the government has adopted the Disaster
       Management Act 2019, Climate Change Policy 2022, and regularly develops annual contingency plans. A
       provincial disaster risk financing (DRF) strategy has also been drafted and is pending provincial cabinet approval.
       Strengthening DRM capacities will enable the provincial government in improving preparedness and response in
       line with its policies and plans, as well as implementing its DRF strategy. At the national level, DRM and flood
       resilience are consistent with Pakistan’s overarching policy commitments, including the National Disaster Risk
       Reduction Policy (2013), National Water Policy (2018), the Government of Pakistan’s Five-Year Development
       Plan, the Nationally Determined Contribution, and the forthcoming National Adaptation Plan, as well as global

    Reduction. Supporting DRM implementation requires both top-down and community-driven approaches, in line
    with the objectives of the National Disaster Management Plan (NDMP 2012–2022).

6. The Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (KP) Irrigation Department oversees flood protection infrastructure such as
   embankments, carrier channels, check dams, and hydromet observatories, but implementation of identified
   priority schemes is constrained by financing gaps, limited technical capacity, and weak inter-departmental
   coordination. Site-specific nature-based solutions (e.g., afforestation, terracing, bioengineering) could
   complement grey infrastructure and fit KP’s landscapes, but the Department lacks sufficient experience to
   design and mainstream them. More broadly, risk-informed development planning remains nascent, as hazard
   maps and risk information are not systematically applied in infrastructure, land-use, or sectoral planning.
   Strengthening institutional and technical capacity is therefore critical to integrate both grey and green measures
   with risk analytics, ensuring that future development reflects KP’s growing exposure to climate-induced
   disasters.

7. The province currently lacks a comprehensive and modern flood forecasting and early warning system capable
   of supporting anticipatory risk management. The existing hydro-meteorological observation network is
   fragmented, with limited information sharing across agencies. While responsibility from observation to first
   response is spread across different state agencies and administrative levels, effective and timely disaster
   management depends on efficient information flow. Real-time data acquisition, modeling, and communication
   protocols remain underdeveloped, constraining the province’s capacity to anticipate and respond to flood
   events. These gaps have contributed to delayed evacuation and response actions during recent disasters.
   Communication channels between state agencies and communities also remain rudimentary and require
   improvements.

8. Khyber Pakhtunkhwa’s disaster management architecture, led by the Provincial Disaster Management
   Authority (PDMA) and supported by District Disaster Management Authorities (DDMAs), provides the
   institutional foundation for emergency preparedness and response. However, capacity constraints at both levels
   limit effectiveness. While PDMA plays a central role in multi-hazard coordination, its systems for early warning
   dissemination, contingency planning, and risk analytics remain underdeveloped. At the district level, DDMAs exist
   legally under the National Disaster Management Act (2010), but are often under-resourced or non-functional in
   practice. The absence of operational District Emergency Operation Centers, warehouses, and basic logistics such
   as vehicles has constrained their ability to manage last-mile preparedness and response. Strengthening PDMA’s
   systems and fully operationalizing DDMAs through targeted infrastructure and capacity support are therefore
   critical priorities for enhancing the province’s disaster readiness.

9. Rescue 1122, the province’s frontline emergency response service, plays a critical role in disaster management
   but is constrained by limited capacity and geographical coverage. The service is mandated to oversee
   preparedness, response, protection and prevention across all types of emergencies, including water-related
   hazards. However, many districts remain underserved due to the absence of rescue centers, while existing
   facilities lack adequate equipment, trained personnel, and disaster-specific protocols. To strengthen provincial
   disaster response, the service requires expanded geographic coverage through new centers, upgraded technical

      10. Vulnerable groups – including women, children, and marginalized populations - are disproportionately
          affected by flood events, yet mechanisms to ensure their protection and participation remain
          underdeveloped. . Given the disaster-prone nature of KP’s geography and the challenging terrain which
          increases response times, communities will inevitably be the first responders even with well-functioning DRM
          institutions in place. However, institutional mechanisms to engage communities in risk identification,
          preparedness, or response planning remain limited. Past initiatives8 have demonstrated the value of
          empowering communities to manage local risks, but such efforts require sustained support and scaling.
          Strengthening community awareness, building local capacities', and establishing stronger linkages with formal
          institutions are therefore essential to foster an inclusive and sustainable approach to disaster
          resilience. Institutionalizing community engagement would also enable local knowledge and capacities to be
          systematically integrated into broader DRM efforts.


    Relationship to CPF

      11. The proposed KP Flood Resilience Project (KPFRP) is fully aligned with the World Bank Group Country
          Partnership Framework (CPF) FY26-35; Report No. CPF0000037. The CPF identifies flood resilience as a strategic
          priority and includes a dedicated outcome—Outcome 3.2: Increased resilience to floods and other climate-
          related disasters—under Pillar 3 on increased resilience to climate change. This outcome commits to enhancing
          resilience for 75 million people through targeted investments in flood protection, nature-based solutions, early
          warning systems, and institutional capacity for disaster risk management. The proposed project also indirectly
          contributes to the World Bank Corporate Scorecard indicators related to enhanced resilience to climate risks and
          people provided with services and livelihoods.

      12. KPFRP directly contributes to this objective by supporting the Government of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa in
          implementing a comprehensive flood resilience program. The project will strengthen provincial institutions and
          systems for flood risk management, helping to operationalize the CPF’s vision of a more climate-adaptive
          development trajectory. It also complements the broader World Bank efforts to mainstream climate resilience
          across the portfolio and demonstrates the Bank’s continued support to subnational governments in delivering
          on the country’s climate and disaster risk reduction agenda.

    D. Concept Description

     1. Description
       The Project will comprise of the following components:

       15. Component 1 – Improving Infrastructure and Systems for Resilience (estimated US$190 million): This
           component will support the design and implementation of priority infrastructure investments aimed at reducing
           the province’s exposure to floods including riverine, flash flooding, and GLOFs. The component includes both grey
           and green solutions for flood protection and will be implemented by the KP Irrigation Department, with technical
           support provided through a dedicated TA subcomponent. . The component will also finance the province-wide
           Flood Forecast and Warning System (FFWS), which will be developed and managed by the Irrigation Department
           as a key non-structural resilience measure.

       16. Subcomponent 1.1 – Flood Protection Infrastructure: This subcomponent will finance the construction, and
           rehabilitation of critical infrastructure to mitigate riverine floods, flash floods, and GLOFs in high-risk areas of KP.
           Investments under this subcomponent will include a combination of flood protection infrastructure—such as
           embankments, flood carrier channels, spurs, retaining walls, revetments, and gabion works —and check dams10
           to attenuate peak flows and reduce downstream impacts. A comprehensive list of candidate schemes are
           identified upfront in coordination with the KP Irrigation Department and initial prioritization is currently
           underway based on criticality and readiness, including technical and safeguards requirements. Nature-based
           solutions will be considered on a site-specific basis to replace or complement grey infrastructural interventions
           where possible.

       17. Subcomponent 1.2 - Telemetry and Flood Forecasting: This subcomponent will establish a province-wide Flood
           Forecast and Warning System (FFWS) to improve real-time data collection, transmission, and analysis. It will
           expand the existing telemetry and hydro-meteorological observation network, upgrade modeling and
           forecasting capacity, and strengthen protocols for information sharing between the Irrigation Department,
           PDMA, and national agencies such as PMD and NDMA. Investments will include modern monitoring equipment,
           data management systems, and training in hydrological modeling to generate reliable forecasts that directly
           inform disaster preparedness and early warning systems.

       18. Subcomponent 1.3 – Technical Assistance for Development of Long-Term Infrastructure Investment Plan: This
           subcomponent will finance the development of a province-wide hydrological model to guide long-term flood

19. Component 2 - Strengthening Institutional and Community Preparedness (estimated US$50 million): This
    component will enhance the capacity of provincial institutions and local communities in KP to prepare for and
    respond to disasters, with a particular focus on floods. It will strengthen the operational outreach and
    institutional capabilities of the Provincial Disaster Management Authority (PDMA) and Rescue 1122, improving
    the application of risk information in development planning and building community-level resilience. The
    component complements the infrastructure-focused investments under Component 1 and is designed to ensure
    that both institutional systems and social preparedness mechanisms are adequately equipped to manage and
    mitigate disaster risks.

20. Subcomponent 2.1 – Strengthening Disaster Preparedness and Management: This subcomponent will enhance
    the operational capacity of PDMA KP to lead multi-hazard disaster preparedness, response coordination, relief
    and recovery planning. Support will include modernization of provincial and district emergency operations
    center systems, refurbishment of warehouses, decision support tools, contingency planning protocols, and staff
    training. It will also facilitate PDMA’s integration with district disaster management authorities and other
    provincial entities during emergency situations. Community preparedness will also be enhanced through
    community-based disaster risk management (CBDRM) interventions and the establishment of early warning
    systems. The community volunteer network under the Relief, Rehabilitation and Settlement Department, will be
    strengthened through the provision of rescue equipment and specialized disaster response training.

21. Subcomponent 2.2 – Enhancing Emergency Response Systems: This subcomponent will support Rescue 1122 in
    expanding and strengthening its disaster response capabilities, with a focus on flood and climate-related
    emergencies. Support will be directed toward scaling the service’s geographical coverage through the
    establishment of additional rescue stations in underserved districts, improving operational logistics, and
    strengthening command-and-control systems. Investments will include provision of water rescue equipment,
    flood-specific personal protective gear, boats and vehicles for evacuation, and upgrading of communication and
    dispatch systems. Rescue 1122 staff will receive specialized training in disaster-related emergency medical
    response, swift water rescue, and community liaison, supported by standardized training modules. The
    subcomponent will also finance resources for establishing district-level warehouses and stockpiles of emergency
    supplies to ensure timely deployment.

22. Subcomponent 2.3 – Technical Assistance for Risk-Informed Planning and Preparedness: This subcomponent
    will provide technical assistance for improving the generation and use of risk information in government
    planning processes. Activities may include long-term strategic studies, hazard and vulnerability mapping,
    integration of risk data, including climate risk data, into spatial planning tools, preparation of spatial plans
    (master plans, strategic development plans, district land use plans), updating of building control standards, and
    the development of standard operating procedures for risk-informed development decisions. It will also support
    the institutionalization of disaster risk considerations in public investment processes.

23. Component 3 – Project Management (US$10 million). This component will support the Project Implementing

    cover project management, procurement, contract management, financial management, M&E (including project

 Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts



   25. Environmental and Social risks are considered as Substantial at the concept stage. These include those related to
       construction of medium to large scale flood mitigation and resilience building infrastructure. These will range
       from building check dams, reinforcing flood embankments, repairing floodwater drains and may evaluate
       possibilities for a large dam. Given KP is ecologically rich and sensitive, fragile and conflict zone, and disaster
       prone, and that the Project interventions will be implemented across the provinces, the risks are rated
       substantial. Risks include pollution and nuisance (dust, noise, visual degradation of landscapes, debris), other
       solid waste generation, and potential ground/surface water contamination in case where project interventions
       might be close to the water streams. Community safety concerns, possible land acquisition, OHS of labor and
       social exclusion are also anticipated especially in the conflict hit districts.
