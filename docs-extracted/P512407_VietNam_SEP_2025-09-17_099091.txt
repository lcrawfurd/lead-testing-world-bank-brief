1.       INTRODUCTION
1.1. Objectives of the project
1.    The overall objective of the project is to invest in infrastructure, enhance the efficiency and
management capacity of exploitation and aquaculture, and increase the value of aquatic products;
thereby contributing to promoting sustainable growth, adapting to climate change, improving local
people's lives, and deeply integrating into international communities.
2.       Specific objectives of the project are:
     •   To invest in fishing infrastructure, including constructing a storm shelter for fishing vessels;
         creating momentum, enhancing efficiency, and reducing post-harvest losses of fishing
         activities.
     •   To upgrade and complete aquaculture infrastructure.
     •   To build infrastructure and provide equipment for ethnic minority communities involved in
         aquaculture and fishing; contribute to effectively and sustainably protecting fisheries resources,
         increased income, and livelihood stabilization.
     •   To improve management and production organization capacity for combatting illegal,
         unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing and sustainably managing fisheries resources.
     •   To reduce post-harvest losses, decrease disease outbreaks, improve shrimp productivity,
         quality, and value; and ensure food safety, environmental protection, and climate change
         adaptation.
     •   To improve fisheries management capacity.

1.2. Project locations
3.   The project is implemented in the aquaculture areas of 14 communes/wards (24 communes and
wards before the merger) in Soc Trang province (now Can Tho city), including An Thanh Nam, An

Commune (now Khanh Hoa Ward), Vinh Tan Commune, Vinh Phuoc Ward (still named Vinh Phuoc

Hoi Thuong), Lieu Tu Commune, Vien Binh Commune (now Lieu Tu Commune), Thanh Thoi Thuan

1.4. Project Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
1.4.1. Positive impacts
28. The benefits of the project are long-term rather than short-term. If the endeavor is successful,
it should not only benefit the current number of fishermen, aquaculture farmers, and service providers
involved in the fishing and shrimp farming in the project area, but also make a significant contribution
to the overall sustainable development of the national fishery industry as follow:
 •   By taking resilience to climate change and extreme weather events into the design process of
     the project items, especially the storm shelter, it is possible to increase its lifespan and decrease
     the operation and maintenance (O&M) cost.
 •   The storm shelter construction will help commercial fishing vessels in Can Tho city and
     neighboring areas anchor safely during storms, reducing fishing costs and increasing value-
     added products by shortening distances between ports and fishing grounds. This will increase
     income and improve the nutritional value of aquatic products.
 •   Fishermen will be able to use the fishing facilities that have better sanitation and weather
     conditions. This lowers the occupational health risks, improves the quality and value of fish
     production, and lowers physical losses by making sure fish are caught on time and stored
     properly.
 •   Improved waste management in the storm shelter and aquaculture farms reduces water pollution
     and sediment discharge into water bodies. As a result, in the long run, the project should help
     to improve the water quality of the project area.
 •   Implementing Good Agriculture Practices (GAP) and Integrated Pest Management (IPM)
     principles, along with the introduction of bio-security practices and disease-free seeds, can
     foster an environment that is less conducive to pests and diseases in aquaculture ponds. This,
     in turn, can reduce the reliance on antibiotics and chemicals in aquaculture, safeguard the
     environment, mitigate losses caused by disease outbreaks, enhance food security, and improve
     occupational health and safety for farming-based laborers.
 •   Increased productivity of shrimp aquaculture areas will help farmers expand their shrimp
     farming adapting to the challenges of rising sea level.
 •   The project will also generate environmental and social benefits through a variety of other
     mechanisms, including (i) improved awareness and concern for environmental and social issues
     on the part of beneficiaries, local communities and districts; (ii) increasing the availability of

1.4.2. Negative impacts and risks
29. The implementation of the project will cause potential negative environmental and social
impacts and risks during pre-construction, construction and operational phases, as follows:
30. During pre-construction phase: Impacts and risks during pre-construction phase are as
follows:
 •   Safety risk relating to UXO. Vietnam in general and the project area in particular undergone
     civil wars which were ended in 1975. Although the land has been used in the last 45 years since
     the war ended, some UXOs may remain underground. If disturbed by construction activities
     such as site clearance, ground leveling and excavation, the UXO may pose hazard to the
     workers, damage to construction works.
 •   Land acquisition. The implementation of the project will unavoidably involve land acquisition
     (both permanent and temporary) in the project area. This has a negative impact on the lives,
     incomes and livelihoods of affected people, especially those severely affected (relocation,
     significant loss of productive land and/or business activities/incomes, and vulnerable groups).
 •   Impacts on income/livelihood: Local residents and affected parties are often involved in small-
     business activities and/or engage in fisheries and aquaculture activities where they live,
     therefore, relocation of affected households may result in loss of customers if affected
     households are relocated or loss of their existing sources of income/livelihood too far from their
     current location and place of business.
31. These impacts and risks can be mitigated by applying the approved resettlement plan (RP) of
the project and the project owner will contract with relevant unit to clear UXOs before commencing
construction activities, and must closely monitor and consult with local people about the land
acquisition and clearance and UXO clearance.
32. During construction phase: Constructing infrastructure would involve activities such as site
clearance, ground leveling, demolishing the old structures, construction of worker camps and site
offices, gathering materials and machines at working sites, soil excavation, transportation the
construction materials to construction sites, preparation of construction materials such as pile casting
and concrete mixing, waste disposal, structure construction, equipment installation for finalizing
works, etc. These activities would take place in the land area. For the storm shelter, construction of
bridges, canal dredging, construction activities will also take place in water. These include dredging,
piling activities, construction of embankments, etc. Environmental and social impacts and risks of
these construction activities are summarized below.
 •   Common construction related impacts and risk: These include increased level of dust, gases
     emission, noise, vibration, loss of vegetation cover and trees, waste and wastewater generation,
     water quality reduction affecting aquatic lives, soil erosion sedimentation and localized

36. The objective of this stakeholder engagement plan (SEP) is to define a program for stakeholder

consultation, throughout the implementation and operation of the project. It specifies how the Can
Tho city Project Management Unit (PMU) and contractors will communicate with stakeholders, as
well as a mechanism for people to express concerns, provide feedback, or file complaints about the
project, and contractors. Consultation and engagement with stakeholders are critical to ensure smooth
collaboration between the project and local communities, as well as to minimize and mitigate
environmental and social risks and impacts of the project, thereby contributing to the project's
success.

38. Project stakeholders are people and entities who have a role in the project, or could be affected

groups or other entities who:
 (i)     are impacted or likely to be impacted directly or indirectly, positively or adversely, by the
         project (also known as “affected parties”); and
 (ii)    may have an interest in the project (“interested parties”). They include individuals or groups
         whose interests may be affected by the project and who have the potential to influence the
         project outcomes in any way.
39. Cooperation and negotiation with the stakeholders throughout the project development often
also requires the identification of people within the groups who act as legitimate representatives of
their respective stakeholder group, i.e., the individuals who have been entrusted by their fellow group
members by advocating the groups’ interests in the process of engagement with the project.
Community representatives may provide helpful insight into the local settings and act as main
conduits for dissemination of the project-related information and as a primary communication/liaison
link between the project and targeted communities and their established networks. Verification of
stakeholder representatives (i.e., the process of confirming that they are legitimate and genuine
advocates of the community they represent) remains an important task in establishing contact with
the community stakeholders. Legitimacy of the community representatives can be verified by talking
informally to a random sample of community members and heeding their views on who can be
representing their interests in the most effective way.

3.5. Disadvantaged/Vulnerable Individuals and Groups
54. Disadvantaged/vulnerable individuals and groups may be affected disproportionately - either
adversely or positively, by the project. Due to low socioeconomic profile, their voices may not be
heard in the formal decision-making process or included in consultation process but their feedback
are not solicited in a manner that offers sufficient understanding of how they are affected, how they
would respond, and what support they may need to avoid or mitigate such impact – from their own
perspective. Identification of vulnerable individuals and groups, including those who are stigmatized,
is an important step to effectively engaging them in the consultation throughout the project cycle.

4.   STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PROGRAM

61. In order to achieve the objectives of the SEP, the project will develop an engagement program
with the following purposes:
 • Provide meaningful information in a format and language that is readily understandable.
 • Provide information in advance of consultation activities when possible.
 • Disseminate information in a manner and location easy for stakeholders to access.
 • Establish a two-way dialogue that allows the project and stakeholders to exchange views and
   information, and have issues heard and addressed.
 • Ensure inclusiveness in the representation of views, including those of women, the elderly,
   people living with a disability, and other vulnerable people as necessary.
 • Ensure any obstacles to participation that are identified are removed so that views of different
   stakeholders can be captured.
 • Ensure there are clear mechanisms for responding to people’s concerns, suggestions, and/or
   grievances.
 • Incorporate feedback into the project, and report back to stakeholders.
 • Monitor stakeholder engagement activities and include project stakeholders in monitoring to the
   extent possible.
 • Incorporate stakeholder engagement as part of the project management responsibilities and
   ensure staff in the PMU and its consultants are equipped with specific responsibilities and
   budget.

4.1. Summary of Stakeholder Engagement during Project Preparation
4.1.1. During the preparation of the project Stakeholder Engagement Framework
62. Numerous meetings were held by the Central Project Management Board (CPMU) at 12 project
provinces in May and June 2020, as part of the Stakeholder Engagement Framework preparation for
the Sustainable Fisheries Development Project. The consultation meeting with Soc Trang province
(now Can Tho City) was held on June 09, 2020.
63. The purpose of the consultation was to familiarize stakeholders with the project's scope and to
determine acceptable communication and consultation procedures throughout the project lifetime. In
addition, The E&S documents developed for the project, such as the ESMF, RPF, EMPF, SEF, LMP,
and ESCP, were also consulted in order to obtain feedback from participants for future improvements.
64. Participants were given general information about the project (objectives, components, project
footprint, potential risks and impacts, financing source, implementation arrangement) during the
consultation. The new applicable World Bank ESF, as well as its E&S standards and criteria, were
also introduced to the participants.
65. The frameworks are no longer applicable due to changes in project implementation
arrangements and design. However, the outcomes of previous consultations have been incorporated
into the overall stakeholder engagement process. This stakeholder engagement plan reflects and takes
into account all relevant feedback from those consultations.

4.3. Strategy for Consultation
69. The project will apply a participatory and consultative approach involving meaningful
engagement with different stakeholders. Project consultations will be a two-way process of dialogue
between the project and its stakeholders, including on issues such as project objectives; working
conditions for contractors; engagement of women; ways to mitigate potential project impacts, and
risks in particular to vulnerable groups. This will depend on the stakeholders as well as the project
timing. For this project, consultation strategies will be proposed for each stage of the project, details
are as follows:
4.3.1. Consultation strategies
70. Project preparation (before project appraisal). Since the details of some proposed project
investments are unknown at this stage, consultations during project preparation will focus on a
broader range of stakeholders to understand their general interests and concerns about the project and
its potential positive and negative impacts and inform project design. This is also the stage where
potential vulnerable/disadvantaged groups relevant to the project will be identified.
71. Project implementation. At this stage, the details of proposed project components and specific
project-affected stakeholders will be determined. The consultation will focus more specifically on
people who are impacted by the project, especially those adversely impacted, to address their
concerns through the implementation of project-specific E&S plans. In addition, the project will
ensure that the GRM is disseminated and active to promptly address any grievances. Special attention
will be paid to vulnerable/disadvantaged groups to ensure inclusion, non-discrimination, and access
to project benefits. Consultations will also focus on job opportunities, specific action plans, etc.
72. Project operation. At this stage, the consultation will focus on feedback from stakeholders,
particularly affected ones, on the performance of E&S impact and risk mitigations, results of
grievance resolution conducted during the implementation stage as well as general project operations,
including implementation of the storm shelter operation. The consultation on employment
opportunities for operation and maintenance (O&M) of the project works will be also conducted.
Also, areas for improvement and lessons learned should be identified for the project. Furthermore,
dissemination and reporting back to stakeholders on the outcome of the monitoring and evaluation
of the project will be also arranged.
4.3.2. Consultation methods
73. The methods used to conduct consultations will have a direct impact on the outcome of the
project consultation strategy. Therefore, these methods should be designed to account for the diverse
characteristics of various stakeholder groups. The following consultation methods will be used for
this project:
74. National consultations. National consultations are particularly useful to target government
representatives, NGOs, and other groups who have an interest in the project and may also have the
ability to influence it. These types of consultations are also useful at the project design stage, and
annually to inform stakeholders of a project’s progress. National consultations may be conducted
face-to-face or virtual in a meeting format and may involve break-out groups or other methods to
ensure there is ample time for group discussion and questions and answers.



                                                                                              49 | P a g e
Soc Trang Sustainable Fisheries Development Project
STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN

75. Local consultations. Local consultations are the main way to reach project affected
stakeholders and will involve face-to-face group meetings. In some cases, one-on-one consultations
or FGD may also be carried out, especially to reach and capture the views of vulnerable or
disadvantaged groups. Particular emphasis should be placed on seeking out the voice of women and
vulnerable groups, such as through female only FGDs. In the case of women, the project will ensure
consultation activities are conducted at a time that is convenient to them and that activities such as
FGDs are run by women if possible (particularly for sensitive topics like health or sexual issues).
76. One important way to engage stakeholders will be through training on important topics related
to the project such as gender, the project’s GRM, labor rights (for both men and women), child labor,
HIV/AIDS and STDs, gender-based violence, sexual harassment, and sexual exploitation and abuse.
Since some of these topics are quite sensitive, particular measures may need to be in place, such as
ensuring some training courses are done in female-only or male-only groups, or that they are
facilitated by men or women, as appropriate. Training may be delivered by Can Tho City PMU/its
consultants.
77. Accessibility issues will also be taken into account, in particular, to ensure people with a
disability can be part of consultations, as well as the specific needs of other groups such as the elderly,
and poor groups.
78. Consultations with project workers. The project will strive to ensure that unskilled workers
come from the community as much as possible, to minimize the influx of labor. Consultations will
encourage local workers to apply for available jobs, including EMs, women and vulnerable or
disadvantaged groups. Contractors and their staff will need to be well consulted and trained in
sensitive issues, including respectful behavior, Codes of Conduct, GBV (including SEA and SH),
HIV/AIDS and STDs, and worker’s GRM, LMP. They also need to be well aware of construction
needs and schedules. Training, on-site interviews, and regular feedback with Can Tho City PMU
and/or CSC will be used as methods of consultation with them.

4.4. Stakeholder Engagement Methods to be Used in the Next Stages
81. Public/community meetings. The Can Tho City PMU will organize meetings in each project
commune with leaders, PAPs, and other interested parties. Meetings will be open-housing events
where the PMU will present all project information and people will be invited to make comments
and express any concerns. A range of consultations with different stakeholders will be conducted and
documented, and community level meetings will also be conducted with men and women (including
vulnerable households) to better understand needs, expectations, and concerns of these population
groups in relation to the project. The feedback received at these meetings will be documented along
with measures that the project will take to address the feedback received. Meetings in each commune
will also be organized on a quarterly basis.
82. Communication materials. Written information will be disclosed to the public via a variety
of communication materials including leaflets, posters, etc. The PMU will also update its website
regularly (at least on a quarterly basis) with key project updates and reports on the project’s
performance in Vietnamese. The website will also provide information about the grievance
mechanism for the project.
83. Training, workshops. Training in a variety of E&S issues will be provided to the PMU, and
possibly other relevant government or non-government service providers. Issues covered will include
sensitization to inclusion/exclusion, labor issues, gender-based violence risks.
84. Grievance Redress Mechanism. In compliance with the World Bank’s ESS10, a project-
specific grievance mechanism will be maintained to handle complaints and issues. This will be
integrated into a country system on GRM that is available to citizens. Dedicated communication
materials (specifically, a GRM brochure) will be developed to help local residents become familiar
with the grievance redress channels and procedures. The PMU will coordinate with administrative
authorities to maintain a grievance register in order to capture and track grievances from submission
to resolution and communication with complainants.
85. The initial effort to resolve grievances to the complainant’s satisfaction will be undertaken by
administrative levels. These will be details in Section 7 below. Grievances may be submitted
anonymously, but in that case, it cannot be known if the complainant is satisfied with the resolution.
The PMU’s website, posters, and leaflets will include clear information on how feedback, questions,
comments, concerns, and grievances can be submitted by any stakeholder. It will also provide
information on the way grievances will be handled, both in terms of process and deadlines.

6.   GRIEVANCE REDRESS MECHANISM
98. The grievance mechanism seeks to resolve concerns promptly, using an understandable process
that is culturally appropriate and readily accessible at no cost. Grievances can be submitted if
someone believes the project is having a detrimental impact on the community, the environment, or
their quality of life. Stakeholders may also submit their comments and suggestions on the project.
99. The Can Tho City PMU’s ESO will be responsible for overseeing those grievances that are
received and resolved in a fair, objective, and constructive manner all concerns or complaints raised
by project stakeholders. The key principles of the grievance mechanism are to ensure that:
 •   The basic rights and interests of affected people are protected.
 •   The concerns of affected people arising from the project implementation process are adequately
     addressed.
 •   Entitlements or livelihood support for affected people are provided on time and in accordance
     with project documents, such as ESMP, EMDP, RP.
 •   Affected people are aware of their rights to access grievance procedures free of charge for the
     above purposes.
100. Based on the SEP, wherever possible, the project team shall seek to resolve complaints as soon
as possible and thus avoid the escalation of issues. GRM shall not impede access to the country’s
judicial or administrative remedies. Stakeholders can approach a court of law at any time and
independent of the project-level grievance redress process. Along with the World Bank requirements
based on ESS10, the GRM will be in line with the Vietnamese national legislation, in particular the
Law on Complaints (2011) and the Law on Denunciation (2018).
101. The Can Tho City PMU shall establish the GRM. The PMU shall appoint a GRM focal point
to collaborate with local authorities in handling project-related grievances. The design of the GRM
shall take into consideration the views of affected stakeholders and what is considered an access
mechanism. The PMU shall be required to disclose and explain to all stakeholders, in particular,
affected stakeholders and vulnerable and disadvantaged ones, the procedure for complaints filing as
early as possible and regularly throughout the project cycle. The grievance mechanism shall be

available at no cost, and it is important that it be easily accessible, with special attention paid to
accessibility for disadvantaged and vulnerable individuals or groups.
102. The PMU will ensure that grievances are registered, tracked, and resolved in line with
stipulated timeframes.
103. The GRM officer will be responsible for:
 •   Developing and publicizing grievance management procedures.
 •   Receiving, reviewing, and keeping track of grievances (as applicable).
 •   Ensuring grievances are settled within the agreed time periods.

6.1. All-functional Local Level Procedures

 •   The first level – W/CPC. An affected stakeholder is to take his/her complaint to any member
     of the W/CPC, through the village/cluster head, or directly to W/CPC, in written or oral form.
     The W/CPC will work directly with the complainant to resolve the complaint and will issue a
     resolution within 15 working days after receiving the complaint. The secretariat of the W/CPC
     is responsible for documenting and recording all the complaints that it is handling. Once the
     W/CPC issues its decision, the stakeholder can appeal to the City PC within 30 working days
     if they are not satisfied with the decision or after 15 working days if they have not heard back
     from the W/CPC
 •   The second level – City PC. Upon receiving a complaint from a stakeholder, the City PC will
     have 30 working days to resolve the case. The City PC is responsible for filing and storing
     documents for all complaints that it handles. After the City PC has issued a decision, the
     stakeholder can appeal to the people’s court within 45 working days if they are not satisfied
     with the decision or after 30 working days if they have not heard back from the City PC.
 •   The final level - Court of Law Decides: In case a stakeholder brings his/her case to the City
     people’s court and the court rules in favor of the complainant. The decision ruling the settlement
     of complaints will have to be sent to complainants and concerned parties and shall be publicly
     posted at the headquarters of the People's Committee of the relevant level. The complainant
     will receive such ruling three days after the result of complaint resolution at the commune level
     has been decided upon and 7 working days at the provincial level.

source, and date of receipt of the complaint, the name, and address of the complainant, action plan,
and current status (see Annex 1), including complaints both received orally and in writing. If needed,
the GRM Officer will be able to contact complainants directly and guide them through the official
GRM channels for the project.

6.3. Procedures in Cases of Gender-Based Violence/Sexual exploitation and abuse/Sexual
     harassment
109. To avoid the risk of stigmatization, exacerbation of the mental/psychological harm, or potential
reprisal, the GRM shall have a different and sensitive approach to GBV/SEA/SH-related cases.
Where such a case is reported to the GRM, it should immediately be referred to appropriate service
providers, such as medical and psychological support, emergency accommodation, and/or any other
necessary services. It should also be reported to the PMU’s GRM Officer, who can advise on relevant
service providers if needed. Data on GBV/SEA/SH cases should not be collected through the GRM
unless operators have been trained on the empathetic, non-judgmental, and confidential collection of
these complaints. Only the nature of the complaint (what the complainant says in her/his own words)
and additional demographic data, such as age and gender, can be collected as usual.
110. In addition, capacity-building for GRM committees on handling GBV/SEA/SH-related
grievances and awareness campaigns about the GRM for communities will be conducted during the
project implementation.

6.4. Procedures in cases of conflicts among ethnic groups
111. In the Project, addressing potential conflicts between the Chinese and Khmer ethnic groups is
critical to fostering collaboration and ensuring project success. The project will establish a Grievance
Redress Committee (GRC) composed of equal representation from the Hoa and Khmer communities,
including community elders, local government representatives, and religious leaders from both
groups. This committee will meet biweekly to review grievances and ensure timely resolution.
112. When a conflict arises, the GRC will appoint a neutral mediator, agreed upon by both parties,
to facilitate a resolution. The mediation process will take place in a neutral and culturally significant
location, such as the community center, and will prioritize open dialogue to understand each group’s
concerns. Decisions will be documented and shared transparently with both groups to maintain trust.
113. To protect the privacy of complainants, the GRC will ensure confidentiality throughout the
process. Additionally, regular workshops will be held to educate both communities about the
grievance process, emphasizing its fairness and neutrality. Progress and outcomes will be monitored,
and lessons learned will be incorporated into the GRM to continually improve its effectiveness. This
mechanism will ensure conflicts are resolved respectfully, fostering harmony between the Chinese
and Khmer communities while supporting the project’s objectives.

6.5. Procedures in Cases of Labors’ Concerns
115. Project workers can submit their complaints (related to employment opportunities, wages,
payment, working conditions, health, safety, administrative acts and decisions, discipline decisions,
etc.) in writing, or face-to-face verbally – except for complaints against discipline decisions to their
employer/contractor who are responsible for solving first-time complaint as outlined in the GRM for
project workers in the Project LMP. Complainants can also lodge a grievance through the PMU using
email, post, or telephone or PMU dedicated channels for GRM. Complainant can bring their case to
the court of law at any stage during the complaint resolution process (See the project LMP for
details).

6.6. Procedures in Cases of Land Acquisition
116. Complaint concerning land acquisition, physical resettlement, and relevant issues are expected
to be lodged through CPCs. Submission of complaints can be done using emails, postal, hand
delivery, or verbally through direct meetings or telephone. Within 10 days from the date of receiving
the complaint, the person in charge of complaint resolution shall accept the complaint and notify the
complainant in writing. The time limit for complaint resolution – from the date of acceptance, is 30
days for first-time complaints, and 45 days for the second time and third-time complaints. The statute
of limitation (for initiating a lawsuit) is 30 days from the date the time-limit for complaint resolution
expires, or from the date the complainant receives the complaint resolution decision. In a remote area
where travel is difficult, the statute of limitation can be extended but not exceeding 45 days. The
complainant can also initiate a lawsuit at any stage of the grievance resolution process if they disagree
with the complaint resolution decision, or if the case is not resolved following the expiry of the time-
limit for complaint resolution (see the project RP for details).

7.   MONITORING AND REPORTING

117. The SEP will be periodically revised and updated as necessary in the course of project
implementation to ensure that the information presented is consistent, and up to date, and that the
identified methods of engagement remain appropriate and effective concerning the project context
and specific phases of project development. Any major changes to project-related activities and its
schedule will be duly reflected in the updated SEP.
118. Monthly summaries and internal reports on public grievances, together with the status of
implementation of associated corrective actions will be collated by responsible staff and referred to
the PMU’s Director. The monthly summaries will provide a mechanism for assessing both the
number and the nature of complaints and requests for information, along with the project’s ability to
address those in a timely and satisfactory manner. The project will make public quarterly project
monitoring reports on its website [https://sonnptnt.soctrang.gov.vn/], which will include reporting on
the SEP and grievance redress.

7.1. Internal monitoring
119. The objective of internal monitoring relating to the SEP is to oversee its implementation to
ensure targeted consultations and disclosure activities are taking place. Internal Monitoring will be
led by the PMU’s ESO, under the overall guidance of the PMU’s Director. The involvement of local
communities in monitoring activities should be encouraged whenever possible. Internal Monitoring
will consist of quarterly reports during the implementation of the project, part of the ESMP or RP
monitoring, including public grievances, inquiries, and related incidents, together with the status of
implementation of associated corrective/preventative actions will be gathered by the ESO and
reported to the PMU’s Director.
120. Monitoring SEP implementation should focus on:
 •   Level of understanding of the project,
 •   Community feedback incorporated into project design and planning,
 •   Knowledge by stakeholders of project documents, including the GRM and impact mitigation
     plans,
 •   Implementation of disclosure and consultation activities as per the SEP,
 •   Main grievances and efficacy of GRM,
 •   Overall community satisfaction,
 •   Minutes of consultation meetings,
 •   Number of staff working on the stakeholder engagement, including at the PMU and its
     consultants or contractors, and
 •   Plans for the next month and long-term plans.
121. Monitoring specific to land acquisition activities will be conducted as per the RP.

7.2. Third Party Monitoring
122. The PMU will engage an IESMC as a third party to assist the PMU in monitoring and
evaluation of the implementation of E&S risk and impact management and mitigation measures,
including compensation and resettlement.
123. This consultant will undertake periodic monitoring every six months on the implementation of
E&S risk and impact management in compliance with regulations set forth in the project’s
ESIA/ESMP, RP, SEP, EMDP and LMP. They will also monitor closely the implementation of
project’s GRM.
124. Key tasks of the consultant related to SEP will be:
 •   Adequacy of stakeholder engagement, including activities, staffing, and budget.
 •   Review of grievances submitted, time to respond, resolution of grievances, and complainant’s
     satisfaction with the process.
 •   Interview of sample households to assess satisfaction with stakeholder engagement and
     knowledge of the project and related programs.

7.3. Community Monitoring
125. As per Decree 84/2015/ND-CP dated 30 September 2015 regarding investment supervision and
assessment, local people can establish a community investment supervision board to conduct
environmental, social, and construction safety supervision voluntarily. During project
implementation, communities that benefit from the project will be encouraged to form a local team
by this Decree to monitor and provide feedback on construction-related issues.

7.4. Reporting back to stakeholders
126. The method of reporting back to stakeholders will depend on the stakeholder itself, as follows:
 •   Regarding community concerns, the project will report back to the community and other
     relevant stakeholder groups, primarily through public meetings and/or public disclosure which
     people of the community can easily access such as the community’s common house or
     communal office. The feedback report will be shared or disclosed to the concerned community
     and other stakeholders within the timeframe set up in the stakeholder engagement program.
 •   Regarding individual concerns, including grievances related to E&S issues such as inaccurate
     measurement of affected land area, undervalued compensation rate, or request for additional
     support for livelihood restoration, the PMU, and local implementing agencies will report back
     individually, via the same channel through which the grievances were received, for easy follow-
     up of the complainant. Feedback received through the GRM will be responded to in writing and
     sent directly to the complainant via postal mail.
 •   Regarding comments of stakeholders both affected and concerned parties on the project design,
     E&S documents, they will be addressed in relevant documents and posted on the websites of
     the PMU and PPC and also posted locally on the public notice board at CPC’s office. Comments
     and questions submitted in association with specific documents disclosed on the above websites
     will be reviewed and responded to promptly as regulated in the EMDP, ESCP, SEP, ESMP,
     RP, and LMP.
 •   Regarding comments of national-level stakeholders, an email and/or official letter will be sent
     after workshops on how comments/suggestions were taken into account and/or to inform them
     of documents that are publicly available.
127. In addition, project documents, including monthly, quarterly, or annual reports will be updated
and uploaded as needed, including this SEP, and available on the project website at:
https://cantho.gov.vn. During consultation activities, stakeholders will also be reminded that these
documents are publicly available, as well as the project’s GRM for lodging their concerns,
grievances, and complaints related to the project implementation, including the implementation of
E&S risk and impact management and mitigation measures via public meeting, consultation and
feedback reports.
