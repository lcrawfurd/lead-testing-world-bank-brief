                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     Agriculture is central to Nepal’s economy and integral to poverty reduction. It contributed to over 60 percent of
                     employment and over 21 percent of GDP in 2019. Despite its importance, the sector has seen low investment, resulting
                     in low productivity, higher food insecurity, and a widening trade imbalance. The lack of year-round irrigation and
                     climate-smart agricultural services are significant factors hindering productivity growth. Currently, less than half of net
                     irrigable land (about 1.1 million ha) is irrigated—and only 40 percent of which has access to year-round irrigation.
                     Consequently, Nepal’s agriculture productivity remains vulnerable to seasonal and annual climate variabilities and

                     shocks. The direct economic cost of climate vulnerability in the agriculture sector in 2020 was 1.5-2 percent of GDP and
                     the human costs may be even higher – 4.6 million Nepali’s were food insecure as of 2016. To address these challenges,
                     the Government of Nepal (GoN) has developed plans to provide year-round irrigation services throughout the country.
                     The Rani Jamara Kulariya Irrigation Project (RJKIP) is one of the GoN’s national pride projects, strategically investing in
                     the far western provinces where the need is the highest. RJKIP has benefited from phased World Bank support since
                     2012. The Bank supported both Phase 1 (2012-2017) and Phase 2 (2018-2025) of RJKIP, which modernized irrigation
                     infrastructure on 14,300 hectares, enhanced flood and erosion control, improved rural roads and bridges for better
                     connectivity and market access, and promoted watershed conservation, capacity building of water user associations,
                     and agricultural support services. These efforts have benefited over 157,000 people. Building on Phases 1 and 2, Phase 3
                     of RJKIP (2025-2031) aims to further improve the irrigation services, increase farm productivity, enhance climate
                     resilience, and boost rural incomes by scaling up the irrigation command area of an additional 17,500 hectares,
                     incorporating climate resilience measures such as multipurpose storage and deepening agricultural support services and
                     value chain development. Phase 3 will be aligned with Nepal’s GRID approach to ensure the sustainability and resilience
                     of irrigation schemes and livelihoods, supported by appropriate watershed management. The Project Development
                     Objective (PDO) continues to focus on enhancing irrigation services, agricultural productivity, and climate resilience. It
                     addresses three of the World Bank’s priority global challenges: i) Water security and access; ii) Climate change
                     adaptation and mitigation; and (iii) Food and Nutrition Security. The project consists of four key components aimed at
                     enhancing irrigation, watershed management, and agricultural support services in the region. Component 1: Irrigation
                     Improvement and Development (US$95 million) seeks to expand and improve the irrigation network in Pathariya to
                     distribute part of the existing intake discharge of 80 m³/s drawn at Chisapani from Karnali River. The main canal
                     bifurcates to supply the allotted 40 m³/s to the RJK area and the remaining 40 m³/s to Lamki and Pathariya. . This

                     the cropping intensity to 226 percent from the current 150 percent. Component 2: Watershed Management (US$20

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The project will be implemented in the Kailali District, which is characterized by flat terrain with elevations ranging from
                     100 to 200 meters and an average gradient of 1 in 700 towards the south. This region lies in a tropical zone known for
                     its fertile land. The area experiences maximum temperatures of up to 43°C in summer and minimum temperatures as
                     low as 5°C in winter. The average annual rainfall is 1641 mm, primarily occurring from June to September.

                     Although the region is predominantly agricultural, it also features tropical forests with dominant plant species such as
                     Sal (Shorea robusta) and Khayer-Sissoo (Acacia-Dalbergia). The area is classified as having low seismicity. The air and
                     water qualities are relatively unpolluted due to the absence of industrial activities.

                     The Pathariya Extension irrigation command area is bounded by the main canal from the Karnali River, the Mohana and
                     Patharaiya Rivers, and the Kandra Khola. Numerous ponds and lakes within this area are used for fisheries and
                     traditional irrigation by the local population. The project will be implemented in areas under traditional irrigation



Public Disclosure
                     schemes.

                     The nearest protected areas are Bardia National Park, located 25 km to the east, and Dudhwa Nature Park in India,
                     situated 7.4 km to the south. The irrigation scheme surrounds the Basanta Biological Corridor, a wildlife passage. This
                     corridor is used for the movement and genetic exchange of wildlife species such as Bengal tigers, Asian elephants,
                     leopards, sloth bears, various deer species, gaur, and numerous bird species. It connects Shuklaphanta and Bardia
                     National Parks. Only already existing agricultural areas will be included in the project.

                     The Environmental Impact Assessment (national EIA) for Component 1 indicates that total 36.9 hectares of forest land
                     will be affected out of which 24.92 hectares lies in 14 community forests. The project is currently considering
                     alternatives to minimize this impact by positioning the main canal alongside the Right of Way (RoW) of the existing East-
                     West Highway.

                     Under Component 2, the project aims to enhance existing surface water storage through two main approaches: a)
                     transferring flows and augmenting water levels, or b) capturing monsoon water to ensure the sustainability and
                     resilience of irrigation schemes and livelihoods.

                     Three lakes/reservoirs currently being considered for the study are Bagaulia Lake, Laukah Baukah Lake, and Koilahi Lake.
                     Besides irrigation, these lakes are also extensively used for fisheries and recreation. Additionally, there are 197 other
                     lakes utilized for irrigation purposes, and further selection will be made during project implementation. The project will
                     finance up to 10 lakes.

                     The project area has a diverse population, with the Indigenous Tharu community being the largest group at 48%.
                     Agriculture and farm wages are the main livelihoods, supplemented by off-farm income from seasonal migration. The
                     prevalent land tenancy systems include Battaya (sharecropping), Thekka (leased for cash or kind), and Bandhaki.

                     Landholdings are mostly small:
                     •      80.03% of households own between 0.1-2.0 hectares.
                     •      5.11% own between 2-10 hectares.
                     •      0.05% own more than 10 hectares.
                     •      11.91% own less than 0.1 hectares.
                     •      2.9% are landless.

                     Some landless populations have been occupying government lands for years. This is crucial for the Resettlement Action
                     Plan to ensure their enumeration and entitlement if impacted.

                     The Phase 2 social assessment report highlights significant gaps between men and women in land ownership, with
                     variations across caste/ethnic backgrounds, household heads, and poverty levels.

                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                    Substantial
                     The environmental risk is classified as substantial based on the potential impacts identified, the scale, and the nature
                     of the activities involved. Key risks associated with developing irrigation schemes and water storage facilities include
                     deforestation, habitat fragmentation, biodiversity loss, pollution, and occupational health and safety (OHS) concerns.
                     Specifically, the project involves the clear felling of 3,900 trees and the construction of irrigation canals and reservoirs
                     near wildlife habitats, which may disrupt migratory routes and local biodiversity. Additionally, the construction and
                     operation of irrigation systems and water storages may lead to issues such as siltation and flooding. The water
                     storage facilities also present risks associated with dam safety. During the construction phase, significant civil works
                     will be involved, including the construction and rehabilitation of canals, distribution networks, and water storage
                     facilities. Key environmental risks and impacts during this phase include air and noise pollution, water contamination,
                     soil erosion, waste generation, and biodiversity disruption. The project will also require substantial resource
                     materials, adding to the environmental impact. The project poses risks to the health and safety of workers and nearby
                     communities during both construction and operation phases. During the operation phase, the project will continue to
                     pose environmental risks, although these are generally more manageable. Key risks and impacts include water
                     resource depletion, water quality issues, flooding, dam safety, biodiversity disruption, and community health and
                     safety concerns. The operation of irrigation systems may lead to overexploitation affecting water availability. Canals
                     can disrupt wildlife migration and serve as death traps for animals seeking water. The operation of these facilities
                     poses ongoing risks to the health and safety of workers and nearby communities.

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                               Relevant

                      The preliminary Dam Safety Study (Risk Assessment) was conducted on three sample lakes: Koilahi Lake, Bagauliya
                      Lake, and Lauka Bhauka Lake. The results indicate a high likelihood of dam failure across all three lakes. The study
                      classifies the initial dam safety risk as substantial, considering structural integrity, failure potential, and associated
                      consequences. The downstream area primarily consists of agricultural land and settlements, with houses typically
                      constructed along roads. While downstream rivers act as natural boundaries for floodwaters, the flat terrain
                      increases the risk of rapid breach flow expansion. This could lead to reduced water depth but spread of floodwaters.
                      However, the downstream consequences are considered moderate due to the nature of the terrain, depth of lakes
                      and the expected floodwater behavior. The assessment also indicated a low in-country capacity to deal with dam
                      safety risks. To address these risks, the project established mechanisms to enhance safety, structured around the
                      Dam Safety Capacity Building Plan presented in the Environmental and Social Scoping Study. The plan aims to
                      enhance the technical, operational, and managerial capabilities of the Borrower to ensure the sustainable operation
                      and safety of rehabilitated dam structures. Key measures include hiring independent technical experts to assess
                      designs and safety reports, developing comprehensive design guides and manuals tailored to local conditions, and
                      organizing training programs for implementing agencies and local communities. These initiatives will ensure
                      adherence to international standards and best practices in dam safety management, with a specific focus on
                      hydrological safety, flood control, and overflow management. To further support dam safety, the plan includes

                      extensive training for implementing agencies on monitoring, periodic inspections, and maintenance activities, as well
                      as emergency preparedness. Community-based training will empower local residents to participate in monitoring and
                      emergency response processes. Regular simulation drills will be conducted to familiarize all stakeholders with
                      evacuation plans and response procedures for various dam failure scenarios. Institutional strengthening efforts will
                      focus on equipping federal and municipal governments and Water Users Associations (WUAs) with the necessary
                      tools and knowledge to oversee dam safety and operations effectively. The plan also emphasizes the importance of
                      establishing a dam safety register to document the condition, maintenance history, and inspection reports for each
                      dam, facilitating ongoing safety assessments and future improvements. Regular condition assessments of lakes will
                      help identify potential risks and necessary remedial actions. Coordination among local authorities, federal agencies,
                      and communities will ensure clear responsibilities for operation, maintenance, and emergency preparedness.
                      Additionally, the preparation of operation and maintenance plans and emergency preparedness plans for each lake
                      will outline the organizational structure, staffing, technical expertise, and roles of responsible parties to manage dam
                      safety effectively. The use of heavy machinery, excavation, and material transportation during construction will
