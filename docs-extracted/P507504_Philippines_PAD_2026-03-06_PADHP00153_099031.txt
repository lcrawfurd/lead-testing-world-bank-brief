 I.       STRATEGIC CONTEXT


 A. Regional and Country Context

1.       The East Asia and Pacific (EAP) region, home to more than 2.1 billion people, is one of the fastest growing and
most dynamic regions in the world. Over recent decades, the region’s sustained economic growth has elevated hundreds
of millions out of poverty, with many nations advancing to upper-middle-income status. Despite expectations of a slower
pace in the future, EAP’s growth rate is projected to outstrip global averages.

2.      The agrifood system has contributed significantly to improving food security and livelihoods in the countries of
the EAP region. Agriculture contributes about 15 percent of the region’s gross domestic product (GDP) and accounts for
35 percent of employment—ranging from about 30 percent in Indonesia to over 80 percent in Papua New Guinea (PNG).
Smallholder farmers dominate the sector, accounting for over 60 percent of all farms, a majority of which are engaged in
subsistence-level rice cultivation. Food, mainly rice production, has more than doubled in the three decades between
1990 and 2020 in countries such as the Philippines, Viet Nam, Laos, and Cambodia, underpinning food security. Between
1990–1992 and 2017–2019, undernutrition rates declined from 20 percent to 8 percent in Indonesia, from 21 percent to
13 percent in the Philippines, from 45 percent to 7 percent in Viet Nam, and from 39 percent to 14 percent in Cambodia.
Agricultural development also contributed to a dramatic decline in rural poverty in the East Asia region, from over 60
percent in the 1990s to below 10 percent by 2020.

3.       Strong historical contribution of the agrifood system notwithstanding, there are persistent weaknesses and
emerging challenges that need to be addressed to modernize and strengthen the region’s agrifood system. These
include:
      •     Low yields. Agricultural yield growth rates have declined over the decades (from above 2 percent per year
            between 1970–1990 to averaging 1.9 percent in the 1990s, 1.3 percent in the 2000s, and 1 percent in the 2010s),
            reducing potential supplies and putting pressure on food prices, thus undermining food affordability. The
            slowdown is due to diminishing returns to input use, land degradation, climate change, and intensifying structural
            constraints, such as small and fragmented land sizes. Across the main rice economies of East Asia, yields realized
            by farmers are between one-third to one-fourth less than the potential yields achievable in those agronomic
            conditions. The situation is similar in the Pacific Island nations; in PNG, for example, yield gaps for major crops,
            such as coconuts, are between one-third to one-fourth of what could be achieved.
      •     Stagnant smallholder farm incomes. Related to the above, agricultural incomes are below the national average
            and have been growing more slowly, making farming increasingly unattractive. For instance, between 1990 and
            2020, the average annual growth rate for agriculture value added compared to the annual growth rate of GDP
            was as follows: Indonesia (3.2 percent versus 5.1 percent), Laos (4 percent versus 6.5 percent), the Philippines (2
            percent versus 4.7 percent), and Viet Nam (4 percent versus 6.8 percent). As such, small agricultural households
            typically fall in the poor category.
      •     High environmental footprint. Agricultural growth in the region has been driven more by intensive application of
            inputs (fertilizer, water, and other chemicals) than by gains in total factor productivity (that is, from more efficient

          use of resources). This ‘growth model’ has led to soil, water, and air pollution, imposing rising environmental
          costs1 and eroding future production capacity.
   •      Limited adaptation to climate change. The EAP agrifood systems are highly vulnerable to climate change. Floods,
          droughts, and other extreme climate events have historically resulted in significant losses in agricultural GDP. In
          the Pacific Islands, rising sea levels pose additional severe risks to food production and water supply, given that
          these islands have limited land, fragile soils, and economies heavily dependent on farming, so that even small
          increases in sea level can have outsized impacts. In the future, climate change is expected to continue to adversely
          impact yields, with estimated declines of up to 10 percent by the 2030s. Projections also show that climate change
          could increase the poverty rate by between 10 and 30 percent (that is, the poverty rate would rise from 29 percent
          to anywhere between 33 and 38 percent).

4.      Agrifood systems need to be reformed to better serve current and future food and nutrition security needs.
Food security is an important government priority across EAP countries. Currently, food security policies are rice-centric,
with a focus on expanding rice production toward the goal of achieving self-reliance, where possible, in the supply of rice.
However, with changing lifestyles, and nutritional needs and preferences following urbanization and income growth, food
security policies need to evolve to focus not just on calorie (rice) adequacy but on nutritious and healthy diets for all.
Agrifood systems need to be transformed to generate more stable, affordable, and climate-resilient supplies of diverse
and nutritious food for all.

5.       In the EAP region, public support for agriculture is significant; however, the focus has been largely on rice. Most
countries promote rice production through a variety of direct and indirect support measures. Although rice makes only a
small and dwindling contribution to agricultural GDP, it still attracts a major share of the public spending in agriculture.
Extensive indirect support for rice production is provided through price support, heavily subsidized infrastructure and
water service delivery, trade restrictions, continuing dominance of state-owned enterprises, and other distortionary policy
instruments. Direct support is provided through subsidies for fertilizer, seeds, and other agricultural inputs. This has led
to an input-driven and uncompetitive rice production system characterized by the high cost of production; increasing GHG
and other emissions, pollution, and land degradation through overuse of fertilizer, water, and other resources; and
declining productivity and farmer incomes.

6.       Rice production suffers from structural constraints. Rice is grown mainly under irrigated conditions, with the
irrigated rice area accounting for 56 percent of the total rice area and 76 percent of the total rice production in the
countries of East Asia. However, irrigation services continue to be exclusively funded through the public sector and, as
such, are often severely underfunded, have a poor performance record, and do not attract meaningful citizen engagement
or private sector involvement. The failure to charge volumetrically for water services has led to wasteful water use. The
plot-to-plot nature of irrigation makes joint decision-making imperative, significantly increases the transaction costs, and
causes inertia. Those structural constraints to the adoption of innovations and crop diversification have increasingly led
farmers to invest in farmer-led irrigation development (FLID) that helps them secure individualized, more reliable access
to water.

7.       Transforming rice-based agriculture is technically feasible, economically sound, and environmentally viable.
Modern technologies and sustainable practices, such as high-yielding varieties (HYVs), hybrid rice, and stress-tolerant
strains, have demonstrated substantial yield increases and improved quality. For example, HYVs have been shown to boost
yields by 20–50 percent over traditional varieties, while hybrid rice can add another 15–30 percent, and stress-tolerant
varieties can increase yields by 10–30 percent in stress-prone areas. Adopting these technologies offers clear economic
and environmental advantages. Although improved seeds may cost 10–20 percent more, their higher germination rates
and reduced seeding requirements make them cost-effective. HYVs typically need less seed and fertilizer per unit of yield,
and pest- and disease-resistant varieties can cut pesticide usage by up to half. Drought-tolerant options can also reduce
water use significantly, offering additional savings and resilience for farmers. The World Bank-supported Viet Nam
Sustainable Agriculture Transformation Project (VNSAT) 2015–2022, which promoted sustainable rice-based systems
development in eight Mekong Delta provinces, demonstrated significant economic and financial returns.2 Another study
from Bangladesh shows that farms that switched from local to high-value crops (HVC) experienced around 35 percent
higher yield and 76 percent higher profit than non-adopting farms.3 With respect to environmental benefits, the adoption
of efficient practices leads to reduced fertilizer and water use, which could decrease overall environmental pollution,
including cutting methane emissions.

8.       To achieve a meaningful transformation of the region’s agrifood system, rice production needs to be
complemented with increased support for high-value growth drivers. Excessive focus on rice in the region has resulted
in public investment gaps in critical areas like horticulture and livestock, creating an uneven playing field between rice and
non-rice subsectors. Public investment needs to be increased in technology development and dissemination (which
consistently show high rates of return), agri-logistics, processing, marketing, and digital infrastructure.

9.      Agrifood system transformation needs to adopt a more job-focused approach. International experience suggests
that the transformation of the agrifood system will lead to significant changes in agricultural employment. On the one
hand, the trend decline in employment in agricultural production is expected to continue as more advanced technologies
and varieties are adopted and more mechanization occurs in land preparation, seeding, crop management, and harvesting
operations. On the other hand, structural transformation will provide opportunities for newer jobs requiring improved
knowledge and skills (hence, more remunerative than unskilled agricultural labor). These include shifts in production from
traditional crops to HVCs such as fruits, vegetables, and aquaculture as well as the growing significance and value of post-
farm activities (processing, logistics, retailing, food preparation, and service) compared to primary production.

10.      The private sector also needs to be mobilized at scale to provide finance and investment and create more and
better jobs in the agricultural sector of the EAP region. Small-scale farmers and agribusinesses are undercapitalized and
face severe credit and liquidity challenges. Demand for smallholder finance in the Southeast Asian region is around US$100
billion annually, but less than one-third of this demand is currently being met. The private sector needs to be incentivized
and supported to provide financial services (for example, loans, insurance, and credit to farmers) and capital for
agribusinesses and infrastructure (for example, storage, processing, and logistics) as well as for job-focused investments.

11.    The Philippines has been among the most dynamic economies in the EAP region. Between 2010 and 2019, the
economy grew by an average of 6.4 percent per year, surpassing that of its peers in the region, including Viet Nam (6.3
percent), Indonesia and Malaysia (5.4 percent), and Thailand (3.6 percent). Growth was driven by a robust domestic
economy, anchored on strong private consumption and increasing investment activity, led mainly by services and industry.

After a sharp contraction in 2020 (a decline of 9.5 percent) in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic, the country has since

12.     While poverty levels have been dropping, they remain relatively high. Poverty levels fell from 23.3 percent in
2015 to 15.5 percent in 2023 (Philippines Statistics Authority). Despite the country’s strong overall growth record, high
income disparities and significant rural-urban divide remain areas of concern. While the incidence of poverty in the urban
areas stands at 10.2 percent, it is more than double that in rural areas, at 21.9 percent. Farmers remain the poorest
segment of society, with poverty rates estimated at 30 percent among this segment of the population.

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

13.      In the Philippines, the agricultural sector has a high potential for contributing to the country’s socioeconomic
development agenda. The country’s fertile land, large workforce (of the 48.7 million employed Filipinos, some 22 percent
are in agriculture), including the relatively young population (64 percent between ages 15 and 64 years),4 and a high adult
literacy rate (97 percent in 2020) offer strong opportunities for transforming the sector. While rice cultivation dominates
the sector, there is good potential for growing HVCs, such as bananas, pineapples, mangoes, coconuts, vegetables, durian,
cacao, and coffee. Potential also exists for developing the livestock and aquaculture subsectors and, from the associated
economic opportunities, for value addition.

14.     Despite years of high and rising budgets for agriculture, outcomes of increased farm profitability, production,
and competitiveness have fallen far short of the goals established through the 1997 Agriculture and Fisheries
Modernization Act (AFMA, RA 8435). Public spending in 2025 is budgeted at PHP 220 billion (US$3.8 billion), about 8
percent of the sector’s gross value added. However, public expenditures have prioritized the provision of input subsidies
for credit, farm machinery, irrigation services, agricultural insurance, and seeds, among others. Over the period 2015–19,
subsidies consumed 32 percent of the commodity ‘banner’ program budget of the Department of Agriculture (DA), with
another 28 percent subsidizing machinery. Only 15 percent was allocated for technology transfer (extension) and 15
percent for other interventions, of which research and development was 8.6 percent and market development 0.8
percent. The oversized allocation of budget resources to subsidies severely limits public investments in growth and job-
creating agricultural programs. Scaling up support in these areas is key to harnessing the potential of the Philippines’
agriculture toward broad-based rural job creation and income growth.

15.     Agricultural policy has focused mainly on the pursuit of self-sufficiency in rice production and other key staples.
Overall, agricultural production in the Philippines increased by only 0.5 percent per year on average between 2011 and
2020, well below the world average of nearly 2 percent per year. A large part of this was due to low total factor
productivity5 growth (+0.4 percent per year) against the global average of 1.12 percent per year. Although around 25
percent of the DA annual budget for its commodity ‘banner’ programs is allocated for rice production, rice yields have
risen only marginally, from an average of 3.4 tons/ha in 2003 to 4.7 tons/ha in 2024.6 This is well below the average yields

16.      The lack of well-developed and integrated food chains across the country is reflected in the high levels of
postharvest losses, which have significant implications for food and nutrition security. Agrifood losses average 30–60
percent when accounting for all losses from harvesting, handling, storage, and transportation. This leads to significant
income loss and GHG emissions. Postharvest losses range from 9 to 37 percent for cereals, 27 to 42 percent for fruits and
vegetables, and 15 percent for rice.9 Such postharvest losses are more than twice those recorded in Viet Nam for rice, at

rice (3.8 million tons in 2022–23). The country has a low rice milling efficiency of 50–57 percent compared to 62.5 percent

of cold storage per 1,000 residents, or a total capacity of 400,000 tons, compared with a cold storage capacity of 116 m3
per 1,000 residents in Viet Nam.

17.     A complex, fragmented institutional and regulatory system has further stymied sector development and
disincentivized private sector investments in the country’s agrifood systems. The organizational structure of the DA has
resulted in competing strategic objectives and limited budget allocations that are spread thinly across the various
department entities. Inefficiency also stems from the lack of data integration between the DA’s agencies and bureaus. On
the regulatory side, logistical costs for shipping companies, traders, and importers arise from informal fees collected by
local government units (LGUs), complex road and ship transport licensing requirements, and historical caps on foreign
equity investment (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development [OECD] 2020).10 Coordination failures also
hinder investment in large-scale processing and pricing based on quality. As a result, farmers and traders have little
incentive to improve product quality due to the small premium paid for higher-quality products.

18.     The Philippines is among the countries most vulnerable to climate-related weather events, ranked fourth
among the countries most affected by extreme weather events over the past 20 years. About 60 percent of the country’s
land area and 74 percent of its population is exposed to multiple hazards such as typhoons, floods, landslides, severe
droughts, and heat waves. According to the 6th Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) assessment report,
released in 2021, extreme weather events will continue to increase in frequency and intensity until the end of the
century.11 For the agriculture sector, these impacts frequently lead to crop damage, yield losses, and disruptions to food

19.     Gender imbalances in the agriculture sector in the Philippines persist, with women farmers continuing to face
systemic barriers to productivity and resilience. Most rely on traditional labor technologies due to limited access to
extension services, modern agricultural technologies, and secure land ownership. In 2017, only 27 percent of beneficiaries
of technical and extension services—including climate-smart agriculture (CSA) practices13—were women. Despite the
importance of CSA adoption for resilience, women remain disadvantaged in accessing information and training. Financial
exclusion also constrains their ability to invest in farming; the 2021 Global Findex shows a six-point gender gap in account
ownership (46 percent women versus 52 percent of men). Land rights remain highly unequal, with the 2022 Census of
Agriculture and Fisheries (CAF) reporting that only 13.4 percent of women in agriculture held secure rights to land,
compared to 29.8 percent of men. While gender-disaggregated data remains limited, the DA’s Registry System for Basic
Sectors in Agriculture (RSBSA) is a promising platform, and strengthening it will be critical for generating evidence to
inform gender-responsive policies and promote women’s advancement in agriculture.

20.       Private sector investments in the agriculture sector have contracted by an average of 6 percent annually
between 2017 and 2022 (Philippine Development Report 2023). Private sector investments are constrained by, among
others, (a) the high cost of doing business due to taxes and tariffs on imported raw materials, cumbersome regulations,
and permit and documentary requirements imposed at national, provincial, and even barangay (village) levels; (b) the
country’s geography which comprises over 7,000 islands, creating significant logistical issues; (c) underinvestment in
critical infrastructure, including in integrated cold storage facilities; (d) fragmentation of land as a result of the 1988
Philippine Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Program, which imposed a ceiling of five hectares on land holdings and
reallocated agricultural land to landless and smallholders with restrictions on transferability for ten years; (e) limited
access to credit from banks; and (f) lack of accredited laboratories and process constraints which adversely affect the
competitiveness of the agrifood products in the international market. Institutional and regulatory reforms are needed for
agribusiness and export development to enhance regional agrifood exports in compliance with regional food safety
standards.

21.      Strategic shifts are underway to accelerate agricultural transformation and ensure a more resilient, inclusive,
and competitive agrifood sector. Through the Rice Liberalization Act (Rice Tariffication Law, Republic Act (RTL-RA) 11203),
extended and amended in 2024, import quotas on rice were abolished, opening up the importation of rice to private
traders. The RTL-RA has also created the Rice Competitiveness Enhancement Fund (RCEF) to support farmers with
productivity-enhancing investments through rice farm machinery/equipment, rice seeds, and improved production
systems. These recent reforms reflect a gradual shift toward market-oriented, productivity-enhancing policies. Building
on these reforms, the agricultural sector would benefit from focusing on diversifying production beyond rice to higher-
value and nutritious crops, strengthening agricultural innovation systems and extension services, expanding digital,
financial, and climate-resilient solutions for smallholders, empowering women and youth to participate in modern agri-
enterprises, and enhancing public-private partnerships (PPPs) and institutional coordination.

 C. Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA)

Rationale for Using MPA

22.       Agricultural transformation, involving technological, economic, and structural changes to modernize the
agricultural sector, is crucial to creating and increasing quality jobs, raising rural incomes, strengthening food and
nutrition security, and building sustainability and climate resilience of the agrifood systems. The proposed MPA
establishes a financing and knowledge platform to accelerate this transformation at scale across EAP countries. It will
promote modern technologies, more efficient and sustainable agricultural practices, climate-resilient irrigation systems,
enhanced value-addition through processing and aggregation, and stronger institutional capacity for a more dynamic and
resilient agrifood sector. The MPA will:
      •      Operationalize the World Bank Group’s Global Challenge Program in Food and Nutrition Security, which is
             designed to support the transformation of agrifood systems to improve global food and nutrition security, as
             well as AgriConnect, a World Bank Group initiative launched in 2025, designed to transform smallholder
             agriculture into a scalable engine for jobs, sustainable growth, and food security by emphasizing, among
             other things, PPPs, digital and financial innovations, integrated approaches, and innovation and knowledge
             sharing.
      •      Provide a common strategic framework to align country-led actions with support from regional and technical
             partners—such as the International Rice Research Institute (IRRI) and International Water Management
             Institute (IWMI)—for addressing current structural problems in agriculture and water management while
             capitalizing on the opportunities afforded by transitioning to more environmentally sustainable, efficient,
             resilient, and inclusive agrifood and water systems.
      •      Enhance operational efficiency through standardized approaches to procurement, environmental and social
             (E&S) management, and monitoring and evaluation (M&E).
      •      Leverage concessional and blended financing as well as grant financing from multiple sources for technical
             assistance and investments for agrifood systems development14.
      •      Enable deeper agricultural and water sector transformation, going beyond investments and infrastructure
             improvements to address structural and capacity constraints through analytics to enhance the conditions for
             entrepreneurship, innovation, and economic performance.

Program Pillars and Activities

25.      The MPA is structured around four mutually reinforcing pillars that provide a menu of investment options for
participating countries, allowing them to select and tailor activities best suited to their national priorities and
implementation capacity. Collectively, the pillars aim to raise productivity, enhance climate and water resilience, diversify
rural incomes, and strengthen the institutional foundations for sustainable agrifood transformation.

26.    Pillar 1: Increasing Agricultural Productivity. This pillar supports on-farm interventions to boost productivity
through new seed varieties, modern farming technologies, more resource-efficient and sustainable agronomic practices,

and better land/soil protection and restoration. Activities could include (a) establishing seed production and distribution
networks, farmer- and private sector-led seed multiplication networks, local adaptation trials, capacity-building services,
and so on; (b) introducing modern technologies and practices, including innovative irrigation/fertilization, nutrient and
pest management technologies, use of organic inputs, and training in precision agriculture technologies; (c) expanding
access to machinery through private sector-led or community-based rental services; and (d) investing in reducing GHG
emissions from irrigated rice through the promotion of CSA measures, emission-reducing practices, and piloting the use
of carbon credits.

27.     Pillar 2: Improving Water Productivity. This pillar enhances water-use efficiency and climate resilience through
investments in modern, ‘fit-for-purpose’ irrigation technologies and improved service delivery. Areas of support may
include (a) improving agricultural water management to reduce water losses and to adapt to climate variability; (b)
improving the quality of irrigation service delivery by introducing performance incentives; (c) investing in climate-resilient
water infrastructure such as water storage facilities, irrigation system modernization, and groundwater irrigation; (d)
improving access to FLID such as solar irrigation pumps, boreholes, and micro-irrigation equipment through matching
grants and by connecting farmers to banks; and (e) upgrading and modernizing hydrological monitoring and management
systems, digital technologies for more efficient water, water-use monitoring and management, improved weather and
flood forecasting, and ex post response mechanisms.

28.      Pillar 3: Promoting Diversification and Value-Chain Development. This pillar focuses on improving income
diversification opportunities, increasing resilience and ensuring nutritious and sustainable food systems through more
diverse, market-oriented production; improving market access; and promoting private sector-led growth. Under this pillar,
support could be provided for the following activities: (a) up-front investments in seeds, equipment, infrastructure and
extension that enable farmers to diversify their production beyond staple (for example, rice) crops; (b) investments into
processing facilities to add value to raw agricultural products, reduce postharvest losses, and increase profitability; (c)
establishment of producer organizations, farmer cooperatives, and aggregator companies to build scale to obtain better
prices and cheaper access to inputs and share knowledge as well as promotion of contract farming and digital platforms
to link farmers to markets; (d) promotion of PPPs for investment in relevant technologies, product quality/variety,
irrigation equipment, new market development, and so on; and (e) training and certification programs to help
smallholders meet quality and food safety standards. The International Finance Corporation (IFC) Agribusiness Leadership
Program (ALP) will be leveraged to strengthen value chains and ensure sustainable sourcing.

29.      Pillar 4: Strengthening Institutional Capacities. This pillar strengthens institutions, governance, and financing
mechanisms to enhance the effectiveness of public spending and mobilize new resources for transformation. Potential
areas of support include (a) improving public resource management through evidence-based planning and performance-
based budgeting; (b) introducing irrigation asset management to systematically plan and anticipate expenditures for
operation and maintenance (O&M); (c) strengthening national food safety and risk management systems; (d) modernizing
procurement through digital tools and capacity building to improve efficiency and transparency; (e) promoting payment
for irrigation services among users; (f) building national capacities and developing policy, regulatory, and institutional
frameworks to enable countries to access carbon finance, prepare measurement, reporting, and verification (MRV)
systems for participation in carbon markets, and implement emission-reduction programs; and (g) establishing knowledge
networks and learning platforms for continuous innovation, technical collaboration, and peer learning across countries.

One World Bank Group Approach

31.      The MPA will adopt the ‘One World Bank Group’ approach to leverage the collective strengths and resources of
the IBRD, IFC, and the Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA) to deliver more integrated and impactful
solutions to the agriculture transformation challenge in EAP. IFC has the expertise to promote private sector participation
and job creation by investing in agribusinesses to expand and modernize agricultural production, processing facilities, and
logistics; improving supply chains; and increasing the efficiency of farming operations. It could potentially facilitate agri-
finance for farmers, small- and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), and cooperatives and provide advisory services to
improve business models, strengthen agricultural value chains, and promote sustainable and climate-smart practices. For
example, the proposed Phase 3 operation in PNG is being developed in close collaboration with IFC to catalyze private
sector investments and strengthen productive alliances between smallholders and agribusinesses. IFC could also offer
tailored advisory services to select agribusinesses as well as modernize PNG’s biosecurity systems to safeguard trade and
expand export opportunities.

Learning Agenda

33.      A robust learning agenda is critical to achieving and sustaining agricultural transformation under the MPA. The
MPA provides a valuable framework for convening governments and key stakeholders from across the EAP region, as well
as globally, to promote the exchange of knowledge and experiences that would inform and advance agricultural
transformation in the EAP region. The MPA would facilitate cross-country learning through peer-to-peer engagement
among participating countries to share proven transformation pathways, innovative technologies, and successful
institutional practices. It would also foster technical partnerships between the participating countries and regional
organizations and research centers, including the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN), and the Consultative
Group of International Agricultural Research (CGIAR) centers, such as IRRI, International Food Policy Research Institute
(IFPRI), and IWMI. In addition, the MPA would provide the opportunity to leverage additional expertise and resources by
strengthening cooperation with leading development partners, such as the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB),
International Fund for Agricultural Development (IFAD), and Asian Development Bank (ADB), to further the goal of
agricultural transformation in the EAP region.

34.      The MPA participating countries would also benefit from the recently launched World Bank initiative, Scaling Next
Generation Rice Impact Program, the objective of which is to establish a knowledge platform to facilitate knowledge
sharing among World Bank Group client rice producers on innovative policies, technologies, and practices and successful
public and private scaling engagements that can improve rice production systems for farmers and the planet. The
knowledge platform aims to scale ‘NextGen’ innovative rice technologies and practices, evidence-based policies, and
successful public and private engagements to improve rice production systems and diversification to HVCs, for more and
better jobs, improved incomes, and environmental benefits, including reduced GHG emissions. The Scaling Next Gen Rice
Impact Program will use the World Bank Group’s convening power to bring together the best researchers, decision-
makers, practitioners, private sector companies, farmers, and others to identify, curate, and share knowledge on scaling
innovations for a more sustainable and remunerative rice sector. This will be done through annual workshops, private
sector consultations, peer exchanges, training, knowledge product development, virtual learning, a community of practice,
and programs to boost innovation scaling. The platform will initially focus on World Bank Group client countries in the EAP
and South Asia regions, where rice production and consumption are most intense. The launch event took place in June
2025 in the Philippines, where government representatives from the Philippines, Indonesia, Viet Nam, China, Cambodia,
India, and Bangladesh were introduced to the program.

35.      In addition, the World Bank’s Food Systems 2030 Initiative is facilitating knowledge and experience sharing among
World Bank Group clients to advance reforms of fertilizer subsidies for enhancing soil health. The Scaling Next Gen Rice
Impact Program, through regional and country events, supports knowledge sharing around already-tested best practices
in technology, management, and policy to support the clients in their journey of repurposing agricultural public
expenditures away from subsidies to drivers of growth and transformation. These learning initiatives will serve as valuable
vehicles to disseminate good practices and lessons learned from the various phases of the MPA, including the strategic
approaches, policies, and institutional reforms proposed under the PSAT operation that could be highly relevant to
neighboring countries undertaking similar reforms.

Risks

36.     The overall risk of the MPA is assessed as Substantial. The MPA is structured into multiple phases, each engaging
an expanding array of diverse stakeholders and participating countries. Given the innovative nature of the MPA and the
varying levels of institutional capacity among the countries involved, there are significant risks that could impact the
achievement of the intended development objectives. To mitigate these risks and promote successful project outcomes,
each phase of the MPA will incorporate comprehensive capacity-building initiatives tailored to the specific needs of
stakeholders. These measures are designed to enhance the effectiveness and efficiency of project implementation,
thereby supporting progress toward the overarching objectives of the MPA.

 II. PROGRAM DESCRIPTION


 A. Relationship to the Country Partnership Framework (CPF) and Rationale for Use of Instrument

37.      The proposed PSAT PforR is closely aligned with two of the three CPF outcomes,16 that is, Outcome 2: Stronger
Private Sector and Jobs (new or better jobs, increased broadband usage, and mobilization of private capital) and
Outcome 3: More Resilient Communities (increased beneficiaries of social safety net programs and enhanced climate
resilience). PSAT will contribute to these outcomes through modernizing farming practices, strengthening value chains,
improving service delivery, and promoting agricultural exports. Collectively, these will drive rural commercialization,
attract private investment, and create new on- and off-farm jobs. PSAT also aligns with the CPF’s proposed new
programmatic approach, including the use of the multiphase approach.

38.      The PforR instrument is particularly suited to address longstanding inefficiencies constraining agricultural
transformation. While the World Bank’s substantial project lending over the past 25 years17 has strengthened the DA’s
institutional and operational capacity, systemic inefficiencies persist across the DA’s agencies, bureaus, and commodity
‘banner’ programs. Crosscutting issues, particularly policy, regulatory, resource efficiency, and climate resilience
challenges, continue to impede the modernization of the country’s largely smallholder-based, low-technology agrifood
sector. Given these issues and the Government’s renewed focus on transforming the sector, the PforR offers the flexibility
to strengthen institutional performance, implement and mainstream good results progressively, and contribute to the
MPA learning agenda through systematic documentation, reporting, and technical partnerships, particularly in the context
of the Philippines’ leading ASEAN role in promoting regional cooperation and economic integration.

2027 (MBP),18 and the strategies of the Philippine Development Plan (PDP) 2023–2028, which prioritize agriculture
modernization and agribusiness development. The MBP and PDP span the duration of the current administration (2022–
2028) and address the policy, regulatory, service, and investment gaps that have constrained agrifood sectoral
modernization efforts to this point. Together, they operationalize the long-term goals of the National Agriculture and
Fisheries Modernization and Industrialization Plan (NAFMIP) 2021–2030, which envisions a productive, climate-resilient

44.      Program beneficiaries. Direct PSAT beneficiaries comprise FCAs, individual farmers, and enterprises engaged
across the agrifood service chain, from input and machinery supply, through postharvest handling storage, logistics, and

Benefits will stem from higher incomes resulting from enhanced agricultural productivity (improved seed, farm
mechanization, and fertilizer use efficiency), along with improved diversification, climate resilience, reduced postharvest
losses, and agrifood export growth. PSAT will also promote more efficient public expenditure in support of a competitive
agrifood sector. Indirect beneficiaries include agrifood producers and a broad range of value-chain stakeholders who
would benefit from greater commercialization, higher productivity and profitability, and the creation of new and better-

 III. PROGRAM IMPLEMENTATION
 A. Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
48.     Program management. A Steering Committee comprising senior DA managers would provide oversight of
implementation, while day-to-day management will be handled by a Program Management Office (PMO) reporting to the
Secretary of Agriculture through the Undersecretary for Special Concerns and Official Development Assistance (ODA), or
an equivalent office within the DA. The PMO would be led by a Program Director, appointed by the Secretary of
Agriculture. To the extent possible, the PMO will be staffed by the DA’s organic personnel from relevant units and services
to ensure strong interagency coordination. Key functions of the PMO include (a) ensuring strategic coordination among
all Program implementing units; (b) supporting operational planning and technical execution of Program activities,
including reporting and resolution of issues with DA management; (c) monitoring and reporting on progress towards
achieving the DLIs and DLRs, and submitting verification reports for achieved DLIs/DLR reports to the World Bank; and (d)
ensuring fiduciary oversight and compliance with the World Bank Anti-Corruption Guidelines, and manage the grievance
redress mechanism. The PMO will serve as the primary interlocuter to the World Bank and will be responsible for
coordinating Implementation Support Missions (ISMs).

49.      Implementation arrangements. The DA, through its programs, units, regional field offices (RFOs), bureaus, and
attached agencies will implement PSAT.25 The NRP will be responsible for RA 1 in coordination with PhilRice and the
Agriculture Training Institute (ATI). RA 2 will be implemented by the HVC Development Program, in coordination with ATI,
AFLO, Bureau of Plant Industry (BPI), and Export Office. RA 3 will be implemented by the Financial Management Service
(FMS), Planning and Monitoring Service (PMS), and the Procurement Office. No activities will be directly implemented by
LGUs. However, DLI 5, which supports the training of agricultural extension workers in CSA practices, will indirectly
strengthen LGUs’ technical capacity. PSAT implementation would build existing structures and M&E and fiduciary systems,
and is supported by robust administrative and legislative instruments providing the legal foundation for PforR26-supported
actions. The Implementation Support Plan for the program is summarized in annex 9.

 B. Results Monitoring and Evaluation

50.      The PMO will be responsible for M&E in coordination with the PMS and build on the substantial M&E capacity
and experiences established under the PRDP, including the Rapid Appraisal of Emerging Benefits (RAEB) for real-time
assessment of benefits and implementation progress. A midterm review (no later than 30 months after effectiveness) and
implementation completion review will be conducted to assess performance and outcomes. The PMO’s M&E activities
will be complemented by the proposed TASAT IPF.

51.     Verification arrangements. The DA, through the PMO, will hire third-party verification agencies (VAs) to validate
the DLRs. The VAs will conduct field and farmer sampling, data analysis, and verification in accordance with the verification
protocols (Annex 2). The VAs will be competitively selected and appointed or hired within six months after PSAT becomes
effective, in accordance with terms of reference (TORs) satisfactory to the World Bank. There is a strong pool of potentially
well-qualified verification entities among academic institutions and consultancy firms in the Philippines, with the capacity
to undertake the required tasks. In the specific case of DLR 2.2, which measures the “annual increase in the average net
income per hectare of RSBSA registered rice-based farmers deriving from rice-based farming within MRIDP areas,” the VA
would be the PSA, because of their unique and independent role as the government statistical authority responsible,
among others, for monitoring and reporting national income data across sectors. For this, the DA would enter into a
Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) with the PSA to enhance their current survey on incomes from rice farming to include
incomes from rice-based farming to capture incomes from diversified rice farming.

 C. Disbursement Arrangements

52.     Disbursement arrangements. The proceeds of the World Bank loan will be made at the Borrower's request upon
achievement of DLRs. Disbursements for scalable DLIs will be proportional to the progress toward achieving the targeted
DLR value. Disbursements under non-scalable DLIs will require full achievement. Amounts not disbursed due to partial or
non-achievement may be carried forward to subsequent periods, within agreed timelines. Disbursement amounts will
follow the formulas outlined in the verification protocols (annex 2).

53.      Disbursement will be based on verified results measured by DLIs. An advance of US$248.8 million (24.88 percent
of loan financing) will be available for withdrawal upon effectiveness. This advance is intended to cover the first 12 months
of the DA’s Program Expenditure Framework supported by PSAT. After verification of prior results (DLR 1.1, DLR 2.1, DLR
5.1, DLR 6, DLR 8, DLR 10.1, and DLR 11) by the IVA, an estimated amount of up to US$51.2 million (5.12 percent) may be
disbursed. Table 5 specifies the allocated amount and achievement requirements for each DLI. All DLI disbursements will
occur after the IVA reviews and verifies appropriate evidence in line with the agreed verification protocol. Upon
achievement (or partial achievement) of a DLI/DLR, the DA PMO will submit evidence through DOF to the World Bank.
There is no restriction on early achievement of DLIs, and payments will be disbursed as targets are met.

through the TASAT to ensure that the transformation agenda will be achieved through PSAT. The TASAT aims to
strengthen institutional capacity and delivery systems for repurposing the fertilizer subsidy program, diversification,
climate resilience, and increased agricultural exports.

 IV. ASSESSMENTS SUMMARY


 A. Technical

55.      The Technical Assessment involved extensive discussions with DA agencies and Philippine agrifood industry
leaders and was further informed by analytical studies and research findings, including studies supported by the World
Bank and research institutes (PhilRice and IRRI).27 The Technical Assessment confirmed the DA’s MBP to be well
formulated and strategically important in focusing on the issues and contributing to achievement of the PDP 2023–2028
indicators for modernizing agriculture and agribusiness. The strategic relevance and technical soundness of DLIs in each
of the RAs are detailed in annex 3 and in the Technical Assessment. The ambitious program of actions to be measured and
verified through DLRs, as detailed in the verification protocol (annex 2), will also significantly contribute to the learning
agenda of the MPA, given the commonality of many of the issues confronting the Philippine agrifood sector as it seeks to
modernize.

56.      Global Challenge Program (GCP). PSAT is aligned with the World Bank GCP on Food and Nutrition Security,
particularly Outcome 3 (Scaling solutions for low-emissions and climate-resilient food systems). Net GHG emission
reduction is measured by the quantity of CO₂ equivalent (CO₂eq) reduced from rice fields. The reduction in GHG emission
is expected to result primarily from improved water and nutrient management in rice fields, including lower emissions
due to the adoption of mechanized direct seeding and CSA practices. This allows cultivation on dry land and for the
diversification to other commodities, particularly lowland vegetables as well as improved input use. Under the with-
program scenario, net GHG emission reduction is estimated to be approximately 118.9 million tons of CO₂eq, based on
calculations using the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) Ex-ACT tool for GHG emissions
(version 9.4.2). This equates to an average reduction of approximately 5.9 MtCO₂eq per year. DLIs 3 and 6 will promote
the adoption of a technological package aimed at reducing GHG emissions, particularly through mechanized direct
seeding. DLI 4 will support the efficient provision of fertilizers and other agricultural inputs for farmers. DLI 5 will promote
the adoption of key climate mitigation measures through capacity building and training. In addition, TASAT will provide
strong incentives for the DA to maximize positive regional spillovers and deepen its contribution to the GCP through DLI 5
(climate-smart technologies) and DLI 9 (export promotion), as well as to undertake needed policy reforms that contribute
to food and nutrition security and the climate agenda. Please refer to Annex 7 for details of climate assessment.

57.      PSAT is consistent with the World Bank Maximizing Finance for Development (MFD) framework, advancing a
range of MFD-enabling (MFD-e) interventions. As summarized in Annex 4, Table 4.8, all DLIs would contribute to enabling
private sector investment. DLIs 1, 2 and 7 will expand private investment in the production and distribution of improved,
certified seeds and planting material. This coupled with the support for diversification will enable private sector led
expansion in logistics, machinery, equipment and packaging. While DLI 8 will promote private sector investment in cool
storage facilities and DLI 9 will reduce costs of doing business through regulatory streamlining. DLI 4 coupled with DLI 5,
will repurpose the distribution of subsidized fertilizer to a market-based system enabling increased business expansion of
local suppliers, while the farm mechanization programs support through DLI 3 and 6 will expand private sector supply and

58.      Gender. PSAT targets persistent gender gaps in agriculture, where women face lower CSA adoption, limited access
to extension services and finance, and underrepresentation in farm decision-making. These barriers are compounded by
time poverty, weak land tenure, and male-dominated institutions. These constraints disproportionately affect some 2
million women in rice-based and HVC farming. Through gender-responsive delivery of CSA technologies (DLIs 1, 3, 5, and
7), rollout of a digital IMC to expand women’s access to inputs and finance (DLI 4), and institutional reforms on gender
budgeting and data systems (DLIs 10 and 11), PSAT aims to increase women’s adoption of CSA practices, strengthen
financial inclusion, and promote sector policies. See annex 3 for detail.

59.      Citizen engagement. This will be supported though (a) consultation with primary beneficiaries of PSAT, including
the farmer groups, cooperatives, and/or organizations, regarding the designs and implementation of interventions and
plans, taking into account their views and inputs on how interventions will be better placed and be responsive to their
needs and contexts; and (b) a grievance redress system (GRS), which is already well established under the DA through
ongoing World Bank-financed projects (PRDP and PRDP-SU). This will be further institutionalized through PSAT. The
mechanism provides for stakeholders to provide their feedback on PSAT, including inquiries, inputs and recommendations,
complaints, and/or any issues concerning how PSAT is implemented. It also conveys back to beneficiaries/citizens how
their feedback was considered.

60.      Program Expenditure Framework. The Government's MBP for CY2026–2030 is estimated at US$13.56 billion. Of
this, the proposed program will spend about US$8.17 billion (of which IBRD is financing US$1.00 billion) on all related
expenditures of the DA, such as earmarked capital outlays (CO), personnel services, and other operating expenses of the
DA agencies, bureaus, and government-owned and controlled corporations (GOCCs). The assessment indicates sufficient
eligible expenditures to achieve the PDO. DLI 10 supports improvements in results-based, multi-year budgeting. Additional
fiduciary mitigation measures are formulated as part of the PAP. A detailed analysis of the Program Expenditure
Framework is in annex 4.

61.      Economic justification of the program. The overall economic results of PSAT are positive, generating a net present
value (NPV), at a 10 percent social discount rate of the net additional benefits, of US$957 million and an economic internal
rate of return (EIRR) of 19.4 percent over a 20-year period, not accounting for environmental externalities. These economic
results are conservative, given that program benefits related to institutional strengthening could not be quantified due to
the difficulty of assigning them a monetary value. The EIRR with GHG reduction is estimated at 37 percent (with a low
carbon shadow price) and 51 percent (with a high carbon shadow price), which are all above the discount rate of 10
percent used, indicating that the PSAT is overall economically viable.28 The analysis encompasses quantifiable incremental
benefits at both local and global levels, including increased paddy yields, reduced input costs for fertilizer and labor in rice
cultivation, lower postharvest losses for rice and other HVCs, increased income from diversification of rice farms, improved
prices for HVCs, and generation of substantial public goods through the reduction of GHG emissions from rice fields along
with an additional 5 percent increase in incremental benefits resulting from efficiency gains. PSAT costs account for all
incremental costs associated with investment in technical package adoption, operational expenses for agricultural
production, training and capacity-building costs, as well as the costs of implementing structural reforms included in RA 3.

 B. Fiduciary

62.      PSAT’s fiduciary systems are assessed as adequate with enhancements identified, given that this is the first
PforR for the country.31 PSAT is not expected to procure any large contracts valued at or above the Operations
Procurement Review Committee (OPRC) thresholds for a ‘Substantial’ risk rating (that is, US$75 million for works, US$50
million for goods and non-consulting services, and US$20 million for consultant services). The PMO will report to the World
Bank if any large contracts appear during PSAT implementation. The World Bank will also monitor fiduciary systems and
contract management reports to identify any large-value contracts throughout the program implementation. PSAT
implementing agencies have acceptable financial and procurement management systems, but compliance with internal
control arrangements will need to be strengthened by (a) conducting quarterly internal audits while also addressing
internal auditor staffing gaps and improving follow-up of audit issues; (b) improving accounting policies and procedures
to address gaps in internal control arrangements; (c) recruitment of staff to enhance compliance with accounting policies
and procedures; and (c) adopting an updated Procurement Operations Manual. Further details are provided in annex 5.
Specific fiduciary actions are included in the PAP (annex 8).

63.     PSAT adheres to the World Bank’s ‘Guidelines on Preventing and Combating Fraud and Corruption in Program-
for-Results Financing’ by adopting the Anti-corruption Protocol. This will be strengthened by (a) improving complaint
monitoring and management systems and (b) designating and capacitating focal office and key officials responsible for
ensuring that the program activities are implemented in accordance with the provisions of the Anti-Corruption Guidelines
and agreed protocols. These measures will build on the Institutional Strengthening Action Plan (ISAP) prepared with the
support of Integrity Vice Presidency (INT) for the implementation of PRDP-SU (P180379) and the Mindanao Inclusive
Agriculture Development Project (P173866). The Anti-Corruption Guidelines protocol is attached to the FSA.

66.      The overall program risk is Substantial. (a) Sector strategies and policy risks are Substantial. This will be mitigated

disadvantaged support for non-rice commodities, particularly HVCs, for which the country has a comparative advantage;
(b) Technical Design risks are rated High primarily because this would be the first PforR for the Philippines. The program
would be overseen by a Steering Committee of senior DA managers, and with day-to-day fiduciary, E&S management and
reporting handled by a high-level PMO enabling rapid resolution of issues. The PMO would oversee field operations
implemented by the responsible DA agency or RFO; (c) Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability risks
are rated as Substantial. This risk will be mitigated through intensive World Bank implementation support, a specific PAP
that addresses fiduciary and E&S issues identified as needing strengthening, as well as through complementary technical
assistance to be funded through TASAT; (d) Fiduciary risks are rated Substantial. Fiduciary strengthening actions have
been included in the PAP and through adoption of the World Bank’s Anti-Corruption Guidelines; (e) E&S risks are rated
Substantial given the wide range of activities to be undertaken and the diversity of risks this entails. High-risk activities
will be excluded, and the PAP includes additional actions to strengthen E&S processes.

               ANNEX 3. SUMMARY TECHNICAL ASSESSMENT AND IMPLEMENTATION ARRANGEMENTS

1.      The Technical Assessment drew on DA’s various programs, agencies, and services and consultations with
Agrifood Industry Leaders. It also involved a review of analytical studies and research findings, some with World Bank
support and premier institutes such as PhilRice and IRRI. Collectively, these sources provided a solid basis for informing
the assessment.

                                           Figure 3.1. Theory of Change for PSAT




2.       The Theory of Change guiding prioritization of actions under PSAT emphasizes a more strategic focus on
productivity and export growth drivers, realigning public expenditure to change ‘ways of doing business’ from the long-
standing subsidized input supply-driven approach (figure 3.1). This requires addressing growth factors that will deliver
more profitable farming and an investment environment for commercialized agricultural practices with higher productivity
and profitability. This, among others, will require intensive diversification through high-value commodities and changes in
farming practices, enabling economies of scale and efficiency. PSAT interventions are designed to promote productive and
efficient farming practices to increase incomes and resilience, encompassing modern climate-resilient technologies,
mechanization, expansion of HVC production, improved postharvest handling, and export growth. Such modernization
will incentivize rural commercialization/private investment in inputs, machinery, product handling, storage, marketing,
and logistics, leading to a new generation of on- and off-farm jobs. Further, the proposed TASAT will provide
complementary support for the implementation of the PSAT Program through a series of technical assistance initiatives
to underpin the DA’s implementation of PSAT and achievement of PSATs ambitious DLIs.

3.      The Technical Assessment confirmed the DA’s MBP, which is well formulated and strategically addresses

RA 1: Enhanced production and incomes in rice-based farming

DLI 1: Increase yield of rice in Rice Competitive Enhancement Program (RCEP) areas using improved varieties

4.       Strategic relevance and technical soundness. Despite allocating about 25 percent of the DA’s annual budget for
its commodity ‘banner’33 programs to rice production, yields have risen only marginally from an average of 3.4 tons/ha in
2003 to a national average of about 4.11 tons/ha in 2024. Irrigated areas produce about 4.5 tons/ha and rainfed areas
closer to 3.3 tons/ha, both below Viet Nam’s average yield of about 6 tons/ha34 and 8–10 tons/ha, respectively, which can
be achieved with good agricultural practices. DLI 1 actions would focus on yield improvement through better inbred and
hybrid seed varieties, complementing other RA 1-DLI actions.35 Farmers face constraints in accessing and receiving
certified seeds on time, resulting in the distribution of many varieties without sufficient attention to local adaptation
and often too late for the planting season—contributing to low germination rates in the following season. Cultivating
multiple rice varieties also lowers farmers’ prices, as grain is priced at an average rate rather than by varietal quality and
consumer preference, such as fragrant rice that commands higher prices. Bulking of grain from multiple varieties also
increases postharvest milling losses through higher levels of ‘brokens’, excessive bran, and overall lower nutritional
quality. About 160 varieties are reportedly grown in key rice areas of the Philippines. Policies underpinning certified seed
production and distribution have failed to fully implement DA Memorandum Order No. 32 Series 2022, which calls for
selecting HYVs and locally adapted varieties. The proposed limit is around five varieties per area, based on the suitability
and availability of locally adapted improved rice varieties.

5.       DLRs require that (a) in FY2026, the DA issues an Administrative Order mandating all relevant DA agencies
implementing the MRIDP to distribute a few registered improved rice varieties aligned with the spatial and climate
suitability of each relevant area and (b) disbursements are based on the annual increase in the average rice yield in RCEP
areas. The Results Framework will include an indicator measuring the annual increase in the total harvested RCEP rice area
planted with registered improved rice varieties adapted to local conditions. (RCEP areas, supported through the RCEF,
encompass nearly the whole country [78 of 82 provinces], with only small islands currently excluded. The MRIDP is a
nationwide DA-led program and is closely aligned with RCEP areas).

6.       Outcomes would (a) improve rice yields, farmer incomes, and climate resilience; (b) reduce postharvest losses of
rice in milling processes; and (c) strengthen NSIC seed registration policies and the DA’s seed distribution efficiency under
the RCEF.

DLI 2: Increase in Income of RSBSA registered rice-based farmers within the Masagana Rice Industry Development
Program (MRIDP) areas

7.       Strategic relevance and technical soundness. Increasing incomes, especially for those in small-scale, marginally
profitable rice-based farming, is a central goal of the Government and essential for achieving PDP 2023–2028 targets for
modernizing Philippine agriculture and agribusiness. While the PSA36 reported an increase in rice farmer’s income in 2024,
despite higher production costs, income levels remain relatively low. Average farmgate price per kg of palay rose to PHP
23.48, up from PHP 19.88 in 2023. This resulted in higher gross returns of PHP 95,906 per ha and a net return of PHP
36,211 per ha, compared to PHP 82,914 and PHP 26,423, respectively, in 2023. While many factors influence farm income,
DLI 2 actions will focus on diversification, complementing other RA 1-DLI actions.37 Diversification helps rice-based farmers
increase incomes from improved farm productivity and resilience to adverse weather, pest, and diseases while also
contributing to broader climate co-benefits. Potential exists to expand production of crops and livestock that yield higher
profits and require less water than rice, such as corn, lowland vegetables (for example, tomato, okra, pumpkin, eggplant,
beans, onion, garlic, bitter gourd - ampalaya), and native chicken. But the NRP has historically focused on rice only, with
50 percent of the DA’s annual commodity program budget allocated exclusively to rice production. The entrenched
commodity approach and resultant institutional ‘silos’ hinder efficient use of financial and human resources and the
development of other agricultural commodities, wherein the country has a comparative advantage. DLRs would require
that (a) in FY2026, the DA issues an Administrative Order mandating the NRP and other commodity programs to
collaborate in promoting crop diversification in suitable MRIDP rice areas and seasons and (b) disbursements be based on
the annual increase in average net income per ha of RSBSA-registered rice-based farmers within the MRIDP. The Results
Framework would measure the annual increase in total MRIDP rice area diversified to include other commodities.

8.      Outcomes would (a) provide farmers with increased and more climate-resilient income and (b) contribute to the
country’s food and nutrition security through increased production of high-value commodities (lowland vegetables,
livestock, aquaculture, native chicken, corn, and so on).

DLI 3: Rice production area under direct mechanical seeding (ha)

9.        Strategic relevance and technical soundness. Rice cultivation in the Philippines is largely labor-intensive—a key
factor behind its marginal profitability. Manual transplanting covers about 65 percent of crop areas in wet and dry seasons
and entails 21 man-days/ha compared with 2 man-days/ha for direct seeding. With rising labor costs, seasonal labor
scarcity, and increased climate vulnerability, scaling up and promoting efficient and climate-resilient technologies are a
priority. While manual seed broadcasting to reduce labor costs has been increasing, now averaging about 35 percent of
the production area, it wastes improved seeds, increases losses to birds and snails, leads to uneven plant establishment
and yield loss, and complicates manual weeding due to lack of organized spacing. In contrast, through mechanical direct
seeding—still not actively promoted—seed is placed in the soil at recommended (lower) rates, requires less water with
associated climate co-benefits, enables fertilizers to be applied at the same time, and facilitates manual weeding. The DLR
will measure the annual increase in the total MRIDP rice area mechanically direct seeded.

DLI 4: Farmers using improved fertilizer through repurposed support using IMC (number)

11.       Strategic relevance and technical soundness. The Philippines has long pursued input-driven policies to promote
crop production, especially rice. Current policies continue to favor a paper voucher system where farmers collect fertilizers
from ‘distribution points’. The system is cumbersome, delays delivery of fertilizers, does not tailor fertilizer composition
to local conditions, and is vulnerable to corruption through on-selling. The subsidy for two 50 kg bags per ha of fertilizer
was meant to complement the usual two bags per ha applied by farmers, but many replaced their purchases with the
subsidized fertilizer, leaving use below the recommended four 50 kg bags per ha. The DA’s introduction of an electronic
modality using an IMC (credit card style/e-voucher) will be important for increasing efficiency and transparency. About
1.8 million of the country’s 3 million rice farms, registered in the RSBSA, have already paid PHP 180 (US$3) to obtain the
card. Through the proposed DLI, an alternative to the current paper voucher system of fertilizer ‘handouts’ would be
trialed to create a mechanism enabling farmers to purchase eligible fertilizers at a discounted price. This will be developed
with technical assistance through the TASAT . Accredited suppliers would provide fertilizers aligned with local DA fertilizer
recommendations. This would be underpinned by technical support to be strengthened through DLI 5, which would
reestablish a core of about 4,000 extension agents in the DA to complement the limited support from LGUs. DLRs would
require that (a) in FY2026, the DA issues an Administrative Order introducing a fertilizer discount system through IMCs
and (b) from FY2027, measure the annual increase in farmers holding IMCs and using them within the year to access
fertilizers from accredited suppliers. The proposed grant financing/IPF would supplement this DLI with technical assistance
to scale up outcomes through PSAT interventions. The technical assistance will recommend ways to (a) support more
sustainable productivity and climate resiliency outcomes; (b) promote integrated use of complementary inputs (seeds,
nutrients, pesticides, and so on), further unlocking productivity gains through inputs/resource use efficiency; and (c)
promote private sector engagement in the provision of inputs and services.

12.      Outcomes would be (a) the repurposing of fertilizer subsidies to incentivize a shift away from dependency on
government ‘handouts’; (b) improved farm nutrient management for increased productivity; and (c) more efficient and
transparent provision of inputs and services through the private sector that provides a pathway for moving away from the
inefficiency issues surrounding government input procurement and distribution programs.

DLI 5: Farmers adopting at least two categories of CSA technologies and practices (number)

13.       Strategic relevance and technical soundness. Locally adapted and climate-resilient technologies and approaches
(CSA) for rice-based farming are defined by ATI-CSA guidelines and readily available through the long-established and
significant research findings of IRRI, PhilRice, National Adaptation Plan (NAP 2023), and the World Bank’s Country Climate
and Development Report (CCDR 2022). Established CSA technologies for rice production include (a) proper use of high-
quality seeds of varieties recommended for that particular agro-ecological zone; (b) well-leveled fields; (c) synchronous
planting after a rest period; (d) establishment and planting of sufficient number of seedlings (in the case of transplanted
rice); (e) correct application of nutrients from tillering to early panicle initiation and flowering; (f) avoidance of stress due
to factors such as drought or excess water; (g) effective pest control; (h) correct timing of harvest; and (i) grain dried,
cleaned, and stored properly. These include technologies and approaches developed under the Adaptation and Mitigation
Initiative in Agriculture (AMIA) and decision support tools, for example, Climate Risk Vulnerability Assessment (CRVA), Soil
Fertility Maps, Climate Information Services (CIS), and National Color-Coded Agricultural Guide (NCCAG) Map. Significant
opportunities exist for improving the efficiency of rice farmers through CSA and for reducing GHG emissions, given the

DLI 6: Level of rice mechanization Improved

15.      Strategic relevance and technical soundness. Mechanizing land preparation through harvesting and threshing
provides a clear path to sustained productivity increases, primarily by reducing labor costs, avoiding delays from limited
labor availability at peak production periods, enabling economies of scale for adopting more advanced production,
climate-resilient, and postharvest technologies, and enhancing opportunities for crop diversification by enabling dryland
cultivation. National farm mechanization levels average 2.78 hp/ha (2024), slightly higher than the 2.31 hp/ha recorded
in 2013. Due to the lack of mechanization, paddy preparation requires an average of 9 to 10 man-days/ha, considerably
higher than in Viet Nam (2.4 man-days/ha) and Thailand (1.8 man-days/ha). Manual harvesting and threshing using small
axial threshers is also widely practiced, taking around 20 man-days/ha, accounting for about 32 percent of total labor use.
The DLI actions closely align with the Government’s transformation agenda to raise productivity as defined in the PDP
2023–2028 and are supported by RCEF financing. The DLR will measure the annual increase in the mechanization intensity
(hp/ha) for rice-based farming within RCEP areas. In addition, the Results Framework will measure the annual increase in
the total MRIDP rice area cultivated by farmer clusters and FCAs using 4-wheel tractors and/or combine harvesting
equipment.38

16.       Outcomes would (a) increase the level of mechanization of rice-based farming, (b) promote transformative
economies of scale in productivity and incomes, (b) reduce GHG emissions (though rice stubble incorporation and dryland
cultivation), (c) enhance crop diversification and intensification (through dryland cultivation and shortened production
cycles), (d) incentivize farmers to form clusters and FCAs to avail more efficient mechanized production, and (e) expand
rural commercial activities and job creation (machinery, servicing, logistics, and so on).

RA 2: Enhanced efficiency and resilience of food value chains

DLI 7: Volume of vegetables produced (tons)

17.      Strategic relevance and technical soundness. Despite the established competitiveness of many HVCs in the
Philippines, levels of technology and productivity are typically low across most commodities. Constraints include lack of
quality, disease-free planting material and seeds; low-level production technology; lack of climate-resilient approaches;
market inefficiencies; and substantial postharvest losses through poor handling and lack of cool storage facilities, which
particularly affects vegetables. Thus, vegetable prices are high, while quality and shelf life are poor. Filipinos consume 59
percent more starchy staples than the recommended amount and 78 percent fewer vegetables than recommended. DLI
actions to increase the volume of vegetables will shift the focus from rice productivity to increased production and higher-
quality vegetables—a key element of the country’s food security agenda and a significant export growth opportunity. The
required budget increase would enable (a) more CSA-focused extension encompassing production through postharvest
handling; (b) increased supply of quality planting material; and (c) promotion of modern technologies, for example,
greenhouses, hydroponics, drip irrigation, and soil moisture conserving techniques. The DLR will require an increase in the
volume of vegetables produced using modern technologies, including demonstrations for wider uptake of such
technologies (use of climate-resilient screenhouses, rain shelters, and so on).

18.     Outcomes would (a) increase the quantity and quality of vegetable production, (b) improve food and nutrition
security and (c) improve incomes through increased production, and reduced postharvest losses and spoilage

DLI 8: Volume of perishable agrifood products being processed through new cold storage facilities (mᵌ)

19.     Strategic relevance and technical soundness. Modernizing and transforming the agri-fishery sectors is hindered
by underinvestment in cold storage facilities and logistical interlinks for efficient transport and quality control of perishable
agrifood commodities. The current system relies on small-scale traders who typically pack farm products in plastic bags
and transport them in over-stacked vans, resulting in considerable postharvest losses, spoilage, and poor quality of fresh
produce. PSAT’s innovative approach will support the development of small, cold storage facilities in production areas,
linked to centralized medium and larger facilities. The small and modular facilities are powered primarily by solar and
wind, operated by FCAs, and interlinked digitally to enable an efficient, reefer van (refrigerated vehicle) logistical system.
The DLR will require an increase in the capacity of cold storage facilities under the DA’s storage facility development
program for processing perishable agrifood products.

20.     Outcomes would (a) increase volumes of quality fresh vegetables reaching markets, (b) improve incomes through
reduced postharvest losses and spoilage, (c) increase rural commercial development associated with the expansion of HVC
production and exports (that is, for storage, crating, marketing, and logistics), and (e) expand opportunities for private
sector uptake and investment through demonstration/promotion of innovative green technology for cold storage.

RA 3: Enhanced institutional and policy environment

DLI 10: Number of DA programs implemented through expenditure efficient and multi-year results-based budgeting
(no. of sub-programs)

23.      Strategic relevance and technical soundness. The Agriculture Public Expenditure Review (2023) supported by the
World Bank highlights that DA’s budget processes have favored input subsidies, infrastructure, and agricultural services
for rice production, distorting budget allocations for other commodities, particularly HVCs (vegetables, tropical fruit,
coffee, cacao, and so on). Rice accounts for about 20 percent of the agricultural sector’s production value but receives
over 50 percent of the DA’s commodity program budget. Such expenditures, however, have been largely ineffective in
raising rice productivity, with the Philippines lagging regional neighbors with a relatively stagnant yield growth since the
mid-2000s. The DLI actions will significantly transform the budgeting process to a results-based approach aligned with
multi-year planning targets and outcomes. It will bring about budget strengthening in the DA, promoted by the Philippine
Department of Budget and Management (DBM) since 2007 but not yet implemented. The Program Expenditure
Classification (PREXC) system introduced by DBM enables the measurement of performance—at the output and outcome
levels—for each program agency. This enables resource allocation to ‘results-based’ programs, reduces ‘line item’
controls, and assigns performance indicators at the level of programs. This shift requires the redefinition of the P/A/P,
which used to refer to any program, activity, and project of an agency. Now, it is defined as “integrated grouping of
activities and projects that contribute to a particular outcome of an agency.” DLRs will require (a) in FY2026, the DA issuing
an Administrative Order introducing an expenditure-efficient, multi-year, results-based budgeting approach for, among
others, the NRP;40 (b) in FY2027, the number of NRP components adopting the results-based budgeting approach
introduced under DLR 10.1; and (c) in each subsequent year, the number of NRP components for which the DA disburses
at least 80 percent of its annual budget, based on the indicators adopted under the results-based budgeting approach
introduced in DLR 10.1.

DLI 12: Increased efficiency, transparency and sustainability of Agriculture Sector procurement

27.      Rationale. The recent revision of the Government’s Procurement Reform Act (Republic Act No. 9184) through
RA12009 in 2024 has introduced opportunities for a range of procurement reforms to address loopholes and inefficiencies
in current procurement processes. With about 50 percent of the DA’s budget dependent on procurement, delays in
procurement have significantly contributed to low budget utilization, which has averaged only 70–75 percent. DLI actions
will streamline the procurement process from the current 12 months to just 60 days. DLRs will require (a) in FY2028, the
DA achieves at least 80 percent disbursement rate of the annual budget allocated for procurable expenditures, with
procurement conducted in accordance with Good Procurement Principles;41 (b) in FY2028, at least 90 percent of the
contracts awarded by the DA are procured through a process that complies with PhilGEPS transparency requirements for
both invitation and award notices; and (c) in Year 4, the DA procures at least 40 percent of eligible green procurement
items using green specifications issued by GPPB Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR).

28.     Outcomes would (a) result in more efficient and transparent utilization/disbursement of DA’s budget (by
expediting procurement processes), (b) improve resource-use efficiency (by enabling ‘qualitative economic value’ not the
‘quantitative value’), and (c) include approaches for expanding ‘green procurement’.

29.      Lessons learned and reflected in the program design. While the IPF approach has strengthened DA’s institutional
capacity, rural infrastructure, and enterprise development, a key lesson is that a broader approach is needed to address
the institutional and resource allocation inefficiencies across DA’s many agencies, bureaus, and commodity ‘banner’
programs, which mostly operate independent of each other, and at times with competing objectives. Another key lesson
is that while IPF development objectives have been largely met, many cross-cutting policy, regulatory, resource efficiency,
and climate resiliency issues still need to be addressed and continue to stifle the required transformation of the
Philippines’ largely small-scale, low-technology agrifood sectors. However, the policy changes through the RTL in 2019
present a unique opportunity to change the agriculture’s growth model for which the PforR is well-suited by balancing
quick results with subsequent medium-term actions. This builds on lessons that change is more effectively institutionalized
when the benefits are internalized by those responsible for on-the-ground implementation. The design, therefore,

ECONOMIC ANALYSIS

Assessment Methodology

4.      The program generates both local and global benefits by (a) improving the productivity and climate resilience of
rice farms; (b) enhancing the resilience and efficiency of other agricultural value chains by reducing food loss and waste
while improving product quality; (c) reducing GHG emissions from agriculture, particularly from rice fields; and (d)
strengthening institutional efficiency and delivering capacities.

5.      The analysis quantifies incremental local and global benefits: (a) local benefits include increased paddy yields,
reduced input costs for fertilizer and labor in rice cultivation, lower postharvest losses for rice and other HVCs, increased
income from diversification of rice farms, and improved prices for HVCs and (b) global benefits include the generation of
substantial public goods by reducing GHG emissions from rice fields. The program costs account for all incremental
expenses for technical package adoption, operational expenses for agricultural production, training and capacity-building
costs, and costs of implementing structural reforms included in RA 3.

6.       The economic assessment adopts a CBA approach to quantify benefits from a series of activities in the program-
supported value chains, including rice and HVCs, and global benefits in GHG reduction. It compares a ‘no government
program’45 scenario to a ‘government program’ scenario, with World Bank support. It determines whether the overall
program, which the World Bank partially finances, is socially beneficial after considering economic benefits and costs.
Given the wide range of the PforR program’s intervention, the economic assessment has been conducted for each RA and
results have then been aggregated at the final stage to assess the overall profitability of the program.

mechanization at various cultural operations, including land preparation, seeding, and harvesting, to reduce labor costs
and enhance water-use efficiency; (c) support diversification of rice-based farms to increase incomes and improve climate

9.        Note on irrigation scheme for rice. Overall, evidence on irrigation for rice production shows costs outweigh
benefits, making it an unviable investment (see box 4.1, Briones 202148). The returns on investment are particularly
sensitive to how much irrigation increases cropping intensity while experience from other countries and projects confirms
that irrigation is often more beneficial for diversifying away from staple crops toward higher value-added crops. Adequate
analysis should ensure that irrigation sites are chosen to be financially and/or economically viable, given the “considerable
gaps between potential and actual benefits of irrigation.” (Briones 2021).

DLIs assessed likely E&S effects and significant risks. The preparation stage (October 2024–January 2025) began with data

attached agencies, and 1 attached corporation. In January 2025, site visits took place in 2 RFOs, PhilRice, and 6 FCAs, which
included LGU staff and private sector and civil society partners of FCAs. These were coordinated with the DA’s SPCMAD
and PforR Technical Working Group. The draft ESSA was disclosed by the DA and on the World Bank external website on
February 18, 2025. Public consultations were held on February 21 and March 5, 7, and 10, 2025. Feedback from LGUs, civil
society organizations, and the private sector was considered and addressed in the revised ESSA.

3.       The ESSA adopted the PforR’s 11 exclusionary principles and identified seven excluded activities in relation to
program boundaries and activities. Excluded activities are those dealing with (a) construction of large-scale infrastructure,

rights redistribution.

and extension services. Non-physical activities comprise program-supported policy studies, research, capacity building,
information sharing, and M&E.

 burning) and dryland cultivation (reduced decomposition). Timing of the practices to maximize the GHG reductions will be covered
 under CSA capacity building and knowledge for farmers (DLI 5). Mechanized farming is expected to enhance crop intensification
 (shortened production cycles), which will (a) reduce the demand on resources and inputs (for example, reduced water demand),
 and (b) increase efficiency of input use and application (for example, fertilizer use). The on-farm efficiency is not only a labor saver
 (that is, direct exposure of farmers to the climatic elements) but also enhances the on-farm capacity to minimize harvest losses
 (that is, improved harvest capacity reducing food loss and waste) and the capacity of harvest practices to avoid exposure to climatic
 events. All mechanical equipment distributed by PhilMech are rated Tier III 56 with enhanced energy efficiency.

 considerations, while enhancing food and nutrition security within the Philippines through reduced agrifood wastage and spoilage,

2.      The net GHG emission reduction is measured by the CO₂eq reduced from rice fields. Overall, the program is
expected to reduce 31.1 million tons of CO₂eq, based on calculations using the FAO Ex-ACT tool for GHG emissions (version
9.4.2). Approximately 70 percent of this reduction is anticipated from improved water and nutrient management,
including lower emissions from mechanized direct seeding that enables dryland cultivation and from decreasing the
cropland allocated to rice cultivation through diversification. The remaining reductions will come from changes in input
usage and improved input management. This equates to an average reduction of approximately 1.55 MtCO₂eq per year.

3.       The program is also aligned with the goals of the Paris Agreement on mitigation as well as adaptation and
resilience. The proposed program supports the implementation of the Philippines’ Nationally Determined Contributions
(NDCs) (2021) and NAP (2023). It specifically focuses on agricultural measures that lower GHG emissions from the
Philippine food systems while safeguarding food security and production—actions critical to meeting the goals of the Paris
Agreement. This includes enhanced CSA-informed production and reduced food loss and waste, supporting the NAP
theme for agriculture, fisheries, and food security—Securing Food Supply: Nurture Nature, Sustain Livelihoods. The
program is also informed by the climate priorities and recommendations identified in the World Bank Group’s Philippines
CCDR, as described in paragraph 1.

         •      Assessment and reduction of mitigation risks. The program engages sectors and activities with moderate to
                high emissions risk, particularly agricultural value chains and rice production. The program adopts universally
                aligned activities across multiple DLIs, with DLIs 1, 3, 5, and 7 delivering CSA activities, DLI 8 utilizing
                renewable energy, and DLI 11 delivering an ICT online platform. The remaining DLIs reduce mitigation risks
                to low through sustainable and low-emissions design adoption. This includes (a) adoption of low-GHG
                production methods and CSA practices (DLI 2 and DLI 6), (b) improved fertilizer management and e-vouchers
                (DLI 4), (c) reduction of postharvest loss and waste, including food spoilage (DLI 9), and (d) green
                procurement practices (DLI 12). DLI 10 supports the enabling environment for these DLIs and does not pose
                an emissions risk. Additionally, through crop diversity and promotion of CSA capacity building and methods,
                the program initiates a shift in production preferences, broadening crop production and reducing the
                dependence on traditional and emission-intensive rice production. Each element reduces sector emissions
                and lowers the mitigation risk.
         •      Assessment and reduction of adaptation and resilience risks. The risks57 identified through the climate and
                disaster screening could affect program objectives if unaddressed, particularly agricultural productivity. The
                inherent level of risk from climate hazards to the program is moderate. To mitigate this risk, the program
                grounds its objective in climate-resilient agrifood systems and introduces measures to reduce the impact of
                climate hazards, including the capacity of agricultural institutions to assess risk via climate data (DLI
                11). Beyond systems and mechanisms to respond to climate risks and hazards, additional measures on
                resilience and adaptation have been incorporated into the program to reduce material risks from hazards.
                This includes targeted on-farm inputs that increase resilience (for example, climate-resilient seeds),
                greenfield infrastructure that reduces exposure to climate hazards (for example, greenhouses and
                postharvest cold storages), capacity building that enhances knowledge (for example, CSA support and
                learning), and enhanced government systems (for example, climate data-informed decision-making). These
                measures reduce the climate hazard risk to an acceptable level while ensuring that the institutional
                framework and applicable systems of the borrower can adequately manage climate risks.

                                         ANNEX 9. IMPLEMENTATION SUPPORT PLAN

1.       Implementation will be facilitated through the long-standing development partnership and understanding of
institutional arrangements that the World Bank has established with the DA, with the oversight support of government
partners (see table 9.3). As this will be the first PforR for the Philippines, particular focus will need to be given to fiduciary,
verification, and institutional issues. There is also significant technical detail underpinning RAs 1 and 2, which will need to
be monitored to ensure timely achievement of agreed results. The Manila-based Country Office, with backstopping from
regional and Washington, DC-based staff, is well equipped to support the fiduciary and Environment and Social Standards
(ESS) issues likely to be encountered. Technical issues, including those related to the uptake of climate-resilient approaches,
both for crop production and reduced postharvest losses, may require a range of technical expertise, most of which is
available locally or regionally. Task team leadership would be based in the Manila Country Management Unit (CMU).

2.        World Bank implementation support will be facilitated through a Program Support Office (PMO) to be
established by the DA to oversee implementation. The PMO would be attached to the Office of the Undersecretary for
Special Concerns and Official Development Assistance (ODA) and would be the main point of contact for World Bank
support. Given the front-loading of DLI results to be achieved, and the magnitude of the loan, a substantial level of staff
and specialist consultant support should be anticipated, at least for the first two years of the program. ISMs will need to
be carefully planned and organized with the support of the PMO, considering the broad cross-section of DA programs and
agencies that will have a role in achieving the DLIs and intermediate outcome indicators. Formal ISMs and field visits
covering all aspects of implementation would be conducted every six months. During the first two years, there will be more
frequent follow-ups. Estimated inputs from different specialists at different stages of implementation as well as task team
skills requirements are outlined in tables 9.1 and 9.2.
