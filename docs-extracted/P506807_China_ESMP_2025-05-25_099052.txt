1 Project Background and Preparation of Ethnic Minority
Development Plan

1-1 Project Background

The World Bank Financed Guiyang Green Urban Development Project mainly
implements nature-based solutions to improve ecosystems, reduce carbon emissions
from the transportation and water sectors, enhance the carbon sequestration capacity
of local ecosystems, and improve the accessibility of national wetland parks and green
spaces. The project will provide opportunities for sustainable economic activities
related to environmental restoration, mitigate climate change, enhance climate
adaptation and resilience, and improve urban livability. Under the guidance of a
professional and expert team, the project will also establish and improve Guiyang's
carbon emission monitoring and accounting system and explore the formation of an
innovative demonstration model for Guiyang's green and low-carbon urban
development.

1-1-1 Project Objectives

This project aims to reduce carbon emissions from the transportation and water
sectors, enhance the carbon sequestration capacity of local ecosystems, improve the
accessibility of national wetland parks and green spaces, and provide sustainable
economic activity opportunities related to environmental restoration to mitigate
climate change, enhance climate adaptation and resilience, and improve urban
livability by improving ecosystems and implementing nature-based solutions. Under a
professional and expert team's guidance, the project will establish and improve the
carbon emission monitoring and accounting system in Guiyang and format an
innovative demonstration model for green and low-carbon development in Guiyang.

1-1-2 Construction Content and Activities

The project content includes two parts: one is hardware, such as infrastructure
construction, and the other is software, such as low-carbon planning system research,


                                          1
project management, and capacity building. The project construction content is
divided into three sub-projects: low-carbon planning system and study, Aha Lake
low-carbon and nature-oriented city demonstration area construction, project
management, and capacity building. The composition of the sub-projects and the
specific construction content are shown in Table 1-1. The project construction period
is 5 years (2026-2030), and the project's total investment is about RMB 2.473 billion,
of which RMB 1.8 billion is from the World Bank Financed and RMB 673 million is
from domestic matching funds. The project construction location map is shown in
Figure 1-1.
(1) Sub-project 1: Promoting low-carbon urban transport for livable cities
This sub-project mainly reduces carbon emissions, improves travel convenience and
environmental quality, and creates sustainable urban transportation through intelligent
management, green infrastructure, and policy support. The construction content
includes constructing a slow-moving system, a public transportation system and
charging service facilities, and improving the urban transportation road network. The
specific construction content includes:
①Newly built main line, radial lines, and connecting lines of the greenway around the
lake: The main line is 18.2km long and 7m wide, and the three radial lines are 4.5km
long. The total length of the greenway connection line is 25.3km, connecting the
Xiaowanhe Greenway, rail stations and other areas in Yunyan District to form a
slow-moving network covering the entire area of Aha Lake.
②Construction of cycling stations around the Aha lake. The content of this sub project
is to construct 17 cycling stations, including toilets, safety and emergency response,
environmental sanitation, rest, bicycle rental, barrier free facilities, tourism reception,
first aid, etc.
③Greening and Landscape Project around the Lake. This sub-project mainly involves
the restoration of green plants and landscapes along the greenway. The total area of the
green plant landscape restoration project is 118000 square meters, with 94 landscapes
belts.
④Supporting services and construction of municipal facilities around the lake.
                                             2
Construct supporting water supply, drainage, strong electricity, telecommunications,
and lighting facilities along the lake greenway; Supporting facilities such as science
education, environmental sanitation, rest and recreation, and activities for the elderly
and children; Upgrade and renovate the water supply facilities in the wetland park.
⑤Construction of public transportation system and charging service facilities. New
public transportation facilities and charging service facilities will be built, and existing
roads will be upgraded to improve the traffic conditions and efficiency of roads and
buses.
⑥Improvement of urban transportation network. Constructing or upgrading key
connections around Aha Lake to the main urban area, reducing congestion to the main
urban area of Nanmingyun, thereby improving travel efficiency while reducing
greenhouse gas emissions.
(2) Sub-project 2: Urban solutions to alleviate, adapt, and promote biodiversity
development
This sub-project mainly aims to protect and strengthen urban ecosystems and
biodiversity, enhance the ecological quality of the city, and promote the prosperity of
biodiversity. The construction content includes: protect and strengthen urban
ecosystems and biodiversity; Improve water quality, prevent floods, and enhance
ecosystem resilience; improve the public facilities and infrastructure of Lake Aha.The
construction content includes:
①Wetland Park Biodiversity Monitoring and Protection Project. Implement 5-year
monitoring and protection of major biological groups such as plants, birds, mammals,
amphibians, and reptiles within the Aha Lake National Wetland Park to prevent
invasive species.
②Construction of wetland natural education and popular science education system.
Build a multi-level science and education system within the Aha Lake National
Wetland Park, and create a "borderless natural museum".
③The Aha Lake Bird Habitat and Watershed Ecological Zone Restoration Project.
Restoration of river water ecology and implementation of river shoreline restoration
and protection within the Aha Lake National Wetland Park.

                                             3
④Construction of sewage treatment facilities. Construct sewage interception ditches
and small-scale water treatment and pollution control facilities around Lake Aha to
improve its water quality.
(3) Sub-project 3: Innovative methods & systems and enhanced capabilities
This sub-project mainly supports the research on carbon sequestration and its
methodology, as well as the development and construction of smart platforms and
applications, and the research on climate-smart and nature-friendly urban spatial
planning and urban green low-carbon transportation, laying the foundation for
building Guiyang into a national, regional and global urban carbon emission
monitoring and emission reduction good practice city. The sub-project will also
support the payment for meeting the World Bank's ESF (ESF), Procurement and
Financial Management Requirements, and the daily expenses incurred in Monitoring
and Evaluation (M&E).
①Climate smart and nature friendly urban spatial planning. This project aims to
promote the construction of low-carbon cities in the Aha Lake area and Guiyang city
through ecological management research and low-carbon city design, providing
feasible paths for low-carbon city construction at both theoretical and practical levels.
②Research on carbon sequestration methods and policy support for carbon markets.
Based on the work of karst carbon sink projects, strictly follow the development
requirements of voluntary greenhouse gas emission reduction projects, prepare design
documents for voluntary greenhouse gas emission reduction projects, and form a
methodology for applying for voluntary greenhouse gas emission reduction projects to
the state.
③Research on urban green and low-carbon transportation. Research on the green
travel needs and service improvement strategies of special groups (disabled and elderly)
in Guiyang City, study the policy measures and monitoring system for collaborative
governance of traffic congestion and carbon reduction in Gui'an of Guiyang city,
promote the inclusiveness and fairness of special groups, and enhance the sustainability
of green city development in Guiyang.
④ Capacity building. Provide training to all parties involved in this project to enhance
                                            4
their decision-making, implementation, technical proficiency, and management and
operational capabilities in green and low-carbon city planning.

1-2 Objectives and Methods of Compiling the Ethnic Minority

Development Plan

According to the definition of the World Bank's Environmental and Social Framework,
ESS7 applies to distinct social and cultural groups with the following characteristics:
(1) Self-identification as members of a distinct indigenous social and cultural group
and recognition of this identity by others;
(2) Collective attachment to geographically distinct habitats, ancestral territories, or
areas of seasonal use or occupation, as well as to the natural resources in these areas;
(3) Customary cultural, economic, social, or political institutions that are distinct or
separate from those of the mainstream society or culture;
(4) A distinct language or dialect, often different from the official language or
languages of the country or region in which they reside.
Based on surveys and interviews with ethnic minorities in the affected areas of the
project, local ethnic minorities identify themselves as members of a distinct
indigenous social and cultural group and recognition of this identity by others, and
they have a distinct language or dialect, often different from the official language or
languages of the country or region in which they reside. However, their income

                                              13
mainly comes from wages in the non-agricultural sector, and their livelihoods do not
rely on geographically distinct habitats, ancestral territories, or areas of seasonal use
or occupation, as well as to the natural resources in these areas, and there are no
customary cultural, economic, social, or political institutions that are distinct or
separate from those of the mainstream society or culture. The lifestyle and production
methods of local ethnic groups has no significant difference from mainstream society.
According to the definition of the World Bank ESF framework and the conclusion of
the project's SIA, the affected ethnic minority groups do not meet all four criteria, but
they have different languages, cultures, and customs. In order to minimize the
negative impact of the project on ethnic minority populations, ensure that ethnic
minorities can fully participate in the preparation, design, and implementation of the
project, and benefit from the project, the project office has prepared the "Ethnic
Minority Development Plan".
The local ethnic minority villages implement a system of village self-governance, and
the village committee is a grassroots mass autonomous organization for
self-governance by villagers. It is responsible for handling public affairs and public
welfare undertakings in the village, mediating civil disputes, assisting in maintaining
social order, and reflecting the opinions, demands, and suggestions of villagers to the
people's government. The village committee consists of a chairman, vice chairman,
and members, who are directly elected by the villagers. Based on the survey, the
majority of village directors, deputy directors, and committee members in the affected
villages are from ethnic minorities. Each village committee has at least one female
member, and village directors such as Wushan and Xuechang villages have already
been appointed by ethnic minority women.
According to Chinese law, representatives of county and township people's congresses
are directly elected by voters. In direct elections, voters are divided into several
electoral districts and representative candidates are nominated according to the
electoral districts. Citizens of the People's Republic of China who have reached the
age of 18 have the right to vote and be elected, regardless of ethnicity, race, gender,
occupation, family background, religious beliefs, education level, financial status, or

                                           14
length of residence. These county and township people's congress representatives are
often important spokespersons for the affected population and play a crucial role in
negotiations. In addition, the PMO also respects the traditional leaders of ethnic
minorities, involves them in the planning and implementation of the project, and plays
an active role in mobilizing and persuading local people.
1-2-1 Objectives of Compiling the Ethnic Minority Development Plan
(1) Provide all relevant project information in a manner consistent with the traditional
culture and customs of the ethnic minorities in the project area, fully communicate
and consult with the affected ethnic minorities, understand and analyze their needs,
and incorporate the needs of ethnic minorities for the project into the project design.
(2) Through information disclosure and ethnic minority participation and consultation
activities, the project planners can understand the attitudes of ethnic minorities
towards project construction in advance, optimize project design and resettlement
plans, and eliminate ethnic minorities' doubts and resistance to project construction
through sufficient information communication, so that the project can gain general
support from ethnic minorities.
(3) Take appropriate measures and actions to minimize the project's potential negative
impacts and risks on ethnic minorities and, simultaneously, enhance the opportunities
for ethnic minorities to benefit from the project in a way acceptable to ethnic minority
groups.
1-2-2 Methods and Steps Compiling the Ethnic Minority Development Plan
According to the World Bank's requirements, The PMO conducted field surveys and
visits to the project-affected area from November to December 2024 and January
2025 to prepare the "Ethnic Minority Development Plan." It collected literature and
statistical data on the project-affected area and prepared the Plan using the following
methods and steps.
(1) Through the analysis of literature and statistical data, the characteristics of the
historical and cultural traditions of ethnic minorities in the project-affected area, the
characteristics of the property rights system of land and natural resources, and the
current situation and trends of local social and economic development were

                                           15
summarized.
(2) A symposium on ethnic minority populations was held in the project-affected area,
field surveys were conducted, and in-depth interviews were conducted with
representatives of ethnic minority populations. Through these interviews and surveys,
free and full consultations were conducted with ethnic minorities to understand the
wishes and demands of local ethnic minorities.
(3) The current local ethnic minority development policies were studied, the
consistency and differences between these policies and the World Bank's policies on
ethnic minorities were analyzed, and the policy framework for the project's ethnic
minority development was determined.
(4) Based on investigation and consultation, design a mechanism for public
participation of ethnic minorities, a way to disclose information, establish channels
for ethnic minority complaints and appeals, and plan a monitoring and evaluation
framework for restoring ethnic minority income and living standards.
(5) Based on the conclusions of the SIA, hold meetings with the participation of
ethnic minorities, project owners, and local governments, incorporate the
requirements of ethnic minorities into the project planning, design, and
implementation process, and fully reflect the requirements of ethnic minorities in the
resettlement policy. The PMO and local governments also introduce policies and
measures to support ethnic minorities and vulnerable groups based on local realities to
increase the opportunities for ethnic minorities to benefit from the project.

2-2-1 Customs and Culture of Buyi People
The Buyi people share the exact origin of the Zhuang people and are the indigenous
inhabitants of the southeastern Yunnan-Guizhou Plateau. They were called
"Southwest Barbarians" in the Tang Dynasty, "Fan" and "Zhongjia Barbarians" after
the Song and Yuan Dynasties, and "Zhong Barbarians" in the Ming and Qing
Dynasties. Only after the founding of New China were they called the Buyi people.
The distinctive feature of the Buyi people's residence is that they live together in
groups near mountains and rivers. When choosing a homestead, the Buyi people often
look at "feng shui" and choose a place near mountains and rivers as a homestead. Not


                                                      20
only should it be backed by green mountains, but it should also face the green peaks.
The best backing mountain is a mountain with "lying lion guarding," "green dragon
surrounding," "nobleman riding," and other mountain shapes; facing the hill should be
"two dragons fighting for treasure," "double dragons playing with pearls," "ten
thousand horses returning to the trough," "longevity star shining high" and other
forms. The Buyi people choose auspicious days when building houses and hold
singing and dancing rituals and banquets when raising the beams. Finally, the
ancestral tablets and the kitchen god (charcoal fire) should be brought to the new
house.
The Buyi people believe that young men and women are free to fall in love before
marriage. Unmarried young men and women like to take advantage of the
opportunities of annual festivals, markets, and collective gatherings to talk and sing,
expressing their feelings to each other.
When visiting a Buyi family, you must not touch the shrine and the altar, and it is
taboo to step on the tripod beside the fire pit. The Buyi people are accustomed to
offering wine to guests, and guests should drink more. No one can touch or cut down
the mountain god tree and the big Arhat tree in the Buyi village. The Buyi people
must give gifts in even numbers.
2-2-2 Customs and Culture of Miao People
The Miao people are ethnic minorities with a large population in China. They have a
long history and are widely distributed. The ancestors of the Miao people can be
traced back to the Chiyou tribe, which was active in the Central Plains during
primitive times. Chiyou, in the ancient legend era, is generally revered as the ancestor
of the Miao people today. The ancestors of the Miao people migrated many times
from the Yellow River Basin to Xiang (Hunan), Qian (Guizhou), and Dian (Yunnan).
Today, the Miao people are distributed in Guizhou, Hunan, Yunnan, Sichuan, Guangxi,
Hubei, Hainan, and other provinces (regions), and Guizhou is the "base camp of the
Miao people" with the largest Miao population.
The Miao people used to believe that all things have spirits, worship nature, and
worship their ancestors. Traditionally, the Miao people often regard some giant or

                                           21
strange natural objects as a manifestation of spirituality, so they worship them and
offer wine and meat. The more typical natural worship objects include boulders
(strange rocks), caves, big trees, mountains, and forests. The worship of artificial
objects includes land bodhisattvas, land milk, family gods, bridge sacrifices, wells, etc.
The land bodhisattva is usually made of several stones, which is relatively simple and
is set up at the intersection beside the village or the pedestrian resting place on the
road.
Ancestor worship is vital in Miao society. They believe that although the ancestors are
dead, their souls will always be with their descendants. They must offer wine and
meat to the ancestors during festivals, and even daily meals must be used to worship
the ancestors. Many areas hold ancestor worship ceremonies regularly or irregularly.
The traditional Miao society is superstitious about ghosts and gods, and witchcraft is
prevalent. In addition to being familiar with the sacrificial methods, most wizards can
also tell the genealogy of their clan, major historical events of their ethnic group, and
the route of their migration sources. They know various myths, legends, ancient songs,
and folk stories. Wizards are significant inheritors of the Miao traditional culture and
play the role of intellectuals in the Miao society.
The Miao people have taboos about the day of Wu. After the beginning of spring in
the first month, it is taboo for them to groundbreaking and carry water on the day of
Wu. In these Miao areas, on the day of the death of a villager, people can go up the
mountain to work, but it is taboo to go down to the fields. Today, people in the same
village are forbidden to carry firewood home. Otherwise, the person holding the
firewood will suffer a disaster. Nothing in the house can be sold or lent to others
within one month after the burial. The Miao people also have the custom of not
stepping over children's heads, believing that if a child is stepped over, the child will
not grow tall. Women are also forbidden to sit on the same bench with their elders.
2-2-3 Customs and Culture of Tujia People
The Tujia people are generally considered descendants of the Ba people (the ancestors
of the Ba people lived and multiplied in Sichuan, Chongqing, western Hubei, and
western Hunan for generations). Most Tujia people have lived with the Han people for

                                            22
a long time and started using Chinese and Chinese characters very early. The Tujia
people like to live in groups and live in wooden buildings with stilts. The main room
in the middle of the Tujia stilt house is used for the shrines of ancestors of all
generations, which is the core of family worship.
The Tujia people's religious beliefs include worship and faith in nature, such as land,
rocks, mountains, rivers, and water. In particular, the white tiger is regarded as the
ancestral god. White tiger temples are in various places, with white tiger shrines and
frequent sacrifices. The Tujia people also worship the kitchen god, the land god, the
grain god, etc. The Tujia people pay great respect to their ancestors during the New
Year and festivals and also pay minor respects on the first and fifteenth days of the
lunar month.
Before Tujia women get married, there is a custom of "crying for marriage." If a girl
does not know how to cry for marriage, she will be discriminated against and
ridiculed. This custom of "crying for marriage" has gradually faded. The Tujia people
are forbidden to treat guests with dog meat. After eating at the Tujia people's place,
guests are prohibited from placing chopsticks in a cross shape on the bowl.
When the Tujia people build a house, all the villagers come out to help, and no one
charges a penny. During the busy farming season, when harvesting wheat, planting
rice seedlings, threshing, and winnowing, families with a labor shortage take the
initiative to help each other, and the host family only needs to provide food. This
custom of helping each other has been passed down for thousands of years.
2-2-4 Customs and Culture of Gelao People
The Gelao people have a long history, and the "Bai Pu" from the Shang and Zhou
Dynasties to the Western Han Dynasty have a relationship with their ancestors. Now,
the Gelao people are mainly distributed in Guizhou Province. The Gelao people have
their own language, but only a few can speak it, and Chinese has become the common
language of the Gelao people.
The Gelao people believe that all things have spirits, worship ancient trees (big trees),
and have the custom of "sacrificing trees" or "worshiping trees”. Some areas also have
the custom of "sacrificing mountains”. The hillside where the sacred tree is located is

                                           23
regarded as a holy place by the Gelao people and is highly protected. People are not
allowed to let cattle and horses trample on it, cut down trees on the mountain, or go
into the mountain to cut firewood and grass at will.
2-2-5 Customs and Culture of Dong (Kam) People
The Dong (Kam) People are said to be descendants of the ancient Yue people. The
ancient Yue people have lived and multiplied in the coastal areas south of the Yangtze
River and northern Vietnam for more than 2,500 years. The villages of the Dong
(Kam) People are generally located near mountains and rivers, and the people live
together. Drum towers, wind and rain bridges, and wind and rain pavilions are the
main symbols of Dong villages. The Dong (Kam) People believe in many gods and
worship natural objects. Ancient trees, boulders, wells, and bridges are all objects of
worship. The taboos of the Dong (Kam) People mainly include not eating on the
doorstep and not watching others eat; not lighting a fire on the first day of the first
lunar month; no outsiders allowed to enter the village during the sacrifice; the shrines
for the ancestors in the Dong (Kam) People's homes are regarded as the most sacred
places, and all weapons, knives, guns, swords, halberds, spears, spears, bows, and
crossbows are not allowed to be placed on them.
The ethnic minorities in the project-affected area have some unique ethnic cultural
traditions and customs. Some have ethnic writing, but Chinese is generally used in
daily communication. Since the construction of the Aha Lake Reservoir in 1958, the
ethnic groups have intermarried and interacted frequently since they moved here from
the flooded area. They use a common language daily, and there is no significant
difference between the lifestyles and production methods of the ethnic groups and the
Han population. There are no groups that rely on natural resources used for
generations or habitats left by their ancestors, and there are no ethnic minorities that
are "voluntarily isolated" or experiencing "first contact."

3-1 Analysis of Vulnerability of the Ethnic Minorities

The People's Republic of China Constitution stipulates: "All nationalities in the
People's Republic of China are equal. The state guarantees the legitimate rights and
interests of all ethnic minorities and maintains and develops equal, united, and
mutually supportive relations among all ethnic groups. Discrimination and oppression
against any nationality are prohibited." In the project-affected area, all ethnic groups,
regardless of their population, level of economic and social development, customs,
and religious beliefs, have equal status, enjoy the same rights, and perform the same
obligations in all aspects of national and social life by the law. There is no
discrimination against ethnic minorities regarding language, culture, politics,
economy, etc. However, due to historical and geographical reasons, ethnic minorities
in the project-affected area have some vulnerabilities and differences, and project
planning must fully consider such vulnerabilities and differences. A development
strategy that is more suitable for local culture and customs should be adopted.

3-1-2 Lower Education Level and Less Human Capital
Education is an essential factor affecting the economic vulnerability of regions and
families. Due to the lower income level and the shortage of local, high-quality
education resources, most residents in the project-affected area have a lower level of
education. Most well-educated young people leave the village and go to the city for
development, resulting in the continuous loss of local human capital. The SIA team
found through the survey that the proportion of disabled residents in the villages
around Aha Lake is significantly higher than in other areas. Many families have
disabled people. "Disease leads to poverty" and "becoming poor again due to disease"
are essential reasons for the local economic vulnerability. In many families, once a
member suffers from a significant disease or chronic disease, the family may quickly
fall into poverty and remain in poverty for a long time.
3-1-3 Imperfect Infrastructure and Low Level of Agricultural Production
Organization
Aha Lake has a beautiful natural environment and rich biodiversity, which provides a
good foundation for tourism development. However, the current road transportation
facilities around Aha Lake are weak, the road grade is low, the road network is not

                                                        29
systematic, and the cross-regional channels are not smoothly connected. The sewage
treatment facilities are also insufficient, and domestic sewage in some rural areas is
discharged arbitrarily. In addition, the village construction lacks planning, the roads in
the village are narrow, and the houses are built in disorder, which significantly
restricts the development of the local area. Due to the imperfect infrastructure in the
ethnic minority areas, the local economy is vulnerable. Natural disasters such as
drought and hail often cause agricultural production to decrease, affecting income
stability. In addition, the degree of agricultural organization in ethnic minority areas is
also low. There are few agricultural professional cooperatives in the region, which
makes it difficult to give full play to the advantages of a professional cooperative
economy. Many farmers are still working alone. In addition, they are blocked from
information, have few opportunities for external exchanges and learning, have a weak
market and commodity awareness, and have a low ability to prevent risks. As a result,
they have significant economic vulnerability.
3-1-4 The Social Network Becoming Increasingly Fragile
In the past, the society in the project-affected area was a typical interpersonal network
linked by blood and geography. The family concept was powerful. Not only did
family members have a strong obligation to help and support each other, but due to
long-term interaction and understanding, the trust between neighbors was relatively
high. The emotional connection between residents was close. There were many
community activities, and mutual assistance and cooperation between family
members and neighbors were common. However, in recent years, with the advent of
the Internet era, the social network relationship based on blood and geography in
traditional society is declining and disappearing. In the network society, interpersonal
connections based on virtual digital information have changed the rules for
information exchange between people. Although the Internet has expanded the time
and space scope of people's social interaction, it has also led to the gradual
disintegration of the traditional acquaintance social relationship network with strong
stability and durability. The opportunity for residents to cope with risks through
traditional social network relationships has been dramatically reduced.

3-2 Difference Analysis of the Ethnic Minorities

The Constitution of the People's Republic of China stipulates that "all nationalities in
the People's Republic of China are equal. The state guarantees the legitimate rights
and interests of all ethnic minorities and maintains and develops equal, united, and
mutually supportive relations among all ethnic groups. Discrimination and oppression
against any nationality are prohibited." In China, there is no discrimination between
ethnic groups. However, due to historical and geographical reasons, some ethnic
minority areas not only differ from the Han people in living habits, food culture,
religious beliefs, etc., but also the economic development level of some ethnic
minority areas is relatively backward. Therefore, the Chinese government has been
implementing preferential policies and support policies for ethnic minority areas,
aiming to accelerate the economic and social development of ethnic minorities and
ethnic minority areas, gradually narrow the development gap, protect the excellent
traditional culture of ethnic minorities, and ultimately achieve shared prosperity for all
ethnic groups.
3-2-1 Differences in Culture and Customs
As mentioned in Section 2-2, the Miao, Buyi, Gelao, and other ethnic minorities in the
project-affected area, all have their own traditional culture and customs, reflected in
clothing, diet, housing, marriage, etiquette, funerals, and other aspects. Although the
cultures of various ethnic groups vary greatly and have different characteristics, there
is no distinction between superior and inferior cultures. The government protects the
rights of ethnic minorities to maintain or reform their customs and habits. The
government advocates that all ethnic groups respect the value of their own ethnic
culture and the value of other ethnic cultures, communicate on an equal footing, learn
from each other, and prosper and progress together. For example, the traditional
festivals of various ethnic groups, such as the New Year's Eve and Spring Festival of
the Han nationality and the "March 3" and "June 6" of ethnic minorities, have become
common festivals for all local ethnic groups.



                                            31
3-2-2 Differences in Language and Scripts
In the affected area of the project, some ethnic minorities have their own languages
and scripts, but Chinese is the common language of all ethnic groups. In daily life and
communication, they all use Chinese. The Constitution of the People's Republic of
China stipulates that "all ethnic groups have the freedom to use and develop their
languages and scripts." Article 10 of the Law of the People's Republic of China on
Regional Ethnic Autonomy also stipulates that "the autonomous governments of
ethnic autonomous areas shall guarantee the freedom of all ethnic groups in the area
to use and develop their languages and scripts." Differences in languages and scripts
do not hinder communication between ethnic groups.
3-2-3 Differences in Income and Living Conditions
The income and living conditions in the project-affected area are relatively
underdeveloped. However, this gap is not based on ethnic difference but rather on
regional factors. In recent years, Guiyang has increased its support for poor and ethnic
minority areas by strengthening infrastructure construction in ethnic minority areas,
vigorously developing industries suitable for ethnic minority areas, supporting and
helping ethnic minority areas to develop science and technology education, and
supporting ethnic minority areas to get rid of poverty and move towards development.

3-3 Relations among the Ethnic Groups

The project-affected area is a multi-ethnic area. Each ethnic minority has its customs
and festivals, such as the Miao ethnic group's "April 8th", the Buyi ethnic group's
"March 3", "June 6" and "Jiaochang" festivals. However, in the long-term exchanges,
each ethnic group has absorbed the culture of other ethnic groups. The ethnic groups
use the same language and writing, and intermarriage is widespread, forming a
relationship of "you are in me, I am in you, we share weal and woe, and depend on
each other". Moreover, the populations of various ethnic groups live together and
intersect. There are ethnic minorities living in Han areas and Han people living in
ethnic minority areas. This residential distribution is formed by the exchanges and
flows between ethnic groups in the long-term historical development process. The

                                           32
population of each ethnic group lives in a situation of "large mixed residence and
small concentrated settlement. "A relationship of mutual dependence, mutual
promotion, harmonious coexistence, and joint common development has been formed
between each other. Ethnic groups live in equality and harmony, and there is no
conflict between ethnic groups.

4 Participation and Consultation of the Ethnic Minorities

4-1 Methods to Communicate with Ethnic Minorities

The World Bank requires that ethnic minorities participate in decision-making
throughout the project's planning, implementation, and evaluation. To ensure that
ethnic minorities can freely and fully participate in project planning and
implementation, the PMO mainly adopts the following methods to consult with ethnic
minorities:
(1) Hold focus group discussions in ethnic minority villages. Social assessment
experts guide the focus group discussions, focusing on issues of common concern to
local people. Participants freely express their opinions, and recorders faithfully record
their speeches. From November to December 2024, the PMO and SIA team held
several consultation meetings in affected ethnic minority villages.
(2) Conduct in-depth interviews with some affected ethnic minority residents to
understand the attitudes of ethnic minority communities and residents towards the
project construction. Local residents generally believe that the project is beneficial for
local development, improving their living environment and quality of life.
(3) The PMO will work with local governments to establish institutional arrangements
and supervision mechanisms for ethnic minority participation in ethnic minority
communities. These mechanisms will ensure that ethnic minorities can participate in
the project's design and supervise the construction phase.
(4) Disclose resettlement information to ethnic minorities and protect their right to
know. Important resettlement information will be publicized in the ethnic minority
villages. The Resettlement Action Plan will also be published online for PAPs to query.
The resettlement compensation standards and policies approved by the governments
will be publicized in the villages.
(5) Establish a system and mechanism for handling complaints and appeals from
ethnic minorities and promptly handling their issues.
Ethnic minorities can freely express their views in all these consultation activities.

Through public participation, project decision-makers can better understand the ethnic

and suggestions in advance, which provides valuable information for improving
project planning and implementation plans.

4-2 Disclosure of Information to Ethnic Minorities

The PMO's timely disclosure of resettlement policies, compensation standards, and

government. It is also necessary for the smooth progress of LA and HD. To protect the

disclose resettlement information.
4-2-1 Publishing Resettlement Information Notices in Ethnic Minority
Communities
The PMO requires all localities to promptly publish information on the impact of

4-2-2 Disclosing Information through News Media
The PRO will select appropriate television stations, newspapers, and other media to
timely release information related to the project's construction and resettlement so that
ethnic minorities can understand the project's construction status and relevant policies
and improve the project's transparency.
4-2-3 Negotiating Resettlement Plans with Ethnic Minorities
The project's areas of impact are ethnic minority areas. Local people have unique
production and lifestyles and may set specific values for some resources. During the

5 Impact on the Ethnic Minorities and Measures to Mitigate
Negative Impact

The PMO conducted a SIA to assess the project's impact on ethnic minorities and
identify potential development opportunities and social risks. In November and
December 2024, the SIA team selected villages with many ethnic minority
populations as samples and conducted field surveys in these villages. The assessment
team organized some focus group discussions, listened to the voices of ethnic
minority representatives, and analyzed and evaluated the impact of the project
construction on ethnic minorities.

5-1 Positive Impact on the Ethnic Minorities

According to the SIA analysis, the project construction can bring residents direct and
indirect social benefits. All ethnic groups can share these benefits. The favorable
impacts include:
(1) The project can greatly facilitate local ethnic minority residents' trips to school,
see a doctor, and shop, solving their travel inconvenience. It can also greatly improve
their quality of life and happiness. It can also make it more convenient for citizens to
enter and exit Aha Lake, providing them with a new leisure and entertainment venue.
(2) The project can improve local transportation and logistics conditions, enabling
local ethnic minorities to transport agricultural products and local resources in a
timely and low-cost manner, as well as means of production and consumer goods. The
improved transportation will improve the trading conditions of local ethnic minority
farmers, increase agricultural benefits, and increase their income.
(3) The project can create conditions for the development of ethnic minority tourism
and the prosperity of non-agricultural industries, bring in more tourists, and
significantly improve the income level of residents in ethnic minority areas. The
transportation infrastructure construction included in the project can enhance local
transportation facilities, create an environment for developing non-agricultural


                                           38
industries, and promote regional economic development.
(4) The project can promote exchanges within and between regions and expand the
exchanges between local ethnic minority residents and the outside world, which help
to broaden their horizons, change their ideas, and create conditions for urban capital
to invest in the ethnic minority areas. The project can also promote exchanges
between local ethnic groups, further enhance the sense of identity among ethnic
groups, and be conducive to the harmonious development of local society and ethnic
integration.

5-2 Negative Impact on the Ethnic Minorities

The project construction involves the expropriation of some land and the demolition
of certain residential houses. As the land acquisition follows a linear pattern, the
impact rate of land expropriation does not exceed 10%. The adverse impact on the
production and life of local ethnic minority residents is minimal. However, the project
construction will requisition some people's land and houses, demolish some houses,
disrupt their daily lives, and also have some impact on the traditional ethnic cultural
heritage. Due to the project area being located at the urban-rural junction, it has a high
population density and low per capita land area. Although the project is a linear-type
project and the amount of land acquisition is relatively small, a large number of
households and people are affected. Some of the land to be acquired consists of
unused land owned collectively by village groups, without specific contracted
households. Once such unused land from a group is expropriated, all residents of that
group are counted as households and individuals affected by the land acquisition.
According to the survey, interviews, and analysis, in addition to the adverse impact on
residents (see the "Project Social Impact Assessment Report"), the potential adverse
effects on ethnic minorities brought by the project are mainly the following aspects.
(1) Impact of LA
According to preliminary estimates, the project construction needs to acquire 1,393
mu of rural collective land around Aha Lake, of which 454 mu is cultivated land. The
per capita cultivated land resources in rural areas around Aha Lake are already very

                                            39
small, and the livelihoods of residents who rely on land face challenges.
(2) Impact of HD
According to preliminary estimates, the project will demolish 18,111m2 of buildings,
and cause the resettlement of 59 households (245 people). Rebuilding new houses and
achieving relocation and resettlement are considerable changes in family life, which
require family members to spend time planning. Neighborhood relations will also be
reshaped, and the convenience of going to school, shopping, and seeing a doctor may
also change. Ethnic minorities need to adapt to these new changes.
(3) The Preservation and Inheritance of Ethnic Culture
The project construction will reduce arable land, but it will also create conditions for
developing non-agricultural industries. More residents of ethnic minority villages will
move to urban non-agricultural sectors to find new employment opportunities and
income sources to improve their living standards. At the same time, many migrant
workers from Guiyang will flock to the villages around Aha Lake to rent houses due
to the lower rent. The outflow of the local population and the influx of outsiders will
change the structure of village residents. The atmosphere of traditional customs and
festivals may be no longer as strong as before, and the preservation and inheritance of
ethnic and cultural characteristics will face challenges.
(4) Public participation and information disclosure
Suppose the opinions of local ethnic minorities are not sought during project planning
and implementation. In that case, they will have no effective channels to reflect their
interests and miss the opportunity to optimize the design. In particular, some
vulnerable groups, such as the elderly, low-income groups, and single mothers, lack
interest and motivation to participate in public decision-making, which makes it easy
for their needs and interests to be ignored. Additionally, if information is not
transparent and compensation is unfair in the LA and HD processes, it will cause
conflicts and contradictions among the people.

5-3-1 Landscape Design Highlights the Characteristics of Ethnic Minorities
Ethnic minorities such as the Miao and Buyi have developed their own unique

local materials to create a unique architectural culture for ethnic minorities. In the
construction of the Aha Lake Greenway and the post station, the PMO put forward
precise requirements for the design unit, requiring it to preserve these buildings and
absorb the unique culture of local ethnic minorities fully, combine the landscape, and

constructed with natural materials such as wood and stone. There are corridors and
pavilions on the bridge deck, and some are equipped with benches and tables for

5-3-3 Promoting the Establishment of Ecological Compensation Mechanisms
Ecological compensation is an institutional arrangement to protect the ecological
environment and restore ecological service functions by adjusting the relationship of
interest between the subjects who damage or protect the ecological environment and
internalize the externalities of the ecological environment. The ecological
compensation mechanism can guide stakeholders to jointly invest in ecological
protection (or reduce ecological damage) by charging for behaviors that damage the
ecological environment and compensating for behaviors that protect the ecological
environment. This mechanism is essential to transform "green waters and green
mountains" into "golden mountains and silver mountains" and achieve a win-win
situation between economic development and environmental protection. It is also
essential to constructing the ecological civilization system in the new era. The essence
of the ecological compensation system is to solve externality problems in ecological
environment protection through the transaction of development rights and
environmental rights. When development generates environmental externalities, the
party that benefits from the development should compensate for the infringement and
damage to the environmental rights caused to others; when one party gives up
development opportunities to protect the environment, he has the right to obtain
corresponding compensation.

The total storage capacity of the Aha Lake Reservoir is 72 mln m3. Since its
completion in 1960, it has been one of Guiyang's primary water sources for urban
water supply. The reservoir supplies about 80,000 tons of water per day and supplies
water to 600,000 people. To protect the water source of the Aha Reservoir, the
Guiyang Municipal People's Government demarcated the Aha Reservoir Water Source
Protection Area in 1993 and adjusted the protection area plan in 2005, 2009, and 2017,
respectively. After approval by the Provincial People's Government in 2017, the
first-level protection area of the Aha Reservoir is 10.85km2, the second-level
protection area is 78.6km2, and the quasi-protection area is 90.75km2. On November
11, 2022, the Provincial Water Resources Department agreed to adjust the
development task of the Aha Reservoir from "flood control, urban water supply, and
landscape water supply" to "urban flood control and ecological water replenishment."
On March 21, 2023, the Provincial Government officially approved the cancellation
of the centralized Drinking Water Source Protection Area of the Aha Reservoir.
Since its completion in 1960, the Aha Lake Reservoir has supplied water to Guiyang
City. To protect the water quality of the Aha Lake water source, the government
restricted the use and development of land and natural resources around the Aha Lake,
and non-agricultural industries are underdeveloped. Local ethnic minorities can only
rely on arable land, an essential reason for the low-income level. Although it is no
longer a source of drinking water, Guiyang City will adhere to the overall requirement
of "unchanged water quality goals and unreduced protection efforts." The area around
Aha Lake urgently needs to explore a green development path that is a win-win for
economic development and environmental protection. This project is part of this
exploration. The PMO will work with relevant departments of the Guiyang Municipal
Government to take the opportunity of project construction to promote establishing an
ecological compensation system. All five tributaries of the Aha Reservoir will be
included in the ecological compensation. Monthly fines will be imposed on rivers that
do not meet the standards, and ecological compensation will be given to coastal
villages and residents whose water quality meets the standards and contributes to the
protection of the ecological environment of Aha Lake. The mechanisms will stimulate

track and monitor the implementation of the "Ethnic Minority Development Plan" and
regard the monitoring of ethnic minority development as an essential part of
resettlement monitoring. The monitoring agency will submit reports to the World
Bank every six months.
To ensure that the "Ethnic Minority Development Plan" can be implemented during
the project's implementation, the internal and external monitoring agencies will
continuously monitor the living and production recovery of the affected ethnic
minority population.

6-2-2 External Monitoring of Ethnic Minority Development Plan
(1) Monitoring of the implementation of ethnic minority development policies and
compensation policies
The Independent Monitoring Organization (IMO) will inspect and verify the
implementation of ethnic minority resettlement through field surveys and interviews
with affected people. The monitoring focuses on whether the compensation standards
are implemented following the "Resettlement Action Plan" or the "Resettlement
Policy Framework," whether the compensation funds are paid to ethnic minority PAPs
on time, and whether the project implementation complies with the "Ethnic Minority
Development Plan."
(2) Monitoring of the reconstruction and restoration of ethnic minority housing
The IMO will conduct field surveys in the rebuilding and restoration of ethnic
minority housing. The monitoring focuses include whether the location of ethnic
minority housing reconstruction is appropriate, whether the housing restoration and
reconstruction progress is appropriate, and whether the relocated households' natural
environment and social network relationships have been restored.
(3) Monitoring of the restoration of ethnic minority living standards
The IMO will conduct a baseline survey of PAPs' living standards before the project's

                                                            51
implementation and every year thereafter to assess the recovery of PAPs' income and
changes in the living standards of PAPs. The assessment focuses on monitoring and
analyzing changes in PAPs' income sources and whether PAPs' income has been
restored and improved. In assessing PAPs' living standards, it is necessary to ensure
that at least 50% of the samples are ethnic minority households.
(4) Monitoring of training for ethnic minority PAPs
Resettlement agencies should provide technical assistance and training to ethnic
minorities to better understand resettlement policies and procedures, master more
production skills, and help them restore their income as soon as possible.
(5) Monitoring the protection of traditional architecture and cultural relics of
ethnic minorities. The PMO should prevent the traditional architecture and culture of
local ethnic minorities from being protected. The design institute is required to
incorporate ethnic minority cultural styles into the greenway landscape design, and
the contractor is required to take measures to protect traditional ethnic minority
buildings and cultural heritage.
(6) Monitoring of ethnic minority consultation and information disclosure. During
the implementation of the project, external monitoring agencies should continuously
monitor the negotiation and information disclosure activities carried out in ethnic
minority villages to ensure that the negotiation activities comply with procedures and
that the information disclosure is true and effective.
(7) Monitoring of Health and Safety in Ethnic Minority Communities. The PMO
and external monitoring agencies should monitor and report on the threats to
community health and safety caused by traffic accidents, prevention of disease
transmission, etc. during the implementation of the project.
6-2-3 Report System of Internal and External Monitoring
(1) The internal and external monitoring agencies shall submit an annual monitoring
report to the PMO and the World Bank during the project's implementation. The
report shall describe the project's impact on ethnic minorities, their opinions and

production of affected ethnic minorities. The annual monitoring report shall be

(3) In the post-project evaluation report, the PMO's internal and external monitoring
agencies should arrange special chapters to systematically review and summarize the
implementation of the ethnic minority development plan, evaluate the development
level of ethnic minority villages, and summarize experiences and lessons.
