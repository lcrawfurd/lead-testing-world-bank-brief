  I. COUNTRY CONTEXT AND OPERATION SUMMARY
1. The proposed Green and Resilient Development for a Decarbonized Economy in Peru Development Policy Loan with
a Deferred Drawdown Option (DPF-DDO I) for US$500 million is the first operation in a programmatic series of two. The
series seeks to support the Government of Peru (GoP) in: (i) strengthening climate change (CC) adaptation; and
(ii) promoting decarbonized development in selected sectors. The proposed DPF builds on long-standing collaboration
between the GoP and the World Bank (WB) in advancing the climate development agenda by expanding to new sectors
and complementing reforms supported under the earlier DPF series 1 and the recently approved Fiscal Policy and
Sustainable Growth DPF-DDO series. This operation is particularly relevant for Peru, a country highly exposed to climate-
related events and committed to meeting the mitigation targets defined in its updated Nationally Determined
Contributions (NDCs). Moreover, in the context of high uncertainty and financial market volatility, the GoP values the DDO
instrument as an important part of its debt management strategy and a hedge against increasing global interest rates, the
volatility of international capital markets, and climate shocks. The proposed DPF is also aligned with the GoP’s strategic
plans and the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development’s (OECD’s) Accession Core Principles for Peru, 2
the Peru Country Partnership Framework (CPF) FY2023–27, as well as the World Bank Group’s (WBG) Global Challenge
Program (GCP). 3
2. Since 2014, Peru’s growth has decelerated, shifting towards a new trajectory marked by stagnation in convergence
and poverty reduction. The aftermath of the commodity boom posed challenges for sustained growth, with annual Gross
Domestic Product (GDP) growth falling to 3.5 percent between 2013 and 2019—half the pace of the preceding decade.
The COVID-19 pandemic led Peru into one of the deepest recessions in Latin America and increased the national poverty
rate by 10 percent. While the economy rebounded to pre-pandemic levels in 2021, and poverty declined to 25.9 percent
that year, poverty remains above pre-pandemic levels, hampered by the lower quality of employment and a high rate of
informality. Following a mild recession in 2023 due to adverse weather events, social unrest, and deteriorating business
confidence, the economy is recovering in 2024, and business confidence is at levels not observed since 2021. Over the
medium term, GDP growth is expected to converge toward a rate of about 2.5 percent, below pre-pandemic levels.
3. Despite the current political volatility, the GoP remains committed to transitioning to a decarbonized economy and
strengthening its efforts toward CC adaptation given its overall development benefits. Innovation from low-carbon
technologies attracts private capital investment and green job creation, increasing labor productivity and competitiveness.
Similarly, climate resilient planning and investments can mitigate the risks from economic and welfare losses from climate
events, and support Peru’s overall development and sustainable growth. Peru’s Strategic Plan for National Development
(PEDN 2050), launched in September 2023, outlines the GoP’s vision with a 2050 horizon, which aspires for a more
sustainable management of the territory and natural resources, the implementation of measures to tackle CC and formal
job creation. Peru reinforced its climate commitments with the updated NDC (2020), 4 which was followed by the adoption
of the Law on Climate Change (Law No. 30754, 2021) and the National Adaptation Plan for Climate Change (2021). 5 The
updated NDC commits to not exceed net greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions of 208.8 million tons of carbon dioxide
equivalent (MtCo2eq) (unconditional) and 179 MtCo2eq (conditional) by 2030. It also commits to strengthening CC

burden from climate driven disaster events.
4. Peru’s high exposure to climate-related disasters calls for enhanced efforts to achieve more resilient development.
Nearly half of Peru’s national territory is classified as being highly to very highly vulnerable to natural hazards, 6 whose
frequency, severity, and impact are expected to increase due to CC. In the past two decades, economic losses linked to
natural events surpassed US$4 billion dollars, with El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO)-caused damages exceeding
US$3.1 billion, equivalent to 1.6 percent of the country’s GDP, in 2017. 7 Patterns of territorial development and disorderly
urbanization in Peru are increasing vulnerability, as unplanned occupation of risk-prone areas rises exposure to natural
hazards. Moreover, Peru is highly vulnerable to changing precipitation patterns, with increased risks of extreme flooding
and prolonged droughts. Significant reductions in water availability are likely to be expected as early as 2030 and water
shortages are likely to become more severe as demand for water increases, challenging food security, particularly for
communities reliant on subsistence farming. The country’s disaster risk management (DRM) system also needs to be
strengthened to incorporate CC adaptation and mainstream DRM across administrative layers. By 2030, without further
CC adaptation actions and investments, the income of Peru’s poorest 40 percent could decline by 5.2 percent, and another
0.6 percent of the population could be pushed into extreme poverty. 8 As outlined by the Country Climate Development
Report (CCDR), earthquakes, landslides, droughts, and floods together cause average annual asset losses of 2 percent of
GDP and will cause welfare losses equivalent to 5.2 percent of GDP by 2050 and negatively affect the economy’s
productive structures. The CCDR emphasizes the urgent need for adaptive measures to mitigate CC impacts and protect
the most vulnerable populations while introducing policies to innovate and help increase productivity.
5. Fulfilling its NDC commitments and improving its resilience for a net zero economy by 2050 is critical for Peru’s
overall development and will require tackling those critical high emitting sectors like energy, transport, while increasing
carbon sinks. Various studies have shown that the energy transition will create significant net additional employment in
the energy and associated sectors and a boost to economic growth and overall jobs in the economy through the new
energy investment. 9 In a business as usual scenario net emissions from highest emitting sectors as the energy, transport,
land use, land-use change, and forestry (LULUCF) sectors expected to increase by 83 percent over the next 30 years. 10
However, to fulfill its NDC commitments Peru needs to mitigate emissions from set baselines in the energy sector
(30.1 percent), and LULUCF (47.9 percent), 11 including reducing deforestation and increasing carbon sequestration to
advance climate mitigation actions. Further, the development of low-carbon technologies in the power and transport
sector are opportunities for green value chain creation that can enhance increased productivity and competitiveness,
especially in a context of Clean Border Adjustment Mechanisms, 12 while at the same time creation of green skilled jobs.
6. The proposed DPF-DDO series aims to strengthen and further advance reforms for a more resilient and low-carbon
development in Peru to foster a greener economy. The operation will support reforms in priority areas, as identified by
the GoP’s PEDN 2050 and climate commitments. Pillar 1 aims to strengthen CC adaptation by: (i) strengthening the DRM
system; (ii) enabling risk-informed territorial planning; (iii) incentivizing circular economy approaches in the water supply
and sanitation (WSS) sector; and (iv) facilitating the execution of disaster risk reduction infrastructure and post-disaster
reconstruction efforts by the National Infrastructure Authority (ANIN). Pillar 2 supports climate mitigation efforts in

 II. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK
  A. Recent Economic Developments
7. Peru's macroeconomic policy framework is considered adequate. The Central Bank of Peru’s (BCRP’s) decisive
monetary policy response to the food and fuel price inflation shocks has brought inflation within the target band. The
BCRP has cautiously lowered the policy rate, stimulating demand as inflation declined. The exchange rate remains flexible,
with limited foreign exchange intervention to reduce volatility. International reserves are high at US$83 billion. The
financial sector is sound, supported by appropriate macroprudential policies and strong supervision. Peru's public debt is
low and sustainable, and the country has been able to maintain a low and stable Emerging Market Bond Index (EMBI)
spread during a period of high political uncertainty, providing access to international capital markets on favorable terms.
The government's commitment to the OECD accession process is expected to provide a clear roadmap for ambitious
structural reforms to boost growth and strengthen institutions.
8. In 2024, the Peruvian economy recovered from the 2023 recession. In 2023, the economy contracted by 0.6 percent
due to adverse weather events, social unrest, and deteriorating business confidence. Output started to recover in 2024 as
the cumulative annual growth rate between January and November was 3.1 percent. The economic recovery is broad-
based across all sectors, with fishing, construction, and manufacturing (19.4, 3.6, and 3.0 percent, respectively) being the
most important. The climate shocks related to the ENSO pattern in 2023 dissipated, business confidence reached levels
not observed since early 2021, and there was an improvement in export prices. In addition, strong public investment and
the easing of monetary conditions by the BCRP complemented the recovery. The estimated poverty rate, defined by the
US$6.85 daily income threshold, increased to 33.8 percent in 2023 from 32.2 percent in 2022.
9. The fiscal deficit closed in 2024 at 3.6 percent of GDP; albeit markets and rating agencies-maintained confidence on
the medium-term fiscal stance. In 2023, the fiscal deficit target was not met due to the recessionary scenario, which was
reflected in a 2.3 p.p. decline in government revenues. The deterioration in the government revenue-to-GDP ratio
continued in 2024, widening the budget deficit and reaching a plateau in October. Unlike 2023, when the government did
not adhere to the fiscal rule due to exogenous shocks, this year, it amended the rule to allow a higher fiscal deficit for
2024, raising it from 2 percent to 2.8 percent of GDP. Despite this adverse scenario, public debt (32.6 percent of GDP in
the third quarter of 2024) and sovereign spreads (157 basis points at the end of 2024) remained historically low and among
the lowest in the region. In April 2024, S&P Global Ratings downgraded Peru's local and foreign currency sovereign ratings,
citing political instability as a key risk. In September 2024, Moody's changed its outlook to stable from negative, citing the
recent adoption of political reforms (approval of a second legislative chamber and re-election of legislators) that reflect a
period of consensus between the legislative and executive branches and a reduced perception of political risk. Similarly,
in November 2024, Fitch revised its outlook to stable from negative, noting that fiscal consolidation is the most likely
future scenario.

 B. Macroeconomic Outlook and Debt Sustainability
13. Growth is expected to have reached 3.1 percent in 2024. The approval by Congress of a law allowing withdrawal of
private pension funds (which represented 2.4 percent of GDP) boosted consumers' real disposable income in a context of
declining inflation rates during the second half of 2024. In addition, higher terms of trade, improved business confidence,
easier financial conditions and public investment acceleration supported growth. In the medium term, annual growth is
projected at 2.5 percent in 2025-2026. New investments in the mining sector (e.g. Zafranal, Corani, Reposición Antamina,
and possibly Tía María) and the opening of the port of Chancay at the end of 2024 would help support medium-term
growth.
14. After two years of missing the fiscal deficit target, consolidation is expected for 2025. The expected improvement
in the numbers for 2025 is due to the regularization of income taxes, because of higher mining commodity prices and
profits during 2024, 14 and a relatively moderate growth in the 2025 government budget. 15 The fiscal deficit is expected to
decline to 2.5 percent of GDP in 2025. The recent revision of the fiscal rule also raised the fiscal deficit ceiling from 1.5 to
2.2 percent of GDP in 2025 and from 1.0 to 1.8 percent of GDP in 2026. Over the medium term, fiscal consolidation will
continue, driven by higher tax revenues from increased economic activity, better commodity prices, and a reform-induced
reduction in tax evasion.
15. Peru’s public sector debt is sustainable and resilient to various shocks. The public-debt-to-GDP ratio is projected to
decline slowly, remaining above pre-pandemic levels, but under the 38 percent fiscal rule debt ceiling. A deterioration of
the primary fiscal balance of 2 p.p. of the GDP (one standard deviation shock) for 2025 and 2026 could result in the ratio
of public debt to GDP increasing by over 2 percentage points. A similar scenario outcome is expected if GDP contracts by
more than 3 percent during 2025 and 2026 after a one standard deviation shock. 16 Real interest rate and exchange rate
shocks would have more moderate effects. Only a combined shock (all previous shocks simultaneously) would elevate the
ratio of public debt-to-GDP ratio temporarily above the 38 percent ceiling. GDP growth higher than the projected
2.4 percent average (from 2027 onwards) could easily expedite the debt-to-GDP ratio decline. Total external debt is
projected to continue declining, to approximately 34 percent of GDP by 2027. In the medium term, gross external financing
will remain low, below 5 percent of GDP.
16. The current account surplus reached 1.8 percent of GDP in 2024. This increase reflects an improvement in the trade
balance, supported by higher terms of trade. The economic recovery in 2025 explains the expected decline in the current
account surplus. In terms of financing sources, foreign direct investment inflows are expected to be the main source as
some medium-size projects (e.g. the mining projects of Zafranal, Corani, Reposición Antamina, and possibly Tía María)
enter their execution phase and are projected to converge to 2.5 percent of GDP in the medium term.
17. The economic outlook for Peru is subject to significant but manageable risks. Domestic risks related to political
uncertainty will continue to drag on economic growth and the presidential election in 2026 will add a layer of uncertainty
that could undermine private investment. If the government fails to adjust its fiscal accounts over the medium term, it
could weaken the credibility of fiscal policy, with potential implications for the market outlook for debt. Lack of progress

 C. IMF Relations
18. The GoP maintains an active dialogue with the International Monetary Fund (IMF) on macroeconomic policy. The
IMF supported the authorities' decision to exit the Flexible Credit Line arrangement when it expired in May 2024, given
the balanced risks and the strength of Peru's buffers. In May 2024, the IMF concluded its Article IV consultation with Peru,
and consultations for the 2025 Article IV are expected to take place in the first half of 2025. The IMF Board noted that
Peru's very strong economic fundamentals and institutional policy framework, as well as its sustained track record of
implementing very sound macroeconomic policies, have helped the country absorb large adverse shocks related to climate
and social turmoil. To revive growth, structural reforms to boost productivity and a commitment to strengthen the
effectiveness of governance institutions are needed. A renewed structural reform agenda in the context of the OECD
accession process will be crucial to improve the business climate and promote sustainable and inclusive growth.

 B. Prior Actions, Triggers, Expected Results and Analytical Underpinnings
PILLAR I: STRENGTHENING CLIMATE CHANGE ADAPTATION
25. Pillar I promotes strengthening CC adaptation by supporting priority reforms to bolster the national DRM system, the
country’s institutional capacity for risk-informed territorial planning, a more resilient provision of WSS services, and the
implementation of investment programs to reduce disasters risks and enhance CC adaptation.
Prior Action # 1. To strengthen the SINAGERD, the Presidency of the Council of Ministers (PCM) has modified Law No. 29664 and its regulations to: (i)
introduce principles of disaster risk (DR) prevention; and (ii) strengthen institutional capacity through professional certification in DRM.

26. Rationale. Peru’s geographical location and environmental conditions make it vulnerable to seismic, geodynamic, and
hydrometeorological events. 19 CC is expected to make climate events more frequent and more intense, in turn impacting
economic development and hindering poverty reduction efforts. Global experience shows that exposure to disaster risk is
correlated with high instances of poverty, 20 while analysis in Peru has revealed both high relevance of DRM agenda to
development, and institutional challenges associated with it. 21 Law No. 29664, enacted in 2011, established the SINAGERD
and outlined its objectives, composition, and functioning. The SINAGERD functions as a collaborative, interinstitutional
system that encourages participation from all levels of government, the private sector, and communities in DRM, but was
missing until now the incorporation of CC adaptation measures. It aims to identify and prioritize preventive measures,
reduce risks, and ensure disaster preparedness and response. In 2021 and 2022, the GoP approved the National DRM
Policy and DRM Plan, which acknowledged the urgent need to strengthen and reinforce the SINAGERD in response to the
continued risk posed by CC and limited local capacity to manage disaster risks. The lack of incorporation of DRM and CC
adaptation approaches into institutional planning instruments has led to a regular occurrence of emergencies and
disasters, due to the lack of a DRM-preventive approach, particularly during the increased recurrency and frequency of
ENSO events. Also, there is a need to strengthen enforcement mechanisms, and who is responsible for enforcement, to
ensure compliance with the law.
27. Substance of the prior action. To address the identified gaps and include CC adaptation into DRM planning, on
November 24, 2023, the Borrower issued Legislative Decree No. 1587, which modifies Law No. 29664. It introduces: (i) the
principles of DRM prevention to manage the emergence of more frequent and recurrent climate related disaster risks;
(ii) the structure and responsibilities of the National Council for DRM, regional and local governments; and (iii) the
mandate of professional competency certification, and enforcement mechanisms to ensure compliance and
accountability. The purpose of these modifications is to strengthen the SINAGERD by emphasizing principles such as
prevention which will be reflected in the effective integration of DRM and CC adaptation into regional risk prevention,
reduction and emergency response plans. Additionally, on June 6, 2024, the Borrower approved the Supreme Decree No.
060-2024-PCM, which updates the SINAGERD regulations requiring regional and local governments to prioritize risk
prevention and reduction; ensure officials have proper certifications; and develop specific plans for risk prevention,

Prior Action # 2. To strengthen land use and territorial planning incorporating DRM and CC adaptation, the PCM has submitted to Congress the Land
Use Planning Bill, that: (i) establishes the National Land Use Planning System (SINADOT); (ii) clarifies roles and facilitates coordination among various
authorities responsible for land use planning; and (iii) integrates DRM and CC adaptation across all land-use planning instruments.

30. Rationale. There has been little progress in land use or territorial planning in Peru in the past decades given the lack
of an adequate regulatory framework and governance system for decision-making on sustainable land occupation.
Constraints in the regulatory framework relate to the lack of: (i) a clear definition of the regulatory power over land-use
planning; (ii) a clear assignment of responsibilities for land-use planning in the current institutional system; and
(iii) articulation of central and local government responsibilities, powers, and instruments with regard to land-use
planning. 22 Land occupation and development in Peru have therefore been led by informal expansion processes driven by
population seeking to address their housing needs, even in areas highly exposed to climatic hazards. Unplanned
occupation of the territory is increasing vulnerability of low-income households and economic activities, diminishing their
resilience and dampening their capacity to be adaptive to CC. This process is also straining ecosystems and its services,
and their capacity to regenerate, thus increasing degradation and biodiversity loss. 23 To regularize land development and
prevent occupation of areas exposed to elevated risks of natural disasters and CC, Peru needs a system that clearly defines
land-use planning instruments and their hierarchies, and assigns clear responsibilities for their development,
implementation, and enforcement.
31. Substance of the prior action. On August 16, 2023, the Borrower submitted to Congress the Land Use Planning Bill 24
that proposes the creation of the SINADOT—a governance system under the leadership of the PCM—to better articulate
land-use planning instruments and facilitate coordination among territorial interventions and decision-making among
different sectoral authorities and levels of government. The new regulatory framework encourages the sustainable
occupation of the territory through the orderly allocation of land-use rights, and adequate natural resource management
under a comprehensive territorial approach, which includes assessments of high-risk DRM and CC adaptation. The
issuance of the regulatory framework will reduce vulnerability to climate events (e.g., hydrometeorological events, floods,
etc.) and help make the territory safer, greener, and more resilient by supporting the identification of risk-prone areas
and informing infrastructure investments and urbanization. The Bill also defines the roles and responsibilities of different
actors within the system and outlines how land-use planning instruments should articulate. As the leading regulatory body

PILLAR II: PROMOTING DECARBONIZED DEVELOPMENT IN SELECTED SECTORS
44. Pillar II promotes a transition to greener and low-carbon development in those high GHG emission sectors to support
Peru’s overall development and increase its competitiveness and job creation. It does so by targeting Peru’s high emitting
sectors: energy, including transport (responsible for 30.1 percent of emissions, with transport accounting for 14.7 of those

for electromobility; and improving control and surveillance of forests to protect carbon sinks.
Prior Action # 5. To promote decarbonization of the power sector and private sector investment in renewable energy, the MEF has sent to Congress

45. Rationale. Although Peru is endowed with world-class solar and wind power resources, it has limited NCRE power

short- and medium-term growth in electricity demand—especially given the lower cost and faster deployment of solar

commitments; (iv) promoting private investment, in turn increasing competition in the power market; and (v) contributing

Trigger # 6. To promote sustainable urban land management and financing mechanisms the MVCS has issued regulations which (i) establish urban
management and land value capture instruments; and (ii) introduce a new set of financial incentives to promote the supply of Social Interest Housing
(SIH) through Supreme Decrees.

53. Rationale. Almost one in four Peruvian households face a housing deficit. 52 Due to the lack of available affordable
housing, most low-income households meet their housing needs through illegal land occupation, with self-constructed
poor-quality housing in cities’ outskirts. 53 In the past 20 years, 93 percent of the urban growth in the country has been
driven by informal homebuilding. 54 Further, high land prices in the central denser areas of cities push developers to build
social housing on cheaper land, at urban peripheries far from services, public transport, and employment opportunities.
Both housing supply and demand for lower-income households are driving low-density urban expansion. Recent studies
of urban expansion provide evidence of the links between sprawl and GHG emissions. A 1 percent increase in the share of
population living in low-density suburban areas is associated with a 0.41 percent increase in GHG emissions in Latin
American cities (this increase reaches 0.8 percent for cities with population above 1 million and 1.3 percent for large cities
like Lima). Urban sprawl drives up the carbon footprint due to higher transportation needs, congestion, and the higher
energy cost of providing basic services; it also reduces economic potential and economic dynamism of urban areas.55 By
contrast, a 1 percent increase in population density is associated with a reduction of 0.58 percent in per capita CO2
emissions. 56 Urban expansion also often leads to occupation of land exposed to natural disaster risks, which is made worse
by the low quality of self-constructed houses.
54. There are important policy gaps to address barriers preventing the private sector in Peru from revitalizing and
densifying urban centers. The lack of a regulatory framework for urban renewal has limited private investment in the
provision of centrally located affordable housing. A critical aspect to break this vicious cycle is to introduce a new toolkit
of incentives and land management instruments to facilitate redevelopment of centrally located but underutilized land
plots and encourage the private sector to develop housing (including affordable units) in city centers. There is also a need
to expand financing instruments for the provision of affordable housing, without which, urban growth will continue to be
led by self-built homes in available plots.
55. Substance of the Trigger. With the aim of promoting the provision of decent, affordable, adequate, and sustainable
housing while fostering low-carbon urban development, the MVCS has prepared draft regulations of the Sustainable
Urban Development Law to: (i) establish urban management and land value capture instruments; and (ii) introduce a new
set of financial incentives to promote the supply of SIH. Together, they are expected to provide a new regulatory
framework that will promote a denser, more compact, and inclusive urban development. The new urban management
and land value capture instruments will boost local governments’ urban management capabilities through the provision
of a set of land management and financing instruments for urban transformation and for regeneration of centrally located
but underutilized lands. In addition, these reforms are expected to support the reduction of the housing deficit with
instruments that incentivize densification, a more compact urban development, reducing the need to invest and expand
trunk infrastructure to urban peripheries and supporting the use of public transit modes and existing urban amenities.

57. Rationale. Deforestation in the Peruvian Amazon results from multiple factors, including limited capacity of regional
governments for monitoring and controlling forest resources. Despite the existence of the Forestry and Wildlife Control
and Surveillance National System (SNCVFFS, for its acronym in Spanish), the actual operational monitoring and
enforcement must be implemented at the regional level to be effective. To achieve this, each regional government in the
Peruvian Amazon must establish a MRCVFFS and a Forestry and Wildlife Information Management Unit to have
appropriate tools and be fully operational to monitor and control illegal forestry and related activities impacting
deforestation and land degradation. In Peru, out of the seven MRCVFFs established, 57 only two are fully operational with
active Information Management Units equipped with geographic information systems (GIS) and datasets to effectively
monitor and control deforestation. The absence of fully operational Forestry and Wildlife Information Management Unit
hinders the forest monitoring and control capacities of regional governments, their capacity to act on reported illegal
forestry activities, and effectiveness of regional response mechanisms, all of which are central to addressing deforestation
and illegal forest activities. 58
58. Substance of the prior action. To strengthen forest monitoring and control of illegal deforestation in the Amazon, the
Borrower, through the regional governments of Ucayali and Loreto, has issued regional government resolutions,
establishing and expanding their respective Forestry and Wildlife Information Management Units responsible for providing
satellite georeferenced data to the MRCVFFSs. This PA addresses critical gaps in regional-level implementation of the
SNCVFFS by ensuring regional authorities are equipped with the infrastructure, funding, and clear mandates needed to
fulfill their monitoring and control roles over illegal forestry and forest degradation, including the availability of geospatial
data for detecting and addressing illegal forestry activities. The PA’s focus is not only on improving monitoring capabilities
but also on fostering better coordination within the SNCVFFS, enabling more effective responses to complaints of illegality.
While outcomes such as reduced illegal deforestation are expected in the long term, immediate benefits lie in
operationalizing regional monitoring systems as an essential foundation for future progress. The PA also supports the
operationalization of the National Forest and Wildlife Policy by enhancing institutional and operational capacities. Through
this PA, the Forestry and Wildlife Information Management Units in Loreto and Ucayali will cover 57 percent of the
remaining forests of the Peruvian Amazon, enabling greater oversight and action in areas prone to deforestation. The PA
directly contributes to Peru’s emission mitigation and decarbonization goals, by strengthening regional monitoring and
control capacities to increase prevention and protection of forestry, and reduce illegal forestry and forest and land
degradation, thereby reducing emissions from deforestation and increasing conservation of carbon sinks.

 V. SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION
74. The overall risk rating for the proposed operation is assessed as Moderate. The key risk ratings are included in the
table below. The major risks identified include: (i) political and governance; and (ii) institutional capacity for
implementation and sustainability.
75. Political and governance risks are considered Substantial. Peru is experiencing significant political volatility,
characterized by frequent turnover of high-level government officials at the ministerial level. This trend is likely to continue
during the implementation period of the proposed DPF program, which poses a risk of slower reform implementation,
and/or a lack of continuity of GoP programs due to shifting priorities. To mitigate these risks, the operation includes
measures such as: (i) ensuring strong commitment from the administration to the proposed reforms through ongoing
policy dialogue; (ii) maintaining continuous engagement with key government bodies to ensure that new officials are
informed of the Program’s objectives and intended outcomes; and (iii) closely monitoring potential political and
governance risks throughout implementation and the use of adequate mitigation strategies, when necessary. Given that
political turnover cannot be fully anticipated or mitigated, its impact on achieving the operation’s development outcomes
is considered Substantial (residual risk).
76. Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability risk is rated Substantial due to the involvement of
multiple agencies under the DPF operation. The success of the proposed reforms will require a high level of coordination
among line ministries, specialized agencies, and regional and local governments. Coordination among agencies in Peru is
often weak, and building consensus can be time consuming, potentially leading to delays in implementation. To address
these risks, the operation prioritizes reforms that benefit from long-standing policy dialogue and TA from the WB and
other donors. Additionally, certain reforms, such as those supported by the new SINAGERD Law, the draft Territorial
Planning Law, and forestry reforms are expected to enhance coordination mechanisms by clarifying responsibilities and
introducing new measures to enforce accountability for policy implementation. Effective mitigation will require
heightened coordination among line ministries, specialized agencies, and regional and local governments. However, the
impact on achieving the operation’s development outcomes remains Substantial (residual risk).
