                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     This Project is the first phase in the Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) and focuses on improving climate and
                     water information management, enhancing water security, irrigation services, and water supply and sanitation in two
                     basins, Aden-Tuban and Wadi Hajir. The Project includes four components designed around a robust systems approach
                     that responds to the complex, FCV context in Yemen. These are informed by deep analysis and contextual awareness of

                     the key issues in the water and agricultural sectors and include a high degree of flexibility built around a combination of
                     top-down and bottom-up interventions. These components are 1: Climate and Water Information Management
                     Systems (CWIMS), 2: Improved Integrated Watershed Management and Irrigation, 3: Resilient Water Supply &
                     Sanitation Systems and WASH Services in selected areas and 4: Project Support, Management, Evaluation and
                     Administration. The Project will be implemented through UN agencies that exhibit technical understanding and

                     APA under the Procurement Framework Policy Section III.F. Under the Project, UNDP and UNOPS will serve as grant
                     recipients and implementing entities. These agencies have robust and adaptable implementation arrangements tailored
                     to local needs and will strengthen the capacity of local partners, whose past commitment demonstrates strong
                     potential. The two UN agencies will provide technical assistance and training to local institutions, progressively
                     transferring implementation technical capacities to them while retaining overall fiduciary responsibility and
                     accountability. Local partners, high-performing private contractors, and community groups will be engaged for
                     implementation. The Third-Party Monitoring Agents (TPMAs) will be engaged in monitoring and verification of all
                     Project interventions, conducting quarterly supervision visits to Project sites for technical, safeguards, and fiduciary
                     verifications.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The operation is designed for Yemen’s highly complex FCV context, where protracted conflict, institutional
                     fragmentation, and recurrent shocks have severely degraded services and governance. It adopts an area-based, basin-



Public Disclosure
                     level approach rather than nationwide coverage, focusing initially on two priority basins -Aden–Tuban and Wadi Hajer -
                     located largely in IRG-governed areas. These basins encompass urban, peri-urban, and rural settings and reflect distinct
                     water security challenges with downstream interfaces to coastal zones along the Gulf of Aden. In the Aden–Tuban basin,
                     water demand already far exceeds supply; rainfall is declining, groundwater tables are dropping by about one meter per
                     year, saline intrusion is spreading, and traditional irrigation leads to substantial losses. In contrast, Wadi Hajer -Yemen’s
                     only perennial wadi- has a large catchment with substantial runoff to the sea but exhibits deep groundwater in upper
                     reaches and relies heavily on spate irrigation for agriculture. Climate projections suggest both opportunities (more
                     precipitation enabling water harvesting) and risks (longer dry periods and more intense flood events), heightening the
                     need for resilient and adaptive management.
                     The ecological setting spans fertile deltas, wadi corridors, and coastal interfaces. While formal protected areas are not
                     highlighted in the initial phase, the program emphasizes landscape stabilization and soil conservation-rehabilitating
                     terraces, reinforcing wadi banks, and deploying nature-based measures (vegetative cover, low-impact retention works)
                     to reduce erosion and flood damage, while protecting productive land and livelihoods. Activities in Aden–Tuban will
                     have implications for coastal reaches flowing to the Gulf of Aden, requiring attention to salinity, sediment transport, and
                     water quality in downstream ecosystem.

                     The project presents significant opportunities to build resilience and reduce risk through integrated climate and water
                     information systems (CWIMS), watershed management, and climate-smart irrigation. Nature-based solutions will
                     stabilize wadi banks and terraces; water harvesting and conveyance improvements will reduce losses; and targeted
                     investments in resilient water supply and sanitation systems will improve service reliability and public health.
                     Strengthened local institutions -Water Basin Committees (WBCs), Water User Associations (WUAs), Agriculture
                     Cooperatives, and service providers- are central to sustained outcomes and inclusive delivery.

                     Screening and selection of basins and site-specific investments are anchored in a prioritization framework that considers
                     security and accessibility; severity of water stress and food insecurity; vulnerability to climate hazards; institutional
                     readiness (including civil society participation and women’s accessibility); population needs; data availability; and
                     investment sustainability. This framework enables adaptive scaling as conditions permit and supports transparent,
                     inclusive decision-making across diverse stakeholder groups.

                     In the Aden–Tuban basin, actions will target chronic scarcity, groundwater depletion, saline intrusion, flood exposure,
                     and system inefficiencies. In Wadi Hajer, efforts will build on its perennial flow regime to enhance spate irrigation
                     productivity, reduce losses, protect soils, and increase local food production without intensifying groundwater
                     abstraction. In both basins, the package includes CWIMS build-out, watershed management planning, irrigation
                     improvements, capacity building for O&M, and urban WSS service restoration and utility strengthening, with targeted
                     hygiene and water quality measures in critical systems.
                     The phased programmatic structure allows iterative learning and scale-up. The first operation lays the CWIMS and pilot
                     basin foundation, with subsequent projects deepening and expanding interventions in Aden–Tuban and Wadi Hajer and,
                     subject to enabling conditions and resources, into additional basins. This sequencing is intended to maximize impact,
                     manage operational risks, and progressively transition responsibilities to strengthened local institutions as capacity and
                     security contexts improve.

                     Yemen is the poorest country in MENA and remains in a protracted humanitarian crisis driven by a decade of conflict,
                     economic collapse, and climate shocks. Around 80% of households require humanitarian assistance; approximately 74%
                     of the population was below the national poverty line in 2022; and an estimated 4.5 million people are internally




Public Disclosure
                     displaced, with limited income sources and weak services. Public institutions are fragmented across rival authorities,
                     undermining service delivery and fiscal capacity. Rapid population growth and a very young age structure intensify
                     pressure on basic services, especially water and sanitation, health, and livelihoods.

                     Yemen also faces absolute water scarcity, with withdrawals far exceeding renewable resources. Groundwater aquifers
                     are rapidly depleting, and contamination risks are high. Major cities face extreme stress and struggle to ensure safe
                     drinking water. Women and girls—who are central to household water management—bear disproportionate burdens,
                     traveling longer distances and facing safety risks to collect water.

                     Conflict has fragmented water governance, weakened utilities, and increased reliance on humanitarian provision, while
                     reduced household purchasing power limits paid access to services. Rural households dependent on rainfed agriculture
                     and small-scale irrigation are especially vulnerable to climate and conflict shocks. Urban poor also face affordability
                     barriers to water and sanitation due to currency depreciation and low incomes. A youthful population and large
                     numbers of internally displaced people are intensifying pressure on services and jobs, amid deteriorating human capital.

                     Interventions in prioritized basins aim to bolster climate- and water-information management, improve irrigation
                     service delivery for livelihoods, and expand access to resilient water supply and sanitation services (WASH) while
                     actively managing social risks and ensuring equitable, conflict-sensitive targeting and stakeholder engagement.

                     II. SUMMARY OF ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL (ES) RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                         Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                  Substantial
                     The environmental risk is assessed as substantial due to the project's support for several interventions that may
                     involve various environmental risks and impacts. While the anticipated risks and impacts are generally site-specific,
                     reversible, and of low magnitude, they require appropriate mitigation measures to address them effectively. Notably,
                     interventions under sub-component 3.1, "Restoring Access to and Improving Quality of Water Supply and Sanitation
                     (WSS) Services," will involve restoring and expanding access to WSS services. These activities include the
                     rehabilitation and construction of medium- to large-scale WSS infrastructure, such as pipelines, networks, water
                     treatment plants, wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs), decentralized desalination plants, water wells, pumping
                     and booster stations, and related civil works. The environmental impacts of these activities, whether on new or
                     existing sites, are expected to be site-specific, limited, and manageable. Common environmental concerns include
                     construction waste, wastewater, dust, noise, traffic deviations, and occupational health and safety risks. Although no
                     large-scale, significant, or irreversible impacts are anticipated, some interventions may pose substantial risks, such as
                     exposure to sewer gases during the rehabilitation of sewer networks or WWTPs. Importantly, these interventions are
                     expected to yield significant positive outcomes for both people and the environment. They will play a crucial role in
                     improving basic living conditions, stimulating local economic activity, and enhancing Yemeni cities' resilience to
                     climate change. Furthermore, the project measures to control floods, enhance groundwater recharge, and improve

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     Social risks are substantial. Key issues include: (i) exclusion of vulnerable groups and elite capture of benefits—such as
                     infrastructure, services, jobs, and training—by powerful actors, leaving women, IDPs, poor/landless households, and
                     marginalized communities behind; (ii) labor and working conditions, including poor standards, OHS incidents, and
                     risks of child labor in civil works, Cash-for-Work, and Community Contracting; (iii) SEA/SH and protection risks, which
                     are heightened at work–community interfaces, among both male and female workers, and during beneficiary
                     selection at water points and services, particularly affecting women and girls; (iv) security risks in areas affected by
                     conflict or instability, especially during field operations, affecting communities and workers; (v) temporary land use
                     and access impacts for contractor camps and equipment storage (with private land acquisition and/or involuntary
                     resettlement excluded); (vi) cultural heritage impacts, particularly at the Aden Cisterns; (vii) temporary service
                     disruption and inequitable access during Water Supply and Sanitation and WASH rehabilitation, including affordability
                     barriers, SEA/SH safety concerns at water points, integrity risks for trucking/fuel, and resistance to women’s
                     participation; and (viii) unequal access and gatekeeping, exclusion of local actors such as Water User Associations
                     (WUAs) and academia from basin planning, and data-governance gaps. Mitigation measures include: (i) regularly
                     updating a Stakeholder Engagement Plan with targeted, culturally appropriate outreach; transparent, criteria-based
                     beneficiary selection; and a project-wide, accessible, survivor-centered GM for sensitive complaints (including
                     SEA/SH); (ii) adoption and enforcement of LMPs, Codes of Conduct, OHS measures and training, proportional site-




Public Disclosure
                     specific ESMPs, and continuous ESHS monitoring/reporting per the ESCP; (iii) a standalone SEA/SH Action Plan;
                     staff/contractor training; an accessible survivor-centered GM for SEA/SH cases with clear referral pathways; gender-
                     segregated consultations using female facilitators; and design improvements (e.g., lighting, safe access) and women-
                     centered roles in service delivery; (iv) a Security Management Plan (SMP) aligned with UNSMS, conflict-sensitive
                     engagement, strengthening local water governance and dispute mediation via WUAs, Water Basin Committees
                     (WBCs), and Irrigation Councils (ICs), phased works, and proactive communication; (v) a Resettlement Framework (RF)
                     with clear procedures for minor/temporary land use and screening out subprojects requiring private land acquisition
                     and/or involuntary resettlement; (vi) ESMF screening and site-specific ESMPs; CFW and Community Contracting
                     manuals with MIS oversight; OHS/PPE, chance finds procedures, heritage-sensitive methods, participatory water
                     allocation via WUAs/ICs; and a robust GM; (vii) phased works with alternative supply, pro-poor connection support,
                     safe and dignified facility designs, transparent trucking inputs, and targeted outreach for IDPs and marginalized
                     groups; and (viii) inclusive, multi-stakeholder engagement via the Ministry of Water and Environment (MWE), System
                     Management Unit, formalized basin coordination, transparent prioritization, and gender-responsive capacity building
                     (e.g., training on CWIMS, O&M of monitoring stations, and decision-support tools).
