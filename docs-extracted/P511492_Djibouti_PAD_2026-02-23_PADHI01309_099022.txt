      I.    STRATEGIC CONTEXT

  1.       This Project Appraisal Document outlines the rationale and objectives of the proposed Djibouti Groundwater
  Resilience and Water Supply Project (DJIRESA – Projet Djibouti pour la Résilience en Eaux Souterraines et
  l'Approvisionnement), Phase II of the Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) supporting the Regional Horn of
  Africa Groundwater for Resilience Program (P512774). The MPA was approved by the World Bank’s Board of Executive
  Directors on May 10, 2022, for an overall financing envelope of US$ 455 million, along with a first operation in the
  amount of US$385 million for the Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia, the Republic of Kenya, and the Federal
  Republic of Somalia, and the Inter-Governmental Authority on Development (IGAD) as Phase I of the MPA (P174867).
  The overall MPA Program aims to enhance sustainable access to and management of groundwater resources across the
  Horn of Africa’s borderlands and follows a common structure built around two core components: (i) delivering inclusive
  groundwater services in the borderlands of the Horn of Africa (HoA), and (ii) generating information on groundwater
  and strengthening regional and national groundwater institutions. This second phase of the MPA reflects the
  Government of Djibouti’s expressed interest in advancing groundwater resilience and water security in Djibouti. To host
  phase II with a duration of five years, the closing date of the MPA has been extended by two years to June 30, 2031.

 A. Country Context
  2.       Djibouti is a lower middle-income country with a total population of 1,168,000 (2024) 1 that plays a pivotal
  economic role in the Horn of Africa. Djibouti’s economy is centered on port operations, logistics, telecommunications,
  and banking, supported by revenues from foreign military bases. The country’s stability and strategic geographic position
  at the crossroads of key maritime routes have made it a regional hub for security cooperation and a refuge for displaced
  populations from neighboring conflict-affected countries. Over the past two decades, Djibouti attracted substantial
  foreign direct investment, amounting to US$2.3 billion between 2000 and 2020 2, and saw steady economic growth,
  attaining a per capita GDP in 2024 of US$3,5003 which represents a growth rate averaging 4.4 percent annually since
  2000. However, despite the economic progress, Djibouti continues to face persistent challenges of poverty, inequality
  and unemployment. The latest national household survey carried out in 2017 showed that 21 percent of households
  lived below the national poverty line, and 44 percent fell below the international lower-middle-income threshold 4.
  Inequality in Djibouti is one of the highest in the MENAAP region with a Gini index of 41.6 in 2017 5. The unemployment
  rate is 26.4 percent (2023)6 and women’s formal employment stands as low as 18.7 percent (2024), compared to 45.8
  percent for men—figures among the lowest globally and below average rates in the MENA region 7.
  3.       Djibouti faces a pronounced rural-urban divide that significantly affects social welfare and economic
  inclusion. The country’s economic and infrastructural development is heavily concentrated in the urban centers, where
  about 79 percent of the population resides. In contrast, rural areas—home to about 21 percent of the population—
  struggle with higher poverty rates8 and limited access to essential services such as education, healthcare, and basic
  infrastructure. Rural livelihoods, predominantly pastoralism, are increasingly undermined by recurrent droughts and
  environmental stress, further widening disparities and accelerating migration toward urban centers. While the private
  sector in Djibouti holds considerable potential for job creation in urban areas, significant challenges, including poverty,

  4.       Djibouti faces critical water challenges arising from both scarcity and flooding, which undermine inclusive
  growth, job creation, food security, and livelihoods. The country’s arid climate10 translates into extremely limited
  renewable water resources estimated at 185 m3 per person per year11, far below the absolute scarcity threshold of 500
  m3 per person per year. Djibouti has experienced rising mean annual temperatures and declining mean annual
  precipitation. Projected changes indicate that by mid-century, higher temperatures and reduced rainfall are likely to
  increase the frequency, intensity, and duration of droughts. Recurrent droughts, increasingly intensified by climate
  change, have severe impacts. During the 2008–2011 drought, more than 100,000 people were affected, livestock herds
  were reduced by about half, and annual GDP losses reached up to percent. Droughts also take a heavy toll on the
  country’s food systems: In 2022, 37.1 percent of rural households and 9.7 percent of urban households were affected
  by moderate to severe food insecurity12. Extreme heat linked to climate change threatens jobs in Djibouti and reduces
  outdoor workers’ productivity by up to 50 percent, undermining livelihoods and economic resilience13. At the same time,
  flood events are becoming more frequent and intense, predominantly in coastal and urban areas. In 2023, a single event
  caused damage estimated at US$47 million or 1.1 percent of GDP.14 Thus, climate change is a significant challenge that
  will impact socio-economic development, as highlighted in the Country Climate and Development Report for Djibouti
  (2024).

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
  5.       Ensuring universal access to drinking water has long been a major challenge for Djibouti, due to its harsh
  climatic conditions, weak Operation and Maintenance (O&M) practices, and traditional heavy dependence on a single
  resource—groundwater—which is increasingly overexploited and deteriorating in quality. Domestic demand accounts
  for 84 percent of total annual water use. While urban areas have benefited from substantial investments, including
  desalination plants and the Ethiopia-Djibouti water transfer pipeline, rural communities lag behind, with only 47 percent
  of the rural population having access to basic drinking water, compared to 83 percent in urban centers. Rural sanitation
  coverage remains critically low, with less than 25 percent of households having access to improved sanitation facilities,
  and open defecation rates in rural areas of the country are high15. These challenges are further exacerbated by poor
  practice in operations and maintenance (O&M): about 30 percent of rural and semi-urban water points are out of
  service16 and 90 percent of the rural population still relies on the unwieldy manual water lifting, which provides minimal
  sanitary protection. Furthermore, low levels of community contribution to O&M costs constrain the system’s ability to
  implement necessary improvements and ensure long-term sustainability. Beyond access constraints, groundwater
  depletion is severe with annual abstraction levels reaching 133 percent of renewable resources, compared to 7 percent
  in Ethiopia and 18 percent in Kenya 17. Many rural communities rely on shallow aquifers that are highly vulnerable to
  seasonality and drought; during the 2008–2011 drought, 80 percent of community wells failed, underscoring this risk.

 Salinity levels are also high, exceeding 900 milligrams per liter in more than half of boreholes 18. Climate change has been
 identified as a key driver of increased groundwater salinity in Djibouti, through sea level rise, and changes to the
 hydrological regime19. Climate change–driven increases in flood risk further undermine water access, as boreholes sited
 in wadis and poorly protected infrastructure are often washed away or damaged.
 6.        In rural Djibouti, women and girls are disproportionately responsible for collecting water—a task made more
 arduous by the fact that 66 percent of rural localities are located more than 3 km from the nearest water source, and
 only 10–15 percent are within 1 km20. In average, women and girls walk between 3 and 5 km daily and up to 6 km during
 droughts to fetch water21. This daily burden significantly restricts the time that women and girls have for opportunities
 for education, training, and income-generating activities, ultimately impacting their well-being and autonomy. It also
 increases the risk for injury and accidents and risks related to gender-based violence (GBV) including sexual exploitation
 and abuse/sexual harassment (SEA/SH). Despite their critical role in securing water for the household, women’s
 involvement in groundwater management at community level remains limited, due to persistent cultural norms, high
 illiteracy rates, and limited representation in decision-making bodies. Currently, approximately 40 percent of water
 committee members are registered as women; however, their participation and representation in leadership roles such
 as President, Vice President, Secretary General, or Treasurer remains very limited.
 7.       Djibouti’s water security is closely tied to its regional relationships, particularly with Ethiopia, due to the
 transboundary nature of key water resources, especially groundwater. Djibouti shares important groundwater
 resources with its neighbors; the Afar Rift Valley /Afar Triangle Aquifer—one of the major aquifers in Djibouti—extends
 across the borders to Ethiopia. In recent years, regional cooperation has expanded with the development of a cross-
 border pipeline between Ethiopia and Djibouti that currently supplies Djibouti with six million cubic meters of water
 annually (roughly one-quarter of urban water use). This pipeline has significantly boosted Djibouti’s water supply and
 has strengthened hydro-political and economic ties between the two countries. While the pipeline has additional unused
 capacity, its long-term reliability will partly depend on climate conditions in Ethiopia. Ensuring long-term water security
 will require cooperation, development plans, and integrated management of these transboundary resources.
 8.       Water management in Djibouti faces significant challenges due to fragmented institutional responsibilities,
 limited human and financial capacity, and the absence of a strategic plan for water resources. The Ministry of
 Agriculture, Water, Fisheries, Livestock, and Halieutic Resources (MAEP-RH) is the lead government agency for water,
 water resources planning, monitoring and overall sector oversight. In addition to these responsibilities, rural water
 service provision in Djibouti is assigned to the Rural Water Resources Directorate (DHR), while the National Office of
 Water and Sanitation of Djibouti (ONEAD) manages urban water supply. The significant resource requirement on the
 DHR for rural water services has constrained its capacity to develop functions related to policy and policy oversight.
 Further, conflicting roles in O&M have weakened accountability for safe and reliable services. The Centre for Research
 and Studies of Djibouti (CERD) is mandated to conduct scientific research and lead groundwater resource exploration.
 The Ministry of Health, through its Public Health and Environmental Hygiene directorates, monitors water quality and
 waterborne diseases. The 2001 established National Water Fund, managed by the DHR, and intended to finance rural
 drinking water infrastructure and its O&M, has not yet fulfilled its mandate due to irregular and insufficient funding.
 Overlapping mandates, insufficient staffing, and inadequate data for informed planning undermine the effectiveness of
 these institutions and hinder sector performance.

 9.        Sustainable access and management of groundwater are constrained by institutional and operational
 challenges that impede effective investment planning and reliable service delivery. Severe resource and staffing
 shortages limit DHR’s ability to plan, explore, develop, operate, and maintain rural water systems. Efficiency is further
 constrained by poor coordination among government agencies, community organizations, NGOs, and development
 partners. Critically, data and knowledge gaps hinder sound planning: groundwater investments are often made on the
 basis of insufficient information, leading to a higher probability of borehole failure, and limited monitoring makes it
 difficult to determine sustainable pumping rates or detect overexploitation. In rural areas, water is provided free of
 charge, and the DHR relies entirely on state funding, making it vulnerable to budgetary constraints and unpredictability.

 and prompt a reconsideration of the current paradigm for rural water provision, in coordination with community driven
 planning, implementation, and monitoring.

 II. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

 A. Project Development Objective
   17.     The Project Development Objective (PDO) of the DJIRESA Project is to increase the sustainable access and
   management of groundwater in rural areas of Djibouti. This aligns with the PDO of the MPA program and Phase I,
   focusing on sustainably tapping groundwater to cope with and adapt to drought and other climate shocks affecting
   the region. Increasing sustainable access means ensuring continuity at the service delivery level through better
   groundwater planning, robust operation and maintenance approaches, climate resilient infrastructure, conservation
   of groundwater resources, and sustainable financing.

 B. Theory of Change and PDO Indicators
 18.        The Theory of Change (ToC) for the proposed Phase II closely mirrors that of Phase I with slight adjustments
 tailored to the context of Djibouti. Based on a set of activities organized as described below, the project aims to achieve
 the following main outputs: Climate-resilient groundwater infrastructure, robust systems for operation and
 maintenance, strengthened governance instruments for groundwater management, and improved access to
 groundwater information. These outputs will lead to the achievement of the envisaged outcomes that are central to the
 project’s Theory of Change and its PDO.
 19.         Project’s progress towards the achievement of the PDO will be measured by the following indicators: (i)
 Number of people with enhanced resilience to climate risks24; (ii) Number of people with at least basic water services;
 and (iii) Increase in the functionality level of water points and piped rural water schemes (Percentage) and (iv) National
 Groundwater Information Management System (GWIS) established, operational, and used for annual sector
 performance reporting and decision-making, with interoperability to IGAD GWIS25. The first indicator provides a
 comprehensive breakdown of the total number of beneficiaries, encompassing all individuals who benefit from improved
 access to water, including those with limited access to drinking water and improved water availability for their livestock.
 The second indicator tracks enhancements in access to water services for people, with basic service 26. The third PDO
 indicator assesses progress towards continuity in service delivery, underpinned by strengthened O&M arrangements.
 While the first three indicators measure the progress toward achieving sustainable access to groundwater, the
 operational GWIS is required for achieving sustainable management of groundwater resources. Its interoperability with
 IGAD’s GWIS will facilitate the transboundary management of water resources.

C. Project Beneficiaries
 20.        The primary direct beneficiaries of the Project are the rural communities of Djibouti who will gain
 improved access to reliable and safe water resources. Improved water access will enhance the resilience of rural
 households to rising climate shocks such as droughts, extreme heat and flooding, ensuring more consistent water
 supply for both domestic use and for livestock, which is a vital livelihood asset for many agropastoral and nomadic
 households. By increasing the availability and sustainability of groundwater, the Project will also reduce the time and
 physical burden of fetching water, a responsibility that falls disproportionately on women and girls, thus freeing up
 their time to pursue education, employment, and other economic activities. Complementary sanitation interventions
 such as hygiene promotion will reduce disease burden and improve household health outcomes. As a result, the Project
 will contribute to improved health, food security, and economic opportunities for some of Djibouti’s most vulnerable
 populations.
 21.        The Rural Water Resources Directorate (DHR) will also be a key direct beneficiary of the Project. The
 Directorate’s institutional capacity will be strengthened through targeted capacity building, provision of modern
 equipment, and technical assistance. This support will enable DHR to better manage and monitor groundwater
 resources in collaboration with community institutions, oversee the design, implementation, and O&M of rural water
 systems, coordinate more effectively with neighboring countries on transboundary water management, and increase
 its capacity to prepare and respond to climate related shocks. The Project will help DHR develop and implement
 improved data collection and information systems, enhance staff skills in groundwater hydrology and engineering, and
 establish robust frameworks for sustainable water resource management. The Project will also empower community-
 based institutions to better manage groundwater resources and contribute to the O&M of groundwater infrastructure,
 which will in turn improve accountability, service delivery, foster ownership, and build resilience.
 22.         The broader population of Djibouti will benefit indirectly from the Project’s contributions to national water
 management capacity and strategic planning, in the face of rising climatic challenges. By strengthening the
 institutional framework and technical expertise for water management, the Project will help ensure more sustainable
 and equitable allocation of water resources across the country. The development of a comprehensive national water
 master plan will provide a sector development blueprint, enable evidence-based decision-making, promote efficient
 and sustainable use of water, and increase the country’s resilience to climate change. Improved water management
 will also reduce the risk of water scarcity and conflict and support economic development.
D. Project Components
23.          The proposed Project is structured as an Investment Project Financing (IPF) operation to be implemented
by MAEP-RH over a five-year period (2026–2031). The primary goals are to expand access to water in rural areas of
Djibouti, professionalize O&M to ensure reliable service delivery, strengthen the capacity of national and subnational
institutions to sustainably manage groundwater resources, and improve information systems, knowledge exchange, and
learning in the water sector. Further, a specific set of activities, implemented under the leadership of IGAD, aim to foster
collaboration on transboundary groundwater management, contributing to regional integration, peace, and security.
24.       The DJIRESA Project is structured around four components. Component 1 will focus on development of
groundwater infrastructure for increased groundwater access and surface water storage, coupled with the development
and implementation of robust O&M arrangements. Component 2 will strengthen the policy and institutional
environment for groundwater development and management; Component 3 will finance project management, learning
and knowledge management, and Component 4 is a Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC).
25.         Component 1: Expanding Groundwater Infrastructure and Strengthening Rural Water Systems
Management (US$ 23.8 million). This component will finance the development of groundwater-based water supply
infrastructure in rural Djibouti, primarily to serve domestic water needs and livestock demand for water. It will also
support the construction of surface water harvesting and storage systems to complement groundwater sources and

26.         Sub-component 1.1: Development of water supply infrastructure (US$22.0 million). This sub-component
aims to expand access to drinking water in rural Djibouti, where coverage remains critically low and uneven across

schemes initiated during preparation and complemented by groundwater assessments conducted by DHR and its
partners. Each site will undergo comprehensive climate risk screening as part of the design process, and piped systems
will be designed to withstand, adapt to, and recover from events—such as droughts, floods, and heat—for climate
resilience while continuing to deliver reliable and safe water services. Key features will include robust source selection
and design, adequate storage and redundancy, flood- and heat-resilient infrastructure, solar pumping, backup power
and supply options, water quality protection, real-time monitoring, and better operation and maintenance
arrangements, combined with community engagement to sustain services. In addition, sensors will monitor and report
groundwater levels, pumping rates, functionality, water quality, and other metrics into DHR’s central MIIS (linked to
Activity 2.2.4). These interlocking activities are essential for comprehensively improving water resources management
in order to improve climate resilience to drought, flood, groundwater salinity, and water scarcity risks for beneficiaries
dependent on groundwater sources, while also diversifying water sourcing for climate resilience purposes to use both
better managed groundwater and surface water resources. Stakeholder mapping and engagements with community
members especially of vulnerable groups and women, will be undertaken on an ongoing basis to ensure that local
priorities and concerns are reflected in the site selection for new and rehabilitated boreholes, inform climate risk
screening and identify and manage risks such as conflict and rangeland impacts.
27.          Activity 1.1.1. Studies, assessments, and final designs for groundwater and surface water investments (US$

assessments; climate risk screening; inclusive consultations and community engagement; and the preparation of
detailed engineering designs, technical specifications, and bidding documents. It will define asset standards (including
solar pumping and treatment), resilience features (e.g., flood protection), monitoring requirements (sensors and data
integration into the central MIS), and other operational guidelines for investments (e.g., water quality standards).

E. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners
48.          The World Bank is uniquely positioned to support Djibouti in addressing its pressing water security
challenges. Drawing on its ongoing experience in Djibouti’s rural and agriculture sectors, as well as its prior engagement
in the rural water sector, the World Bank is well placed to support the government in delivering essential water services
to the vulnerable rural population. Leveraging its global expertise in rural water supply, groundwater management,
community driven development and climate resilience, the Bank can help strengthen institutions and ensure that
investments are both technically sound and socially equitable.
49.         The Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) will play a central role in the implementation of
DJIRESA, leveraging its mandate and expertise to strengthen transboundary water management, regional resilience,
and knowledge exchange among the HoA countries that are its Member States. IGAD coordinates regional analytical
work, joint studies, and shared data platforms, and promotes regional learning and capacity-building. As a key partner
and Implementing Agency of Phase I, IGAD has been pivotal in fostering regional collaboration and building capacity for
sustainable groundwater management, notably through its Water Unit, which leads activities such as assessing and
monitoring transboundary aquifers and working closely with Project Implementation Units (PIUs) in member states to
define and validate regional priorities.
F. Lessons Learned and Progress on Learning Agenda
50.          The design of the DJIRESA Project reflects lessons learned from Phase I of the MPA that will strengthen the
implementation performance. The experience from Phase I underscores the need to apply advanced hydrogeological
assessment methods to more accurately identify and prioritize viable investment sites. Quality and durable borehole
design, efficient drilling, and procurement tailored to local markets are essential, as is addressing policy and capacity
gaps in the sector. Early engagement of community groups in site selection and system design is crucial, with climate
resilience, conflict mitigation, and gender-sensitive features incorporated from the start. The MPA Phase I introduced a
climate-resilience and gender checklist to guide this process. Experience shows that community-led O&M rarely

                                                                                                                                  16
         The World Bank
         Horn of Africa - Groundwater for Resilience Project, Phase II (P511492)


 succeeds without professional service providers, external support, strong governance, and accountability systems. An
 additional challenge to professionalizing O&M is the difficulty of establishing viable rural user fees due to affordability
 constraints, and weak enforcement. Phase I and global experience offer a wealth of lessons to inform the transformation
 towards more effective O&M models.
 51.         After three years of implementation, Phase I has shown that regional integration is fundamental to
 advancing groundwater resilience. IGAD’s leadership, with reinforcing capacity development under Phase I, has enabled
 a pivotal regional platform for facilitating dialogue, data sharing, and cooperation on transboundary aquifers. Another
 key lesson is that effective transboundary groundwater management extends beyond the technical—it requires time
 and capacity to navigate political and bureaucratic hurdles. The Merti Aquifer initiative has provided a practical model
 for structured cooperation, illustrating how joint monitoring and institutional dialogue can be prioritized to yield tangible
 progress32.
 52.         Successful project implementation depends on sound planning, adaptive management, and ongoing
 capacity strengthening. Experience from Phase I confirms that effective project delivery requires a stable, dedicated
 implementation team with strong expertise in technical design and supervision, procurement, community-driven
 development and engagement, and environmental and social risk management, including gender-based violence risk
 mitigation and gender inclusion. The use of digital M&E tools enables real-time learning and adaptive management,
 allowing projects to make timely adjustments and restructuring decisions. A key lesson from Phase I is that procurement
 speed strongly influences project performance. Fragmented procurement limits competition, while aggregated contracts
 attracted higher-capacity firms and improved delivery. Community engagement on site selection, design, tariffs, and
 O&M is essential, and must be inclusive, particularly of women, youth, and minority ethno-linguistic groups to ensure
 ownership and investments that are sustainable and responsive. To improve governance and accountability, water
 resource sharing or management agreements must be clearly agreed, public and enforceable, and incorporate adequate
 provisions to receive and address feedback and complaints of water users and other project affected people. Finally,
 E&S risks assessment and management must be integrated early into the project’s design and activities.

 C. Sustainability

 59.         At the national level, the Project will advance the sustainability of groundwater management by
 strengthening the DHR and enhancing the policy and institutional framework. It will support the development and
 refinement of policies, regulations, and operational processes that guide groundwater use, protection, and long-term
 planning. Central to this effort is the strengthening of systematic monitoring, including accurate data collection and
 analysis on water resources, as well as on water supply systems and infrastructure assets. These enhancements will
 allow DHR to provide more effective oversight of operations and maintenance, as well as improve the planning of new
 systems. The Project will also deepen understanding of groundwater systems and aquifer dynamics. By strengthening
 planning and monitoring processes and equipping DHR with robust information and methodologies, the Project will
 foster informed decision-making and support the adoption of best practices in groundwater governance, ensuring that
 management strategies remain responsive to evolving challenges.
 60.           The Project’s approach is designed to enhance the sustainability of rural water supply systems. It will
 address the underlying causes of system failure seen in recent years, including inadequate planning, poor equipment
 selection, unsuitable locations, and insufficient operation and maintenance (O&M). It will apply robust, climate-resilient
 design standards and integrate climate risk into each stage of system development to ensure long-term functionality.
 Community oversight and engagement will be strengthened to foster local ownership and accountability. The Project
 will assess the financing environment for rural water, and explore professionalized O&M models, including private-
 operator arrangements. Additional downstream sustainability indicators, including metrics on repair time, service
 reliability, and financial performance, will be incorporated into all O&M agreements signed with private operators or
 other service providers. These indicators will be tracked throughout the project period and will help inform broader
 sector policies and frameworks. Together, these measures will improve the resilience, effectiveness, and sustainability
 of rural water infrastructure.

    Economic Analysis
 67.       Economic Analysis. A cost-benefit analysis was carried out, which estimated the economic benefits versus costs
 of the Project, by comparing “with Project” and “without Project” scenarios. The economic analysis considered the
 economic costs and benefits associated with the development of groundwater infrastructure for increased groundwater
 access and surface water storage. For the economic analysis, the Net Present Value (NPV) was calculated with a discount
 rate of 10 percent and with a 20-year horizon. 34
 68.     Economic benefits. The main economic benefits include: (a) improved water access for the targeted
 communities; (b) improved sanitation and reduced incidence of waterborne illness for the local populations; (c) reduced
 time spent collecting water; and (d) reduced GHG emissions as a result of solar-powered pumping to replace or
 supplement diesel systems.
 a. Improved water access for the targeted communities: Through the increased provision of water for beneficiaries,
    there is an expected increase in productivity through avoided lost days of work due to illness. This benefit is valued
    based on an estimate of rural wages of US$2 per day and a reduction in sickness of 5 days per year.
 b. Improved sanitation and reduced incidence of waterborne illness for the local populations: The reduction of
    morbidity and mortality due to diarrhea after improving water supply are based on the latest meta-analyses. 35 The
    burden of health is valued by using the cost of illness technique for morbidity and the value of statistical life (VSL)
    for mortality.36 The cost of illness is derived from the number of visits to doctors and hospital visit day and is set at
    US$2.37 The value of statistical life (VSL) is an expression of preferences for reducing risks of death in monetary terms,
    and the benefits to society of mortality risk reductions are usually valuated by people's willingness-to-pay (WTP) for
    such risk reductions. WTP is then converted to a value of statistical life (VSL) that is applied to estimated cases of
    mortality avoided from the investment-related improvements to arrive at an estimate of the monetary benefits of
    the improvement. The VSL varies across countries in proportion to GDP/capita (PPP terms). It should be emphasized
    that VSL estimates have nothing to do with the value of life as such but rather reflect people’s willingness to
    reallocate their resources from consumption of market goods and services to paying for reductions in the risk of
    mortality.
 c. Reduced time spent collecting water: The opportunity cost of time is calculated based on the avoided time spent
    fetching water, a burden which falls disproportionately on women and girls. The daily burden of collecting water for
    a household limits the ability for women and girls to participate in education, training, and income-generating
    activities. More than 66 percent of the rural households are located more than 3km (one-way) from the current
    waterpoint, and 90 percent are manually pumped, requiring more time and effort to collect water. The analysis is
    based on the reduction of the time required for one household to fetch water from three hours per day to 30 minutes
    per day. The reduction in time spent fetching is valued by using an estimate of rural wages of US$2 per day.
 d. Reduced GHG emissions: Solar power pumps will be used to replace or supplement current diesel engines, leading
    to a reduction in GHG emissions of 1,026 tCO2-eq/year.

E. Gender and Citizen/Community Engagement

 92.          Gender. The Djibouti CPF (2022-2026) highlights the need to support market-responsive vocational training
 to strengthen productive skills and access to better jobs, especially for women45. Labor force participation among women
 stands at 18.7 percent (2024), compared to 45.8 percent for men—figures among the lowest globally 46. Women's
 employment is predominantly concentrated in informal and vulnerable occupations (48 percent for women versus 24.2
 percent for men)47, with barely any recorded female employment in the water and sanitation sector in rural areas.
 Women (at least 35) will be hired in project areas through financing vocational training linked to a one-year job
 placement system (see Activity 1.2.2). This includes O&M roles, such as mason, plumber, repairer, water quality officer,
 pumping station operator (including solar panel systems), and watchman - all responsible for on-site water and
 sanitation infrastructure maintenance. Training will be organized in Chef lieux by the Direction Générale de la Formation
 Professionnelle and the Ministry of Education, with support from private sector partners. Curricula will follow
 professional O&M models established by the Project. Apprentices will receive monthly stipends during the one-year
 program along with starter toolkits. Scholarships, insurance, and/or travel reimbursements during the training will be
 offered to apprentices based on pre-established criteria (e.g. minimum literacy). An NGO will deliver sensitization
 sessions both in Afar and Somali areas to local communities before and during training, focusing on women's integration
 into traditionally male-dominated professions and community acceptance. These jobs will eventually be permanent and
 necessary to ensure the sustainability of the planned activities.
 93.         Women’s leadership is a critical gap in the water sector in Djibouti. Women’s leadership in water
 management committee in Djibouti is barely inexistent with only eight women recorded in leadership positions in the
 project areas. Participation tends to be low due social norms and female illiteracy. Typical roles involve water quality
 and sanitation officers and treasurer, with no representation in the role of President, Vice-President, Secretary General.
 The Project will invest in training, awareness raising, leadership programs for women and in community acceptance. The
 Project will prepare a pipeline of up to 154 women leaders to serve in these roles. These elected positions will be
 continuously supported by the hired NGOs and women’s advocacy groups to sustain them in the long term and foster
 female networks, peer-learning and mentorship.

 V. GRIEVANCE REDRESS SERVICES
   95.         Grievance Redress. Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by a project
   supported by the World Bank may submit complaints to existing project-level grievance mechanisms or the Bank’s
   Grievance Redress Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed in order to address
   project-related concerns. Project affected communities and individuals may submit their complaint to the Bank’s
   independent Accountability Mechanism (AM). The AM houses the Inspection Panel, which determines whether harm
   occurred, or could occur, as a result of Bank non-compliance with its policies and procedures, and the Dispute
   Resolution Service, which provides communities and borrowers with the opportunity to address complaints through
   dispute resolution. Complaints may be submitted to the AM at any time after concerns have been brought directly to
   the attention of Bank Management and after Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For information
   on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s GRS, visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to
   submit complaints to the Bank’s Accountability Mechanism, visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.

 VI. KEY RISKS

  96.        The overall risk to achieving the PDO is rated as substantial. This reflects challenges related to technical
  design, institutional capacity, fiduciary management, environmental and social safeguards, and stakeholder
  engagement. The following paragraphs summarize each substantial risk and outline proposed mitigation measures:
  97.          The technical design risk is rated Substantial, driven by three key considerations. First, private sector
  capacity in Djibouti’s rural water sector remains limited. Historically, service delivery has relied on government and
  community management, and there is uncertainty regarding the availability of qualified operators and interest. Second,
  tariff collection and cost recovery pose significant challenges. Evidence from the Horn of Africa shows that establishing
  viable user fee systems in rural contexts is difficult due to affordability constraints, limited willingness to pay, and weak
  enforcement mechanisms, which could undermine the financial sustainability of performance-based contracts. Third,
  institutional readiness for oversight is constrained. The DHR faces human resource limitations and lacks a regulatory
  framework to supervise, monitor, and enforce contractual obligations with private operators. To address these technical
  risks, the Project will adopt a phased implementation approach that allows for adaptive learning and refinement of
  contractual arrangements based on pilot results before scaling nationally. Analytical work, including infrastructure
  condition assessments, tariff feasibility studies, and market sounding exercises, will inform the identification and pre-

98.         Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability is rated Substantial due to limited staffing and

These constraints increase the risk of procurement delays, uneven quality control, and weak long-term functionality of

the PIU; (ii) clear governance and coordination mechanisms, updated POM, and a detailed procurement plan; (iii)
sequencing of capital disbursements; and (iv) performance-based contracts and routine monitoring of service providers
and system functionality to sustain outcomes beyond construction.
99.         Fiduciary risk is also assessed as Substantial, given the complexity of financial management and
procurement arrangements. The Project will involve negotiating and implementing multiple contracts, including
technical assistance agreements, which require strong capacity and oversight. While DHR gained initial experience with
World Bank procurement during project preparation under the GFPP, gaps remain in contract management and
familiarity with Bank procedures. For fiduciary risk, qualified financial management and procurement specialists will be
hired, and the PIU-DHR and PIU-IGAD will receive training in Bank regulations and contract management. Internal
procurement processes will be streamlined, with regular World Bank support, annual procurement planning,
standardized documents, and a complaints mechanism in place. Transparency and effective contract management will
be enhanced through participatory monitoring, periodic internal audits, and focused capacity-building.
100.        Environmental and social risk is rated Substantial due to potential site-specific impacts from borehole
drilling and construction (noise, dust, waste, groundwater contamination), land access issues, community tensions
around water allocation, and risks of exclusion of vulnerable groups and SEA/SH. Mitigation, detailed in the Project
Appraisal Summary above, includes ESF-compliant instruments (ESMF, RPF/RAPs, ESCP), robust screening and
management plans, groundwater protection and monitoring measures, minimization and fair compensation for land
impacts, a strengthened GRM with SEA/SH-sensitive pathways, and ongoing stakeholder engagement and supervision.
101.         Stakeholder risk is rated Substantial given the need for sustained coordination across ministries, where
competing priorities, overlapping mandates, and limited ownership may slow decisions and constrain O&M policy
adoption. Additional risks include diverging technical preferences and duplication with parallel initiatives. Mitigation
measures include the establishment of the Project Steering Committee, formalization of roles and responsibilities
through new groundwater management procedures and the adoption of the O&M policy; and the alignment of project
activities with sector strategies and annual budget cycles.
102.         Residual risks are expected to be Moderate following the implementation of these measures. While the
phased approach, capacity building, and strengthened oversight mechanisms will significantly reduce technical and
fiduciary risks, and procurement risks challenges may persist. These include unforeseen difficulties in scaling private
sector participation, affordability constraints among rural communities, and the need for ongoing institutional
strengthening within DHR. Continuous monitoring, adaptive management, and stakeholder engagement will be critical
to ensure that residual risks do not adversely affect project outcomes.

I.   INSTITUTIONAL AND IMPLEMENTATION ARRANGEMENTS

Project Implementation Units (PIU)
The project will be managed by two Project Implementation Units (PIU):
     The PIU-DHR, within MAEPERH and will report to the Ministry’s Secretary General. It will function
        as the central coordination hub for the Project, acting as the main point of contact with the World
        Bank and with relevant ministries. The PIU will oversee all routine implementation activities. Its
        core responsibilities will include:
            - Planning and budgeting: preparing the Annual Work Plan and Budget and ensuring
                 effective execution.
            - Technical delivery: managing contracts and agreements under FAR and supervising the
                 implementation of sub-projects.
            - Monitoring and evaluation: conducting data collection for the Results Framework and
                 carrying out periodic special surveys to gauge project outcomes
            - Environmental and social safeguards: verifying and documenting compliance with all
                 safeguard requirements.
            - Outreach, communication, and knowledge management: ensuring beneficiaries
                 understand project activities and maintaining open two-way channels of communication.
     The existing Phase I PIU within IGAD’s Water Unit (Agriculture and Environment Division) will lead
        IGAD-implemented activities under DJIRESA, providing technical coordination and day-to-day
        management, and ensuring collaboration with DHR, ICPAC, and other IGAD divisions to integrate
        hydro-climate data, strengthen GWIS, and align with IGAD’s water security and resilience
        objectives.

Both PIUs will prepare and submit Progress Reports every six months, as well as comprehensive Annual
Reports.

Project Operations Manual (POM)
The Project Operations Manual (POM) will set out detailed implementation arrangements and procedures
acceptable to the Association. It will cover: (i) institutional and implementation arrangements; (ii)
disbursement, reporting, financial management, and audit procedures; (iii) procurement procedures,
standard documents, and contract administration; (iv) measures to mitigate fraud, corruption, and other
integrity and fiduciary risks; (v) environmental and social risk management arrangements; (vi) procedures
for preparing and reviewing the consolidated annual work plan and budget; (vii) monitoring, evaluation,
reporting, and communications; (viii) arrangements to address community health, safety, and security risks
and impacts; (ix) measures to facilitate sharing of groundwater information and data among the Recipient’s
ministries, departments, and agencies, and across borders with IGAD and IGAD Member States; and (x) any
other administrative, financial, technical, and organizational arrangements required for Project
implementation.

Project Steering Committees (PSC)

The Project Steering Committee (PSC), led by MAEPE-RH, will serve as the Project’s apex body for strategic

(MEFI), the Ministry of Environment and Sustainable Development (MESD), the Ministry of Women and

II.      FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT
Fiduciary risk. The overall FM residual risk of the project is assessed as Substantial because of the need
to strengthen the governance and internal control system of the CEP to the new scale of the Project. The
risk will be reviewed and revised during the project implementation. The following risks were identified:
(a) Weak prospective budgeting and monitoring of commitments, (b) limited or inexistent internal audit
function, and (c) portfolio wide issues in project closing management
Risk mitigation measures. To address the above weaknesses, the project will recruit qualified financial
management specialists; the manual of procedures will define the arrangements for budget formulation
and budgetary control and the requirements for budgeting revisions, ensuring that the entirety of the
project duration is covered by the projections, the POM will include a clear definition of the types of
allowable expenditures, the internal audit function will be outsourced to an external professional services
firm based on agreed upon ToRs and the POM will contain an extensive chapter dealing with project
closing considerations.

Financial Management Arrangements49. The PIUs will prepare an Annual Work Plan and Budget (AWPB)
aligned with the procurement plan and attendant disbursement plan. The AWPB will include all
components as per the Financing Agreement and will be submitted by no later than November 30 of
each year. The PIUs will also be responsible for preparing Project financial reports that will include
unaudited Interim Financial Report (IFRs) and annual Project Financial Statements (PFSs).
The project financial statements will be audited annually by an independent external auditor. They will
cover all aspects of the project implementation, and attendant use of funds and expenditures incurred.

     ANNEX 2: Description Regional technical cooperation with IGAD to strengthen groundwater
                                  governance and data systems

The Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) will play a pivotal and multifaceted role in
supporting the implementation of P511492. By leveraging its extensive regional expertise and the
developed technical platforms, IGAD will support DHR in the implementation of specific activities and this
way help strengthen Djibouti’s institutional capacities, knowledge base, and information systems for
sustainable groundwater management. In particular, IGAD will focus on enhancing capacities for
transboundary aquifer management, thereby directly contributing to the overarching Program Objective
of the MPA to promote sustainable, equitable, and climate-resilient water resources management across
the region. IGAD’s involvement ensures that Djibouti benefits from regional best practices, harmonized
data systems, and innovative approaches to groundwater governance, while also fostering collaborative
solutions for shared aquifers and advancing climate resilience and water security. By facilitating
knowledge exchange and technical cooperation, IGAD will help Djibouti address both immediate and long-
term challenges in groundwater management, ensuring that interventions are grounded in regional
experience and tailored to local needs. The specific areas of IGAD’s support are outlined in detail below.

Support for Activities 2.1.1 and 2.1.3
In support to Activity 2.1.1, IGAD will facilitate the transfer of regional experience and knowledge to build
the organizational and technical capacities of DHR. This support will encompass targeted training, and
eventually benchmarking exchange visits, drawing on IGAD’s extensive track record of delivering capacity
building for groundwater institutions throughout the Horn of Africa. By leveraging these regional
resources, IGAD’s approach will emphasize practical learning and peer-to-peer exchanges, enabling DHR
to continuously improve its technical capabilities and adapt to emerging challenges.

For Activity 2.1.3, IGAD will provide technical expertise and regional perspectives to strengthen Djibouti’s
groundwater policies, regulations, and governance frameworks. IGAD will contribute to the
comprehensive assessment of Djibouti’s legal and regulatory environment, ensuring alignment with
international best practices and harmonization with regional standards. Through its collaborative
platforms, IGAD will facilitate knowledge sharing, and the exchange of experiences, supporting the
formulation and adoption of new policies and regulatory measures that promote sustainable and climate-
resilient groundwater management. This process will help Djibouti establish a robust legal foundation for
responsible groundwater use and long-term resource protection, while also fostering a culture of
transparency, accountability, and stakeholder engagement in water governance.

Support for Activity 2.2.1: Establishment of the National Groundwater and WASH Services Information
Management System
IGAD will provide comprehensive guidance, leverage its regional expertise, and share lessons learned to
support the establishment of Djibouti’s integrated Groundwater–WASH Services Information System.
IGAD’s support will include:
    Guidance in System Design and Integration: IGAD will offer technical guidance for the design,
        integration, and operation of the information system, ensuring it builds on existing datasets and
        incorporates real-time data from monitoring equipment installed on new and rehabilitated
        boreholes. This will help Djibouti develop a robust and responsive system for tracking
        groundwater and WASH service delivery.

      Leveraging Regional Expertise: IGAD will support the development of a structured, national-scale
       groundwater database, ensuring compatibility and interoperability with the regional HoA-GWIS
       platform and facilitating seamless data exchange. This will enable Djibouti to participate in
       regional monitoring efforts and benefit from comparative analysis and benchmarking.
      Connecting to Regional Systems: IGAD will facilitate the connection of Djibouti’s system to IGAD’s
       regional platforms, enabling real-time, sustainable WASH data management and advancing
       regional collaboration for improved water sector performance. This integration will support
       coordinated responses to water challenges and enhance the effectiveness of interventions.
      Development of Forecast Tools: IGAD will offer expertise in the use of advanced forecasting tools
       and methodologies, supporting the development of groundwater level fluctuation models and
       recharge maps using satellite-based inputs and machine learning techniques. These tools will
       enable proactive management and help anticipate future needs and risks.
      Support for Data Sharing Agreements: IGAD will guide the development of data sharing
       agreements, promoting transparency, cooperation, and mutual benefit between Djibouti and its
       regional partners. Such agreements will facilitate joint planning and resource management,
       reducing the risk of disputes and enhancing collective resilience.
      Capacity Building and Sharing Lessons Learned: IGAD will provide targeted training and share
       practical experiences from other countries in the region, ensuring DHR and other stakeholders
       are equipped to manage, analyze, and report groundwater and WASH data effectively, and to
       utilize these systems for evidence-based decision-making. Continuous learning and adaptation
       will be central to IGAD’s approach, helping Djibouti stay at the forefront of groundwater and
       WASH management.
Support for Activity 2.2.2: Assessment of the Afar Aquifer
IGAD will lead the assessment and development of the management plan for the transboundary Afar Rift
aquifer, providing comprehensive guidance, technical expertise, and leveraging its regional experience.
This support will include several key elements:
     Baseline Study: IGAD will produce a technical overview of the Afar aquifer, compiling and
        analyzing hydrogeological, climatic, and socio-economic data from both Djibouti and Ethiopia.
        This study will define aquifer boundaries, assess current usage patterns, and identify critical
        information gaps that need to be addressed for effective management. The baseline will serve as
        a foundation for future planning and investment, ensuring that decisions are informed by
        accurate and up-to-date knowledge.
      Feasibility Study: Building on the baseline, IGAD will guide the feasibility assessment of the Afar
       aquifer’s potential for sustainable groundwater development, drawing on its experience from
       similar regional studies. This will involve comprehensive data collection, geophysical and field
       investigations, drilling of exploration wells, groundwater quality testing, and the development of
       a mathematical groundwater model. The results will inform management scenarios, investment
       needs, and long-term sustainability strategies, helping stakeholders to prioritize interventions and
       allocate resources effectively.
      Development of a Non-Binding Aquifer Protocol: IGAD will support the drafting of a voluntary
       protocol for the Afar aquifer, outlining principles for data sharing, joint monitoring, and conflict
       prevention. The protocol will build on the Draft Articles on the Law of Transboundary Aquifers

       adopted by the UN International Law Commission. It will serve as a pilot for transboundary aquifer
       cooperation in the region, fostering trust and collaboration between Djibouti and Ethiopia, and
       providing a model for similar initiatives elsewhere in the Horn of Africa.
      Sharing Lessons Learned: IGAD will facilitate joint inception and validation workshops with
       stakeholders from both countries, ensuring methodological alignment, consensus-building, and
       cooperative decision-making throughout the assessment and planning process. These workshops
       will promote mutual understanding, encourage the adoption of best practices, and help build
       lasting partnerships for shared aquifer management.


Through these activities, IGAD will support Djibouti to establish robust information systems, enhance
data-driven decision-making, and foster regional cooperation for sustainable groundwater and WASH
services management. In doing so, IGAD will contribute significantly to the achievement of the Program
Objective of the MPA and help strengthen resilience and sustainability across the region.
