    B. Introduction and Context

    Country Context

    1. Colombia is among the most inequitable countries globally, with persistent regional disparities that are
       particularly pronounced in the Pacific region, but with an incredibly rich cultural heritage and tourism potential.
       Income inequality in Colombia is the highest among fifteen countries in the Latin America region and is also high by
       OECD and world standards. The COVID-19 crisis led to a rise in the country’s inequality, yet the subsequent
       economic recovery lowered this indicator down to its pre-pandemic level with a still high Gini coefficient of 0.539
       in 2023.1 Yet, people in the Pacific region, comprised of four departments: Chocó and Valle del Cauca in the north
       and Cauca and Nariño in the south, face monetary poverty at rates nearly three times higher than people in the
       capital, Bogotá.2 This uneven territorial development has been exacerbated by a history of internal conflict, lack of
       trust between the regions and the Government of Colombia (GoC), underinvestment in basic infrastructure, and
       weak local governance. Illegal activities that drive the economy in some areas, along with associated security risks,
       further contribute to instability. Additionally, inadequate rural-urban linkages continue to widen socioeconomic
       gaps. At the same time, Colombia’s Pacific region also stands out for its rich Afro-Colombian and Indigenous cultures
       and impressive natural assets, including rainforests, mangroves and beaches, offering significant opportunities for
       sustainable development and ecotourism.

    2. Colombia’s Pacific region faces persistent challenges in basic service provision, with limited and unreliable access
       to water, sanitation, transport, energy, and digital connectivity. Water and sanitation coverage is particularly low,
       with urban rates below 40 percent in Chocó and Nariño, and rural coverage ranging from 50 percent in Cauca to
       just 6 percent in Nariño.3,4 Transport infrastructure is also underdeveloped due to difficult geography, resulting in
       a road density far below national benchmarks (0.285km/km2 compared to 0.765km/km2 nationally) and making
       waterway transport essential for many communities. However, investment in waterway infrastructure is minimal.
       Roads account for 80 percent of public investment in infrastructure,5 while waterways have received only 1 percent,

    3. Colombia’s labor market is marked by persistent challenges of high informality, unemployment, and significant
       regional and gender disparities, with these issues particularly acute in the Pacific region. Nationally, labor force
       participation is 75.7 percent for men and 51.4 percent for women,6 with an even wider gender gap in poorer
       regions; in Pacific departments like Chocó, only 27 percent of women and 55 percent of men are active in the labor
       market.7 Informality is pervasive, affecting nearly two-thirds of all jobs in the Pacific and Atlantic regions. In the
       water sector, Colombian women represent only 18 percent of utility workers,8 a figure shaped by the limited
       pipeline of female STEM graduates—just 15 percent of women in tertiary education choose STEM fields, compared
       to 35 percent of men.9 These gaps are compounded by challenges in the school-to-work transition: 30 percent of
       young Colombian women are not in education, employment, or training (NEET), nearly double the rate for young
       men.10 The prevalence of violence against women is also alarmingly high, with 71 percent of women in the Pacific
       reporting physical violence and 12 percent reporting sexual assault, the highest rates in Colombia.

    4. Climate change is worsening water, sanitation, and transport challenges in Colombia’s Pacific region by reducing
       water availability, increasing extreme weather events, and intensifying natural hazards. Over the past two
       decades, Colombia’s water resources in several macro basins have declined due to climate change and population
       growth, increasing the risk of shortages despite high annual rainfall (2,500–10,000 mm). The Pacific region is
       particularly vulnerable, facing periods of drought—especially during El Niño events—now intensified by climate
       change, with 2024 seeing historically low water levels in the Telembí and Patía rivers in Nariño. These water
       anomalies have a pronounced economic impact, with effects on GDP estimated to be twice the global average.
       Compounding these challenges, Colombia is highly exposed to climate-related hazards such as flooding, landslides,
       and sea level rise, which are especially problematic in rapidly growing, poorly planned urban and coastal areas.
       Towns like El Charco, situated less than five meters above sea level, frequently suffer disruptions from tides, floods,
       and landslides, isolating communities and impeding economic activity, particularly where water supply, sanitation,
       and connectivity are already lacking and isolation is longstanding. 11 Existing waterway docks are informal and highly
       susceptible to flooding and erosion, limiting their usability and raising safety concerns, while the region has also
       suffered from tsunamis and earthquakes, causing significant material damage and loss of life. These interconnected
       challenges highlight the urgent need for improved water level monitoring, navigation aids, and resilient
       infrastructure to support vulnerable communities.12

 Sectoral and Institutional Context
5. Colombia’s decentralized water and sanitation system faces major challenges in rural and in the Pacific region,
   resulting in poor service, water quality, and untreated wastewater due to weak capacity, infrastructure gaps, and
   climate risks. Colombia’s decentralized institutional framework for water and sanitation assigns national policy to the
   Ministry of Housing, City, and Territory (MVCT), regulatory oversight to the Regulatory Commission for Water and
   Sanitation (CRA), and performance monitoring to the Superintendence of Public Services (SSPD). Municipal mayors
   are responsible for service delivery, which is carried out by public or private utilities ranging from large companies like
   Bogotá’s EAAB to small, community-based providers, particularly in rural areas. Many rural systems operate outside
   the regulatory framework, complicating enforcement of standards and coordination of investments and subsidies.
   These challenges are especially acute in the Pacific region, where municipal utilities often lack technical and financial
   capacity, resulting in gaps in coverage, continuity, and water quality as shown in Table 1. Sewerage infrastructure is
   limited and deteriorating, with poorly maintained pipelines and inadequate solid waste management, while many
   households rely on substandard septic tanks and latrines. This leads to overflows and significant discharge of
   untreated wastewater into local water bodies, harming public health and ecosystems. Climate change further
   exacerbates vulnerabilities, with increased flooding and extreme rainfall events raising health risks and the potential
   for water source contamination.

   region manage smaller projects but struggle with limited resources. The Ministry of Transport leads policy
   formulation for all transport modes, coordinating with planning, implementing, and regulatory agencies. While

     national authorities manage major infrastructure, local governments, especially in the Pacific region, handle small-
     scale interventions but face significant technical and financial constraints. Limited coordination between national and
     subnational entities, along with private sector hesitancy due to perceived risks and logistical challenges, contributes
     to persistent connectivity gaps, particularly in rural and underserved Pacific areas. Notably, 69 percent of rural Pacific
     communities are over three hours from departmental capitals, and 32 percent are more than ten hours away,
     highlighting the urgent need for improved connectivity.13 Transport options are largely informal, relying on costly,
     polluting, and unsafe fossil fuel-powered boats. Poor visibility, lack of signaling, and frequent accidents further hinder
     navigation. In the north Pacific region, the Atrato River is a vital transport corridor, carrying nearly 40 percent of
     maritime and river passenger traffic, but its six ports suffer from poor infrastructure and limited accessibility.14 In the
     south Pacific, transport services and docks are predominantly informal and lack climate-resilient features, impeding
     economic development and social inclusion. Most passengers travel by motorboat (74 percent), followed by cargo
     boats (16 percent) and motorized canoes (10 percent), with work being the primary reason for commuting.15 Dock
     infrastructure is particularly insufficient, with Cauca and Nariño having fewer than two docks per 100,000 km of river
     coastline. Cauca currently has 1.60 docks per 100,000 km of river coastline, and Nariño has 1.68 docks per 100,000
     km. Targeted investments are needed to expand dock distribution, improve safety, and enhance connectivity to
     services and markets and support year-round waterway operations, especially given fluctuating water levels and
     challenging terrain.

7. In the wake of Colombia’s peace process and in recognition of the persistent social and economic disparities in the
   Pacific region, the Government launched the “Plan Todos Somos PAZcífico” (PTSP) in 2015. It was conceived as a
   flagship initiative to foster development, inclusion, and improved living conditions across the historically neglected
   departments of Cauca, Chocó, Nariño, and Valle del Cauca. The Plan sought to improve access to basic services,
   infrastructure, and economic opportunities, and promote alternatives to illegal activities, in line with national
   development and territorial equity goals. To ensure effective implementation and financial sustainability, the
   Government established the Fondo para el Desarrollo del Plan Todos Somos PAZcífico (FTSP) in 2015 under Law 1753
   and Decree 2121. The FTSP is an extra-budgetary, autonomous trust fund that pools resources from various
   government levels and international donors, and can contract debt, providing a stable funding base for large-scale
   multi-sectoral16 interventions without affecting Colombia’s fiscal accounts.

8. In 2023, a major legislative reform significantly broadened the geographic and operational scope of FTSP, paving
   the way for greater development impact in Colombia’s Pacific region. Law 2294, enacted as part of the National
   Development Plan 2022–2026 ¨Colombia Potencia Mundial de la Vida¨, expanded FTSP’s coverage to an additional 11
   municipalities in Chocó, Valle del Cauca, Cauca, and Nariño,17 and introduced a new digital connectivity component to
   complement existing investments in water, sanitation, energy, and transport connectivity. The law also mandated the

 Relationship to CPF
11. The proposed Plan PAZcífico operation is fully aligned with the strategic priorities (HLO) outlined in the World
    Bank’s Country Partnership Framework (CPF) 2024-2027 for Colombia. The Project contributes to HLO1 -promoting
    equitable territorial development and social inclusion by expanding access to quality water and sanitation services and
    transport connectivity infrastructure in historically underserved, conflict-affected municipalities. The operation also
    supports HLO2 -advancing sustainable and inclusive economic transformation through the completion of strategic
    infrastructure expected to unlock broader economic opportunities in remote areas. Finally, the project contributes to
    HLO3 –strengthening resilience to climate change and promoting a low-carbon transition, by incorporating climate-
    resilient infrastructure and sustainable service delivery models tailored to the region’s vulnerabilities which includes
    floods, droughts, landslides and sea level rise.

12. The proposed operation, aligned with the World Bank’s jobs agenda, is expected to generate significant
    employment opportunities in the Pacific region, both directly through infrastructure works and indirectly through
    the activation of local productive chains. By improving transport and WSS infrastructure, the project will facilitate
    access to broader markets and services, enabling the development of sectors such as logistics, agroindustry, tourism,
    and maintenance. This is particularly relevant given the region’s labor market and informality challenges: Chocó
    currently registers one of the highest unemployment rates in the country at 15.9 percent, while Valle del Cauca, Cauca
    and Nariño report rates of 12.1, 8.2 and 6.5 percent, respectively.18 Moreover, labor informality remains a structural
    barrier to inclusive growth. Nationally, 55 percent of employed individuals work informally, but in rural and dispersed

 D. Concept Description

17. The proposed Project extends the Bank’s continued support to the provision of potable water, sanitation and
    transport connectivity services in the Pacific region of Colombia. It will complete and operationalize the
    infrastructure originally envisioned under the Water Supply and Basic Sanitation Infrastructure and Service Delivery
    Project, and the Enhancing Waterway Connectivity and Water Service Provision in Colombia’s Plan PAZcífico Project,
    while also strengthening the institutions in charge of delivering services from this infrastructure. By carrying forward
    and finalizing these investments, the proposed Project aims to convert partial progress into sustained service delivery
    outcomes for water, sanitation and waterway connectivity in one of Colombia’s most underserved regions, while
    addressing the bottlenecks that have slowed execution to date.

18. The proposed project’s design directly addresses the multiple challenges and lessons learned from ongoing
    operations, aiming to foster more effective delivery and sustainable outcomes. Persistent implementation issues,
    such as repeated procurement delays and bid failures, high turnover of consultants and public officers in the Project
    Implementing Unit (PIU), weaknesses in the readiness of design studies, cumbersome technical clearances, contractor
    cash-flow constraints, and misaligned institutional arrangements, have hindered progress. These challenges are
    compounded by the region’s remoteness, vulnerability, and complex regulatory, administrative, and financial
    processes managed from Bogotá, as well as limited capacity and participation of local authorities and stakeholders.
    To overcome these obstacles, the FTSP has identified and will implement targeted actions: (i) simplifying internal
    procedures to reduce response times; (ii) developing a digital strategy to promote the adoption of digital tools and
    enhance interoperability with internal and external systems; (iii) retaining a core team of consultants for cross-cutting
    and supervisory tasks, supported by a management contract to alleviate administrative burdens and introduce
    performance-based incentives; and (iv) decentralizing operations by establishing regional management offices,
    strengthening collaboration with municipalities and local operators, and centering community engagement.

19. As a key lesson learned, the proposed project has a high level of readiness, only financing infrastructure that will
    be fully designed by the closing of the current water and transport operations. The Project features a highly ready
    and diverse portfolio of water, sanitation, and transport projects, ensuring speedy implementation. Of the 20 water
    and sanitation investment projects, about half are already under construction, awarded, or have received technical
    approval, while the remainder will have detailed designs completed by project approval. Contracts from the first
    project will be transferred, with retroactive financing available for those bridging the gap between the two projects,
    and updated technical approvals will be secured as needed. The portfolio also includes three pre-investment
    sanitation projects, with additional municipalities to be identified during preparation and implementation. On the
    transport side, all designs and technical approvals for connectivity infrastructure have been finalized, and the
    structuring of docks across seven municipalities, including construction and operational permits, will enable a smooth
    and timely rollout. Electromobility and signaling strategies, such as the procurement of electric boats and navigation
    buoys, are fully prepared, and the introduction of floating docks provides a modular, strategic solution to complement
    the main infrastructure and further accelerate effective loan implementation.

20. Project cost, duration and scope. The Project’s preliminary cost is estimated at US$205.6 million. Given the complexity
    of interventions in the Pacific Region, implementation is planned over five years to ensure successful achievement of
    the PDO and allow the consolidation of service provision. The Project will achieve its PDO by: (i) implementing water
    supply and sanitation infrastructure not covered by current loans in Tumaco, Guapi, Timbiquí, and Francisco Pizarro,
    as well as transport connectivity infrastructure in La Tola, Santa Bárbara de Iscuandé, Mosquera, Olaya Herrera, El
    Charco, Timbiquí, and López de Micay; (ii) strengthening service providers’ institutional and operational capacities to
    sustain investments and establishing community-based micro enterprises for routine dock maintenance; and (iii)
    preparing technical designs for future water supply, sanitation, and transport connectivity projects in selected
    municipalities.

21. Project Components. Considering the PDO and the Project scope described above, the Project will finance the
    following five components:

    •   Component 1: Improving water supply and sanitation services in participating municipalities (US$ 134.6 million,
        all IBRD). This component finances end-to-end water and wastewater infrastructure to deliver sustainable, safe
        services in prioritized municipalities, accounting for climate, demographic, and environmental stressors. Priority
        is given to underserved urban areas to expand coverage, reduce health risks, and protect water resources. Eligible
        activities include technical studies and designs; rehabilitation and construction of water intakes, treatment,
        storage, and distribution systems; metering and monitoring; sewerage networks and wastewater treatment; and
        targeted solid-waste measures (e.g., disposal cells for residues from WASH works) to mitigate environmental
        impacts and build community acceptance. The component also supports WASH in schools and health centers—
        reliable water supply, gender-segregated and accessible toilets, menstrual-hygiene facilities and materials,
        handwashing stations, and, in health centers, safe healthcare-waste management. Land acquisition,
        compensation, and supervision are eligible across activities. The component includes retroactive financing for
        ongoing water contracts in Tumaco, Guapi, and Timbiquí.
    •   Component 2: Improving waterway transport in participating municipalities (US$ 53.3 million, all IBRD). This
        component will enhance resilient, safe, and accessible waterway transport infrastructure and support zero-
        emission mobility in seven municipalities. Eligible activities include the construction of the already prioritized river
        and maritime docks, and their access paths, with retroactive financing for works in La Tola and Santa Barbara.

22. Gender and Jobs: The Project is designed to address jobs and employment through a multi-faceted approach that
    integrates infrastructure development, capacity building, and gender inclusion. It will create direct jobs during
    construction and operation, formalize roles for waterway operators, and expand the water supply and sanitation
    workforce. Building on previous experience, the Project will train male and female operators in safety, dock
    management, and gender-based violence prevention. The water utility internship program, developed with academic
    partners, will offer technical training, mentorship, and gender-responsive recruitment to address the sector’s gender
    gap. The micro-enterprises created to manage routine maintenance of the new docks will generate local jobs and
    actively promote the inclusion of women in the workforce. The Project will also support the “Mujeres Cuidando
    Territorio” initiative, empowering women as community leaders to address social risks and strengthen protection for
    vulnerable groups.

 Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts



24. Environmental Risk Rating Substantial: The environmental risk of the project is rated as Substantial, given the nature
    and scale of the proposed interventions. The project is expected to deliver positive environmental benefits, including
    improved drinking water quality, sanitation services, and connectivity. A key benefit is the anticipated reduction in
    pollution of natural water bodies through the construction of wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) in selected
    municipalities. The project scope includes: i) completion of the water supply infrastructure of the current project of
    the Todos Somos Pazcífico Plan; ii) construction of new water supply systems with existing designs and Environmental
    and Social Management Plans (ESMPs) previously not objected to by the World Bank; and iii) implementation of new
    sanitation infrastructure, including WWTPs, for which designs and environmental instruments are yet to be
    developed. However, the construction phase may generate adverse impacts in urban areas. Activities include
    trenching along roads, installing water and sewer pipelines, transporting and disposing of materials and excavation
    waste, upgrading existing water treatment plants, and building new WWTPs. Potential risks include: i) traffic
    disruptions; ii) increased accident risks due to diversions; iii) disturbances from noise, vibration, and dust; iv)
    occupational health and safety risks; v) runoff potentially affecting water bodies; and vi) possible alteration of
    riverbanks during dock construction. An Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) will be prepared
    to assess risks and define mitigation measures aligned with the Environmental and Social Standards (ESS). It will also
    include provisions to complement existing ESMPs developed under the Bank’s Safeguard Policies and develop new
    ESMPs for new projects, ensuring full compliance with the Environmental and Social Framework (ESF).


25. Social Risk Rating Substantial: The recommended social risk rating at concept stage is substantial. The project will
    improve well-being by completing water and sanitation works to expand access in homes and in health and education
    facilities, reducing the region’s high burden of water-related disease. Similarly, activities aimed at improving
    infrastructure and river connectivity management will facilitate trade and improve access to social services, such as
    healthcare, education and protection, which are unevenly scattered throughout the territory. Despite the benefits,
    the following social risks and impacts have been identified at this stage: (i) low community trust stemming from past
    failures by national/departmental entities (poor contractor performance, prolonged delays) in the execution of
    activities under current operations which has led to prolonged negative impacts to citizens, particularly to businesses
