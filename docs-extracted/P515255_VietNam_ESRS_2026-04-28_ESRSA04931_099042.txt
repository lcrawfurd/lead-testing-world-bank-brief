                     The proposed MERIT project helps to operationalize the vision and strategies laid out in the 2022 Mekong Delta
                     Regional Master Plan and address current and future climate change impacts. The project would (i) enhance the
                     information and management tools to support adaptive delta management; (ii) build new infrastructure and repurpose
                     and upgrade existing regional infrastructure to enhance climate resilience and promote more sustainable and climate-
                     smart management of water and land resources; and (iii) facilitate climate-smart livelihoods transition and

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The operation will focus on Vietnam's Mekong Delta Region (MDR), a low-lying delta plain comprising mixed soil types
                     — including young alluvials, peat soils, and saline and/or acid sulfate soils — and a mosaic of freshwater, brackish, and
                     saline habitats, including wetlands, mangroves, and coastal ecosystems. These are areas of high biodiversity but also
                     considerable sensitivity to disturbance.

                     Key waterways include the Tien and Hau rivers, the principal distributaries of the Mekong River, which flows through six
                     countries with Vietnam as the lowest downstream riparian. Beyond these main rivers, the delta is served by a dense
                     network of distributaries and canals totaling approximately 66,000 kilometers, with downstream sections subject to
                     severe dry-season salinity intrusion. Seasonal flooding historically delivered sediment and nutrients that sustained the
                     delta's fisheries and wetlands; however, a sharp reduction in incoming sediment in recent years has significantly
                     accelerated coastal and riverbank erosion.

                     Agricultural production in the MDR is intensive, encompassing rice cultivation, fruit orchards, and aquaculture. Climate
                     change and sea-level rise are intensifying salinity intrusion, with dry-season events — such as those documented during
                     2015–16 — now reaching far inland and threatening both rice and aquaculture systems. In response, an extensive
                     system of hydraulic infrastructure has been developed across the MDR, primarily for salinity control and dry-season



Public Disclosure
                     irrigation and water supply, contributing to improved climate resilience in the region. Periodic canal dredging has also
                     been an established practice in the delta to maintain waterway function and drainage capacity.

                     While waterway transport remains an important mode for moving agricultural products, the MDR is also well connected
                     by road and air. Economically, the region supports a predominantly rural population engaged in agriculture, fisheries,
                     and related livelihoods.

                     The MDR comprises five provinces and Can Tho City, with a majority rural population and notable ethnic diversity.
                     Khmer communities are concentrated in Tra Vinh and Soc Trang provinces and frequently face socioeconomic
                     disadvantages relative to the majority population, necessitating inclusive engagement and equitable benefit-sharing
                     arrangements throughout project implementation.

                     Key opportunities for the project lie in integrating socio-environmental considerations and solutions into project design
                     — such as coastal erosion protection measures and the beneficial reuse of dredged materials — to maximize co-benefits
                     and minimize adverse impacts across the region.

                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                     Substantial
                     The Substantial environmental risk rating reflects the scale, nature, and areas of influence of Component 2

                     dredging and embankment upgrading, including small sluices in low-lying deltaic areas where potential acid sulfate
                     soils exist (SP 2.2); and (iii) construction and rehabilitation of sluice gates combined with ship locks at selected sites
                     (SP 2.3 and 2.4). These investments are implemented across a highly modified and hydrologically interconnected
                     Mekong Delta environment characterized by high environmental sensitivity and significant exposure to climate
                     stressors. Common construction-phase impacts are mostly moderate, temporary, and reversible — such as dust,
                     noise, solid waste, and wastewater generation; disturbance to drainage and localized flooding; and erosion and
                     sedimentation. However, certain investment activities and baseline conditions give rise to more substantial impacts
                     and risks. Canal dredging will generate approximately 2.4 million cubic meters of material, requiring around 112

                      employment; nondiscrimination and equal opportunity provisions; prohibition of forced labor and child labor; and

                      fisheries, and biodiversity. The project’s footprint and area of influence are all within modified habitats and not within
                      legally designated protected areas or known critical habitats.. Key ESS6 risks arise from dredging, embankment
                      construction, coastal protection works, and the installation and operation of sluice gates and ship locks. Dredging and
                      in-water construction may result in temporary loss or disturbance of benthic habitats, increased turbidity, reduced
                      dissolved oxygen, and localized impacts on aquatic fauna. Coastal and estuarine works may modify hydrodynamics,
                      sediment transport, salinity gradients, and ecological connectivity, with potential implications for fish movement,
                      spawning and nursery habitats, and nearshore ecosystems. The operation of sluice gates and other hydraulic

                      ESS8 - Cultural Heritage                                                                                       Relevant

                      ESS8 is relevant to this project given that it involves substantial earthworks and dredging in the Mekong Delta, a
                      region with a rich cultural heritage, where there is a risk of encountering known or previously unknown physical
                      cultural resources during construction. Cultural heritage in the project area includes pagodas, temples, shrines,
                      cemeteries, monuments, and other culturally significant sites, many of which are dispersed across rural and canal-
                      side landscapes. Initial screening undertaken as part of the ESIA indicates that major project works are not located
                      within designated cultural heritage sites. However, a residual risk remains of disturbing or damaging undocumented
                      archaeological materials or culturally significant features. Construction activities may also result in temporary access
                      restrictions that could affect religious or cultural practices in nearby communities. Cultural heritage risks therefore
                      extend beyond physical artifacts to include intangible cultural heritage — such as community traditions, rituals, and
                      customary practices — that may be sensitive to construction activity, noise, vibration, or changes in access. The ESIA
                      includes screening and assessment of potential impacts on both physical and intangible cultural heritage, covering
                      the identification of known sites within areas of influence and an evaluation of construction-related access
                      constraints. Mitigation measures to avoid, minimize, or manage potential impacts will be proportionate to the nature
                      and significance of the identified risks. Chance Find Procedures, consistent with national legislation and ESS8
                      requirements, will be incorporated into the Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP) and applied to all
                      civil works. These procedures will require the immediate suspension of works upon discovery of a chance find,
                      protection of the site, notification of relevant authorities, and the application of culturally appropriate management
