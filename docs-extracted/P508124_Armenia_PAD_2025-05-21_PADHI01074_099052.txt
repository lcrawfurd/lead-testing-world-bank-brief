 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT
 A. Country Context
1.      Armenia has made significant strides since gaining independence in 1991, transforming its economy through
sustained growth and market-oriented reforms. From 2000 to 2022, average annual growth reached 6.3 percent,
reducing poverty from 32.1 to 23.7 percent. Growth has been volatile; after reaching 7.6 percent in 2019 following the
Velvet Revolution, the economy contracted by 7.2 percent in 2020. It rebounded in 2021, fueled by inflows of people,
remittances, and rerouted trade, with growth averaging 11 percent in 2022–2023 before slowing to a projected 5.5
percent in 2024. Despite progress, 51.3 percent of Armenians remain below the upper-middle-income country poverty
line.
2.       Climate change poses high economic and social risks for Armenia. The average annual temperature has risen by
2.38 ± 0.29°C since 1960, exceeding the global average, with projections of continued above-average warming.1 Rising
temperatures and more frequent extreme weather events are damaging infrastructure, affecting health and productivity,
and threatening key sectors. Hazardous hydrometeorological events (droughts,2 floods,3 landslides, and heatwaves) have
intensified and are expected to worsen.4 Agriculture is highly vulnerable to climate change, facing declining land and labor
productivity, water shortages, and shifting growing seasons. Women face increasing burdens due to added household
water duties, agricultural labor from male out-migration, and related health and time poverty impacts.5 As highlighted in
the Country Climate and Development Report (CCDR),6 Armenia faces high water stress, with a withdrawal-to-supply ratio
of 57.8 percent and unsustainable groundwater use in areas such as the Ararat Valley. By 2050, unmet irrigation demand
is projected to exceed 50 percent in key basins. These trends underscore the urgency of investing in irrigation
modernization and water resource management to safeguard agricultural productivity, food security, and rural
livelihoods.
3.       Severe droughts and water scarcity harm Armenia’s economy, especially agriculture, highlighting the need for
water sector reforms. Existing water stress and climate risks, such as drought and flooding, make adaptation investments
urgent. By 2050, climate change could reduce crop, and livestock yields by up to 37 percent, causing US$363 million in
agricultural losses. Droughts pose a major threat, with 98 percent of land at risk.7 Rising temperatures and hot winds,
particularly in the Ararat Valley, Vayots Dzor, and Syunik regions, are worsening evaporation and drought. This raises
irrigation demand, encourages unsustainable aquifer use, and undermines irrigation effectiveness. In the Ararat Valley,
hot winds persist 120–160 days per year, and 15 percent of arable land is already drought-prone. Aging and poorly
maintained irrigation, water supply, and sanitation systems are vulnerable to such hazards, leading to service disruption
and yield losses. Increasing climate hazards demand resilient infrastructure and proactive planning. These risks are one of
the main drivers of the proposed Water and Irrigation Services Enhancement (WISE) Program.
4.      Agriculture has declined in its contribution to Armenia’s economy, while industry and services have become
stronger growth drivers. Agricultural gross domestic product (GDP) fell from US$2.1 billion in 2014 to US$1.7 billion in
2018 but grew by 1.6 percent in 2023–2024, reaching US$2.4 billion.8 Agriculture’s share of GDP declined from 37.0
percent in 2012 to about 8.5 percent in 2023. The sector still employs over 225,000 people, 48 percent of whom are
women, representing 19 percent of the total workforce,8 and supporting about 1 million people (30 percent of the
population). Since 2000, services have been the main driver of per capita growth, increasing by 28 percent and rising from

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
5.      Irrigated agriculture is vital to Armenia’s economy, supporting over 225,000 people and accounting for around
80 percent of horticulture output. Yet only 25 percent of arable land is equipped for irrigation, limiting resilience to
climate shocks. Of Armenia’s 208,000 ha of irrigable land, just 95,000 ha (46 percent) are irrigated. Globally, irrigation can
double yields, reduce poverty, and enhance climate resilience by ensuring water access during droughts and extreme
weather. However, aging Soviet-era infrastructure causes major water losses and inefficiencies and reduces farmer
profitability. Irrigation efficiency is only 25 percent, far below the 70–85 percent typical of modern systems, with half of
water lost in distribution. Modernizing infrastructure and improving management could boost water productivity by up
to 40 percent without increasing water use.
6.       Water security in Armenia has deteriorated over the past decade due to groundwater depletion in the Ararat
Artesian Basin (AAB), climate variability, competing uses, and emerging water quality concerns. Increasing irrigation
demand—driven by climate-change-exacerbated longer growing seasons, droughts, and higher crop water needs—
intensifies pressure. River Basin Management Plans (RBMPs) have been developed for five of six Basin Management Areas
(BMAs), estimating usable water resources at 7.55 billion cubic meters (m³), significantly lower than past figures. Per capita
availability is 2,585 m³/year but unevenly distributed, with Hrazdan BMA under the most stress. Groundwater decline,
reduced river flows, and risks to Armenia’s share of the Araks basin further undermine water security. While the Program
does not invest directly in basin organizations or RBMP implementation, it supports an enabling environment through
planned revisions to the Water Code and a new National Water Strategy that aims to improve legal and policy frameworks
for sustainable water allocation. Water quality is also worsening with 29 percent of surface water and 7 percent of
groundwater bodies 'at risk' due to untreated wastewater, agricultural runoff, mining, and landfill contamination. In some
rural areas, potable water systems are used for irrigation, especially during summer shortages, creating tension over water
distribution. According to Statistical Committee of the Republic of Armenia (ARMSTAT),9 87 percent of water use is for
agriculture, fish breeding, and forestry; 7 percent for drinking; and 6.1 percent for other uses. Improving potable water
and irrigation systems in shared watersheds can enhance water quantity and quality outcomes, justifying integrated
investments in water supply and sanitation (WSS) and irrigation in these marzes.10

7.       Armenia’s water resources management is governed by modern legislation aligned with the European Union
(EU) Water Framework Directive, separating resource management from service delivery. The National Water Council
(NWC), once the top coordinating body, was dissolved in 2020, with its functions transferred to the Ministry of
Environment (MoE), raising concerns over diminished influence and cross-sector coordination. The MoE, through the
Water Resources Management Agency (WRMA), oversees water policy and permitting and serves as the technical
secretariat for strategy development. The Water Committee (WC), under the Ministry of Territorial Administration and
Infrastructure (MTAI), leads policy for irrigation and WSS. The Water Supply Agency (WSA), a closed joint stock company
(CJSC), is the designated bulk water supplier to 15 water users associations (WUAs), which manage surface irrigation
delivery through secondary and tertiary systems. Each WUA serves around 10,000 members. Despite notable progress,
the absence of a unified water sector strategy has resulted in fragmented planning, poor policy coordination, and slow
reforms. A comprehensive strategy and a cross-sectoral coordination mechanism are urgently needed.

8.       Access to safely managed WSS in Armenia remains inadequate, particularly in unserved rural settlements where
services are poor and inefficient, posing public health risks and reducing productivity. As of 2022, 100 percent of the
population had access to at least basic water supply, but only 82 percent had safely managed services.11 This masks a stark
urban-rural divide: rural areas outside the national operator’s coverage face low efficiency and high non-revenue water
(NRW), around 70 percent in some settlements. About 83.5 percent of the rural population has access to basic sanitation,12
but only 11 percent of the total population has access to safely managed sanitation. Wastewater infrastructure is
insufficient in both coverage and treatment quality; Armenia’s plants offer only mechanical treatment, and 48 percent of
wastewater is discharged untreated.13 These gaps heighten the spread of water- and excreta-related diseases, especially
during climate-related flooding. Expanding access to safely managed WSS will improve public health and strengthen
climate resilience in vulnerable settlements.
9.      Armenia’s irrigation sector faces critical institutional challenges that undermine effective water delivery. The
WC lacks technical capacity for oversight, with an underperforming Technical Support Unit and weaknesses in planning,
project management, and contract administration. The WSA also struggles to modernize, constrained by limited capacity
and deteriorated infrastructure. Unrealistically low irrigation tariffs hinder maintenance due to limited cost recovery, and
the lack of tools, equipment, and vehicles limit operational capacity. Sector monitoring and asset management remain
weak, with no geographic information system (GIS) or computerized maintenance management system (CMMS) in place.
Addressing these gaps requires investment in infrastructure and sustainable maintenance financing. Despite current
constraints, the WC and MTAI are committed to strengthening institutional capacity and pursuing comprehensive water
sector reform.
10.      Since 2015, WUAs have lacked training and support, facing large service areas, outdated infrastructure, and low
farmer engagement, resulting in significant water losses. The 2024 World Bank survey of six WUAs identified 10 key
challenges: (1) large service areas limiting participation (for example, Kotayk WUA covers 18,755 ha with 20,733
members); (2) no recent training and weak capacity in planning, accounting, and measurement; (3) deteriorated
infrastructure causing major losses; (4) absence of independent financial audits; (5) no investment reserve funds; (6)
outdated irrigation service fees (ISFs), unchanged since 2010 at AMD 11/m³ versus AMD 24/m³ average full cost; (7) debt
to WSA; (8) lack of water measurement devices; (9) low salaries hindering recruitment, especially of women and youth;
and (10) an outdated GIS system that limits planning and monitoring.
11.      Over the past two decades, Armenia has improved WSS services through public-private partnership (PPP)
reforms, reversing post-Soviet decline. The first generation of PPPs enhanced coverage, service continuity, efficiency, and
revenue collection. In 2016, WSS was consolidated under a single 15-year lease contract covering Yerevan, secondary
cities, and some rural settlements, serving around 2 million people.14 Veolia Djur15 now manages services under this
contract, which will expire in 2031. However, about 650,000 people in 579 unserved rural settlements16 remain outside
the PPP scheme, relying on aging systems, spring or groundwater sources, and community-managed services with limited
treatment, efficiency, and oversight. Water quality generally meets physical standards, but bacteriological safety is a
concern due to poor sanitation and inadequate disinfection. These areas also face deteriorating infrastructure, limited

12.       The current policy framework is insufficient to ensure safely managed RWSS in settlements outside the
national lease. Low local tariffs hinder sector development, while the Water Code exempts local non-commercial services
from regulation. The WC and Public Services Regulatory Commission (PSRC) lack oversight of local governance bodies,
resulting in minimal supervision of non-lease water systems. Weak institutional mechanisms limit maintenance and
service expansion. Under its existing contract, the lessee is not always obliged to integrate new settlements. Legal, policy,
and institutional reforms are essential to improve RWSS and ensure long-term infrastructure sustainability.
13.       The sector’s financial model is unsustainable. Bulk irrigation tariffs have remained unchanged since 2007,
creating a 59 percent average financing gap for WSA (2018–2022). The ISFs to be set by WUAs have also been fixed by
GoA at AMD 11/m³ (~US$0.03/m³) since 2010, further straining operation and maintenance (O&M) financing. This
underfunding leads to infrastructure degradation and limits participatory irrigation management. WSS tariffs face similar
challenges. In lessee-covered areas, users pay around AMD 200/m³ (with exceptions for disadvantaged groups), but in 579
unserved settlements, tariffs are minimal or nonexistent, set informally by communities and beyond PSRC oversight. These
unregulated rates17 often fail to cover operational costs, perpetuating underfunding and neglect. The lack of cost recovery
weakens services, accelerates infrastructure decline, and increases dependence on subsidies. Urgent reforms are needed
to establish a cost-recovery policy and secure sustainable financing for irrigation and WSS O&M.
14.      Armenia faces major challenges in water information management, affecting irrigation and water supply
operations. At present, there is no integrated system for ensuring proper management of water sector data, information,
and its sharing among the data producers, decision-makers, academia, and other stakeholders in Armenia. The WC
produces data on water infrastructures (irrigation and WSS) and WUAs produce data on irrigation water supply to farmers.
A Water Infrastructure Information System (WIIS) was developed to integrate water sector data, but it is outdated and
incomplete, preventing coordination across irrigation, drinking water, and sanitation. This fragmentation leads to poor
planning, inefficient resource allocation, and service delivery challenges. Outdated infrastructure and limited technical
capacity further accelerate investment deterioration. Strengthening water information management is essential for a
more sustainable and efficient water sector in Armenia.
15.       Armenia faces climate-change-exacerbated extreme heat, floods, droughts, wildfires, and landslides along
with earthquakes that threaten water infrastructure and require resilient investment strategies. Aging systems,
inefficient water management, and limited financing make irrigation and RWSS particularly vulnerable. While some
investments have been made, such as the Vedi Reservoir, most irrigation infrastructure remains outdated, climate-
sensitive, and prone to high water losses. Unserved rural areas face even greater risks due to inadequate storage and
limited adaptive capacity. The WISE Program aims to strengthen water security through climate-resilient designs, guided
by the World Bank Resilient Water Infrastructure Design Brief,18 and improve risk management across sector agencies. It
focuses on modernization, governance, and climate-informed planning to enhance irrigation and WSS resilience in
targeted marzes, with three marzes receiving integrated investments in both sectors. Addressing RWSS and irrigation
together will strengthen water quantity and quality management, improve service reliability, and enhance rural water
security.

 D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach
     (i) Rationale for Using an MPA
23.      The MPA is the most appropriate modality to support Armenia in addressing the complexity of its water sector
challenges through a long-term, integrated ‘water services’ approach. A 10-year engagement is needed to rehabilitate
and modernize the existing irrigation and water supply infrastructure in Armenia and to ensure access to improved
services to farmers and households. As the current estimated financing needs for water sector investments in
infrastructure and institutional reforms exceed US$1 billion, a programmatic approach is more suitable than standalone
IPFs. The MPA offers a long-term financing framework for the GoA to attract capital at scale, including from international
partners and private capital, enabling scale, sustained engagement, and flexibility. In addition, the WISE MPA Program
promotes long-term climate- and non-climate-related resilient development initiatives for the sector that cannot be solely
handled under a single operation. For instance, under Component 2, the MPA’s phased structure will enable the use of
pilots and learning from them before scale-up of infrastructure investments in the Program’s second phase. In addition,
the institutional capacity-building activities require extended time frames to allow for sustainable, institution-wide
transformation. Under Components 1 and 3, the Program’s goal is to motivate fundamental shifts in the service delivery
capacity of the WC, the WSA, and WUAs, such as introducing innovative approaches related to water accounting using
remote sensing, asset maintenance planning, and shifting towards on-farm water use efficiency. This requires long-term
learning and knowledge exchange throughout the agencies and farmers to be effective. This approach provides flexibility

 D. Rationale for World Bank Involvement and Role of Partners
53.      World Bank financing is justified as the proposed project will invest in supporting an economic transition from
water insecurity to water security for farmers and households. Poor quality of irrigation and WSS services hampers
overall economic productivity, and the cost of inaction is substantial. The urgency of the issues being addressed; the size
of the planned investments; and the market, sector and macroeconomic context, make commercial financing
unaffordable. International experience points to the need for public, concessional financing for investments of this nature,
especially for those targeting rural and poor settlements/communities. No country has achieved universal WSS coverage
or financed irrigation system modernization without significant public financing.
54.     The World Bank brings added value through good practice and current global knowledge in water resource
management, irrigation, and RWSS service provision. The project will draw on lessons from over 20 years of World Bank
engagement in Armenia and leverage its financial, knowledge, and convening services, critical to successful
implementation and proven effective in the past. The World Bank also introduces cutting-edge innovations to Armenia,
including disruptive technologies for agricultural water accounting, water security assessments, and investment models
that crowd in public and private finance. Over recent decades, the World Bank has supported WSS reform and PPP

     E. Lessons Learned
55.      The World Bank has extensive experience supporting Armenia’s irrigation and WSS sectors. This includes the
World Bank’s Irrigation Rehabilitation Project (P075513, 1994–2001), Dam Safety Project (P164879, 1998–2009), Irrigation
Development Project (P055022, 2001–2009), Irrigation Dam Safety Project II (P088499, 2004–2011), IREP (P116681, 2009–
2013), and Irrigation Systems Enhancement Project (P127759, 2015–2019), which have informed the WISE Program's
design. Key lessons include the need for a comprehensive approach integrating resilient infrastructure, improved asset
management, and enhanced O&M capacity. WISE promotes an ‘integrated water services’ approach by addressing both
irrigation and RWSS. It emphasizes effective irrigation and WSS performance management and service delivery, supporting
WC and WSA reforms and developing related sector strategies for resilient water service management.
56.      The proposed RWSS activities are designed to incorporate key lessons from the World Bank’s engagement in
Armenia’s WSS sector reform since the late 1990s as well as from the World Bank’s global experience and analytical work.
The main lessons include (a) clearly defined roles and responsibilities, strong commitment from implementing agencies,
and effective social accountability mechanisms (AMs) are essential for efficient and sustainable RWSS delivery;38 (b) sector
reforms are key to service sustainability at the national, regional, and local levels; the project will support revisions and
implementation of key GoA policies, strategies, and investment programs, development of a national RWSS strategy,
institutional strengthening, and global best practice in sector management, O&M, and post-construction support will
enhance sustainability; and (c) targeted social and behavior change and communication strategies are vital for sustaining
WSS investments—the project will train communities on infrastructure use, tariffs, and responsibilities at the utility, marz,

Armenia. Previous projects (the Municipal Water and Wastewater Project [P063398, 2004–2012]; the Yerevan Water
Wastewater Project [P087641, 2005–2011]) improved WSS efficiency and provider capacity. The phased PPP reforms,
supported by the World Bank’s TA, enhanced service continuity, efficiency, revenue collection, and coverage.
57.      MPA lessons learned. Given the Program's complexity and multi-scale, multi-pronged approach, MPA is the most
suitable instrument, as it enables the delivery of scaled-up solutions. It incorporates lessons learned from past MPA
engagements,39 including the effectiveness of MPAs in achieving long-term impact. However, a key trade-off is that the
planned outcomes may emerge later in Phase 1 and continue into Phase 2. To maintain agility, Phase 2 of the Program is
designed to be flexible, allowing the inclusion of additional activities requested by the GoA. This ensures that MPA learning
activities are directly linked to project implementation, adequately resourced, closely monitored, and adaptable to
incorporate learning moments. The WISE Program emphasizes the importance of mobilizing external financing beyond
IDA/IBRD, leveraging AFD co-financing while creating opportunities for additional funders to join in Phase 2.

68.      The incremental cost-benefit analysis of the project indicates a promising return on investment, considering a

total project costs, including beneficiary contributions, and incorporate the estimated impact of GHG emission reductions,
valued using the low shadow price of carbon in accordance with the World Bank’s guidelines. Without accounting for GHG
emission reduction benefits, the economic IRR is expected to decrease slightly. The sensitivity and scenario analysis, which
examines hypothetical variations in variables and parameters that could affect investment returns, underscores the
robustness of the analysis (see annex 2 for more details on the EFA).

investment for Components 1, 2, and 3 are also projected to be economically viable. All irrigation schemes, except for the

D. Environmental and Social
76.      The project’s overall E&S risk is Substantial. Environmental and Social Standards (ESSs) 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8 and 10
are considered relevant for the operation. While the project is expected to generate positive E&S outcomes by
strengthening institutions and improving the sustainability, resilience, and quality of water services in Armenia, residual

stages. Potential impacts from medium-scale rehabilitation and construction include waste generation, dust and noise

inequitable water access and reduced supply could lead to settlement/community tensions, particularly in relation to tariff

feasible, alternative engineering solutions will be considered, including adjustments to siting and alignments.

disclosure of E&S instruments were done at the pre-appraisal stage, with final revisions incorporating feedback within

78.      Gender and social inclusion. Women, as home makers, and farmers bear the disproportionate burden of poor
irrigation water availability and lack of WSS in the project area. Women’s workload and responsibilities are compounded

by outmigration of male family members, making it more important for them to participate in decision-making. WUA
membership and voting rights are linked to land ownership. While 23 percent of the landowners are women,43
representation of women in WUAs is less than 1 percent and no women have been elected to leadership roles in

handling daily operations. The Dispute Resolution Committee addresses member disputes over water use, and the
Supervisory Committee oversees financial activities. The six WUAs supported by the project cover 48 percent of Armenia’s
irrigated land and include 320 Administrative Council members. The project will strengthen women’s technical and
leadership skills by sensitizing women on WUA roles, responsibilities, and governance functions. Targeted awareness and
sensitization campaigns will be conducted for WUA members and communities highlighting the importance of women as
leaders and decision-makers. The project aims to increase the number of women in WUA leadership roles in the WUA
governance bodies from 0 to 15, as reflected in the Results Framework. The project will identify and address barriers to
women’s participation and representation through as staff training, HR policy improvements, awareness campaigns, and
gender integration into national policies and sector plans. Women’s participation in WUA leadership will help address key
governance issues identified in the pilot, including gaps in communication, representation, financial sustainability, anti-
corruption, and risk management.

 VI. KEY RISKS

85.      The overall residual risk rating for the Program is Substantial. The substantial risks to Program implementation
are political and governance, macroeconomic, technical design of the Program, institutional capacity for implementation
and sustainability, fiduciary, and E&S.
86.      Political and governance risk is Substantial. This is due to ongoing geopolitical challenges and fragility. While
political instability declined after the June 2021 elections, the upcoming 2026 national elections, may impact political
commitment to reforms. This could negatively affect the success of legal reforms, particularly changes to the Water Code
and tariff structures, which require strong political will. To mitigate these risks, efforts will focus on building consensus
among stakeholders and institutionalizing reforms and investments. The residual risk remains Substantial.
87.     Macroeconomic risk is Substantial. This is due to Armenia’s geopolitical instability, refugee integration, and
potential slowdowns in key trading partners' economies, which could create fiscal pressures. Growth is projected to slow
to 4.5 percent, with rising financing needs potentially jeopardizing the US$31 million counterpart financing for Phase 1.

However, Armenia’s sound macroeconomic framework, including inflation targeting, a fiscal rule, and upfront financing

88.      Technical Design of Project risk is Substantial. The residual technical risk for designing, constructing, and
modernizing irrigation and RWSS systems is assessed as Substantial due to the client’s limited capacity for large-scale
water projects. While the World Bank will provide technical support, the project will also engage an international firm to
conduct feasibility studies, design, and supervise construction. Training will be provided for engineering staff on
modernization, works supervision, and MOM. Additional risks stem from the use of PBCs in Component 1, which is new
to the client. To address this, workshops will be held for the PIT on PBC implementation. Armenia’s PforR experience,
including the Power Sector Financial Recovery PforR (closed Satisfactory in 2022), demonstrates its ability to meet
institutional reform targets.
89.      Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability risk is Substantial. Following the 2018 Velvet
Revolution and government transition, there was a termination of all World Bank-financed Project Implementing Units.
The existing WC has limited capacity to manage complex World Bank-financed infrastructure projects, making the
establishment of a dedicated project management structure essential at the start of Phase 1. To mitigate this risk, the GoA
is considering forming a high-level steering committee, at the Deputy Prime Minister level, to oversee coordination and
approvals. A project coordination team within the WC would lead Program implementation. An additional risk is the
national and regional market capacity to absorb a doubling in sector infrastructure development, which will be mitigated
through institutional capacity building and support from international expertise for system design and construction.
90.      The overall fiduciary risk is Substantial due to the WC’s limited experience with World Bank-financed projects
and the newly formed PIT leading Phase 1 implementation. Key risks include lack of knowledge of World Bank procedures,
internal control gaps, inappropriate level of engagement of technical staff in all stages of procurement and contract
implementation, inappropriate chain of procurement-related approvals and lack of appropriate collaboration among
stakeholders leading to procurement delays, and potential fraud. To mitigate these, the World Bank team will provide
training and hands-on support, while the PIT will adopt an appropriate FM system, and establish clear fund flow,
procurement, and audit arrangements. These measures will be in place by effectiveness and closely monitored, with the
risk rating reassessed during implementation.
91.      The overall E&S risk is Substantial. The main environmental, social, health, and safety risks and impacts are
associated with the activities under Components 2 and 3. The risks will be mitigated through the preparation,
implementation, and monitoring of E&S instruments and capacity building for project staff. Specific risks under Phase 2 of
the MPA are generally unknown and will be assessed during the preparation of this phase in the future. Adjustments in
water tariffs in Armenia may disproportionately affect low-income households. However, such risks are expected to be
limited due to Armenia’s well-established social safety nets and implementation of differentiated tariffs with minimal
tariffs applicable to socially disadvantaged groups. Under the project, the distributional impacts of tariff adjustments,
along with mitigation measures and strategic engagement, will be analyzed through an SESA. These assessments will
inform future policy and regulatory decisions in the water sector, ensuring that social impacts are systematically addressed
as part of broader sector reform. Additional risks include beneficiaries’ willingness to pay cost-recovery tariffs and
resistance to new WSS service-provider models. These can be mitigated through behavior change and education
campaigns included in the project.

Description                       This indicator measures the number of people who benefit from project-supported interventions that contribute to increased food

  PBC 1. Asset Maintenance Fund has been established by the WSA (US$2.145 million)
 Formula                     US$2.145 million: advance disbursement method to be applied, with expenditures subject to verification upon achievement of related PBC.
                             PBC 1 supports the establishment of a dedicated Asset Maintenance Fund (or dedicated account) set up by the WSA to finance operation
                             and maintenance (O&M) expenditures of newly modernized irrigation schemes under the WISE project. In cases where WSA tariff revenues
                             or the annual allocation are insufficient, the WC will top up the gap with additional funds from the subsidy received from the State Budget.
                             The AMF will be structured as a ring-fenced account for WSA, providing stable and sustained financing for infrastructure upkeep, for WISE
                             irrigation systems that will be modernized under Component 3. This fund will provide stable, long-term financing for infrastructure upkeep,
 Description
                             preventing deterioration due to insufficient maintenance and enhancing the efficiency and longevity of irrigation investments. The total
                             value of the fund will depend on the types of modernization design and will be calculated according to specific O&M standards for that
                             design by the consulting company responsible for the design, which is spelled out in the design document, cleared by the World Bank. By
                             securing financial resources for O&M, PBC 1 strengthens service reliability, cost recovery, and overall irrigation efficiency in Armenia,
                             supporting the achievement of the PDO of Phase 1.
 Data source/Agency          WC (as a designated agency within MTAI for irrigation)

disbursements, FM aspects, arrangements for training and operating costs, supervision, and reporting provisions. The

feedback measures. The PSC will be responsible for high level project coordination (along with other ministries and IFIs)

will help better monitoring of program activities.
2.       The PIT will have a team of environmental and social specialists mandated to ensure project implementation
is consistent with all relevant environmental and social requirements. These requirements include implementation of
framework environmental and social management instruments as well as development and application of site-specific
environmental and social management tools, oversight on environmental and social performance, and reporting on the
environmental and social impacts and outcomes of the Program. Additional specialists will be hired during
implementation on a need basis and will be re-assessed during the initial year of implementation. These additional
specialists will include at a minimum an Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) specialist, and a gender and community
outreach specialist. The necessity to mobilize dam safety experts will be determined once technical designs and
specifications of the proposed regulating reservoirs are available. These requirements are outlined in the Program’s ESCP.

                                                        ANNEX 3: Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Analysis

1. A greenhouse gas (GHG) accounting analysis was conducted for Component 2 water supply activities and Component
   3 irrigation activities expected to be completed in Phase 1 of the MPA. Under component 2.2, the project plans to
   provide potable water service to an estimated additional 17,000 people using gravity-based systems. Under components
   3.1 and 3.2, the project will improve and expand five irrigation schemes in Hrazdan, Arzni-Shamiram, Kotayk, Lori, and
   Debetavan using energy efficiency and water saving modernization techniques. These activities were included in the GHG
   analysis.
2. For component 2, the GHG analysis balanced an increase in treatment energy with a decrease in pumping. The analysis
   utilized the World Bank‘s GHG Accounting Tool, with project provided data on population served, volume of water
   processed per person, type of treatment to be used and data on current pumping done in areas to be served by the
   project water supply scheme. Although some water boiling might be present in the project area, energy used for boiling
   was not included due to lack of data. For the baseline scenario, the project team estimated that 5-10 percent of the
   population currently uses pumped systems, averaging 125 l/day/person with 70 percent non-revenue water (NRW)
   pumping 85-150 m in height at 70-72 percent efficiency. The project scheme will provide water to an estimated 17,000
   people when first implemented. The project team provided the assumption of 125 l/day/person of water treated by the
   scheme, with NRW lowered to 45 percent thanks to water saving modernization techniques. The analysis used the World
   Bank’s Databank to project population change over the economic lifetime of the project (assumed to be 25 years), during
   which Armenia is projected to decrease in population and used the midpoint of the start and end points of the project
   for the accounting.
3. The component 2 water supply activities are estimated to reduce emissions due to the elimination of pumping. The
   baseline scenario emissions from pumping were estimated at 275 tCO2e over the economic lifetime of the project or just
   under 11 tCO2e/yr, while the emissions from the chlorine treatment provided by the gravity-powered project water
   supply system were estimated at 17 tCO2e over the economic lifetime or 0.67 tCO2e/yr. This amounts to a 94 percent
   decrease in emissions.
4. For component 3, the GHG analysis accounted for increased use of irrigation, energy saving modernization techniques
   and change in cropping patterns. The analysis utilized the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) ExACT Tool, with
   project-provided data on current irrigated area, area to be newly irrigated by irrigation schemes improved by the project.
   The project team also provided data on the cropping patterns in the currently irrigated areas and expected patterns in
   the currently and newly irrigated areas post rehabilitation. This included crop types45 and the area of each, yields (which
   are expected to increase due to improved irrigation) management modalities including tillage, organic material and
   residue management. Information on the energy sources of the irrigation schemes was also provided—all schemes
   currently use gravity-based systems except for Hrazdan (there are some pumps in Arzni-Shamiram but data for them was
   not available, and so they were thus conservatively excluded from the GHG analysis), which involved electrical grid
   powered pumping for 30 percent of the irrigation volume. Due to introduced water and energy saving techniques such
   as implementation of SCADA, reduction in leakages and pressurization of canals, the project expects to reduce the volume
   of water needed per irrigated hectare by about 43 percent and further reduce the amount of pumping needed in Hrazdan
   by 20 percent.
5. Results show that emissions will be reduced in three schemes (Hrazdan, Debetavan and Lori) and increased in two
   schemes (Kotayk and Arzni-Shamiram). The results show that schemes which will have the largest change in cropping
