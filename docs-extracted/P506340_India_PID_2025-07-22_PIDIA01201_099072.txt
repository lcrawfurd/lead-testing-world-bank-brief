                           B. Introduction and Context
                           Sectoral and Institutional Context
                          1.       Maharashtra is one of India’s economic growth engines but faces increased frequency of extreme weather
                          events and climate change impact that could affect sustained development. Maharashtra is India’s second most
                          populous and third most urbanized state1, contributing 14 percent of national GDP and targeting a US$1 trillion
                          economy by 2027.2 However, the state has been experiencing recurrent floods, landslides, droughts, heatwaves, and
                          cyclones, with 1,246 lives lost between 2019 and 2022.3 Climate change, combined with growing exposure of people and
                          assets, threatens lives, livelihoods, and infrastructure.
                          2.      Urban flood risk is growing with extreme rainfall, degraded river systems, and insufficient water management
                          infrastructure. In 2019, the districts of Kolhapur and Sangli experienced rainfall 6 to 18 times the seasonal norm, leading
                          to over US$500 million in damages.4 Flooding now occurs more frequently, driven by increasingly intense precipitation,
                          encroachment on floodplains, and inadequate stormwater and river conveyance capacity. River channels have narrowed
                          due to sedimentation, obstructions, and unregulated development, reducing their ability to absorb floodwaters. Key
                          flood control structures, including weirs and spillway gates, are outdated and insufficient to manage today’s risk levels.

                          The cities of Kolhapur, Sangli5, and Ichalkaranji are particularly exposed but have limited capacity to address
                          compounding risks of fluvial flooding, poor drainage, and increased precipitation. Tailored solutions, including strategic
                          river restoration, climate-resilient dam infrastructure upgrades, and drainage upgrades can safeguard lives,
                          infrastructure, and economic activity. Meanwhile, increased rainfall intensity also raises landslide risk, threatening
                          densely populated slopes and critical access to heritage and religious sites.
                          3.       The project promotes climate- and disaster-resilient development in Maharashtra through an integrated,
                          multi-pronged approach. It targets critical gaps in flood risk governance, emergency preparedness, and financial
                          resilience. The project focuses on: (i) state-level systems for risk management, reservoir operations, and emergency
                          response; (ii) flood risk reduction in the Upper Krishna River sub-basin, where climate-driven floods have caused
                          repeated loss and damage; (iii) multi-hazard resilience in urban and peri-urban areas, including Kolhapur, Sangli, and
                          Ichalkaranji; and (iv) risk financing mechanisms for vulnerable households and MSMEs. The project also strengthens
                          technical and institutional collaboration across agencies managing water, forecasting, emergency response, and early
                          warning systems.
                          4.      The project aligns with national and World Bank strategies on resilience building. It supports India’s Nationally
                          Determined Contributions and the Long-term Low-Carbon Strategy. It contributes to the FY18–25 World Bank Country
                          Partnership Framework (Report No. 126667-IN) under Objectives 1.5 and 2.3 by strengthening disaster preparedness,

                          climate-smart infrastructure, and institutional capacity.
                          5.       The project contributes to the MFD approach and will mobilize private capital (Private Capital Mobilization).
                          Component 4 is verified MFD-e and PCM. MFD-e activities in Component 4 include technical studies for climate finance
                          products, unlocking financing for climate resilience investments and catastrophe insurance products, and increasing
                          overall public awareness of climate finance products. The interventions are expected to leverage commercial bank
                          lending for climate resilience of private assets in Maharashtra and will increase the share of disaster-related losses
                          covered by private capital through catastrophe insurance.

                          6.        The project will measure progress toward the PDO via the following proposed indicators: (i) People benefitting

                          warning systems; and (iii) People covered by risk finance and insurance.
                             D. Project Description
                          7.       The project seeks to strengthen resilient development in Maharashtra by demonstrating an integrated climate
                          and disaster risk management approach. This includes strengthening overall risk governance and emergency
                          management capacity at state level, flood risk reduction activities, especially in the upper Krishna River sub-basin, multi-
                          hazard risk reduction in the settlements such as in the cities of Kolhapur, Sangli, and Ichalkaranji, and risk financing to
                          mitigate residual risks at MSME- and household-level across the state. In addition, the project will facilitate technical and
                          institutional collaboration between authorities to ensure seamless data flows and feedback loops. These include

                          authorities responsible for water resources management, flood hazard monitoring and forecasting, emergency
                          preparedness, multi-hazard early warning system, and emergency response. The project components are as follows.
                          8.        Component 1 – Climate-Informed Flood Risk Management. This component addresses systemic flood risk,
                          especially in the Upper Krishna River sub-basin, a hotspot of climate vulnerability and infrastructure exposure. To reduce
                          fluvial flood risk, the component includes: (i) enhancement of reservoir operations and forecasting systems using AI-
                          enabled models and upgraded hydromet networks; (ii) targeted river interventions such as cross-section restoration,
                          widening, embankment reinforcement, removal of obstructions, construction of flood bypasses, and replacement of
                          weirs and spillway gates; (iii) rejuvenation of nalas, restoration of minor irrigation tanks, and scoping of nature-based
                          solutions (NbS); and (iv) capacity building and feasibility studies for climate-resilient flood and drought management.
                          These investments are informed by climate-informed modeling and will establish an integrated water management and
                          early warning system.
                          9.       Component 2 – Multi-Hazard Resilience in Districts and Cities. This component supports urban and district-level
                          resilience in downstream cities increasingly affected by floods, landslides, and extreme heat. Kolhapur, Sangli, and
                          Ichalkaranji are growing urban centers with limited stormwater management capacity, encroached water channels, and
                          high climate risk exposure. Subcomponent (2a) will finance flood risk mitigation measures such as drainage upgrades,
                          culvert improvements, desilting, and NbS that link parks, water bodies, and green spaces. These measures will be

                          prioritized based on high-resolution flood risk maps and will also address urban heat and pollution. Subcomponent (2b)
                          will address rising landslide risks, particularly in Kolhapur district through risk assessments, early warning systems, and
                          tailored mitigation works, including bioengineering solutions as guided by zonation and risk maps.
                          10.     Component 3 – Enhanced Emergency Management Capacities. This component will strengthen Maharashtra’s
                          emergency response infrastructure and coordination jurisdictions. It will upgrade district and city levels Emergency
                          Operation Centers with modern command-and-control systems, resilient IT and communication infrastructure, and an
                          Integrated Emergency Management System. These investments will support impact-based early warning and faster
                          response. The component also includes state-wide multi-hazard risk assessments and early warning dissemination
                          measures, including community-level capacity building. This will improve preparedness for climate-amplified hazards
                          such as lightning, landslides, and floods.
                          11.      Component 4 – Private Capital Mobilization for Risk Financing. This component will mobilize private capital to
                          strengthen household and MSME financial resilience to climate and disaster risk. Climate shocks disproportionately
                          impact vulnerable populations and businesses. This component promotes disaster risk financing through public–private
                          partnerships and market-based solutions. It will support analytics and policy design, operationalize the Maharashtra
                          Resilience Financing Program, and provide incentives for housing and business climate resilience building. It will also
                          build capacity for creating green jobs and increase public awareness of insurance and climate finance products, helping
                          shift residual risk coverage from public to private mechanisms.
                          12.      Component 5 – Implementation Support and Knowledge Management. This component will ensure effective
                          project delivery, institutional coordination, and long-term learning. It covers core project management functions
                          including financial management, procurement, safeguards, stakeholder engagement, and monitoring and evaluation. It
                          will also support the establishment of institutional frameworks for cross-sector coordination between water, disaster,
                          and emergency management authorities. A study will assess the feasibility of institutionalizing an interagency flood risk
                          coordination mechanism. The component will further promote knowledge generation, capacity development, and
                          dissemination at state and national levels to inform future investments and institutional reforms.

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts


                          13.      The environmental and social (E&S) risk classification is rated 'High' due to the scale and complexity of proposed
                          interventions. Preliminary hydrodynamic modeling indicates that flood risk mitigation in the Upper Krishna River sub-
                          basin under Component 1 will require a combination of large-scale structural and nature-based interventions. These may
                          include: (i) enhanced climate-informed reservoir operations and forecasting; (ii) river cross-section restoration, selective
                          widening, removal of obstructions, construction of flood bypasses, and embankment reinforcement along the Krishna and
                          Panchganga rivers; (iii) replacement of outdated weirs and spillway gates at Radhanagari Dam (heritage site); (iv)
                          rejuvenation of natural drainage channels; (v) installation of sluice gates or rubber dams at tributary confluences; (vi)
                          restoration of minor irrigation tanks; and (vii) implementation of NbS to be identified through a scoping study. These




_For Official Use Only_
                          activities carry significant E&S risks that are being proactively managed through a robust institutional framework.
                          Anticipated environmental impacts include disruption to aquatic and riparian habitats, changes in flow regimes,
                          vegetation clearance, and construction-related pollution. Social risks relate primarily to land acquisition and temporary
                          economic displacement. Preliminary estimates suggest 215 hectares of land may be required, potentially affecting around
                          600 people, though physical relocation is not expected.
                          14.      MRDP is supported by comprehensive mitigation measures aligned with the World Bank’s Environmental and
                          Social Framework (ESF). The Environmental and Social (E & S) risk management approach is guided by robust
                          Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) that establishes processes for screening, impact assessments,
                          and preparation of sub-project-specific Environment and Social Management Plans (ESMPs). The ESMF covers risk
                          identification, impact assessment, mitigation plans/strategies as well as Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP), Labor
                          Management Procedures (LMP), Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF), Indigenous Peoples Planning Framework (IPPF) as
                          well as risk mitigation plans, procedures, and codes of practice related to community health and safety, occupational
                          health and safety, cultural heritage, biodiversity management and sexual exploitation and abuse/sexual harassment
                          (SEA/SH). Specific environmental measures include sustainable sourcing and efficient use of construction materials,
                          protocols for managing construction waste and pollution (including dust, noise, and water contamination), biodiversity
                          assessments and management plans, exclusion of protected areas from interventions, as well as dam and flood safety
                          protocols such as emergency preparedness plans. Key social risk mitigation measures include proactive measures for
                          minimizing physical and economic displacement, compensation and livelihood assistance for project affected persons,
                          community and occupational health and safety measures, stakeholder engagement and grievance redressal mechanisms
                          and integration of E&S commitments into contract and bidding documents. These measures will be implemented by
                          Environment and Social (E&S) nodal officials and experts in the Project Management Unit (PMU) and Project
                          Implementation Units (PIUs).

                           Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
                          15.      The proposed project implementation setup involves the establishment of a Project Management Unit (PMU)
                          and several Project Implementation Units (PIUs). The PMU will be set up at MITRA, including a High-Level Steering
                          Committee for regular monitoring and coordination. A PIU for disaster risk management activities will be within the
                          Disaster Management, Relief and Rehabilitation Department (DMR&RD) and for activities at river basin level within the
                          Maharashtra Krishna Valley Development Corporation (MKVDC) within the Water Resources Department. Both agencies
                          have experience of implementing externally aided projects, including World Bank financed projects.6 Three PIUs will be
                          set up at the municipal corporations of Ichalkaranji (IMC), Kolhapur (KMC), and Sangli, Miraj and Kupwad City (SMKMC)
                          respectively. A PIU at MITRA will be responsible for the implementation of Component 4.
