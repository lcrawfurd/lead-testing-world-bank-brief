1.       PROJECT DESCRIPTION
1.      Hai Phong City is an important economic, port, and industrial center of the Northern coastal
region. The rapid urbanization process, population growth, expansion of industrial zones, together
with the increasing impacts of climate change, have created major pressure on the drainage–flood
control infrastructure system, water environment, and smart urban management.
2.       Some main challenges the city is facing include:
     •   Flooding occurs frequently in the central urban areas.
     •   Water quality of the Re River and the canal system is declining.
     •   Insufficient regional transport connectivity;
     •   Impacts of extreme heavy rainfall, storms, and high tides are increasingly strong due to
         climate change.
3.     In this context, the Hai Phong City Climate Resilience Development Project (HPRDP) is
proposed to:
     •   Reduce urban flooding;
     •   Improve the water environment and landscape of the Re River;
     •   Enhance strategic traffic connectivity (Ring Road No. 3);
     •   Strengthen capacity for smart urban–environmental management;
     •   Increase the city’s resilience to climate change.
4.    The HPRDP will be implemented in 13 wards of Hai Phong City, including the wards of:
Bach Dang, Hoa Binh, Dong Hai, Luu Kiem, Thuy Nguyen, Nam Trieu, Hong Bang, An Hai, Ngo
Quyen, Gia Vien, Le Chan, An Bien, An Duong.
5.     Objectives of the HPRDP include: The overall objective is to develop and promote socio-
economic growth of Hai Phong City, and ensure sustainable urban development through an integrated
multi-sector investment package focusing on urban connectivity and regional linkages, ensuring
environmental sustainability, water security and reducing urban flooding, climate adaptation,
upgrading public spaces and enhancing urban resilience.
6.       Specific objectives:
     •   Reduce urban flooding and adapt to climate change for the inner urban areas of Hai Phong
         City through construction and upgrading of drainage systems.
     •   Improve environmental conditions toward climate adaptation, combining drainage, improved
         sanitation, urban space development and landscape creation, promoting green development
         and advanced services, improving the living quality of residents in inner-city areas, along
         both sides of the Re River in particular and Hai Phong City in general.
     •   Improve regional traffic connectivity to promote development along the Northwest–Southeast
         marine economic corridor, boost logistics development, aiming toward building Hai Phong
         as a regional linkage center.
     •   Enhance flood risk management and water quality control capacity in river basins, ensuring
         safe and sustainable water supply for the city through construction, training, and technological
         transfer for smart management and monitoring systems; contributing to administrative reform
         and the formation and development of a smart Hai Phong City.
7.       The investment scale of the Project includes 4 components, as follows:

Component 1: Development of Thuy Nguyen urban infrastructure to adapt to climate change
This component involves construction of Ring Road No. 3 connecting Hai Phong’s ring road system
with regional traffic in Thuy Nguyen and synchronized development of urban infrastructure to
minimize environmental pollution, avoid traffic congestion, and reduce travel time.
Total length: approximately 18.68 km, including about 2.107 km of Ngo Quyen bridge and 428 m
Ruot Lon bridge sections crossing the Cam River and Ruot Lon River.
The initial investment stage will implement a 27 m cross-section, with four major intersections and
two large bridges (Ngo Quyen Bridge and Ruot Lon Bridge).
Component 2: Upgrading the environment of Re River
This component aims to ensure freshwater security, improve water quality and environmental
sanitation of the Re River, strengthen stormwater drainage management, prevent saline intrusion and
adapt to climate change and rising tides. At the same time, combine synchronous investment in roads
and infrastructure works on both sides of the Re River to connect with traffic along the river and
south of the Re River, creating landscapes and public green space axes for the area from An Duong
to Hong Bang. This component is divided into 2 sub-components including:
   •   Subcomponent 2.1 (Upgrading the infrastructure of the Re River), including: i) Construction
       of a road along the Re River with the main function of a management road along the river,
       creating space for arranging a wastewater collection system; ii) Upgrading existing
       embankments and constructing additional river bank embankments in other areas with a total
       length of about 11km.
   •   Subcomponent 2.2 (Upgrading environmental sanitation of the Re River basin), including: i)
       Construction of a sewer system to collect wastewater on both sides of the Re River with
       17.3km of self-flowing sewers, 1.4km of pressure sewers and 12 wastewater pumping stations
       along the Re River management road, a pumping pipeline leading to the An Duong 1
       Wastewater Treatment Plant (Hoang Mai), construction of about 11.6km of a level 3 sewer
       network to collect household wastewater to connect synchronously with the wastewater
       collection sewer line. ii) Construction of the An Duong 1 Wastewater Treatment Plant (Hoang
       Mai) phase 1 with a capacity of 10,000m3/day. iii) Investing in equipment for urban flood
       risk management systems, water quality management and air environment management for
       areas within the Project's investment scope.
Component 3: Central Urban Flood Reduction
This component aims to enhance the capacity to contain, regulate, reduce flooding, control water
levels and improve the water environment of Tam Bac River/Lake, creating conditions for the
development of public spaces, urban redevelopment, cultural and historical values and a modern,
attractive urban area. The main items include:
   •   Construct 01 regulating sluice at Tam Bac bridge with Thuong Ly river and 01 regulating
       sluice at Thuy Doi cape at the mouth of Tam Bac river flowing into Cam river, forming a new
       Tam Bac river regulating lake. In the body of the regulating sluice dam at Thuy Doi cape, a
       rainwater pumping station with a capacity of 12m3/s will be built to pump rainwater from
       Tam Bac river lake to Cam river outside the urban area. Tam Bac river lake combined with
       the existing Tam Bac lake forms a chain of regulating lakes in the central urban drainage
       basin, with a total area of about 14-15ha.
   •   Constructing a box sewer line BxH=2.5x2.0m - length L=280m connecting the existing Tam
       Bac lake with Tam Bac river lake. Constructing and renovating a number of drainage lines to
       ensure corresponding capacity and enhance drainage capacity, including sewer lines with

       dimensions from BxH=1.0x1.0m to BxH=3.0x2.0m, 4.36km long. The total length of the
       stormwater drainage lines is about 4.64km.
   •   Constructing a wastewater collection system including wastewater collection sewers and
       wastewater pumping stations from both sides of Tam Bac River leading to Vinh Niem
       Wastewater Treatment Plant with about 2.6 km of main wastewater collection sewers,
       wastewater separation wells, 05 wastewater pumping stations and renovating and increasing
       the capacity of the current Lan Be Pumping Station and level 3 sewers.
Component 4: Developing systems and capacities to improve urban resilience to climate change
Component 4 is divided into 3 sub-components, including:
   •   Sub-component 4.1: Improving flood risk management capacity
   •   Sub-component 4.2: Improving water quality management and environmental management
       capacity
   •   Sub-component 4.3: Project management

2.        OBJECTIVES AND SCOPE OF STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT
          PLAN
2.1.      Objectives of the SEP
9.       The overall objective of this SEP is to define a program for stakeholder engagement,
including public information disclosure, grievance redress mechanism and consultation, throughout
the implementation and operation of the proposed Projects. The SEP outlines the ways in which PMU
and contractors will communicate with stakeholders and includes a mechanism by which people can
raise concerns, provide feedback, or make complaints about the Project, contractors, and the Project
itself. Consultation and engagement with the local population is essential to the success of the Project
in order to ensure smooth collaboration between Project staff and local communities and to minimize
and mitigate environmental and social risks related to the proposed Project.
10.       The specific objectives of the SEP include:
      •   Establish a program for stakeholder engagement, including public information disclosure,
          grievance redressal mechanisms and consultation, throughout the implementation and
          operation of proposed Projects;
      •   Develop a mechanism through which stakeholders can raise concerns, provide feedback, or
          make complaints about the Project to minimize and limit environmental and social risks
          related to the Project;
      •   Demonstrate the commitment of Project implementing agencies with engagement of
          stakeholders in the Project implementation process, including identifying investment
          priorities and considering stakeholder’s concerns.
2.2.      Scope of the SEP and the Project’s Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
11.     The scope of the SEP is to assess the level of stakeholder interest and support for the Project,
enable stakeholder views to be taken into account in Project design and environmental and social
performance, promote and provide means for inclusive engagement throughout the Project life-cycle,
ensure that appropriate Project information is disclosed to stakeholders in a timely, understandable,
accessible, and appropriate manner and format, provide citizens with accessible and inclusive means
to raise issues and grievances and enable the Project implementing entity to respond to and manage
such grievances.
2.2.1. Positive Impacts
12.     The assessment results indicate that the Project will generate significant long-term
environmental and social benefits, particularly in the areas of flood control, water quality
improvement, enhancement of environmental sanitation conditions, strengthening climate resilience,
and promotion of sustainable urban development. These positive impacts are expected to outweigh
the potential negative impacts, which are largely short-term, localized, and can be mitigated.
13.     Key benefits include: (i) reduction in the frequency and severity of urban flooding in key
catchment areas such as the Tam Bac Canal and the Re River; (ii) significant improvement in surface
water quality through the collection and treatment of domestic wastewater; (iii) enhancement of raw
water security for the urban water supply system; (iv) improvement of transport connectivity,
logistics efficiency, and traffic safety; and (v) enhancement of urban landscape, public health, and
living conditions of local residents.
14.   Component 1 – Urban Roads and Associated Infrastructure will generate long-term socio-
economic and environmental benefits through the establishment of a high-capacity transport corridor
                                                                                               Page|5
STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN (SEP)

connecting the Hanoi–Hai Phong Expressway with National Highway No. 10. By alleviating pressure
on the existing road network, the Component will help reduce travel time, ease traffic congestion,
and enhance the efficiency of goods transportation. Drainage and lighting facilities integrated into
the design will contribute to reducing localized flooding and improving traffic safety. This
Component also plays an important role in climate-resilient urban development, as its design
standards take into account future increases in heavy rainfall and extreme weather events.
15.     Component 2 – Re River Embankment, Collection Network, and An Duong 1 Wastewater
Treatment Plant is identified as the Component generating the most significant positive
environmental impacts. The construction and rehabilitation of the river embankment will stabilize
riverbanks, reduce erosion, and protect housing, agricultural land, and riverside infrastructure. The
wastewater collection and treatment system will significantly reduce the direct discharge of untreated
wastewater into the Re River, thereby improving water quality and environmental conditions along
the entire river reach. In particular, this Component will contribute to improving raw water quality
for the An Duong and Vat Cach water treatment plants, thereby enhancing the safety and reliability
of the domestic water supply for more than one million residents and the city’s industrial zones.
16.     Component 3 – Flood Control Structures, Stormwater Drainage, and System
Modernization will deliver direct and substantial benefits in reducing urban flooding, particularly in
the Tam Bac catchment area. The integrated combination of regulating culverts, pumping stations,
detention ponds, and the stormwater drainage network will form an effective flood management
system, helping attenuate flood peaks, reduce hydraulic overload, and limit street inundation. The
collection of wastewaters along the Tam Bac River will also contribute to improving water quality,
reducing odors, and enhancing the urban landscape.
2.2.2. Risks and Negative Impacts
2.2.2.1. Impacts and Risks during the Preparation Phase
17.     During the preparation phase, the most significant impacts are related to land acquisition,
resettlement, livelihood impacts, and safety risks associated with unexploded ordnance (UXO)
remaining from past conflicts. The Project requires large-scale land acquisition, affecting thousands
of households, including several households requiring resettlement and many households losing
agricultural land, aquaculture land, and business activities.
18.     Resettlement and livelihood impacts are assessed as significant, particularly for households
depending on agricultural production and aquaculture, vulnerable groups, and households that must
relocate their residences. However, these impacts can be mitigated through the full implementation
of the Resettlement Plan (RP), including compensation at replacement cost, resettlement assistance,
livelihood transition support, and income restoration programs.
19.   UXO risk is assessed as moderate due to the historical context of the Hai Phong area and the
deep excavation required for certain Project components. This risk will be managed through
mandatory UXO survey and clearance prior to construction in accordance with national regulations.
2.2.2.2. Impacts and Risks during the Construction Phase
20.     The construction phase will generate various temporary environmental and social impacts,
including dust, air emissions, noise, and vibration; construction wastewater and solid waste; impacts
on traffic and existing technical infrastructure; occupational health and safety and community safety
risks; as well as social risks related to labor influx.
21.     Quantitative assessment indicates that dust and noise are the most significant impacts at
certain sub-components passing through densely populated areas and narrow urban corridors.
However, these impacts are localized, short-term, and controllable, as construction will be carried
                                                                                             Page|6
STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN (SEP)

out in sections, and impacts can be mitigated through technical and management measures proposed
in the ESMP and ESCOPs.
22.     Impacts on water quality during construction are mainly associated with workers’ domestic
wastewater, construction wastewater, and runoff carrying suspended sediments. With the application
of appropriate management measures (collection, temporary treatment, covering, and runoff control),
these impacts are assessed as effectively mitigable.
2.2.2.3. Impacts during the Operation Phase
23.     During the operation phase, residual negative impacts are expected to be low to moderate and
mainly related to waste generation, wastewater, and traffic safety risks. In contrast, long-term benefits
in flood reduction, water quality improvement, enhanced raw water security, and improved efficiency
of urban infrastructure will continue to be realized. Operational impacts can be managed through
appropriate operation, maintenance, and environmental and social monitoring procedures.
2.2.2.4. Indirect, Cumulative, and Climate Change Impacts
24.    The Project may generate certain indirect impacts, such as changes in land use, increases in
land values, traffic growth, and additional pressure on urban infrastructure. However, these impacts
are generally consistent with planned urban development trends and are assessed as manageable.
25.    Cumulative impact assessment indicates that, when considered together with other Projects
in the area, the Project does not significantly increase environmental pressures beyond carrying
capacity thresholds. On the contrary, several Project components (drainage, wastewater treatment,
and flood control) will contribute to reducing adverse cumulative impacts.
26.     With regard to climate change, the Project has been designed taking into account key climate
risks in Hai Phong, including heavy rainfall, flooding, sea level rise, and strong storms. The
infrastructure components contribute to enhanced climate resilience, while the Project’s greenhouse
gas emissions are assessed as limited relative to the long-term benefits delivered.
2.2.2.5. Overall Conclusion
27.     Overall, the assessment results indicate that, provided environmental and social mitigation
and management measures are fully and effectively implemented, the Project’s environmental and
social benefits outweigh the potential negative impacts. From an environmental and social
perspective, the Project is suitable for implementation and will make a significant contribution to the
objectives of sustainable urban development, climate change adaptation, and improvement of living
conditions for the residents of Hai Phong City.

3.      STAKEHOLDER IDENTIFICATION AND ANALYSIS
28.     Stakeholders of the HPRDP are individuals and organizations that have a role in the Project,
may be affected by the Project, or have an interest in the Project. Project stakeholders are identified
as individuals, groups, or other entities that: (i) are affected or likely to be affected, directly or
indirectly, positively or negatively, by the Project (also referred to as “affected parties”); and (ii) have
an interest in the Project (“interested parties”). This group includes individuals or organizations
whose interests may be affected by the Project and who have the potential to influence the Project’s
outcomes in any way.
29.    Cooperation and negotiation with stakeholders throughout Project development typically
require the identification of individuals within stakeholder groups who act as legitimate
representatives of their respective groups, namely those entrusted by other group members to
represent and safeguard the group’s interests during engagement and participation in the Project.

                                                                                                  Page|7
STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN (SEP)

Community representatives can provide valuable insights into the local context and serve as the
primary channel for disseminating Project-related information, as well as acting as focal points for
liaison and coordination between the Project and target communities and their existing networks.
Verification of stakeholder representatives (i.e., the process of confirming that they are indeed
legitimate and trusted representatives of the communities they represent) is an important task in
establishing engagement with community stakeholders. The legitimacy of community
representatives can be verified through informal exchanges with a random sample of community
members and by listening to their views on who can most effectively represent the community’s
interests.
3.1.   Methodology
30.    To meet the best practice approaches, the Project will apply the following principles for
stakeholder engagement:
   •   Openness and life-cycle approach: public consultations for the Project will be arranged during
       the whole lifecycle, carried out openly, free of external manipulation, interference, coercion,
       or intimidation.
   •   Informed participation and feedback: information will be provided to and widely distributed
       among all stakeholders in an appropriate format; opportunities are provided for
       communicating stakeholders’ feedback, and for analyzing and addressing comments and
       concerns.
   •   Inclusiveness and sensitivity: stakeholder identification will be undertaken to support better
       communications and build effective relationships. The participation process for the Project
       will be inclusive. All stakeholders at all times will be encouraged to get involved in the
       consultation process. Equal access to information will be provided to all stakeholders.
       Sensitivity to stakeholders’ needs is the key principle underlying the selection of engagement
       methods. Special attention will be given to vulnerable groups, in particular women, displaced
       persons, persons with disabilities, youth, elderly.

stakeholders have different levels of interest and influence with respect to the Project design and
implementation. Therefore, it is necessary to clearly understand: (i) the level of interest of each
stakeholder in the Project’s investment activities; (ii) the extent of Project impacts on them,

and implementation to varying degrees. Their level of interest in the Project’s investment activities
also varies across different stages of the Project life cycle, thereby creating different levels of
influence on the Project’s design and implementation processes.

4.       STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PROGRAM
49.     To achieve the objectives of the Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP), the Project will develop
a stakeholder engagement program with the following purposes:
     •   To provide meaningful information, in forms and languages that are understandable to
         stakeholders;
     •   To provide advance information on consultation activities whenever possible;
     •   To disseminate information in ways and at locations that are easily accessible to stakeholders;
     •   To establish two-way dialogue, enabling the Project and stakeholders to exchange views and
         information, while ensuring that issues are heard and addressed;
     •   To ensure inclusiveness in the expression of views, including those of women, the elderly,
         persons with disabilities, and other vulnerable groups, as appropriate;
     •   To ensure the removal of barriers to participation where identified, in order to obtain
         comprehensive feedback from different stakeholder groups;
     •   To ensure that clear mechanisms are in place to receive and respond to public concerns,
         suggestions and/or complaints;
     •   To integrate stakeholder feedback into Project implementation and provide feedback to
         stakeholders;
     •   To monitor and supervise stakeholder engagement activities and, where feasible, to mobilize
         stakeholder participation in monitoring activities;
     •   To integrate stakeholder engagement as part of Project management responsibilities and to
         ensure that PMU staff and consultants are assigned clear responsibilities and provided with
         adequate budget allocation.

4.3.   Consultation Strategy
63.     The Project implementing agencies will mainly use methods such as interviews, focus group
discussions (FGDs), public meetings, and workshops to conduct consultations. These activities will
be organized directly in the affected areas to facilitate the participation of vulnerable and
disadvantaged groups. The consultation strategy will also be adjusted to suit each phase of the
Project, as presented below.
4.3.1. Consultation Strategy
4.3.1.1.Project Preparation Phase
64.     During this phase, as the details of some proposed investment items have not yet been fully
identified, consultation activities will focus on a broad range of stakeholders to understand their
concerns and general views on the Project as well as potential positive and negative impacts, thereby
serving as a basis for the development of the Project’s environmental and social frameworks. This
phase is also for identifying vulnerable/disadvantaged groups related to the Project.
65.     The consultation methods to be carried out by the PMU with the support of relevant
consultants are proposed as follows. Information disclosed during the consultation process includes
Project contents, environmental and social requirements, relevant legal regulations, and programs:
   •   Community consultations (at ward/commune level);
   •   Focus group discussions (FGDs);
   •   Public posting at ward/commune People’s Committee offices;
   •   Provincial People’s Committee websites;
   •   World Bank websites.
4.3.1.2. Project Implementation Phase
66.     During the implementation phase, Project activities are expected to include community
consultation and stakeholder engagement activities that have been planned and committed since the
Project design stage. These activities may be described in various Project documents and will
mobilize the participation of multiple stakeholder groups. Commonly applied engagement methods
include public hearings, community meetings, focus group discussions, site surveys, and individual
interviews. Considering growing concerns about the risk of disease transmission, it is necessary to
adjust approaches and methods for continuing consultation and stakeholder engagement activities.
Project implementing agencies should pay attention to the following:
   •   Identify and review planned activities within the Project framework that require stakeholder
       engagement and community consultation;
   •   Assess the expected level of direct engagement with stakeholders, including meeting venues
       and scale, frequency of engagement, stakeholder groups (international, national, local), etc.;
   •   Identify Project activities for which consultation/engagement is critical and cannot be
       postponed without significantly affecting the Project schedule, for example, the selection of
       resettlement options for affected persons during Project implementation; based on each
       specific activity, consider feasible methods to adequately collect necessary inputs from
       stakeholders;
   •   Assess the level of information and communication technology (ICT) penetration among key
       stakeholder groups to identify communication channels that can be effectively used in the
       Project context.

67.    Based on the above considerations, Project implementing agencies need to consider selecting
appropriate communication channels when conducting consultation and stakeholder engagement.
Some notes in selecting communication channels include:
   •   Where small-scale meetings are permitted, conduct consultations in small groups, such as
       focus group discussions; where not permitted, make maximum efforts to organize online
       meetings through platforms such as Webex, Zoom, Google Meeting, or Microsoft Team;
   •   Diversify communication methods, increase the use of social media and online channels;
       where appropriate, establish dedicated online platforms and exchange groups suitable for the
       purpose, based on the type and stakeholder groups;
   •   Use traditional communication channels (television, press, radio, dedicated telephone
       hotlines, and postal mail) for stakeholders who do not have access to or rarely use online
       channels; traditional channels can also be very effective in disseminating relevant information
       and receiving feedback and recommendations;
   •   Where direct engagement with affected people or Project beneficiaries is necessary, such as

   •   Each proposed engagement channel should clearly specify how stakeholders can submit
       feedback and recommendations;
   •   In most contexts and situations, an appropriate engagement approach can be established.

4.3.1.3. Project Operation Phase
69.     During this phase, consultation activities will focus on collecting feedback from PAPs on the
effectiveness of the implementation of environmental and social mitigation measures during the
construction phase. Consultation on employment opportunities for operation and maintenance
(O&M) will also be conducted. Areas for improvement and lessons learned will be identified for
subsequent Project components. Monitoring and evaluation results, including GRM results, will be
disclosed and fed back to stakeholders in general. These activities will be carried out through
quarterly community consultations and information bulletins.
70.     The SEP will be reviewed and updated during Project implementation as necessary to flexibly
adjust stakeholder engagement methods, particularly the proposed approaches, methods, and forms
of engagement, while also assessing potential risks related to disease transmission when conducting
engagement activities.
4.3.2. Consultation Methods
71.     The methods used to conduct consultations have a direct impact on the outcomes of the
Project’s consultation strategy. Therefore, these methods need to be designed to suit the diverse
characteristics of each stakeholder group. The following consultation methods will be applied for the
Project:
72.     National-level consultation. National-level consultation is particularly useful for
representatives of state agencies, non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and other groups that
have an interest in and the potential to influence the Project. This form of consultation is also useful
during the Project design phase and will be organized annually to provide updates on Project progress
to stakeholders. National-level consultation may be conducted in person or online in the form of
conferences, which may include small discussion groups or other methods to ensure sufficient time
for group discussions and Q&A sessions.
73.     Local-level consultation. Local-level consultation is the primary method for engaging
stakeholders affected by the Project and is usually conducted through direct group meetings. In some
cases, individual consultations or focus group discussions may be conducted, particularly to reach
and capture the views of vulnerable or disadvantaged groups. Special attention should be paid to
listening to the voices of women and vulnerable groups, for example through women-only FGDs.
For women, the Project will ensure that consultation activities are organized at times convenient for
them and, where possible, activities such as FGDs will be facilitated by women (particularly for
sensitive topics such as health or sexual issues).
74.     An important method to mobilize stakeholder participation is through training courses on key
topics related to the Project such as gender, the Project GRM, labor rights (for both men and women),
child labor, HIV/AIDS and sexually transmitted diseases (STDs), gender-based violence (GBV),
sexual harassment, sexual exploitation and abuse. Due to the sensitivity of certain topics, appropriate
measures should be applied, such as organizing gender-segregated training groups or having
male/female trainers conduct the training as appropriate. Training courses may be delivered by the
PMU or the Project’s consultants.
75.     Accessibility issues will also be considered, particularly to ensure that people with disabilities
can participate in consultation activities, as well as to address the specific needs of other groups such
as the elderly and poor households.
76.     Consultation with Project workers. The Project will make efforts to prioritize the use of local
unskilled labor to minimize labor influx from outside. Consultation activities will encourage local
workers, including ethnic minorities, women, and vulnerable or disadvantaged groups, to apply for

                                                                                               P a g e | 40
STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN (SEP)

available job positions. Contractors and their personnel need to be fully consulted and trained on
sensitive issues, including respectful behavior, the Code of Conduct (CoC), GBV (including SEA
and SH), HIV/AIDS and STDs, as well as GRM and LMP for workers. At the same time, they need
to be fully informed of construction requirements and schedules. Methods such as training, on-site
interviews, and regular feedback exchanges with the PMU and/or the Construction Supervision
Consultant (CSC) will be used as consultation methods.

4.4.   Stakeholder Engagement Methods to Be Applied in Subsequent Phases
Public meetings/community meetings
78.     The Hai Phong PMU will organize Project kick-off meetings at the City level and meetings
in each ward within the Project area, with the participation of local leaders, Project affected persons
(PAPs), and other interested parties. These meetings will be conducted in an open-meeting format,
in which the PMU will present Project information and participants will be encouraged to provide
comments, opinions, and express their concerns.
79.     Various forms of consultation with stakeholder groups will be implemented and fully
documented; at the same time, community-level meetings will also be organized with the
participation of both men and women (including vulnerable households) to better understand the
needs, expectations, and concerns of these population groups in relation to the Project. Feedback
collected during the meetings will be recorded, together with the measures that the Project will
implement to address such feedback. Meetings at each district will also be organized on a quarterly
basis.
Communication materials
80.      Written information will be widely disclosed to the public through various types of
communication materials, including leaflets, posters, etc. The PMU will also regularly update its
website (at least on a quarterly basis) with key Project updates and reports on Project implementation
results in Vietnamese. The website will also provide information on the Project’s Grievance Redress
Mechanism (GRM).
Training, workshops
81.     Training courses on various social issues will be organized for the PMU and may be extended
to other relevant state agencies or non-governmental organizations. Training contents will include
raising awareness on social inclusion/exclusion, labor issues, and risks related to gender-based
violence (GBV).
82.     In accordance with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Standard 10 (ESS10), a
Project-specific grievance redress mechanism will be established to handle complaints and emerging
issues; this mechanism will be integrated with the existing national GRM system for citizens.
Dedicated communication materials (specifically leaflets or GRM handbooks) will be developed to
help local people clearly understand grievance channels and procedures. The PMU will coordinate
with administrative agencies to maintain a grievance register to record and track complaints from
receipt through resolution and feedback to complainants.
83.    Initial efforts to resolve complaints to the satisfaction of complainants will be undertaken at
the administrative levels. This mechanism is described in more detail in Section 5 below. Complaints
may be submitted anonymously; however, in such cases, it will not be possible to determine the
complainant’s level of satisfaction with the resolution outcome. The PMU website, together with
posters and leaflets, will provide clear information on how any stakeholder can submit feedback,
questions, comments, concerns, and complaints. At the same time, these materials will clearly
describe the grievance handling process, including procedural steps and timelines for resolution.

6.       GRIEVANCE REDRESS MECHANISM (GRM)
96.      The Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) is publicized and operated to timely address
concerns/complaints through a process that is easy to understand, culturally appropriate, easily
accessible, and free of charge. Complaints may be submitted when individuals/organizations believe
that the Project is causing adverse impacts on the community, the environment, or their quality of
life. Stakeholders may also submit comments and recommendations regarding the Project.
97.     The Environmental and Social Officer (ESO) of the PMU is responsible for overseeing the
receipt and resolution of complaints/concerns of Project stakeholders in a fair, objective, and
constructive manner. The core principles of the GRM include ensuring that:
     •   The lawful and legitimate rights and interests of affected people are protected;
     •   Concerns arising during Project implementation of affected people are adequately considered
         and addressed;
     •   Entitlements or livelihood restoration support for affected people are provided in a timely
         manner and in accordance with Project documents such as the ESMP and RP;


                                                                                                                     P a g e | 53
STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN (SEP)

   •   Affected people are fully informed of their right to access free grievance procedures for the
       above purposes.
98.     Based on the SEP, to the extent possible, the Project Team will make efforts to resolve
grievances at the earliest stage to avoid escalation of issues. The GRM does not impede access to
judicial or administrative remedies as provided under national law. Stakeholders may access the
courts at any time, independently of the Project-level grievance resolution process. In addition to
complying with the World Bank’s requirements under ESS10, the GRM will be consistent with
current Vietnamese laws, in particular the Law on Complaints (2011) and the Law on Denunciations
(2018).
99.     The PMU will establish the GRM and appoint a GRM focal point to coordinate with local
authorities in handling Project-related grievances. The GRM design will take into account the views
of affected stakeholders and factors considered to ensure accessibility. The PMU is responsible for
disclosing and explaining to all stakeholders—particularly affected persons and
vulnerable/disadvantaged groups—the grievance submission procedures as early as possible and
maintaining such disclosure throughout the Project life cycle. The GRM is provided free of charge
and must ensure ease of access, with particular attention to accessibility for vulnerable and
disadvantaged individuals/groups.
100. The PMU will ensure that grievances are registered, tracked, and resolved in accordance with
the established timelines. The GRM officer is responsible for:
   •   Developing and disclosing the grievance management procedures;
   •   Receiving, screening, and tracking grievances (as applicable);
   •   Ensuring that grievances are resolved within the agreed timelines.
101. Stakeholders will be consulted on the GRM during stakeholder engagement to ensure
accessibility and adequacy of the mechanism. Separate GRMs are applied for: (i) Project workers, as
detailed in the LMP; and (ii) Project affected persons (PAPs) due to land acquisition, as detailed in
the RP. All information on grievance procedures, grievance forms, and responses will be provided in
both Vietnamese and English, as follows:
6.1.   Local-Level Grievance Redress Procedures (multi-tier)
102. First level – Ward People’s Committee (WPC). Affected stakeholders may submit
complaints to any staff member of the ward/commune People’s Committee through the
hamlet/residential group head or directly to the commune-level People’s Committee, either in writing
or verbally. The commune-level People’s Committee will work directly with the complainant to
resolve the issue and issue a decision within 15 working days from receipt of the complaint. The
office unit of the commune-level People’s Committee is responsible for recording and storing all
complaints under processing. After the commune-level People’s Committee issues a decision, if the
complainant does not agree with the decision or does not receive a response after 15 working days,
the complainant may escalate the complaint to the higher-level People’s Committee within 30
working days.
103. Second level – City People’s Committee (CPC). After receiving the complaint from the
stakeholder, the provincial-level People’s Committee has 30 working days to resolve the case. The
provincial-level People’s Committee is responsible for maintaining records of all complaints under
its jurisdiction. After the provincial-level People’s Committee issues a decision, if the complainant
does not agree with the decision or does not receive a response after 30 working days, the complainant
may file a lawsuit with the People’s Court within 45 working days.

                                                                                            P a g e | 54
STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN (SEP)

104. Final level – Court decision. In the event that the stakeholder brings the case to the provincial-
level People’s Court and the court issues a ruling in favor of the complainant, the decision ensuring
grievance resolution must be sent to the complainant and relevant parties and publicly posted at the
offices of the relevant People’s Committees. The complainant will receive the decision within 03
days from the grievance resolution outcome at the commune level and within 07 working days at the
commune or provincial level.
105. The GRM officer will coordinate with the above levels to establish and maintain a database
of Project-related grievances from stakeholders, including information such as: the nature/content of
the grievance, source of the grievance, date of receipt, name and address of the complainant, action
plan, and current status (see Annex 1); including both verbal and written complaints. Where
necessary, the GRM officer may directly contact the complainant and guide them through the steps
in accordance with the Project’s official GRM channels.
6.2.   Project-Specific Procedures for Grievances Arising During the Construction Phase
106. During the construction phase, the GRM will also be managed by the Contractors under the
supervision of the Construction Supervision Consultant. Through consultation and information
disclosure activities, the Contractors will inform stakeholders about the GRM and the procedures for
submitting Project-related complaints and feedback. The Contractors will also appoint an
Environmental and Social Officer (ESO) responsible for handling grievances. All complaints and
corresponding corrective actions undertaken by the Contractors will be recorded in the Project
monitoring reports. Complaints and claims for damages may be submitted through the following
channels:
   •   Verbally: directly to the Contractor’s ESO or the Contractor’s representative at the site
       office;
   •   In writing: submitted directly or sent by post to the designated Project address;
   •   By telephone, fax, or email: sent to the Project GRM focal point or the Contractor’s
       representative.
107. Upon receipt of a complaint, the Contractor’s ESO will register the complaint in the records
and maintain a grievance log until the complaint is resolved (see Annex 1). Immediately upon receipt,
the complaint will be copied into four (04) copies: the original for filing; one (01) copy for the ESO;
one (01) copy to be forwarded to the PMU; and one (01) copy to be sent to the complainant. The
information recorded in the grievance log will, at a minimum, include:
   •   Date and time of receipt of the complaint;
   •   Name, address, and contact details of the complainant;
   •   Brief description of the complaint;
   •   Actions taken to address the complaint, including engagement with relevant parties and
       verification results at each step of the resolution process;
   •   Final resolution outcome of the complaint.
108. The primary objective is to resolve issues at the site level to the maximum extent possible.
Where immediate resolution is not feasible, the complaint will be addressed within one (01) week. If
the complaint cannot be resolved within 15 days, local authorities will be mobilized to participate in
the resolution process (in accordance with Section 6.1). Such situations may arise, for example, where
there are claims for compensation, disagreements over compensation levels, or the need to determine
the cause of damage.

6.3.   Procedures for Handling Cases Related to Gender-Based Violence (GBV/SEA/SH)
109. To avoid risks of stigmatization, further psychological/emotional harm, or retaliation, the
GRM will apply a separate and sensitive approach to cases related to GBV/SEA/SH. When such a
case is reported through the GRM, it must be immediately referred to appropriate service providers,
such as medical and psychosocial support, emergency shelter, and/or other necessary services. At the
same time, the case must be reported to PMU’s GRM Officer, who may advise and coordinate
relevant service providers as needed.
110. Data on GBV/SEA/SH cases will not be collected through the GRM unless the GRM operator
has been trained in empathetic, non-judgmental, and confidential complaint intake. Only information
on the nature of the complaint (as described by the complainant) and limited basic demographic data
such as age and gender will be recorded, in line with good practice.
6.4.   Procedures for Handling Project Worker Grievances
111. Project workers may submit grievances (related to employment opportunities, wages,
payments, working conditions, health, safety, administrative and disciplinary decisions, etc.) either
in writing or verbally; except for grievances related to disciplinary decisions, which must first be
submitted to the employer/Contractor for initial resolution. Complainants may also submit grievances
through the PMU via email, postal mail, telephone, or the PMU’s dedicated GRM channels.
Complainants have the right to bring their case to court at any time during the grievance resolution
process (see details in the LMP).
6.5.   Procedures for Handling Cases Related to Land Acquisition
112. Grievances related to land acquisition, physical resettlement, and related issues are expected
to be submitted through the ward-level People’s Committees (WPCs). Complaints may be submitted
via email, post, direct submission, or verbally through direct meetings or by telephone. Within 10
days from the date of receipt of the complaint, the competent authority responsible for grievance
resolution must receive the complaint and notify the complainant in writing.
113. The grievance resolution timeframe—calculated from the date of receipt—is 30 days for first-
time complaints, and 45 days for second and third-time complaints. The statute of limitations for
filing a lawsuit (submission to court) is 30 days from the expiry of the grievance resolution period,
or from the date the complainant receives the grievance resolution decision. For remote or difficult-
to-access areas, this period may be extended but shall not exceed 45 days. Complainants may also
bring the case to court at any time during the grievance resolution process if they disagree with the
resolution decision or if the case is not resolved within the prescribed timeframe (see details in the
Project RP).

7.4.   Feedback to Stakeholders
123.   Feedback mechanisms for stakeholders will depend on specific target groups, as follows:
   •   Community concerns: The Project will provide feedback to communities and other

       stakeholders within the timeframes specified in the stakeholder engagement program.
   •   Individual concerns, including grievances related to E&S issues such as inaccurate
       measurement of affected land areas, inappropriate compensation unit prices, or requests for
       additional livelihood restoration support: the PMU and local implementing agencies will
       respond directly to individuals through the same channels used to receive the grievances, to
       facilitate follow-up by complainants. Responses through the GRM will be provided in writing

       posted on the websites of the PMU and the City People’s Committee (CPC), and publicly
