 I. PROJECT STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING

A.      Project Background
     1. The Loan of US$ 200 million was approved by the Board of Directors on June 15, 2023 with a closing date of
        December 30, 2028. The total Project cost is US$300 million, which is financed by a Bank loan in the amount of
        US$ 200 million; co-financing from the Agence Française Développement (AFD) of US$ 50 million equivalent in
        Euro and a Global Concessional Financing Facility (GCFF) concessional financing of US$ 50 million. The Project
        is an IPF with Performance Based Conditions (PBCs) which incentivize performance improvement in the sector.
     2. The Project implementing agencies are the Water Authority of Jordan (WAJ), the Jordan Valley Authority (JVA)
        and the Ministry of Water and Irrigation (MWI), as well as the three water companies (WCs) to which WAJ has
        delegated implementation of infrastructure works. Since detailed designs were not available for investments under
        this Project at the time of Appraisal, the procurement strategy for the Project is to hire consultants to provide design
        and supervision services, followed by smaller, lower-value and lower-risk works contracts with one consultant and
        one contractor hired per geographic area. To accelerate implementation, the Project shifted three sub-projects to
        design-build contracts during the mid-term review (MTR) held in September 2025.
     3. The Project remains highly relevant to Jordan’s economic growth and development, aligning with the Economic
        Modernization Vision, the Reform Matrix 2.0, and the Country Partnership Framework as it will significantly
        contribute to reducing non-revenue water (NRW), improving water supply availability for 1.8 million people and
        improving financial sustainability of the sector before the Aqaba-Amman Water Desalination and Conveyance
        Project is operational (expected 2029).
B.      Project Implementation Status
     4. Project disbursements to date have reached US$11.3 million (US$ 9.5 million IBRD+GCFF and US$ 1.8 million
        AFD1). Funds committed undersigned contracts to date amount to US$13 million, primarily in contracts for design
        and supervision of NRW reduction and energy efficiency works contracts across multiple governorates (designs for
        US$ 93 million works are underway), as well as contracts to develop a drought monitor and to assess the safety of
        all large dams in Jordan. Procurement is underway for design and supervision of an additional five sub-projects,
        with consultancy contract awards anticipated by May 2026. Each implementing unit has a clear procurement plan
        and corresponding timeline that will deliver the works planned under the Project before the closing date if the
        Project does not experience additional major delays. In addition, the client is at the award stage of a contract with a
        firm to provide technical assistance to all implementors (commencement anticipated in March 2026), which is an
        important step toward enhancing operational and technical capacity, and accelerating implementation.
        Implementors have achieved several of the objectives set out in the PBCs, however, these have not yet been verified
        for disbursement.
     5. Despite the low disbursement and limited funds committed under contracts, the Project Development Objective
        (PDO) can be achieved by the Project closing date. The pace of implementation has accelerated over the past eight
        months, benefitting from Hand-on Extended Implementation Support (HEIS) for both procurement, and
        environmental and social risk management provided by the Bank since May 2025. A projection of design
        completion, procurement of works, commencement and finalization of works shows that without additional major
        delays, all sub-projects can be completed by June 30, 2028, allowing for a six-month buffer period before the
        Project’s closing date. The remaining funds will be disbursed over the coming two years and 10 months remaining
        for Project implementation, with a significant increase in disbursement projected in FY27 when all works contracts
        will be underway2.

     6. Given the slow pace of implementation, the Implementation Progress (IP) rating was downgraded to Moderately
        Unsatisfactory (MU) in July 2025. The PDO rating remains Moderately Satisfactory (MS) since the PDO can still
        be achieved within the Project’s implementation period. The team will recommend upgrading the Implementation
        Progress rating to Moderately Satisfactory (MS) once the majority of design and supervision contracts have been
        awarded, and the technical assistance firm contract has been awarded.
C.      Rationale for Restructuring
     7. The MTR that was carried out in September 2025 found that the client remains committed to implementation of the
        Project and that the Project objectives can be achieved by the closing date. However, restructuring is necessary to
        support continued acceleration of implementation by (i) streamlining the PBCs; (ii) transferring fiduciary
        responsibilities of MWI to WAJ; (iii) aligning financial resources with agencies with more urgent needs and
        implementors with stronger implementation capacity; and (iv) revising result indicator targets to align with sub-
        projects prioritized for implementation.
     8. Streamline PBCs. The PBCs remain important to focus attention on institutional strengthening and performance
        improvement in the water sector, however, four of the PBCs require streamlining to reduce duplication and to
        accelerate implementation (PBCs 1 and 2) and an additional sub-PBC needs to be added to cover the full range of
        institutional strengthening actions undertaken by JVA (PBCs 4 and 5). The changes proposed in this restructuring
        reduce the complexities of the PBCs by limiting the total number and aligning PBC targets more closely with the
        sector’s requirements. These changes will facilitate achievement and simplify verification while maintaining a high
        standard for performance.
     9. The Project will expand the eligibility of expenditures for the PBCs to include a broader set of WAJ, MWI and JVA
        expenditures as well as to include expenditures of WCs to ensure that all recurrent expenditures required to achieve
        the PBCs may be documented for disbursement to the Treasury upon achievement. The Project will use the Jordan
        Audit Bureau (JAB) for verification of the PBCs, in line with other projects in the Bank’s portfolio and with projects
        supported by other development partners in the water sector on similar topics.
     10. The Project will continue using the same fiduciary arrangements. However, the fiduciary responsibilities of
         MWI of the Project (Sub-Component 3.1) will be transferred to WAJ, given MWI’s limited capacity for
         procurement and financial management. This requires WAJ to handle procurement of services and contract
         management for all activities under Component 3.1, while relying on MWI’s technical leadership for the Sub-
         Component and with payments processed upon MWI’s certification that deliverables are acceptable. The Bank
         deems this modification to financial management and procurement to be acceptable. The Project has been
         submitting the Semiannual Interim Unaudited Financial Reports (IFRs) in acceptable format and content and there
         are no overdue audit reports. The first audit report covering the period extending from inception to December 31,
         2025, will be due on June 30, 2026.
     11. Align financial resources with implementation capacity. Procurement of Component 2 investments intended to
         reduce energy requirements of the water sector have been particularly slow due to capacity constraints. The MTR
         recommended maintaining the sub-projects that are currently under contract or under procurement (sub-project 1
         energy efficiency: US$ 21m; design and supervision contract awarded, procurement of works anticipated in
         December 2026; sub-project 2 solar PV: bids for US$ 17m for design-supply-install renewable energy investments
         are currently being tendered), but defer initiation of additional studies originally planned under this Component.
         This recommendation will allow the sector to deliver on the actions critical to achieving the PDO. This restructuring
         will reallocate US$ 12m from Component 2 to Component 1 to augment NRW reduction designs for future
         investments.
     12. Revise indicator targets to reflect GOJ priorities. The Project was prepared using a framework approach for
         identification of sub-projects for NRW reduction and energy efficiency improvements. The indicator targets for
         these areas were estimated based on nominal unit per cost basis for PDO 1 - Volume of NRW reduction achieved
         through network replacement and rehabilitation works and preliminary feasibility studies for PDO 3 - Electricity
         use reduced. After approval, WAJ and the WCs selected the sub-projects that are supported by this Project based

    13. Environmental and Social. No change to the Project scope is planned under the proposed restructuring, therefore

 II. DESCRIPTION OF PROPOSED CHANGES
    15. This section contains a description of the following proposed changes: (i) streamlining the PBCs; (ii) transferring
        fiduciary responsibilities of MWI to WAJ; (iii) aligning financial resources with agencies with more urgent needs
        and implementors with stronger implementation capacity; and (iv) revising result indicator targets to align with sub-
        projects prioritized for implementation.
    16. Revise PBCs. PBC 1 focuses on modernizing NRW monitoring and reporting. Since the Project was approved,
        WAJ has developed a more strategic approach to monitoring the water distribution which is based on a sector-level
        IT Roadmap and is aligned with best practice. PBC 1 will be updated to reflect and incentivize adherence to this
        more strategic approach but will retain the important focus on disaggregating commercial and physical losses.
    17. PBC 2.1 focuses on WC performance improvement actions, and this restructuring will simplify targets related to
        preparation of strategic plans. PBC 2.2 seeks to incrementally increase budgetary allocation for NRW maintenance.
        However, because of the strategy WAJ and the WCs use to create audited balance sheets, WC budget used for NRW
        maintenance and use of grant financing for the same are difficult to disentangle and will make verification overly
        complex. This restructuring will drop PBC 2.2 and split its allocation between PBC 2.1 and PBC 5 ($498,165
        reallocated to PBC 5, $2,502,836 reallocated to PBC 2.1)3.
    18. PBC 3 incentivizes use of field surveys to inform customer relationship management (CRM). During the MTR,
        this PBC was deemed to be too complex for the implementation capacity of the client and was considered to
        duplicate PBC 2 as CRM actions are also performance improvement actions. To reduce complexity and eliminate
        the possibility of double counting, this restructuring will drop PBC 3 and will consolidate its resources into PBC
        2.1.
    19. For PBC 4, MWI identified that improving digital systems to modernize water allocation systems is a critical gap
        in water allocation planning, therefore this PBC will reallocate resources from yearly water allocation plans to
        incentivize use of digital systems.
    20. During implementation of PBC 5, the task team and JVA identified the need to develop maturity matrices as an
        important step toward development of the Dam Safety Risk Index. Therefore, PBC 5 is amended to adequately
        capture the full range of institutional strengthening actions being carried out by the implementing agency.

  4. This restructuring will also amend Schedule 5 of the legal agreement to expand the eligibility of expenditures for
     the PBCs to include expenditure categories for fixed costs of WAJ, MWI and JVA as well as to include expenditures
     of WCs.

  5. Transfer fiduciary responsibilities of MWI to WAJ. The legal agreement will be amended to remove financing
     from Category 2, currently designated for implementation by MWI, and funds will be reallocated to Category 1, for
     implementation by WAJ. The client will update the POM accordingly to reflect revised implementation
     arrangements.

  6. Align financial resources with implementation capacity. Reallocate US$ 12m from Component 2 to Component
     1 to augment NRW reduction investments. This figure is based on calculations of the Component allocation ($54m)
     less than the actual or estimated values of the three contracts that are awarded or under procurement ($37.5m) and
     cost escalation contingency of 20 percent of estimated values for contracts that are not yet awarded to account for
     recent price escalation anticipated in the global market.

  7. Revise indicator targets to reflect GOJ priorities. The Project uses a framework approach to sub-project
     identification. At appraisal, the client had not selected the specific sub-projects for financing under the Project,
     preferring to wait until after completion of the Capital Investment Master Plan to prioritize investments for which
     completion was planned after appraisal but before anticipated signing of the Loan Agreement. Therefore, the
     volume of NRW reduction, number of district metered areas (DMA) and length of pipe was estimated on a nominal
     unit per cost basis and the total financing allocated to Component 1. Since appraisal, the client selected the specific
     sub-projects that will be financed by the Project and the client provided an updated estimate of NRW reduction due

     development partners, resulting in a moderate reduction in scope of the Component. To reflect the revised
     production capacity PDO indicator 3 - Electricity use reduced and IR 2.0 - Net greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions
     will be revised.
