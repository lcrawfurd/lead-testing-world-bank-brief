                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The Metro Dhaka Water Security and Resilience Program is situated in one of the most densely populated, climate-
                     exposed and environmentally degraded urban regions in the world. The broader Government program targets the
                     metropolitan Dhaka conurbation — spanning Dhaka North City Corporation (DNCC), Dhaka South City Corporation
                     (DSCC), and Narayanganj City Corporation (NCC), with institutional linkages to BWDB, WARPO, DoE and DWASA for
                     water-related regulatory and operational functions. The IPF TA will finance strategic upstream work that will shape the
                     design, sequencing, and social acceptability of downstream PforR investments in drinking water supply, on-site
                     sanitation and fecal sludge management, stormwater and canal rehabilitation, and solid waste systems — particularly in
                     high-risk built-up urban settings where informal occupation, land scarcity, service inequities, and legacy environmental



Public Disclosure
                     liabilities converge.

                     Dhaka’s primary water bodies (Buriganga, Turag, Balu, Shitalakhya) and feeder canals act simultaneously as drainage
                     sinks, informal dumping points, navigation routes, ecological corridors, and — for the urban poor — physical living
                     space. Persistent discharge of untreated sewage and industrial effluent has erased ecological function in large stretches,
                     while encroachment by informal and formal actors has constricted hydraulic capacity and fuelled recurrent flooding.
                     These pressures intersect with demographic patterns: along canal embankments and drainage alignments live high
                     concentrations of non-title holders (NTHs) and informal occupiers (IOs) exposed to both eviction risk and health
                     exposure from polluted sediment and wastewater. The Program context is further complicated by fragmented
                     institutional mandates — DNCC/DSCC/NCC, DWASA, BWDB and DoE each hold partial jurisdiction, resulting in systemic
                     coordination failures.

                     The environmental profile is dominated by legacy contamination (heavy metals, hydrocarbons, pathogenic load in canal
                     sediments), high climate exposure (pluvial flooding, prolonged waterlogging), mobility constraints during construction,
                     and cumulative pollution from multiple sectors discharged into a shared hydrological network. Social risks stem not only
                     from physical displacement along canals but also from constraints to meaningful participation, asymmetric information,
                     SEA/SH risk patterns in dense informal settlements, labor influx during construction, and weak recourse mechanisms.
                     While the Program area does not lie in an FCV province nor in areas with constitutionally recognized Indigenous
                     Peoples, the urban poor — particularly women-headed households, migrant laborers, waste pickers, and people with
                     disabilities — constitute functionally vulnerable minorities from a social risk management standpoint.

                     Unlike a pure planning TA, this IPF directly finances (i) the design of rules, systems, and institutional arrangements that
                     will determine how downstream physical works are selected, sequenced, governed and socially licensed, and (ii)
                     preparation of subproject-ready assessments and instruments for high-risk interventions such as canal dredging, sludge
                     disposal chains, FSM systems, and solid-waste transfer infrastructure. By doing so, the TA itself shapes the risk profile of
                     future investments — positively if E&S risk reduction is embedded ex ante, or negatively if risk is not anticipated or
                     institutional capacity remains weak.

                     Specifically, the TA will:
                     • Develop environmental and social policy frameworks and implementation mechanisms for (a) managing adverse
                     impacts on NTHs/IOs during canal rehabilitation, and (b) safe and sustainable handling, transport, treatment, disposal
                     and/or reuse of dredged material and sludge. These frameworks will inform achievement of DLI-6 under the PforR.
                     • Develop Environmental and Social Management Systems (ESMSs) for IAs to institutionalize screening, exclusion,
                     scoping, supervision, contractor control, and community-facing risk mitigation.
                     • Finance E&S instruments for high-risk downstream subprojects, including SESA/ESIAs/ESMPs/RAPs/LMPs and SEA/SH-
                     integrated measures consistent with ESF standards.
                     • Strengthen regulatory and oversight capacity in DoE and WARPO for water quality monitoring, sludge oversight, and
                     cumulative impact analysis; BWDB for drainage/canal interface; and city corporations for operational enforcement and
                     grievance redress.

                     The IPF TA provides the first decisive opportunity to address two structural governance vacuums that routinely trigger
                     E&S failures across Bangladesh’s urban investments: (i) absence of a workable, non-legal but operational resettlement




Public Disclosure
                     mitigation framework for NTH/IOs in urban utility corridors, and (ii) absence of dredged/sludge management standards
                     and SOPs that prevent secondary pollution and health risk. If successfully implemented, these measures would not only
                     de-risk the PforR investments but would set a transferable precedent for subsequent World Bank, ADB, AIIB, JICA and
                     national operations targeting canal/riparian upgrading. The TA also allows proactive integration of SEA/SH risk controls,
                     cumulative impact logics (through strategic planning tools like SESA/CIA), and climate/health linkages into upstream
                     design before civil works commence.

                     Given the TA’s direct role in preparing instruments for substantial to high-risk activities (canal cleaning, sludge chains,
                     land-adjacent works) and in shaping policy choices with potentially pervasive social consequences, the E&S risks of the
                     IPF component are not merely “indirect” but carry a material downstream footprint. Considering the above and in line
                     with the 2019 OESRC Advisory guidance on TA and ESF, the proposed risk rating of the IPF component is High.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Borrower’s capacity to manage E&S risks is characterized by fragmented responsibility, weak institutionalization,
                     and uneven readiness across agencies. The Local Government Division (LGD), as the coordinating ministry for the PforR
                     and IPF, currently does not maintain a permanent ES unit or ESMS, nor does it have established protocols for
                     supervising municipal E&S compliance across city corporations and utilities. LGD’s prior engagements under Bank-
                     financed operations have relied on project-specific ESMFs and temporary consultants, rather than system-embedded
                     capacity or budget lines. Consequently, LGD’s supervisory role — essential for harmonizing practices across DNCC,
                     DSCC, NCC and DWASA — lacks the technical instrumentation, staffing, and enforcement architecture required for
                     consistent compliance.

                     At the implementation tier, DNCC, DSCC and NCC do not have dedicated ESUs, nor standardized procedures for
                     screening, stakeholder engagement, community health and safety management, or NTH-related mitigation. DWASA has
                     prior WB project exposure but depends on deputed, not institutional, staff and lacks a corporate ESMS. WARPO holds
                     analytical competence but not operational oversight functions for ES risk management of urban works. BWDB has
                     limited experience with urban canal E&S issues. DoE exercises statutory approval authority but has no mechanism to
                     supervise or guide E&S management of canal dredging or informal settlement displacement.

                     Notwithstanding these gaps, the Borrower has signaled formal willingness to institutionalize capacity through the IPF
                     TA, which will finance: (i) establishment of ESUs in LGD and each IA with assigned staff and TOR-linked authority; (ii)
                     development of ESMSs for LGD, DNCC, DSCC, NCC and DWASA to embed screening, exclusion, approval, and contractor
                     oversight; (iii) development of policy frameworks and implementation mechanisms for (a) non-title holder/informal
                     occupier impact mitigation, and (b) sustainable dredged material & sludge management—informing canal improvement
                     under DLI#6; (iv) preparation of ESIAs, ESMPs, RAPs/LAPs, LMPs for high-risk downstream subprojects per ESF; (v)
                     structured capacity building for LGD, DoE, WARPO, BWDB, DWASA and CCs on ESMS implementation, OHS, SEA/SH
                     mitigation, stakeholder engagement and GRM; and (vi) independent monitoring and verification of E&S performance
                     and PAP/DLI-linked system actions.

                     The IPF TA therefore serves not only to enable project-level compliance but to institutionalize durable capacity within
                     LGD and the participating agencies. By design, institutional strengthening outputs are anchored in PAP actions and tied
                     to DLI progression, ensuring that capacity gains are not advisory but performance-enforced. Accordingly, the Borrower’s
                     current institutional capacity is assessed as low but reform-committed, with a credible strengthening path contingent

                     rehabilitation, dredged material and sludge management, drainage improvements, fecal sludge and wastewater

                     and safety, and cumulative urban environmental risks if not adequately planned. The TA also includes development of
                     policy frameworks and implementation mechanisms for (i) non-title holder mitigation in canal clean-up, and (ii)
                     dredged material and sludge management, which will shape future implementation across dense settlements with
                     high exposure of communities. The Borrower currently lacks ESMS, SOPs or institutionalized capacity for
                     environmental screening, contractor oversight, or pollution control in canal/drainage works. DPHE and DWASA have
                     limited ESF experience confined to other Bank IPFs; city corporations and LGD have no embedded ES units or systems.
                     The absence of national procedures for contaminated sediment handling further elevates the systemic dimension of
                     risk. The IPF itself will not finance civil works, but the TA products will generate binding implementation inputs (e.g.,
                     master plans, DPRs, ES instruments for high-risk subprojects) and may include pilot demonstrations with direct

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The social risk of the IPF Technical Assistance is rated High. Although the IPF will not finance civil works, its analytical
                     outputs, policy frameworks, and preparatory studies will directly inform downstream canal clean-up and drainage
                     activities in dense urban settlements where a large number of non-title holders and informal occupiers (NTH/IOs)
                     reside along embankments and canal corridors. Downstream activities may trigger displacement, livelihood impacts,
                     access restrictions, and social tension if not preceded by structured engagement and calibrated mitigation
                     mechanisms. The TA also includes development of a policy framework and implementation mechanisms for NTH/IO
                     impact mitigation — a politically sensitive and legally unregulated area. Without carefully designed consultation,
                     sequencing, and institutional anchoring, the TA could inadvertently influence government actions outside the PforR
                     footprint and create reputational risk if forced evictions occur in parallel under the broader government program.
                     Institutional capacity across LGD, DNCC, DSCC, NCC and DWASA to manage such risks is currently low: no ES units, no
                     GRM for urban involuntary impacts, no protocols for engagement with NTH communities, and limited experience with
                     SEA/SH mitigation, labor oversight, or social monitoring in contested urban spaces. TA preparation of
                     ESIAs/RAPs/LAPs for high-risk subprojects further reinforces that downstream footprint may fall in contexts with
                     elevated displacement and vulnerability profiles. Given the risks described above, the risk is considered High.

                     B.3 Other Salient Features

                       Use of Borrower Framework                                                                                      In Part
                       The project will partially rely on the Government of Bangladesh’s existing environmental and social framework as a
                       baseline—particularly the Environmental Conservation Act/Rules, the Labor Act and Rules, BNBC, the national GRS
                       system, and the legally mandated roles of LGD, DoE, WARPO, DWASA and city corporations for environmental
                       clearance, occupational safety, water sector planning, and grievance handling. These instruments and mandates are
                       relevant to the types of technical assistance being financed and to the downstream domains (sanitation, drainage,
                       canal rehabilitation, sludge management) that the TA will influence.

                       However, the Borrower framework has critical gaps in areas that are material to this operation, including: (i) the
                       absence of any legally backed system for managing adverse impacts on non-title holders and informal occupiers
                       during canal-related activities, and (ii) the lack of a coherent framework for environmentally sound management of
                       dredged material and sludge in dense urban settings. Further, while laws exist on paper, institutional implementation
                       capacity is uneven, particularly at city corporation level.

                       To address these gaps and ensure consistency with the ESF, the IPF TA will finance the development of policy
                       frameworks, SOPs, ESMS and capacity-building instruments to operationalize national laws in line with ESS
                       requirements, without attempting to create or amend binding legislation. These TA outputs will guide the PforR

                       downstream investments, and their preparation will follow ESS-consistent TOR requirements. Where the Borrower
                       framework is silent or insufficient, ESF gap-filling commitments are already captured in the ESCP.

                       In summary, the project will use the Borrower system as a legal and administrative floor, but will not rely on it
                       exclusively; structured ESF-aligned instruments developed under the TA will provide the necessary safeguards where
                       the national framework is absent, weak or not yet implementable. This calibrated approach is consistent with ESF
                       policy on selective use of Borrower systems.


                       Use of Common Approach                                                                                              No
                       At this stage, a Common Approach is not applicable. Although development partners such as ADB, AIIB, AFD and EKN
                       have expressed interest in supporting the broader metro Dhaka water security agenda, the IPF TA component
                       financed under this operation is solely funded and governed by the World Bank, and no co-financier is formally
                       participating in or jointly applying safeguards to these TA activities at appraisal.

                       The TA outputs (policy frameworks, ESMS, SOPs, master-planning inputs and institutional capacity measures) are
                       expected to inform broader programmatic implementation over time, including potential parallel financing by other
                       DPs. However, no joint E&S due diligence, harmonized implementation procedures, or agreed joint supervision
                       framework with other financiers currently exists that would trigger the application of a Common Approach under the
                       ESF.

                     C. Overview of Required Environmental and Social Risk Management Activities

                     C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or
                     required by implementation?
                     By appraisal, the Borrower will prepare an Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP) for the IPF component,
                     setting out the key actions, timelines, and responsibilities for managing environmental and social risks. The ESCP will
                     draw from the ESSA, A-ESRS, and PAD risk analysis, and will include binding commitments to (i) establish Environmental
                     and Social Units (ESUs) within DNCC, DSCC, NCC, and DWASA; (ii) develop and adopt Environmental and Social
                     Management Systems (ESMS); and (iii) integrate E&S requirements into procurement and contract management.

                     In addition, the ESCP will include the principles of a Labor Management Procedure (LMP) covering OHS, SEA/SH, fair
                     treatment of workers, and grievance provisions for direct and contracted workers engaged under the IPF TA. A
                     Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) will also be prepared by appraisal, specifying mapping of affected groups (including
                     vulnerable and disadvantaged populations), modalities for inclusive consultation and disclosure, and a functional GRM
                     applicable to TA activities and sensitive policy work such as the NTH/IO framework and dredged material/sludge
                     management policy framework.

                     During implementation, a mandatory environmental and social screening mechanism will be established and applied to
                     all TA activities to: (i) classify TA by potential downstream E&S risk; (ii) determine the relevance of ESS requirements;
                     and (iii) ensure that the outcomes of screening are translated into strengthened TORs, technical scopes, and review
                     criteria prior to Bank clearance. All TORs for upstream analyses, feasibility studies, master plans, and policy work will
                     explicitly incorporate ESF-consistent clauses based on screening results.

                     Further during implementation, the IPF TA will finance the preparation of: (a) A non-binding policy framework and
                     implementation mechanisms for managing NTH/IO risks to enable socially inclusive canal clean-up; (b) A policy
                     framework, SOPs and feasibility guidance for sustainable dredged material and sludge management; (c) Institutional
                     ESMS for DNCC, DSCC, NCC and DWASA to govern pollution reduction and canal restoration activities; and (d) Capacity-
                     building programs for DoE, BWDB, WARPO and Bangladesh Bank to strengthen oversight and compliance functions.

                     The World Bank will carry out due diligence through review of national systems, consultations with agencies,
                     assessment of ESCP/SEP/LMP quality, and review of screening-based TORs prior to clearance. The ESCP will be updated
                     if required at later stages based on DM or Appraisal decisions.

                     In summary, by appraisal the Borrower will have prepared ESCP (with LMP principles) and SEP; and during
                     implementation will operationalize ES screening, finalization of policy frameworks, ESMS establishment, and technical
                     guidance instruments to ensure that the IPF TA is aligned with the ESF and enables safe downstream implementation of
                     the broader program.
