                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The project will be the first operation of an MPA, conceived to allow scalability of World Bank support for local
                     development in rural northern Mozambique. The program design envisages two simultaneous phases implemented
                     over an eight-year period. The phases will address three key development needs of rural northern Mozambique
                     communities that are impacted by fragility: social cohesion, access to economic opportunities, and access to resilient
                     infrastructure. These will be achieved by revitalizing forums for dialogue between the northern provinces, within
                     communities, and between communities and the state, supporting a cash transfer program for vulnerable households,
                     supporting youth upskilling and SME development, providing GBV and psycho-social support services, building systems

                    Apr 09, 2026                                                                                                  Page 2 of 12
                               The World Bank
                               Jobs, Social Cohesion And Community Resilience In Northern Mozambique - Phase I (P514199)



                     for community participation in local development, enhancing the capacity of local government for better planning and
                     efficient service delivery, and investing in priority community and public infrastructure. Both phases will be
                     implemented in the three northern provinces of Cabo Delgado, Nampula, and Niassa. This US$100 million IDA Phase I
                     operation will develop and remobilize foundational regional, province, and district-level dialogue forums, while local
                     economic development activities and community infrastructure and basic service planning activities and investments
                     will concentrate in selected 80 localities across the provinces’ 56 districts and public infrastructure will be concentrated
                     in 10 districts of Cabo Delgado province. A US$150 million IDA Phase II operation will deepen local development
                     systems and capacities and make sure they are sustainable while developing community and public infrastructure and
                     services in additional localities according to readiness criteria.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The Project will be implemented in all 56 districts across Cabo Delgado, Nampula, and Niassa provinces, that are
                     predominantly rural, characterized by dispersed settlements, limited infrastructure, high poverty levels, and heavy
                     reliance on natural resources for livelihoods. In addition, there are areas affected by ongoing fragility and insecurity,
                     where protracted conflict in Cabo Delgado and the recent spillover of violence into northern Nampula have resulted in
                     displacement, disruption of basic services, weakened local governance, and heightened social tensions among IDPs,
                     returnees, and host communities.

                     The social setting is complex, involving a significant number of vulnerable population groups, including IDPs, returnees,
                     host communities, unemployed youth, and female-headed households, often competing for limited services and



Public Disclosure
                     economic opportunities. Child labor remains prevalent in rural areas of Niassa, Nampula, and Cabo Delgado, largely
                     driven by chronic poverty, household reliance on subsistence agriculture and informal livelihoods, limited access to
                     education, and the compounded impacts of displacement and insecurity, which increase economic pressures on
                     households to engage children in productive activities. Weak local governance capacity and fragile trust in institutions
                     create risks of exclusion, elite capture, and inter-community tensions if project benefits are not allocated transparently.
                     The project therefore emphasizes participatory planning, inclusive representation, transparent prioritization criteria,
                     and a responsive grievance mechanism (GM) to support conflict-sensitive implementation and strengthen social
                     cohesion.

                      Environmentally, the project areas include ecologically sensitive landscapes typical of northern Mozambique, including
                     coastal ecosystems (mangroves and estuaries in parts of Cabo Delgado and Nampula), riverine systems and wetlands
                     associated with the Rovuma, Messalo, Montepuez, and Lúrio basins, degraded miombo woodland mosaics, fragile sandy
                     soils, and agro-ecological landscapes. Although the project will finance only small-scale community and public
                     infrastructure—such as classrooms, water points, small bridges, community centers, and feeder paths—these
                     investments may occur near seasonal streams, wetlands, or modified natural habitats. Some districts are located within
                     broader biodiversity-sensitive landscapes, requiring robust environmental screening and careful siting to avoid
                     vegetation clearance, erosion, sedimentation, informal material sourcing, and improper waste management.

                     Project activities will include labor-intensive public works and small-scale construction in remote rural settings, where
                     contractor capacity and enforcement of environmental, social, SEA/SH, and OHS standards are often limited.
                     Community health and safety risks may arise from worksite proximity to residences and public spaces, while SEA/SH and
                     security risks are heightened in the FCV context.

                     Institutional capacity at provincial and district levels remains constrained due to staffing shortages, limited logistical
                     resources, and uneven familiarity with ESF requirements. The newly established National Administration of Public
                     Works (ANOP) is still consolidating its operational systems. Given the fragile context, dispersed investments,
                     environmentally sensitive landscapes, and capacity constraints, there is a clear nexus between contextual risks and
                     project activities. In practice, this nexus is expected to manifest through heightened implementation and supervision
                     risks, as small‑scale infrastructure investments are delivered across remote and insecure areas by a newly established
                     institution with evolving systems and local government, increasing the likelihood that contextual fragility, access
                     constraints, and limited local capacity may affect screening quality, site oversight, and the consistent application of
                     environmental and social risk management measures. Therefore, effective implementation will require strong screening
                     procedures, conflict-sensitive engagement, and proportionate environmental and social oversight consistent with the
                     Substantial risk classification.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Borrower’s institutional capacity to manage environmental and social (E&S) risks is moderate but uneven, with
                     notable constraints at provincial and district levels, further compounded by the fragile and insecure context in northern
                     Mozambique.

                     The lead institutions—the Ministry of Planning and Development (MPD) and the Ministry of Public Works, Housing and
                     Water Resources (MOPHRH)—have prior experience implementing World Bank-financed operations, including projects
                     under the Safeguard Policies and more recent ESF-aligned operations. However, familiarity with ESF requirements varies




Public Disclosure
                     across entities and administrative levels. While central ministries have some exposure to E&S procedures, subnational
                     structures face capacity limitations in staffing, logistics, supervision, and documentation.

                     The Northern Integrated Development Agency (ADIN), under the purview of MPD, is a Government of Mozambique
                     agency mandated to coordinate, articulate, and manage integrated, multisectoral development programs in the
                     northern provinces of Cabo Delgado, Nampula, and Niassa, including regional planning, investment promotion, and
                     coordination of donor‑financed operations. ADIN’s experience with the application of the ESF is still evolving, with prior
                     operations indicating the need to further strengthen dedicated environmental and social staffing, supervision practices,
                     and familiarity with ESF procedures, particularly for implementation at scale in fragile and conflict‑affected contexts. In
                     addition, the newly established National Administration of Public Works (ANOP), under the purview of MOPHRH, which
                     will oversee community and public infrastructure activities, remains in an early stage of institutional consolidation and
                     does not yet have fully dedicated environmental and social personnel. At provincial and district levels, technical services
                     operate with limited human resources, high turnover, restricted field mobility, and uneven enforcement of
                     environmental, labor, and occupational health and safety (OHS) standards. Security-related access constraints further
                     limit effective supervision in certain districts.

                     To mitigate these gaps, the project will establish and maintain a Program Coordination Unit (PCU) within ADIN,
                     adequately resourced and staffed with qualified environmental, social, GBV, and security specialists, to manage
                     environmental, social, health and safety (ESHS), security and SEA/SH risks throughout implementation. Moreover, ANOP
                     will designate qualified technical staff, including environmental and social focal points, to coordinate with the PCU in
                     the implementation and supervision of activities under its mandate.

                     Early in implementation, the Borrower will also operationalize a targeted Environmental and Social Capacity
                     Development Plan, focusing on: (i) ESF application and screening procedures; (ii) supervision of small-scale and labor-
                     intensive works; (iii) SEA/SH risk mitigation and survivor-centered response; (iv) grievance redress management; and (v)
                     conflict-sensitive stakeholder engagement. Capacity strengthening will be delivered through structured training,
                     practical tools embedded in the ESMF, on-the-job mentoring, and phased reinforcement of provincial and district
                     teams, including collaboration with civil society organizations where government presence is limited. In addition, the
                     use of environmental and social instruments developed under NCRP and MozNorte projects will support the Borrower’s
                     institutional capacity development by enabling the adaptation and continued application of ESF‑aligned tools and
                     procedures already used in overlapping locations, including Cabo Delgado, and for similar activities such as public
                     infrastructure rehabilitation and community‑driven development (CDD) interventions.

                     The Project will also engage a third party monitor (TPM) to conduct bi annual monitoring of environmental and social
                     compliance, including SEA/SH related risks and the effectiveness of associated mitigation measures, to complement
                     supervision and support timely corrective actions during implementation.

                     Overall, while institutional capacity constraints remain significant—particularly at decentralized levels—the Borrower
                     has demonstrated willingness to establish E&S systems, which will be developed and strengthened over the course of
                     implementation. The planned measures are expected to contribute to the management of E&S risks in a manner
                     consistent with the ESF. These measures may be supplemented by additional actions resulting from an adaptive
                     management response.

                     II. SUMMARY OF ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL (ES) RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                         Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                  Substantial
                     The environmental risk of the project is rated Substantial. Although investments will primarily involve small-scale
                     community and public infrastructure works, these activities will be implemented across a wide geographic area
                     characterized by fragility, limited institutional presence, and environmentally sensitive landscapes including coastal
                     ecosystems, riverine systems, wetlands, and degraded miombo woodlands in northern Mozambique. Potential risks
                     include soil disturbance and erosion, improper management of construction debris and solid waste, localized dust and
                     noise emissions, disruption of natural drainage patterns, and impacts associated with informal or unregulated
                     sourcing of construction materials (e.g., borrow pits and quarries). Rehabilitation or installation of small water supply
                     systems may also generate risks related to water quality, wastewater management, and resource efficiency.
                     Occupational health and safety (OHS) risks are anticipated during labor-intensive public works and small-scale
                     construction, particularly given the reliance on community workers and locally hired labor, variable contractor
                     capacity, and weak enforcement of OHS standards in remote districts. Community health and safety risks may arise
                     from increased traffic, movement of materials, and inadequate site demarcation in densely populated rural
                     settlements. The project is not expected to finance activities within protected areas or critical habitats; however,
                     some subprojects may be located near wetlands, seasonal streams, or modified natural habitats, requiring careful
                     screening and site-specific mitigation under the ESMF. The ESMF will be adapted from existing frameworks prepared
                     under the NCRP and MozNorte projects and updated to reflect the scope and implementation arrangements of this

                               The World Bank
                               Jobs, Social Cohesion And Community Resilience In Northern Mozambique - Phase I (P514199)



                     operation. Cumulative impacts are expected to be limited due to the dispersed and small-scale nature of investments.
                     Considering the moderate intrinsic environmental impacts, geographic dispersion, institutional capacity constraints,
                     FCV-related operational challenges, and the reliance on framework instruments to guide subproject-level mitigation,
                     the environmental risk is appropriately classified as Substantial.

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The social risk of the project is rated Substantial. The operation will be implemented in all districts of the Northern
                     provinces, inlcuding districts affected by fragility, conflict, displacement, and weak institutional presence. While the
                     Project is expected to generate important social benefits—improved access to livelihoods, basic services, and
                     community infrastructure—the context in which it will be implemented presents significant social risks that require
                     adequate mitigation measures. Key social risks identified include: (i) labor-related risks, particularly associated with
                     the use of local labor in community works, uneven contractor capacity, and child labor among the community
                     workers and beneficiaries; (ii) SEA/SH risks, particularly given the expected worker–community interactions
                     associated with the labor-intensive public works, and potentially in the access to Project benfits as well; (iii)
                     community health and safety risks linked to small-scale construction activities in close proximity to residences and
                     public spaces; (iv) risks of exclusion, particularly for vulnerable groups, whereby IDPs, returnees, unemployed youth,
                     female-headed households, and remote communities may face barriers to equitable access to project benefits or
                     meaningful participation in decision-making, as well as the risk of elite capture of project benefits; and (v) security
                     risks associated with ongoing insurgent activity in parts of Cabo Delgado and neighboring districts, which may affect
                     consultations, supervision, and the safety of workers and implementing partners. The social risk rating also takes into
                     account the fragile operating environment, conflict-related displacement dynamics, and institutional local capacity

                     geographical accessibility.

                      ESS10 - Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure                                                          Relevant

                      Effective and transparent stakeholder engagement is central to achieving the project’s development objective and
                      mitigating risks of exclusion, elite capture, and social tension, and ensuring adequate management of the Project’s
                      E&S risks.. Some measures are already embedded into the project’s design, which relies on participatory planning,
                      community prioritization of investments, labor-intensive public works, and decentralized implementation across
                      multiple administrative levels. Project beneficiaries include IDPs, returnees, host communities, unemployed youth,
                      female-headed households, traditional leaders, district authorities, civil society organizations, and service providers.
                      These will be considered as core participants in the stakeholder engagement process. Given the diversity of
                      stakeholders and differentiated vulnerabilities, structured and inclusive engagement processes will be required to
                      ensure equitable participation in subproject identification, prioritization, and oversight. To promote meaningful
                      stakeholder engagement, the Project will prepare and publicly disclose an initial Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP)
                      prior to appraisal. The SEP will define engagement principles, consultation modalities, information disclosure
                      arrangements, and the structure of the project-level Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM), including confidential
                      intake channels and procedures for processing SEA/SH-related complaints. The SEP also includes a record of
                      stakeholder engagement activities carried out so far in each province with provincial and district government, civil
                      society organizations, private sector and local communities, which will continue during implementation, including as



Public Disclosure
                      part of the site-specific environmental and social management plans to be prepared during implementation. During
                      implementation, engagement approaches will be adaptive to local capacity and security conditions and will be
                      progressively strengthened during implementation, informed by the progress reports and implementation support. In
                      terms of the key issues and concerns raised during the consultations, in Nampula province, stakeholders emphasized
                      the need for strong district leadership in participatory planning, clearer roles for districts and local actors, and greater
                      involvement of the private sector and civil society in employment creation, youth employability, and beneficiary
                      selection, alongside concerns about infrastructure gaps and coordination mechanisms. In Niassa province, key
                      concerns focused on poor transport and economic infrastructure, requests to prioritize the province in infrastructure
                      investments, calls for transparency in fund management and beneficiary selection, and the need to adapt project
                      design (including CDD modalities and group sizes) to local realities, while ensuring clarity on environmental
                      safeguards. Finally in Cabo Delgado, stakeholders highlighted urgency of implementation in a fragile context, the
                      importance of strong coordination by ADIN with decentralized planning at district level, inclusion of local service
                      providers and civil society, private sector participation, faster execution, and alignment with lessons learned from
                      NCRP and MozNorte, particularly for infrastructure rehabilitation and community-driven interventions.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                             Relevant

                      ESS3 is relevant as the project will finance small-scale community and public infrastructure works that may generate

                      practices. Construction activities may generate solid waste, construction debris, packaging materials, and small

                      management, resource efficiency, pollution prevention, and responsible material sourcing. Where relevant, site-
                      specific Waste Management Plans (WMPs) or similar measures will be required under contractor ESMPs. Practical
                      implementation guidance and supervision mechanisms will support field-level compliance.

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                             Relevant

                      ESS4 is relevant as the project will support small-scale civil works, labor-intensive public works, and community-based
                      activities implemented in districts with limited infrastructure, weak service provision, and heightened vulnerability.
                      Although the physical footprint of investments will be modest, construction and rehabilitation activities may pose
                      community health and safety risks, including accidental injuries, unsafe work areas, falling hazards, increased dust
                      and noise, and risks associated with vehicle and material movement near residences, schools, and public spaces.

                      manage community health and safety risks, including site demarcation, traffic management, emergency
                      preparedness, guidance on interactions with public security personnel, and those risks related to SEA/SH, consistent
                      with ESS4 and good international practice, to ensure protection of communities and respect for human rights.

                      ESS8 - Cultural Heritage                                                                                        Relevant

                      ESS8 is relevant as the project will finance small-scale civil works across rural districts where tangible and intangible

                     B.3 Other Salient Features

                       Use of Borrower Framework                                                                                             In Part
                       The Project will partially use the Recipient’s E&S frameworks regarding the assessment and management of risks and
                       impacts, as well as labor and working conditions. Measures required by the ESSs and not covered by the Borrower’s
                       E&S framework will be complemented by additional activities, as needed, to ensure consistency with the ESSs. While
                       Mozambique has an established legal and regulatory system for environmental impact assessment, land management,
                       labor, and public health, the current framework does not fully align with the requirements of several ESSs—
                       particularly ESS2 (labor and working conditions), ESS4 (community health and safety), ESS5 (land acquisition and
                       resettlement), and ESS10 (stakeholder engagement). The project will be implemented in fragile and conflict-affected
                       districts where institutional capacity is significantly constrained at all levels (central, provincial, and district). Public
                       authorities, including the ADIN and the newly established ANOP and local planning and infrastructure services, have
                       limited experience applying World Bank ESF requirements and face operational challenges such as restricted access to
                       project sites and staff shortages.
