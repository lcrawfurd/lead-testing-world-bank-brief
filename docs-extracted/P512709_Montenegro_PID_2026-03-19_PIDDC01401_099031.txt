B. Introduction and Context

Country Context

1. Montenegro is a small, upper-middle-income economy whose strategy is anchored in EU accession. After a historic
   15.3 percent decline in 2020, growth rebounded strongly in 2021–23 but moderated to about 3 percent in 2024 and
   is projected around that pace in 2025 amid softer industrial output and a challenging global environment. Private
   consumption and investment continue to support economic activity, helped by higher real wages, solid employment,
   and credit growth. Inflation eased to nearly 3-4 percent in 2024 and is expected to converge toward 2-3 percent over
   the medium term.

2. Fiscal and debt dynamics have improved since the pandemic, but vulnerabilities persist. Public debt fell from 103.5
   percent of GDP in 2020 to about 60–61 percent by 2023–24, yet sizable and lumpy Eurobond redemptions in 2025-
   27, together with higher financing costs, point to rising debt ratios in the mid‑60s without additional consolidation.
   Deficits are projected above 3 percent of GDP due to higher social spending and capital outlays and recent labor tax
   and contribution cuts, underscoring the need for disciplined policy, medium‑term fiscal frameworks, and revenue
   and expenditure measures to preserve sustainability.

3. EU accession remains as the reform anchor. A positive Interim Benchmark Assessment in June 2024 on the
   rule‑of‑law track enabled the country to begin closing chapters, signaling renewed momentum. The country is widely
   expected to join the EU around 2028, provided it completes accession negotiations by 2026 and secures unanimous
   approval from all member states. Alignment with the acquis - especially Chapter 27 on Environment and Climate

     Change - drives the agenda and shapes investment priorities, with the FY25-29 CPF centering on better economic

 4. The economic structure - highly reliant on tourism - brings jobs and external receipts but leaves the economy
    exposed to shocks and seasonality. Tourism contributes more than 30 percent of the country’s GDP, underscoring
    its outsized role. The current account deficit remains large and only partly financed by foreign direct investment (FDI),
    contributing to external vulnerabilities. The financial sector is well capitalized and liquid, with declining non-
    performing loans (NPLs), but credit and real‑estate cycles require careful supervision. Labor market outcomes have
    improved: employment and activity rates reached record highs; unemployment fell to around 11-12 percent in 2024,
    and poverty (upper‑middle‑income line) is projected to decline toward the high‑single digits by the mid‑2020s.

 5. Climate and environmental risks are material for growth, fiscal stability, and EU accession. Montenegro is
    experiencing increasing climate variability, characterized by more frequent and severe droughts, reduced summer
    precipitation, and episodic flooding linked to intense rainfall events. Rising temperatures and declining rainfall during
    the summer months are reducing surface and groundwater availability, while floods - though episodic - strain water
    infrastructure without significantly improving usable water quantities. These dynamics are particularly challenging in
    tourism-intensive areas, where peak water demand coincides with the driest period of the year, exacerbating supply
    constraints. Climate change is intensifying hydrological stress and increasing the vulnerability of water supply
    systems. Decarbonization and adaptation priorities1 include scaling energy efficiency, integrating renewables,
    strengthening climate‑resilient infrastructure and land/water management, and enhancing disaster risk
    management - actions that are consistent with the Western Balkans Country Climate and Development Report2
    (CCDR) and the CPF focus on improved environmental outcomes.

 Sectoral and Institutional Context

 6. Montenegro is a water‑rich country with abundant resources and high precipitation shaped by its mountainous
    landscape. Nevertheless, achieving water security and climate resilience is challenging due to complex hydrological
    conditions influenced by the Dinaric Karst and compounded by increasing climate variability. Seasonal droughts,
    episodic flooding, and karstic groundwater dynamics create spatial and temporal mismatches between availability
    and demand, heightening operational and investment needs for resilient water management.

 7. Service performance issues further constrain sector outcomes. While access to piped water supply exceeds 90
    percent, significant gaps persist in wastewater services: less than half of wastewater is treated, and many treatment
    plants face operational difficulties, particularly with sludge management and rising energy costs. The efficiency and
    sustainability of water supply systems are pressing concerns, with non‑revenue water (NRW) averaging about 68
    percent nationally and ranging from approximately 34 percent in Tivat to 85 percent in Danilovgrad3. These losses
    and commercial inefficiencies underscore the urgency of modernizing water supply infrastructure and strengthening
    utility business operations.

8. These water security risks are further intensified by unsustainable water and sediment management practices,
   which threaten the country’s strategic water resources. Scientific studies conducted in recent years suggest that
   extensive gravel extraction from the Morača River has caused riverbed deepening and, in turn, reduced discharge
   from the Bolje Sestre spring - the main strategic water source for the coastal water supply - over the past decade
   (Figure 1). As a result, in July 2025, the Regional Water Supply Company for the Montenegrin Coast operated at nearly
   90% of its delivery capacity even before the August peak season, when demand surges due to tourism. This situation
   poses a growing risk to coastal tourism, which contributes more than 30% of the country’s GDP (Figure 2).
   Institutional fragmentation among sector stakeholders further hampers efficiency and weakens governance, limiting
   the sector’s ability to respond effectively to these challenges.

9. Montenegro’s EU alignment in the water sector requires major investments and targeted policy and institutional
   reforms. The World Bank is supporting Montenegro’s water sector through a range of initiatives, including the
   ongoing Sava‑Drava Integrated River Corridor Project (SDIP) as well as technical assistance and capacity building
   efforts in the frame of the Danube Water Program and the Utilities of the Future Centre of Excellence. Furthermore,
   the World Bank is supporting the ongoing sector reform in Montenegro, which includes a revision of the Law on
   Utility Services, and the development of a new Law on Water Services. Under the ongoing negotiations for EU
   accession, envisaged for 2028, timelines for compliance with the EU Drinking Water Directive (DWD) and the Urban
   Wastewater Treatment Directive (UWWTD) are being discussed for 2035. Meeting these requirements will mean
   expanding and rehabilitating water supply and wastewater networks, ensuring adequate treatment, upgrading
   sludge management, and modernizing monitoring and reporting - alongside energy‑efficiency improvements in
   utilities. On the policy side, alignment entails transposing and implementing risk‑based Water Safety Plans, stronger
   leakage control, and clear consumer information for drinking water. For urban wastewater, it requires robust
   permitting, sensitive‑area designation, compliance monitoring, and transparent performance reporting. These
   actions must be supported by regulatory and tariff reforms that secure service quality and financial viability,
   consistent with the EU Water Framework Directive’s (WFD) cost‑recovery principle. Timely, well‑prioritized
   investments and modernization of sector policies, regulation, and institutions are therefore a national imperative.

10. The proposed project seeks to support Montenegro in addressing a number of these challenges through strategic
    investments in water services infrastructure, enhanced and integrated management of river basins, and
    strengthened governance frameworks for water management. Improving policies, institutions, and regulations will
    be crucial to enhancing service provision and ensuring the long-term resilience and sustainable use of water
    resources, ultimately supporting Montenegro’s socio-economic development and advancing its EU accession agenda.

Relationship to CPF

11. The proposed project is closely aligned with the World Bank Group’s Country Partnership Framework (CPF) for
    Montenegro, which prioritizes sustainable, resilient, and inclusive growth. By addressing critical gaps in water
    supply, wastewater management, and climate resilience, the project contributes directly to CPF objectives related to
    improving infrastructure efficiency, strengthening environmental sustainability, and enhancing institutional capacity.
    Investments in water services and integrated river basin management will support improved public service delivery,
    reduce vulnerability to climate change, and safeguard the natural resources that underpin Montenegro’s tourism-
    driven economy. Furthermore, the project will advance the country’s EU accession agenda by supporting compliance
    with the EU Drinking Water Directive and the Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive, thus reinforcing Montenegro’s
    long-term development and integration goals. Additionally, the project is consistent with Montenegro’s Nationally
    Determined Contribution (NDC)4, which has a GHG emission wide reduction target of 35% by 2030 compared to base
    year (1990) emissions, excluding land use, land-use change, and forestry (LULUCF). Moreover, the water sector -
    particularly the projected increase in droughts and seasonal pressure on water use during summer, and the resulting
    impacts on tourism and the coastal region - is identified as a priority.

 Key Results (From PCN)

12. Proposed Key Results Indicators. A concise, activity‑linked set of indicators is proposed that captures service
    improvements, climate resilience, coastal water security, and enabling policy and regulatory reforms. These include:
    (i) people provided with access to improved water supply and/or sanitation services in participating municipalities;
    (ii) reduction in non-revenue water (%) in targeted utilities; (iii) coastal water security: supplementary
    treatment/abstraction capacity for the Malo Blato/Bolje Sestre coastal system commissioned and operational
    (liters/second), with availability during summer peak demand; (iv) adoption and initial implementation of a national
    water supply and sanitation sector strategy; (v) adoption and application of an updated, cost‑recovery‑oriented
    tariff methodology; (vi) operationalization of a national utility performance platform with regular reporting by
    participating utilities; and (vii) number of utilities adopting improved O&M practices following capacity‑building and
    training. Key Results Indicators will be further developed and refined during the further project preparation.
    Baselines and feasible end targets will be confirmed at appraisal, with institutional indicators tracked through formal
    adoption and operational status.

D. Concept Description

13. This Phase 1 (hereinafter, Project) will focus on three key areas: (i) Strategic Investments in Water Services
    Infrastructure and Resilience; (ii) Integrated Approaches for Coastal Water Security; and (iii) Strengthening the
    Enabling Environment for Effective Water Sector Governance and Management to support Montenegro’s water
    sector and climate resilience.

14. The Project offers a timely opportunity to advance the modernization and strengthening of Montenegro’s policy,
    institutional, and regulatory framework as part of the ongoing water sector reform and in the context of climate-
    induced water scarcity and change precipitation patterns. In addition, it will tackle the most urgent water security
    challenges exacerbated by climate change, with a particular focus on the coastal region, while promoting broader
    improvements in access, quality, and efficiency of water supply and sanitation services nationwide. The proposed
    Project will be structured around four key components.

15. Component 1 - Strategic Investments in Water Services Infrastructure and Resilience. This component aims to
    rehabilitate, modernize and expand water services infrastructure, thereby increasing access, efficiency, safety, and
    climate resilience of services for households and businesses. This includes targeted investments in: (a) the
    rehabilitation of dated centralized water supply systems and replacement of asbestos pipes to reduce water losses
    and mitigate health risks associated with long-term ingestion of contaminated water; (b) the expansion of centralized
    wastewater treatment services to reduce water pollution and improve environmental health; and (c) the
    modernization and expansion of decentralized water services infrastructure to better serve rural populations in
    remote areas - all of which ultimately increases climate resilience.

16. Component 2 - Integrated Approaches for Coastal Water Security. This component is designed to tackle pressing
    challenges in securing the coastal water supply managed by the Regional Water Supply Company for the Montenegrin
    Coast. The water is sourced from the Bolje Sestre spring in the vicinity of the Morača River, which discharges into
    Lake Skadar, the largest lake in the Balkans, and transported through bulk supply lines to the coastal region. However,
    a decrease in the spring's discharge over the last decade in connection with gravel excavation activities from the
    Morača River, necessitates immediate action to ensure the continued reliability of the coastal water supply. To
    address these challenges, the component will focus on two key issues: (i) sub-component 2.1 – Enhancement of lake
    water treatment capacity and (ii) sub-component 2.2 – Technical Assistance for Enhancing the Morača River’s Hydro-
    Morphological Regime and Water Resource Sustainability.

17. Component 3 - Strengthening Enabling Environment for Effective Water Sector Governance and Management.
    Presently, water sector services are threatened both for their financial and operational sustainability. The
    implementation plan for the Drinking Water Directive lists an estimate of EUR 580 million needed for water supply
    alone, including EUR 220 million identified as priority investments by 2036, while the Municipal Wastewater
    Management Plan (2019) foresees the need for EUR 471.5 million in wastewater-related investments by 2035. To
    address these challenges, strengthening policies, institutions, and regulatory frameworks is essential. Such
    improvements will foster an enabling environment for the necessary investments by enhancing governance,
    increasing sector resilience and preparedness for climate change, ensuring compliance with EU Directives, and
    improving operational efficiency. In addition, Component 3 will seek to create conditions that progressively enable
    alternative financing and service delivery arrangements, including through greater involvement of the private sector
    over time. Ultimately, these measures will support the goal of providing universal access to resilient and safe water
    supply and sanitation services. Proposed activities under discussion for Component 3, mainly supported via
    specialized consultancy services, will focus on: legislative and regulatory reforms; tariff methodology updates;
    techno-economic analysis of water services delivery; development of a National Water Services Provision Strategy
    and Action Plan; strengthening of sector data collection, monitoring, and benchmarking systems; and capacity
    building at both the water operator and local government levels.

 Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts




The Project is classified as Substantial Environmental and Social Risk due to its nationwide, multi-site civil works to
rehabilitate, modernize, and expand water and wastewater infrastructure. Relevant ESF standards: ESS1, ESS2, ESS3, ESS4,
ESS5, ESS6, ESS8, ESS10. Activities may include replacement of asbestos-containing pipes, WWTP
expansion/reconstruction, extensive urban/rural network works, capacity expansion at Malo Blato near sensitive water
bodies, and TA for the Morača River basin. Substantial environmental risk stems from: (i) hazardous materials and legacy
issues (asbestos, sludge) requiring licensed handling, transport, and disposal; (ii) dispersed construction interfaces with
communities creating CHS risks (open trenches, traffic conflicts, dust, noise, vibration, utility strikes); (iii) works
near/discharges to sensitive aquatic systems with hydrology and water quality risks; (iv) uncertainty and oversight
complexity across multiple entities, contractors, and remote sites. While largely site-specific and mitigable, risks demand
robust instruments, capacity, and supervision. CHS risks include exposure to noise, dust, traffic, hazardous materials
(asbestos, biological pollutants), and hazards linked to sludge facility rehabilitation (transport, storage, treatment). CHS
risks may also involve safe access to adequate water quality during rehabilitation, pipeline replacement, and
commissioning of new facilities due to temporary disruptions or water quality fluctuations. Mitigation will include an ESMF
with screening/exclusion of high-risk activities; site-specific ESMPs and contractor CESMPs; and other instruments
prepared before works based on subproject risk screening. TA ToR for the Morača basin will embed ESF-consistent
assessment of hydrology, sediment, biodiversity, ecosystem services, and cumulative impacts to manage downstream risk.
Social impacts are expected to be localized and temporary, especially in rural/peri-urban areas. Most construction will
occur in existing utility corridors or public land, but some locations remain undefined. Certain subprojects (e.g., rural

expansion or new treatment capacity) may require limited land acquisition or temporary land use, potentially causing
minor physical/economic displacement, to be addressed through an RPF and site-specific RAPs per ESS5. Temporary
disruptions to access, livelihoods, and small businesses may occur during construction, especially in remote areas.
Ineffective inclusion and accountability mechanisms may constrain meaningful stakeholder participation and equitable
access to project benefits. Specific drivers include insufficient consultation, uneven information disclosure, weak grievance
redress mechanisms (GRMs), and limited institutional coordination. The risk is elevated where interruptions to quality
water services occur, potentially increasing dissatisfaction, grievances, and erosion of trust. Vulnerable and marginalized
groups—including low-income households, rural communities, and Roma—may face additional barriers to information,
access, and participation, heightening the likelihood of exclusion, inequitable outcomes, and social tensions. Mitigation
will strengthen municipal/PIU capacity, ensure inclusive engagement, enforce CHS measures, monitor equitable access,
minimize land impacts, and establish effective, well-coordinated grievance mechanisms. Labor risks are moderate. Most
works will use local contractors, limiting influx; larger subprojects may involve non-local workers, posing CHS and social
tension risks. These will be managed via LMP, worker codes of conduct, and community awareness, consistent with ESS2.
Overall social risk is substantial due to project complexity and limited capacity at central/local levels. Given multiple
activities with locations/designs/schedules confirmed during implementation, a framework approach will be adopted. The
Borrower will prepare, consult, and disclose ESMF, RPF, LMP, SEP, and ESCP by appraisal (or, where justified, by
effectiveness as specified in the ESCP). These frameworks establish screening procedures, eligibility/exclusion criteria,
mitigation measures (including SEA/SH-sensitive grievance pathways), and roles/responsibilities. Subsequently, site-
specific instruments (e.g., ESIAs/ESMPs and RAPs/LRPs, as relevant) will be prepared, consulted, disclosed, and cleared
prior to bidding and strictly before any civil works, land acquisition, or access restrictions commence; requirements will
be included in procurement documents and contracts. For the lake water treatment facility expansion, an E&S audit will
be conducted prior to negotiations to identify compliance gaps, legacy issues, and corrective actions; findings will inform
the ESCP and choice of instrument. The Project will rely on qualified social specialists and targeted capacity-building to
identify, mitigate, and manage risks throughout implementation.
