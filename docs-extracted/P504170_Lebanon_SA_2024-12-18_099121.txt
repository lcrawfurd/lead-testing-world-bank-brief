EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Introduction

Greater Beirut has been facing a deficit in potable water for the past fifty years. In 1970 the
Lebanese Government of the day passed a decree (Presidential Decree No. 14522, May 1970)
in which it allocated water from the Litani and Awali river catchments to different regions in
Lebanon. As a result, Greater Beirut was allocated 50 million cubic meters for the dry season.
This corresponds to 250,000 m3/day (3m3/s) of water.

In 1977 the Ministry of Energy and Water (MoEW) on behalf of the Government requested from
the Council of Development and Reconstruction (CDR) to study the options for providing
additional water resources to Greater Beirut. Significant number of studies dealing with
conveying water by means of a tunnel and pipelines has been carried out.

As such the Greater Beirut Water Supply Project (GBWSP) was developed and implemented in
two phases:

    -    Phase 1 implemented through a loan signed between the World Bank and the Beirut
         Mount Lebanon Water Establishment (BMLWE) as well as the Council for Development
         and Reconstruction (CDR) and approved on December 16, 2010. This loan which was
         effective on December 4, 2012 closed on April 30, 2025.

    -    Phase 2 is currently being prepared by the BMLWE and the CDR to be financed by the
         World Bank. This phase aims at completing the remaining civil works related to the
         network, the three reservoirs (Hadath 125, Hazmieh 90 and Hadath 90), the Joun
         Regulator and the Wardanieh Water Treatment Plant add-ons and bringing the water
         supply system into operation.
An ESIA addendum is required because while the first phase of the GBWSP complied with the
WB safeguards operational policies, the second GBWSP (SGBWSP), under preparation,
complies with the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) requirements, the
applicable World Bank Environmental and Social Standards (ESSs), and Lebanese
environmental and social legislations. The ESSs which apply under this project include: ESS1
(Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts), ESS2 (Labor
and Working Conditions), ESS3 (Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and
Management), ESS4 (Community Health and Safety), ESS5 (Land Acquisition, Restrictions on
Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement), ESS6 (Biodiversity Conservation and Sustainable
Management of Living Natural Resources), and ESS10 (Stakeholder Engagement and
Information Disclosure). In addition to this, the ESIA addendum considers changes in the
baseline conditions and contextual situation to reflect on the impact of the remaining and
additional works.

Under the SGBWSP, the CDR and BMLWE PMUs prepared, cleared and disclosed the following
E&S instruments and will ensure their implementation throughout the project life cycle:

    -    Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP),
    -    the Stakeholders Engagement Plan (SEP),
    -    the Addendum to the Resettlement Action Plan (RAP)

Legal and Institutional Framework

This ESIA complies with the Lebanese Legislative requirements as well as with the World Bank
Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) The ESSs which apply under this project include:
ESS1 (Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts), ESS2
(Labor and Working Conditions), ESS3 (Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and
Management), ESS4 (Community Health and Safety), ESS5 (Land Acquisition, Restrictions on
Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement), ESS6 (Biodiversity Conservation and Sustainable
Management of Living Natural Resources), and ESS10 (Stakeholder Engagement and
Information Disclosure)..

The policies of the World Bank and the related Lebanese Legislation were reviewed and
analyzed including the legal texts enacted after 2010 were which corresponds to the year of
submission of the ESIA related to the GBWSP Phase 1.
The CDR and the BMLWE will be responsible for the overall implementation of the project in
terms of, (i) managing the project in line with the overall project design and agreed
procurement and financial management arrangements; (ii) ensuring the implementation of
Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) requirements including the Environmental and
Social Commitment Plan (ESCP) of the project; (iii) monitoring the project results towards the
achievement of agreed outcomes; and (iv) complying with all reporting requirements of the
project as per the Loan Agreement (LA).

The overall control of water supply and quality is under the Beirut and Mount Lebanon Water
and Wastewater Establishment acting under the Ministry of Energy and Water (MoEW) while
the Ministry of Environment and various line Ministries are charged with specific regulatory
duties.

Regionally the Project area is under the Governorate of Mount Lebanon and its subordinate
cazas and Municipalities


Project Description

The Project encompasses the following components:

    •     The lining of two water tunnel conveyors (of 3 km and 21 km), their connection with the
          water transmission pipelines and the construction of three regional reservoirs;

    •     The remaining works at the Wardanieh Water Treatment Plant (WTP) to enhance its
          capacity to treat raw water based on the latest water quality measurements and to
          incorporate an early detection system for water quality deterioration; and

    •     The capacity strengthening of the BMLWE geared towards improving the operational
          efficiency, financial sustainability, and citizen orientation of the BMLWE.

    •     Land acquisition and resettlement compensation to displaced people for: (i) the
          reservoirs Hadath 90 and Hazmieh 90; (ii) a section of the twin transmission pipelines; (iii)

Water Balance Results
An updated water balance was prepared in 2014. Similarly to the 2011 water balance, the
updated water balance was based on two type of years, wet year and dry year.
Wet Season Water Balance:

The needed flow rate and occurrence percentage from the Awali River needed to supply the
project during the wet season was calculated. The results revealed that a 100% occurrence
rate from the Awali river during the wet season for year 2026 till year 2050. Thus the water supply
is greater than the water demand during the wet season and neither the project nor the
ongoing and planned projects in the area will be subjected to water supply shortage.

The impact of climate change was taken into consideration. In the catchment of Litani and
Awali the climate change impact was estimated to be 0.05/30 of yearly decrease in water
resources. The occurrence of 100% remained during the wet season after taking into
consideration the impact of climate change.

Dry Season Water Balance:

The needed flow rate and occurrence percentage from the Awali River needed to supply the
project during the dry season was calculated.

The results revealed that the first tranche (2026-2030), almost all days (99.9%) could meet the
daily requirement of 0.12 MCM, while the water availability to meet daily requirements
dropped to 68.8% by the last tranche (2044-2050). This implies a negative impact on the
ongoing projects and the GBWSP during the dry season, especially from year 2036 till year 2050.


The impact of climate change was taken into consideration. In the catchment of Litani and
Awali the climate change impact was estimated to be 0.05/30 of yearly decrease in water
resources. The results revealed that the first tranche (2026-2030), almost all days (99.8%) could
meet the daily requirement of 0.1 MCM, while the water availability to meet daily requirements
dropped to 47.9% by the last tranche (2044-2050).

Environmental and Social Impact Assessment

The proposed Second phase of GBWSP has the potential to create a range of impacts on the
environment. These potential impacts can be both positive (beneficial) and negative
(adverse) depending on the resources and receptors involved along with other parameters
such as geographical scope (magnitude and extent), temporal scope (duration) and
reversibility.

The Social and environmental impacts are assessed in order to propose preventive measures
which will be incorporated in the Project design, construction and operation. Actions will also
be undertaken in order to mitigate, if not eliminate, the potential adverse impacts of the
Project to as low as reasonably practicable (ALARP), and to meet international and national
Lebanese standards and regulations

It is anticipated that this project will have long-term term positive impacts on the economic
sector, employment (national scale), infrastructure and services, water supply and sanitation,
environment and public heath sectors among others.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1 BACKGROUND INFORMATION

Greater Beirut has been facing a deficit in potable water for the past fifty years. In 1970 the
Lebanese Government of the day passed a decree (Presidential Decree No. 14522, May 1970)
in which it allocated water from the Litani and Awali river catchments to different regions in
Lebanon. As a result, Greater Beirut was allocated 50 million cubic meters for the dry season.
This corresponds to 250,000 m3/day (3m3/s) of water.

In 1977 the Ministry of Energy and Water (MoEW) on behalf of the Government requested from
the Council of Development and Reconstruction (CDR) to study the options for providing
additional water resources to Greater Beirut. Significant number of studies dealing with
conveying water by means of a tunnel and pipelines has been carried out.

The Greater Beirut Water Supply Project loan was approved on December 16, 2010.. The Loan
Agreement between the Borrower and the Bank was signed on February 8, 2012, and declared
effective on December 4, 2012. The Project Agreements between the two project
implementing agencies was made between the Bank and the Beirut Mount Lebanon Water
Establishment (BMLWE) as well as with the Council for Development and Reconstruction (CDR)
and these agreements were signed on the same date as the Loan Agreement While the
original closing date of the GBWSP was June 30, 2016, this was extended till April 30, 2025.

Since the construction works have not been fully completed, the BMLWE and the CDR are
currently preparing a second phase of GBWSP, to be financed by the World Bank, with the aim
to complete the remaining civil works related to the network, the Joun Regulator and the
Wardanieh Water Treatment Plant and bring the water supply system into operation.
An ESIA addendum is required because while the first phase of the GBWSP complied with the
WB safeguards operational policies, the second GBWSP (SGBWSP), under preparation,
complies with the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) requirements (which
was launched on October 1, 2018), the applicable World Bank Environmental and Social
Standards (ESSs), and Lebanese environmental and social legislations. The ESSs which apply
under this project include: ESS1 (Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social
Risks and Impacts), ESS2 (Labor and Working Conditions), ESS3 (Resource Efficiency and
Pollution Prevention and Management), ESS4 (Community Health and Safety), ESS5 (Land
Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement), ESS6 (Biodiversity
Conservation and Sustainable Management of Living Natural Resources), and ESS10
(Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure). In addition to this, the ESIA addendum
considers changes in the baseline conditions and contextual situation to reflect on the impact
of the remaining and additional works.
ELARD Group was assigned to prepare an addendum to the ESIA of the first phase (issued in
August 2, 2010). The purpose of the addendum is to present the project description focusing
on the remaining civil works and changes/additions brought to the design, assessing the
impact of these taking into consideration the requirements of the WB ESF.

1.2 GENERAL PROJECT DESCRIPTION AND LOCATION

The proposed Second Greater Beirut Water Supply Project would extend the Bank’s support to
the provision of potable water in Greater Beirut while supporting a series of institutional reforms
to ensure adaptive governance and enhance operational efficiency of the BMLWE.

The Project encompasses the following components:

    •    The lining of two water tunnel conveyors (of 3 km and 21 km), their connection with the
         water transmission pipelines and the construction of three regional reservoirs;

    •    The remaining works at the Wardanieh Water Treatment Plant (WTP) to enhance its
         capacity to treat raw water based on the latest water quality measurements and to
         incorporate an early detection system for water quality deterioration; and

    •    The capacity strengthening of the BMLWE geared towards improving the operational
         efficiency, financial sustainability, and citizen orientation of the BMLWE.

    •    Land acquisition and resettlement compensation to displaced people for: (i) the
         reservoirs Hadath 90 and Hazmieh 90; (ii) a section of the twin transmission pipelines; (iii)
         the Joun Regulation Structure (JRS) to control the raw water flow to the WTP, and (iv)
         the land for the outflow of the Wardanieh WTP; and

    •    Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC)

Upon completion, the Project will provide potable water to 1.2 million residents of the Greater
Beirut area, including 350,000 low-income beneficiaries. The Project is expected to supply an
additional 250,000 cubic meters of potable water per day during the wet season (October to
May) increasing the piped water supply by 75 percent in the Greater Beirut area, allowing a
substantial increase in the hours of service, which are currently below 3 hours every 2-3 days in
some areas. A detailed description of sub-components is provided in the following section.

3. PROJECT DESCRIPTION
3.1 CONSTRUCTION ASPECTS

3.1.1    Tunnel and Ancillaries Works

The remaining works include the lining of 24 kilometers of excavated tunnel, shafts, syphon,
and other tunnel-related structures works. Details of the remaining works are as follow:

    •    Lining of the entire length of the tunnel (24km): the completion of the permanent lining
         is aimed at providing a hydraulically smooth surface, and long-term durability of the
         water way;

    •    Inverted Siphon: in the Damour river with ventilation shafts at the hills to the south and
         north of the valley. A washout will be provided for use in emergencies and for
         maintenance; and

    •    A surge shaft in the hillside above Khalde (Joun Regulation Structure): 2,800 mm
         diameter shaft in reinforced concrete with surface venting structure 7 m diameter in
         reinforced concrete, including improved access road.

3.1.2    Remaining Pipeline Works

Remaining pipeline works consist of the construction of the initial section of the twin
transmission pipelines conveying the treated water from the end of the bulk water tunnel at
Khalde to the regional reservoirs. It will include, among other: (i) excavation and installation of
remaining pipeline (about 1660 m of twin pipes), (ii) hydrostatic testing of newly installed
pipelines, (iii) construction of piles and thrust blocks and, (iv) final testing and commissioning of
the system. The works are contingent on the successful expropriation of 6 plots (about 1037
m2), all located in Chouiefet, Al Qebbeh Area.

3.1.3    Regional Reservoir

To improve the reliability of the water supply system and ability of the BMLWE to better manage
fluctuations in water demand, construction of the three regional reservoirs are planned to be
completed. The reservoirs (Hadath 125, Hadath 90 and Hazmieh 9012) will increase BMWLE’s
storage capacity by about 120,000m3, and increase the overall resilience and efficiency of
the distribution system by allowing the BMLWE to regulate the flow of the stream depending
on the fluctuations in water.

Under the second GBWSP (2024), necessary adjustments will be made to the Wardanieh Water

The eventual modifications will include the addition of a Powder Activated Carbon (PAC)
system, which comprises powder storage, a feeder, and a dosing system allowing for a 2-hour
contact time. This setup will enable the removal of medium-range organic pollutants, such as
natural organic matter (Total Organic Carbon and Dissolved Organic Carbon (TOC/DOC)),
volatile aromatic compounds, and cyanotoxins. Additionally, depending on the raw water
quality and type of PAC used, it may also adsorb some NH4/NO2. The inclusion of this add-on
will ensure the supply of water that complies with Lebanon’s drinking standards for at least 6
months per year.

PAC is a fine, powdered form of activated carbon that is used to adsorb contaminants from
water. Activated carbon is produced by heating carbon-containing materials, such as coal or
coconut shells, to very high temperatures.

The PAC system usually requires the following process:

    •    Pre-treatment and Preparation: Which comprise PAC storage (usually stored in
         containers within the treatment plant) and Dosing System (usually includes mechanical
         conveyors and feeders).

    •    Dosing and Mixing: Which compromise PAC dosage calculation (based on water
         quality), injection (usually done through a slurry, a dry powder disperser, or a solution),
         and mixing (usually done through rapid-mix chambers or by mechanical agitators).

    •    Absorption: Contaminants in the water, including organic compounds, tastes, odors,
         and some inorganic substances, adhere to the surface of the PAC particles

    •    Separation: The water moves to a settling or clarification tank where PAC particles, now
         loaded with contaminants, settle out. PAC is denser than water, so it tends to fall to the
         bottom of the tank.

    •    Removal and Disposal: The PAC, along with the adsorbed contaminants, forms a
         sludge that is removed from the bottom of the settling tank. This sludge is typically
         dewatered and disposed of according to environmental regulations. Moreover, PAC
         is usually not regenerated but replaced. The used PAC is disposed of, and new PAC is
         introduced into the system.

4. ANALYSIS OF ALTERNATIVES
The original 2010 ESIA considered 5 options (link). This ESIA assesses two alternatives related to
the following:


     •    Choice of the reservoirs focusing on above or underground option

     •    Selection of the pilot neighborhood in relation to reducing non-revenue water.


The alternatives were compared based on their potential environmental impacts, economic
costs and benefits, technical and institutional feasibility, monitoring and control requirements
and suitability in the current context.


4.1 CHOICE OF THE RESERVOIRS

Two options for the new water reservoirs were assessed namely above ground or underground
reservoirs and the decision to go for underground reservoirs made based on the following:


     •    Higher initial costs related to excavation works and engineering needs but lower long
          term operational costs due to the reduction in evapotranspiration losses and
          minimization in temperature fluctuations thus reduction in energy needed for water
          temperature control.

     •    Minimal visual and aesthetic impact as they are not visible on the surface,

     •    Above-ground reservoirs carry a higher risk of structural failure which could lead to
          flooding in downstream areas,

     •    Above-ground reservoirs require a large footprint, often leading to clearing or larger
          areas of land.

4.2 SELECTION OF PILOT PROJECT AREA

The selection of the pilot areas for the installation of smart water meters (under component 3)
was mainly based on the condition of the existing water networks. The selected areas have a
relatively new water network which limits water losses within the network.

5.2.3.2    Geology/Hydrogeology

Figure 5-4 presents a hydrogeological map of the project area and components showing the
different hydrogeological formations and general groundwater flow direction.

The main formation in which the project components are located is the C4-C5, Sarafand-
Khalde Cretaceous Basin (19 b).                 The Sannine-Maameltain Group (C4-C5) is generally
considered to constitute one of the major aquifers in Lebanon, given its highly karstified nature
and extent that favor a high storage and recharge capacity. Groundwater in the Sarafand-
Khalde Cretaceous Basin (19 b) is expected to flow towards the west-northwest (Figure 5-4).

Groundwater tends to flow along preferential pathways enhanced by fractures and cavities
in karstic limestone such as the Sannine-Maameltein aquifer. This implies that potential pollution
from the second GBWSP may reach the aquifer through direct percolation or surface water
runoff.

The other two formations in which lay the project components are the Beirut Quaternary Basin
(26 a) and the C6 (30 b), Unproductive Basin. For the Beirut Quaternary Basin (26 a), the
hydraulic connection between this aquifer and the underlying C4-C5 aquifer is well
established. The northern and western parts the aquifer is in direct contact with the Sea. The
C6, Unproductive Basin, is composed of white chalks, marly chalks with phosphate and chert
nodules and bands. The upper unit with Paleocene is not well defined. The marls of this
sequence act as aquiclude separating major aquifers above and below this unit. The
groundwater flow direction within the Beirut Quaternary Basin (26 a) is towards the west (Figure
5-4).

It is important to note that as per the UNDP 2014 Study, the salt water intrusion limit indicates
that the groundwater in Khalde is expected to be salty. Also in Damour, even if the salt water
intrusion limit is not as close to the site as in Khalde, the abundance of public wells and private
licensed wells in the area is alarming.

Thus, the main issues of concern to be taken into consideration when planning for water
abstraction can be summarized as follows:

    •     Salt water intrusion in Khalde;

    •     Potential salt water intrusion in Damour, given the fact that the underlying aquifer
          extend all the way to the Mediterranean Sea and due to the high number of existing
          wells; and

    •     Groundwater availability in Damour and impact on other water uses given the high
          number of existing wells.

It is worth noting that during the construction phase under the first GBWSP, the contractor
(CMC), implemented mitigation measures to ensure minimizing the impacts of potential salt
water intrusion and groundwater availability due to groundwater abstraction.

The extent of the soil cover layer is shown in Figure 5-5. Soil cover characteristics are described
in the paragraphs below.

    •     Cambisols are developed in medium and fine-textured materials (mostly colluvial and
          onalluvial deposits) which justifies their presence in the Bekaa valley. Cambisols are
          considerd to be good for agricultural usage and can be one of the most productive
          soils in terms of agriculture if the climate is appropriate (FAO soil group).

    •     Leprosols are shallow soils overlying calcareous rocks. Leptosols are unattractive for
          rainfed agricultures because of their inability to hold water. They however allow shrubs
          and small trees to grow over the rocky terrain, which gives them their importance in
          sustaining such terrestrial ecosystem (FAO soil group).

    •     Regosols are shallow soils with low-humus surface horizon overlying directly the
          weathering rock in an actively eroding landscape. Such soils allow orchards to grow
          and thus are important in sustaining such terrestrial ecosystem (FAO soil group).

    •     Anthrosols are formed from heavily modified soils due to long term human activities
          related to irrigation and cultivation (FAO soil group).

5.3.2.1    Chouf

Nearly half of the employed population in Chouf (51.3%) held informal jobs as their main
occupation. This category of workers has a higher likelihood of being vulnerable and taken
advantage of than formal workers who have contracts, defined benefits and legal protections
according to the International Labour Organization (ILO). ILO further defined a category of
workers engaged in “Vulnerable Employment”. This refers to the combined total of own-
account workers (self-employed individuals who do not employ others) and contributing family
workers (those who work without pay in a family business or farm) within the overall
employment sector. Workers in these categories typically have a lower chance of being
engaged in formal employment contracts, which often means they lack important benefits
such as social security, health insurance, and legal protections. Additionally, these workers are
less likely to have a formal platform or representation to voice their concerns and rights in the
workplace, making them more susceptible to economic instability and exploitation. In Chouf,
vulnerable employment was slightly below the national average (18.5% compared to 20.1%).
It was also more prevalent among men (21.2%) than among women (11.8%; Figure 5-15).

In general, the traffic conditions in Lebanon are known for facing infrastructure challenges,
congestion, and deteriorating quality of already mismanaged public transport services.

Public transport modes are available in Lebanon; however, they are characterized by being
unreliable and in most cases improperly distributed over the market. The city of Beirut is
overserved compared to the demand, resulting in severe competition among operators, while
other cities have shortage of public transport services (Choueiri, Choueiri, & Choueiri, 2011).
The available public transport means are not facilitated with the proper infrastructure to make
them accessible by the public; ultimately resulting in the utility of -the only reliable option-
private vehicles. Both public and private vehicles operate in the GBA. The Lebanese
Commuting Company (LCC) operates on six lines with 73 buses and the Railway and Public
Transportation Authority (RPTA) operates with 27 buses (World Bank, 2015). The estimated
public transport fleet for the GBA and North region is shown in Table 5-8. The private operators
carry the majority of the demand. For example, there are mini-bus lines that operate in Beirut,
some even transporting around 50,000-60,000 passengers/day, e.g. van number 4, and are
reported to have considerable acceptability by the public; however, that is not the general
case for all public transport systems (Samaha & Mohtar, 2016). According to the RPTA, the
approximate number of passengers using public transport in 2014 was 1,213,268, based on

There are several factors that define the safety and reliability of road transport system, such as
proneness to accidents and injuries, infrastructure conditions, i.e. availability of signage,
regular maintenance and monitoring, and exposure to pollutants resulting from exhaust
emissions that are usually enhanced during congestion.

maintenance, and signage.

8.4 GRIEVANCE MECHANISM

According to the provisions and requirements of the WB’s ESS10 (Stakeholder Engagement
and Information Disclosure), CDR and BMLWE will put in place a functioning, accessible, and
inclusive Grievance Mechanism (GM) that is proportionate to the potential social risks and
impacts of the project. Nevertheless, the key purpose of this GM is to provide an avenue for
complaints and suggestions regarding project’s components.

CDR and BMLWE will activate different channels to submit a complaint. Complaints could be
related to several issues related to the project’s interventions including those related to services
or works that were incomplete or not executed, inappropriate/ unfair treatment by the staff or
any of the workers, and/or harming of the environment or resulting in a social tension.

Users of the system include all stakeholder categories. It would enable all affected parties
submit their grievances directly, or indirectly through an intermediate. CDR and BMLWE shall
maintain, throughout the project implementation, and publicize the availability of grievance
mechanisms, and take all measures necessary to implement the determinations made by such
mechanism in a manner satisfactory to the Bank. The complainants should be given the
possibility to remain anonymous if they do not wish to reveal their identity. Enquiries or
complaints can be raised through different channels: The main GM uptake channels are

9. ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL MANAGEMENT PLAN
Table 9-1 presents the mitigation measures to be implemented in order to eliminate or minimize
potential environmental and social impacts associated with the construction and operation
activities of the Project. The ESMP framework for the construction phase will be included in the
contractor’s tender documents. The contractor will develop an operational instrument (C-
ESMP) based on this ESMP that will detail the occupational health and safety and other
measures the contractor will take, as well as the grievance process and code of conduct to
be shared with workers. The Project Implementing Agency will be responsible for monitoring of
the construction works to ensure proper and safe implementation in line with the prepared
ESMP and the Contractor's C-ESMP that will be prepared before commencement of the works.
Monitoring during the operation phase of the project will be the responsibility of BMLWE and
CDR.

The ESMP of the sub-projects involving vulnerable groups which may be impacted will pay
specific attention to woman and children with regards to GRM, Sexual Exploitation and Abuse
(SEA), and Sexual Harassment (SH), child/forced labor, and perception of exclusion. In addition
to this, effective measures of communication should be applied to the people affected
including all vulnerable groups. Moreover, the ESMP of the sub-projects involving vulnerable
groups which may be impacted will pay specific attention to woman and children with
regards to perception of exclusion of vulnerable groups, SEA/SH mitigation measures. In
addition to this, measures of communication and provision of installment options for payment
of water services post operation should be applied to the people affected including all
vulnerable groups

It is worth noting that a Resettlement Action Plan (RAP) was prepared as per the World Bank
standards. This aims at identifying the mitigation measures to be taken and specifies the legal
and institutional framework responsibilities that, together, will ensure that all losses incurred by
the taking of land are fully compensated and do not face any kind of diminution of livelihoods
or assets (For additional details see RAP Addendum prepared for the SGBWSP- Link).
