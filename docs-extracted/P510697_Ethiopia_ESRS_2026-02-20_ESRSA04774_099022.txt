                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     This hybrid operation—combining Program-for-Results (PforR) and Investment Project Financing (IPF)—supports the
                     National Strategic Investment Framework for Sustainable Landscape Management (ESIF 2, 2024–2038) by advancing
                     long-term landscape restoration. It aims to: (i) expand sustainable land management in degraded highland areas; (ii)
                     sustain and expand certification, registration, and digitalization of rural land rights in Ethiopia’s highlands, including
                     communal forests and grazing lands; and (iii) raise farm incomes and create jobs by strengthening agribusiness value-

                     chain linkages. Primary beneficiaries are smallholder farmers in program watersheds. The program will increase
                     smallholders’ livelihood security and broaden income earning opportunities. Efforts will be made for the equitable
                     inclusion of all beneficiaries in Community Watershed Users’ Cooperative Societies (CWUCS) including vulnerable
                     groups such as landless, youth, elderly, and female-headed households. The PforR component will finance watershed-
                     level sustainable land and natural resource management, strengthen rural land administration, improve tenure security
                     for private and communal lands (including selected forests and grazing areas), and catalyze private-sector participation
                     to ensure sustainability. Under Result Area 1 (Participatory Watershed and Forest Management), Disbursement-Linked
                     Indicators (DLIs) incentivize scaling up restoration and market linkages by: (1) registering Community Watershed Users’
                     Cooperative Societies (CWUCS) with participatory, approved Watershed Management Plans; (2) expanding the area
                     under sustainable landscape management; (3) establishing Participatory Forest Management with legally recognized
                     cooperatives and approved Forest Management Plans; (4) treating acid soils; (5) integrating cooperatives into existing
                     and new value chains within program catchments; and (6) verifying non forest emission reductions. Under Result Area 2
                     (Rural Land Administration), DLIs focus on expanding and digitizing secure tenure by: (1) issuing Second Level Land
                     Holding Certificates (SLLCs) for individual parcels; (2) issuing SLLCs for communal forests and grazing lands; (3)
                     operationalizing the National Rural Land Administration Information System in program woredas with digitized SLLCs;
                     and (4) increasing connectivity of land administration offices and systems to national digital infrastructure. The IPF
                     component—financed by IDA, SCALE, Nordic Development Fund (NDF), and ENABLE—will provide cross-cutting
                     technical assistance and capacity building across both result areas and the broader government program (ESIF 2). It will
                     support: (i) agribusiness development by linking landholders to agribusinesses; (ii) design and implementation of
                     payment for ecosystem services (PES) schemes; (iii) access to carbon markets; and (iv) Operationalize pilots to promote
                     voluntary land consolidation in Ethiopia’s highlands. These pilots involve baseline surveys, voluntary land exchange, and




Public Disclosure
                     design, testing, and documentation of a computerized platform in real-world conditions. The likelihood and magnitude
                     of risk for the voluntary exchange program pilot have been assessed. The voluntary land exchange will take place in 12
                     designated kebeles, involving small parcels of land and following a national legal proclamation. Targeted TA will also
                     promote inclusive alternative livelihoods for women and youth through group-based training and livelihood grants;
                     strengthen community capacity to scale ecosystem conservation and carbon sequestration via participatory forest and
                     watershed management; advance land tenure and property rights awareness; and establish community-based
                     performance monitoring. In addition, the operation will enhance MoA and Ethiopia Forest Development (EFD)
                     operational capacity for CALM 2, including: (i) environmental and social risk management; (ii) procurement and financial
                     management; and (iii) monitoring, reporting, and verification, including advanced remote sensing. IFC will complement
                     implementation with integrated upstream and downstream support for agribusiness linkages, value-chain development,
                     and access to finance, strengthening market access and supply chains through its portfolio, pipeline, and early-stage
                     leads. IFC’s approach will promote business models that directly engage smallholder farmers and local communities,
                     with close IFC– World Bank collaboration to ensure coordinated delivery.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The proposed CALM-2 Program will provide support for sustainable landscape management and land administration,
                     consistent with the ongoing CALM Program. It will also broaden its scope to additional areas within ESIF 2, including
                     participatory forest management, value chain development, and the land certification of communal forests and grazing
                     lands. The Program will be implemented in Dire Dawa City Administration; and Oromia, Amhara, Tigray, Sidama,
                     Southwest, Central Ethiopia, South Ethiopia, Harari, Benishangul, and Gambelia regions of Ethiopia. The IPF component
                     activities will be carried out to provide cross-cutting support for achieving outcomes in both Result Areas (Participatory

                     watershed and forest management, and Rural land administration) and the broader government program. This will be
                     accomplished through technical assistance and capacity-building. Ethiopia, with a population of around 132 million
                     (2024, est.), is Africa's second most populous nation after Nigeria. Despite rapid economic growth estimated at 6.4% in
                     FY2021/22, it remains one of the poorest nations, with a per capita gross national income of $1,020. Ethiopia aims to
                     reach lower-middle-income status by 2025. Real gross domestic product (GDP) growth slowed from FY2019/20 to
                     FY2021/22 due to COVID-19 and other shocks. However, agriculture, which employs over 70% of the population, saw
                     improved growth during this period.

                     Land degradation in Ethiopia significantly impacts rural growth and poverty reduction, with approximately 80 % of land
                     prone to soil degradation. Today, approximately 27 million hectares (ha) are significantly eroded, affecting almost 50
                     percent of highlands and one in five people. Of the total area degraded, 14 million ha are seriously eroded, with over 2
                     million ha beyond reclamation that makes economic sense. Barriers to adopting conservation practices include land
                     tenure insecurity and limited technical support including poor community participation. Previous successful
                     program/projects targeting vulnerable groups & historically underserved will be built upon to improve tenure security
                     and support for female headed households, landless youth, and others, delivering climate, biodiversity, and ecosystem
                     co-benefits. The FCV context of the country, specifically armed conflicts in Amhara region and partly southwestern part
                     of the Oromia region, may affect the implementation of the Program.

                     Ethiopia faces significant GBV/SEA/SH risks driven by entrenched gender norms, poverty, harmful practices, and
                     conflict-related displacement. Surveys indicate that approximately one third of ever-married women aged 15–49 have
                     experienced physical, sexual, or emotional spousal violence, and only around 23% of survivors sought help (World Bank,




Public Disclosure
                     2022; DHS, 2016). Violence varies by region, with higher rates in Oromia and Gambella, and harmful practices like child
                     marriage and FGM/C persist. Service access is limited by stigma, low awareness, and uneven availability across health,
                     justice, and social sectors.

                     Conflict and displacement heighten SEA/SH risks, especially for IDPs, while disrupting essential services. World Bank
                     operations consistently rate SEA/SH risk as substantial to high due to contextual vulnerabilities, labor influx, and
                     capacity gaps, necessitating dedicated action plans, strengthened GRM for sensitive cases, codes of conduct, service
                     mapping, and targeted GBV risk mitigation in project design and supervision.
