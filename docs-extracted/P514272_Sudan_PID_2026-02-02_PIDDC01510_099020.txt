    B. Introduction and Context

    Country Context

    1.     Sudan is facing an unprecedented crisis, with the ongoing conflict since April 2023 triggering the world’s fastest-
    growing displacement emergency and a severe economic contraction. As of October 2025, more than 11.7 million
    people have been forcibly displaced, including over 7.2 million internally and 3.4 million refugees and asylum seekers
    who have crossed into neighboring countries.1 Sudan now has the largest number of internally-displaced persons (IDPs)
    globally, and the humanitarian situation is compounded by recurrent outbreaks of disease, the destruction of critical
    infrastructure, and widespread food insecurity. According to the latest Integrated Food Security Phase Classification
    (IPC) analysis, an estimated 21.2 million people—around 45 percent of the population—were facing high levels of acute
    food insecurity (IPC Phase 3 or above) in September 2025, with famine (IPC Phase 5) conditions already observed in El
    Fasher (North Darfur) and Kadugli (South Kordofan). The conflict has devastated urban centers, particularly Khartoum,
    and has disrupted the delivery of essential services, leaving millions without reliable access to health, education, water,
    and sanitation.

    2.     The economic impact of the conflict has been profound. Sudan’s economy was already strained by high debt and
    stalled reforms; these structural weaknesses were compounded by the conflict. The real gross domestic product (GDP)
    is estimated to have contracted by 29 percent in 2023 and a further 15 percent in 2024, driven by the collapse of private
    and public consumption, investment, and the destruction of productive assets. Inflation surged to 170 percent in 2024,
    fueled by currency depreciation, supply chain disruptions, and soaring food prices. Although inflation returned to double
    digits in mid-2025, elevated prices continue to put pressure on households. The fiscal position has deteriorated sharply,
    with government revenues declining due to reduced economic activity and a shrinking tax base. While high frequency
    data suggest a nascent recovery in selected urban areas, the path to recovery remains highly challenging, as the conflict
    continues, with damaged production capacity, financing and cash shortages, weak banking infrastructure, and persistent
    macroeconomic instability.2

    3.     Poverty and vulnerability have deepened across Sudan. Extreme poverty has more than doubled since the
    outbreak of the conflict, rising from 23 percent in 2022 to an estimated 59 percent in 2024. The agrifood system –
    traditionally a cornerstone of Sudan’s economy and employment - has been severely affected. According to a study by
    the International Food Policy Research Institute (IFPRI), GDP in the agrifood sector has contracted by over 33.6 percent

    Sectoral and Institutional Context

    4.    Sudan’s agriculture sector stands as a vital lifeline amid escalating fragility, economic collapse, and widespread
    food insecurity. Prior to the recent conflict, agriculture was the most resilient contributor to GDP and employment,
    employing 40 percent of the labor force and accounting for one-third of GDP.4 Cereal and livestock exports comprised
    44 percent of total exports between 2012 and 2022, underscoring the sector’s importance to livelihoods and
    macroeconomic stability. However, two years of conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid
    Support Forces (RSF) have triggered a humanitarian catastrophe, with 24.6 million people now in urgent need of food
    assistance and famine conditions emerging in several regions. The collapse of industrial capacity, soaring inflation, and
    the near-total breakdown of market systems have further eroded the sector’s ability to sustain rural incomes and food
    security.

    5.     The conflict has sharply undermined agricultural production and productivity and disrupted the entire agrifood
    value chain. Access to inputs, credit, and rural infrastructure is severely constrained, with high transport costs and fragile
    seed security. Many farmers have been displaced, fields left unharvested, agriculture and irrigation equipment lotted
    or destroyed, and seed production and storage disrupted. The ongoing conflict has severely disrupted Sudan’s livestock
    sector, leading to the displacement of herders, loss of grazing lands, and destruction of critical infrastructure. The
    destruction of veterinary facilities has limited the availability and distribution of essential animal vaccines increasing the
    risk of disease outbreaks and further threatening livestock health and productivity. The collapse of banking services and
    commercial activity in Khartoum has deeply impacted small and medium enterprises (SMEs), resulting in extensive asset
    loss, rising unemployment, and fragile supply chain dynamics from farm to market. Insecurity and fuel shortages have
    curtailed trade flows, while the destruction of rural infrastructure has limited market access and increased post-harvest
    losses. Irrigated agriculture—long the backbone of Sudan’s farming economy, particularly in Gezira and other major
    irrigation schemes—has deteriorated sharply, with damaged canals, fuel shortages, and halted maintenance reducing
    water delivery and leaving large tracts of irrigated land underutilized or abandoned.

    6.    Despite these challenges, recovery is possible. The World Bank’s June 2024 assessment indicates that modest
    gains in access to inputs, particularly fertilizers and seeds, could spur agricultural GDP of 0.8 percent in 2025 and 1.1
    percent in 2026, with medium-term potential reaching 3–4 percent. This growth would account for up to 40 percent of
    total economic recovery (as a contribution to a pre-conflict GDP in 2023), demonstrating the sector’s central role in
    contributing to Sudan’s recovery and growth. Revitalizing agriculture is not only a pathway to economic recovery but
    also a key instrument for sustaining livelihoods and mitigating the impacts of displacement, inflation, and disrupted
    trade. The proposed Sudan Transition to Agriculture Recovery and Resilience (STARR) Project aims to achieve this by
    restoring and enhancing agricultural and livestock productivity, strengthening seed systems, and promoting private
    sector–led value chain development in selected areas in of the country.

7.    The STARR Project’s design reflects lessons learned from the ongoing Sudan Thabat Community Enhancement
Project (Sudan Thabat, P181490). Under the Sudan Thabat project, agricultural inputs, small scale irrigation
rehabilitation and solarization have been provided to 148 Farming Production Organizations (FPOs), representing
approximately 25,000 farmer households, and over 73,000 MT of wheat were harvested during the 2024/25 winter
season (which represented 18.4 percent of the national output). The project has also addressed logistics constraints by
supporting SMEs involved in transport and market integration, demonstrating the importance of coordinated value
chain interventions. In addition, the expansion of digital platforms such as Mahsul has strengthened the value chain
ecosystem by connecting farmers to markets and improving price transparency. The platform has also enabled
consumer cooperatives to play a price-stabilizing role for households by purchasing directly from farmers and other
primary suppliers, leveraging the SMEs supported by the project as the logistical and value-chain bridge.


Relationship to CPF

8.     Sudan does not have a Country Partnership Framework (CPF) in place. The most recent strategic engagement with
Sudan was through a Country Engagement Note (CEN) for FY2021–2022, which guided the Bank’s activities during a
period of transition and fragility. Following decades of very limited engagement, the program under the “re-engagement
CEN” focused on supporting macroeconomic reforms, complementary social impact mitigation, and helping the country
meet the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) debt relief benchmarks. It also aimed to contribute to a renewal of the
social contract and building systems towards a full country program. However, the military coup in 2021 abruptly halted
the transition and disrupted the process of reengagement with international organizations, a situation further
exacerbated by the outbreak of the conflict in April 2023. Currently, WBG’s engagement in Sudan is framed by
Operational Policy 7.30, which led to a shift in project implementation modalities from government-led to third-party
entities, and repurposing of project funds to respond to urgent needs.

9.     The STARR Project is fully consistent with IDA21’s strong emphasis on placing FCV considerations in WBG’s
strategic approach in FCVs. The project’s focus on revitalizing agriculture is expected to improve food security and
support livelihoods, in a sustainable way. At the same time, the project’s results will directly contribute to the dedicated
indicator in the IDA21scorecard that tracks displaced people and people in host communities provided with services and
livelihoods in FCVs. Furthermore, the project is aligned with the WBG’s FCV Strategy 2020-25 and in particular its pillar
on remaining engaged during conflict and crisis situations, which is the guiding framework for IDA’s operations in Sudan
and aims to preserve hard won development gains, protect essential institutions, build resilience, and be ready for
future recovery.

D. Concept Description

12.   The proposed project will comprise three components.

Component 1: Revitalizing Irrigated Agricultural and Livestock Production Systems
13. This component will focus on improving the productivity of key food security crops such as wheat, sorghum, millet,
vegetables, and beans as well as livestock through the demand-based provision of improved inputs (including seeds,
fertilizers, and animal feed), extension services, and veterinary support. It will finance the rehabilitation of community-
based agricultural and rural infrastructure to boost agricultural and livestock production. Activities will adopt a demand-
responsive approach, adopting Farmer-led Irrigation Development (FLID) to prioritize interventions such as
rehabilitation of agricultural land and irrigation infrastructure (e.g., land leveling, terracing, clearing of
irrigation/drainage canals and repairs of pumping equipment), restoration of rainwater harvesting structures (haffirs,
hand-dug wells, earth dams) for multi-purpose use, and rehabilitation of livestock watering points, as well as solarization
of irrigation pumping equipment, which would further enhance water availability for both crop and livestock systems,
particularly in targeted drought-prone and conflict affected areas. The component will also support the repair of canals,
headworks, and control structures to improve water use efficiency and revitalize irrigated agriculture. To reduce post-
harvest losses and associated methane emissions, the project will support the rehabilitation and restoration of storage
facilities, including grain silos and warehouses. The project support will also include climate-adaptive interventions and
improved rural market access through the rehabilitation of feeder roads, bridges, and water crossings.

Component 2: Strengthening Local Seed Systems and Community Seed Banks
14. This component will support long-term access to quality seeds through the restoration and expansion of various
types of seed banks, including training for local cooperatives on seed selection, multiplication, storage, and
management. It will also support the restoration of research and development efforts for improved seed production
and capacity building for seed certification in line with global good practices. Investments will be made in existing local
seed enterprises to enhance the availability and diversity of climate-resilient varieties. By laying the foundation for a
more sustainable seed system, the project will reduce dependency on external seed sources and enhance timely
availability and recovery readiness in targeted communities.

Component 3: Enhancing Agricultural Value Chains and Private Sector Engagement
15. This component will strengthen agricultural and livestock value chains as a rapid response to food insecurity, while
laying the foundation for durable recovery. It will mobilize private investment and position SMEs, farmers’ associations,
and agricultural businesses as core market actors responsible for input supply, servicing, and processing across the value
chain (e.g., milling, oil pressing, solar pump supply and servicing, irrigation system rehabilitation, transport, and
logistics). By anchoring the local private sector, the component aims to reduce transaction costs, improve price discovery
and transparency, and create employment opportunities for vulnerable groups, particularly youth and women. Building
on the SME sub-component of the ongoing Sudan Thabat Enhancing Community Resilience Project, registered farming
cooperatives, associations, and SMEs will be eligible to implement activities such as small-scale rehabilitation and
upgrading of irrigation infrastructure (including solar pumps), transport and logistics (including storage and packaging),
and post-harvest processing. Investments will be financed through competitively awarded matching grants based on
technical proposals, complemented by technical assistance and business development services. This approach will

Digital Market Platform linking farmers, SMEs and markets
16. The project will also support the expansion and operationalization of the Mahsul digital platform, currently
maintained under the ongoing Sudan Thabat Project, to enhance market access and value chain integration for
smallholder farmers, consumer cooperatives, and agri-SMEs. Since its launch in December 2024, Mahsul has been
deployed in three states (Northern, River Nile and Kassala) and has onboarded 70 cooperatives. The platform enables
individual farmer registration, tagged to their respective cooperatives, and facilitates both subsidized and B2B
transactions, linking farmers directly to markets, SMEs and consumer cooperatives and other buyers in a transparent
marketplace. Acting as a market ecosystem, the project will reduce price distortions created by intermediaries, promote
price discovery and transparency, and contribute to more stable and predictable prices across targeted value chains. At
the market points, aggregated commodities will be bulked and sold through organized selling points managed by
cooperatives or local vendors, enabling vulnerable communities to access fairly prices food commodities.

Geographical Targeting
17. Target localities, communities, and agricultural cooperatives will be identified using a formula established under
the ongoing Sudan Thabat Project, based on clearly defined criteria including security conditions, agricultural and
livestock potential, access to water resources, availability of private sector services (e.g., transport, agro-processing),
and complementarity with other development partner interventions. This project is designed as a national project to
ensure geographical targeting based on agriculture potential and the situation on the ground. Given the improved
security situation and high agricultural potential in Gezira and Sennar states, the project may prioritize these two states
for initial targeting. Should conflict escalate in any of the proposed project target areas, project resources may be
reallocated to alternative localities or states, in accordance with procedures and criteria defined in the Project
Implementation Manual (PIM), and in consultation with the World Bank.

Complementarity between Sudan Thabat and STARR Projects
18. The STARR Project is designed to build on and complement the ongoing Sudan Thabat Project by scaling up proven
approaches and leveraging established systems for agricultural recovery in fragile and conflict-affected environments.
The Sudan Thabat Project’s Component 2 on Improving Food Security and the STARR Project are designed to work in
tandem to strengthen food security and agricultural resilience in Sudan’s conflict-affected areas. Specifically, the Sudan
Thabat Project has laid the groundwork by supporting farming cooperatives and SMEs with climate-smart inputs,
technical assistance, and matching grants for irrigation, logistics, and post-harvest processing, while also scaling up
community consumer cooperatives to stabilize local food supply and affordability. Building on these foundations, STARR
will expand the scope and impact by deepening support for agricultural and livestock productivity, strengthening seed
systems (including the restoration and expansion of community seed banks and access to climate-resilient varieties) and
mobilizing private sector investment along the value chains. The STARR Project will leverage the networks, market
linkages, and institutional capacities established under the Sudan Thabat Project, enabling a seamless transition from
emergency food assistance and local market stabilization to broader, market-integrated recovery and sustainable
livelihoods.
