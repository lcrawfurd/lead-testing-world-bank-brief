                           B. Introduction and Context

                           Country Context

                          1. Djibouti, though small in land area and population, holds significant strategic importance in the Horn of Africa.
                             Geopolitically, Djibouti’s location at the crossroads of key maritime routes—particularly the Bab-el-Mandeb Strait, a
                             chokepoint between the Red Sea and the Gulf of Aden—has made it a vital platform for international trade and
                             regional connectivity, particularly for landlocked neighbor, Ethiopia1, which relies heavily on Djibouti’s deep-water
                             port. In addition to port services, Djibouti’s economy has evolved around logistics, telecommunications, and banking
                             sectors that remain central to its development. Djibouti’s stability and strategic location near conflict zones have made
                             it a key host for foreign military bases, boosting international security efforts and generating significant fiscal
                             revenues. Additionally, Djibouti has emerged as a place of refuge for displaced populations from neighboring states,
                             such as Somalia, Ethiopia, and Yemen, reinforcing its humanitarian significance in a volatile region.

                          2. In the last two decades, Djibouti saw steady economic growth, attaining lower-middle-income status with a per
                             capita GDP of over US$3,200. Driven by its port services, logistics, and infrastructure development, Djibouti has
                             attracted considerable investment and experienced a steady GDP growth rate averaging 4.4 percent annually from
                             2000 to 2021. Key drivers of this growth include strategic infrastructure investments, Djibouti's advantageous geo-
                             strategic location, and a stable macroeconomic framework. These strengths have attracted substantial foreign direct
                             investment, amounting to US$2.3 billion between 2000 and 20202.

                          3. Despite the economic progress, Djibouti faces persistent challenges of poverty and inequality. As of 2017, 21 percent
                             of households lived below the national poverty line, and 44 percent fell below the international lower-middle-income
                             threshold3. Inequality in Djibouti is one of the highest in the MNA region with a Gini index of 41.6 in 20174. In 2023,
                             the country ranked 175 out of 193 countries and territories in the Human Development Index (HDI). The
                             unemployment rate—particularly among youth, women, and those outside the capital—is more than three times the
                             MENA average.

                          4. The rural-urban divide in Djibouti critically impacts social welfare and economic development. Policies must address
                             rural needs and enhance infrastructure for inclusive growth. Due to the country's economic and infrastructural
                             development centered around the capital, 79% of the population are residing in urban areas, primarily in Djibouti City.
                             In contrast, only 21% of the population5 lives in rural areas which face significant challenges such as higher poverty




_For Official Use Only_
                             rates and limited access to essential services like water, education and healthcare. Rural livelihoods, mainly agriculture
                             and pastoralism, are increasingly threatened by environmental factors like droughts, further increasing rural-urban
                             disparity and prompting migration to urban areas.


                           Sectoral and Institutional Context

                          5. Djibouti faces critical water challenges from both scarcity and flooding, affecting development, job opportunities,
                             food security, and livelihoods. With an arid climate and average annual rainfall of just 225 mm, the country has
                             extremely limited renewable water resources—only 185 cubic meters per person per year, far below the 500 cubic
                             meter absolute scarcity threshold. Recurrent droughts (notably 2008–2011 and 2021–2022) have affected over
                             100,000 people, cut livestock herds in half, and driven GDP losses of up to 4 percent per year; at the same time, major
                             flood events in Djibouti City over the past two decades have affected up to 200,000 residents, with a 2023 event alone
                             causing damages estimated at US$47 million (around 1.1 percent of GDP). Groundwater is essentially the country’s
                             only water source, but it is being overdrawn and degraded: annual abstraction is about 133 percent of renewable
                             groundwater (versus 7 percent in Ethiopia and 18 percent in Kenya), and salinity exceeds 900 mg/L in over half of
                             boreholes, with shallow aquifers highly vulnerable to drought (during 2008–2011, nearly all traditional shallow wells
                             and 80 percent of community wells failed).6 Djibouti’s water security also hinges on transboundary resources and
                             cooperation, particularly with Ethiopia: the Afar Rift Valley/Afar Triangle aquifer is shared with Ethiopia and Eritrea,
                             and the Ethiopia–Djibouti pipeline currently supplies roughly six million cubic meters per year—about one-quarter of
                             urban use—though its long-term reliability is partly climate-dependent, underscoring the need for integrated,
                             cooperative management of shared water resources.

                          6. Access to safe water and sanitation remains a major challenge in Djibouti, with stark urban–rural disparities and
                             significant service quality gaps. While urban areas have benefited from desalination and the Ethiopia–Djibouti
                             pipeline, together supplying about half of urban water, only 47 percent of rural residents have basic drinking water
                             access versus 83 percent in urban areas. Urban sanitation services also remain limited: sewer connections and
                             wastewater treatment cover only a fraction of the city, and most households rely on on-site systems (septic tanks or
                             pit latrines) that are often poorly maintained. During flood events, these systems frequently overflow, contaminating
                             water sources and increasing public health risks. Fewer than one in four rural households have improved sanitation
                             and open defecation remains high in rural localities7. In rural areas, most communities rely on groundwater (deep
                             wells, traditional shallow wells) and government-provided water tanks; however, weak operations and maintenance
                             (O&M), reliance on manual water lifting at many points, and limited availability of spare parts and energy mean
                             systems are frequently unreliable or nonfunctional. Climate change is compounding these challenges: more frequent
                             and intense droughts are shrinking recharge and particularly affecting shallow wells, while more frequent flash floods
                             are often damaging the water infrastructure. Rural water is generally provided free of charge with low community
                             contributions, undermining cost recovery and O&M sustainability; by contrast, urban users pay for service, and the
                             utility has made some progress on recovering recurrent costs despite high non-revenue water (about 44 percent) and
                             uneven reliability. The consequences of poor service are wide ranging: women and children in rural areas often spend
                             hours each day collecting water, constraining schooling and income opportunities; pastoralists and farmers face




_For Official Use Only_
                             reduced productivity when water points fail; and health risks remain elevated.

                          7. Water management in Djibouti faces significant challenges due to fragmented institutional responsibilities, limited
                             capacity, and the absence of a clear strategic plan for water resources. The Ministry of Agriculture, Water, Fisheries,
                             Livestock, and Halieutic Resources (MAEPE RH) leads overall sector policy, planning, and oversight for water resources
                             in Djibouti. Within MAEPE RH, the Rural Water Resources Directorate (Direction de l’Hydraulique Rurale, DHR) is
                             responsible for rural water supply and groundwater management, while the National Office of Water and Sanitation
                             of Djibouti (ONEAD) manages urban water services under ministerial supervision. The Centre for Research and Studies
                             of Djibouti (CERD) undertakes scientific research and source exploration, and the Ministry of Health monitors water
                             quality and waterborne diseases through its Public Health and Environmental Hygiene directorates. A National Water
                             Fund intended to finance rural water infrastructure, including operations and maintenance, has been proposed under
                             DHR management but has not yet been fully operationalized. Despite these institutional arrangements, sector
                             performance is constrained by overlapping mandates, limited staffing and financial resources, fragmented
                             information systems, and weak enforcement—challenges that are particularly acute in rural areas where water
                             services are provided free of charge and DHR depends on budget transfers. At the regional level, the
                             Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) plays a key role in strengthening cooperation on shared aquifers
                             and groundwater governance, including through platforms such as the Groundwater Information System (GWIS) that
                             support data generation, sharing, and coordinated planning among member states.

                            Key Results

                          9. The project will increase access to safe water in rural Djibouti by expanding and rehabilitating groundwater-based
                             systems, raising the number of people with at least a basic water service. It will also strengthen resilience to climate
                             change by investing in climate-resilient boreholes and rural piped schemes that are sited and designed using
                             hydrogeological investigations to withstand droughts and floods. Service reliability will be strengthened by
                             professionalizing operations and maintenance: qualified private operators will deliver O&M under clear,
                             performance-based contracts, while DHR and local communities provide oversight and accountability. Groundwater
                             management will be reinforced through capacity building of DHR, aquifer studies and monitoring, development of
                             policies and technical standards, and preparation of a national Water Resources Master Plan, enabling sustainable
                             abstraction and evidence-based investment planning. Finally, better data systems and strengthened transboundary
                             cooperation—via IGAD platforms and joint work on shared aquifers—will improve information sharing and
                             coordinated management of groundwater across borders. Project outcomes will be measured through the following
                             PDO indicators:
                             (I)      Enhanced resilience to climate change with a target of 150,000 people benefiting from climate resilient

                                      infrastructure.
                             (II)     Improved access to water supply with a target of 114,000 people provided at least basic water service.
                             (III)    Increased in the functionality level of rural water schemes with a target of 95% of all systems supported
                                      through the project.
                             (IV)     National Groundwater Information Management System (GWIS) established, operational, and used for

                          10. Main outputs of the project are:
                              • Construction of at least 27 new boreholes and associated distribution networks.
                              • Rehabilitation of at least 40 existing boreholes and associated distribution networks.
                              • Construction of at least 12 surface water harvesting and storage systems.
                              • Adoption of the rural O&M policy, including standard rural O&M agreement template and the signing of rural
                                 O&M agreements.
                              • Strengthened Rural Water Resources Directorate with trained staff, a refurbished main office and two new well-
                                 equipped regional offices.
                              • Feasibility studies for aquifer development and a Water Master Plan (2026-2024).
                              • Establishment of a national Groundwater and WASH services information management system.
                              • Strengthened transboundary cooperation on shared aquifers.


                            D. Project Description

                          11. The Djibouti Groundwater Resilience and Water Supply Project (Projet Djibouti pour la Résilience en Eaux
                              Souterraines et l’Approvisionnement – DJIRESA) is Phase II of the Horn of Africa Groundwater for Resilience
                              Program, which began in 2022 with Phase I with Ethiopia, Kenya, Somalia, and the Intergovernmental Authority on
                              Development (IGAD). Building on the many lessons learned from Phase I, DJIRESA is structured around four

                              components: 1. Expanding Groundwater Infrastructure and Strengthening Rural Water System Management; 2.
                              Strengthening institutional capacities and information systems for sustainable water resources planning,
                              development and management; 3. Project management, monitoring, knowledge, and operations; and 4. Contingent
                              Emergency Response Component (CERC) for rapid crisis response.


                          12. Component 1 (Groundwater infrastructure and rural water systems, US$ 19.6 million) will finance the design and
                              construction of new boreholes, rehabilitation of existing boreholes and associated distribution systems, and
                              rainwater harvesting solutions, while mainstreaming sanitation across design and implementation (Subcomponent
                              1.1). Activities will cover hydrogeological diagnostics, feasibility and engineering design, drilling and testing,
                              equipping of production and monitoring wells, community standpipes and rural networks, and integration of
                              climate-smart features such as solar pumping and water-quality protection measures. Sanitation mainstreaming will
                              include safe siting, hygiene promotion, and simple onsite solutions to protect groundwater and public health,
                              informed by environmental and social screening and community engagement. To ensure sustainability,
                              Subcomponent 1.2 will help transform and professionalize operations and maintenance (O&M) by engaging private
                              operators through service contracts where appropriate, and by strengthening the Directorate of Rural Hydraulics
                              (DHR) and communities for effective oversight.




_For Official Use Only_
                          13. Component 2 (Strengthening institutional capacities and information systems, US$ 9.2 million) will enhance
                              Djibouti’s enabling environment and capability to manage groundwater sustainably by financing: (i) institutional
                              strengthening, equipment, and tools for core water resources functions within the Rural Hydraulics Directorate (e.g.,
                              field vehicles, spare parts for boreholes, and IT infrastructure); (ii) planning instruments such as a national water
                              resources master plan; and (iii) studies and monitoring to expand the groundwater knowledge base, including
                              aquifer characterization in priority areas, saline intrusion and water-quality risk assessments, and mapping of
                              rainwater harvesting and managed aquifer recharge opportunities. The component will also establish and
                              incrementally expand a national groundwater monitoring network (levels, abstraction, and quality), consolidate a
                              borehole and asset inventory, and develop data management and visualization tools so that information directly
                              informs siting and design under Component 1, day-to-day operations and maintenance, and long-term resource
                              stewardship and regulation.


                          14. Support through IGAD. At the regional level, IGAD will facilitate transboundary cooperation and knowledge sharing,
                              including harmonized methods and data protocols, a joint regional study of the Afar transboundary aquifer, and the
                              operation and enhancement of regional collaboration platforms and information systems (such as the regional
                              Groundwater Information System and the Platform for Groundwater Collaboration). IGAD’s convening role helps
                              align national monitoring and planning, build trust among countries, and strengthen the sustainability and resilience
                              of shared groundwater resources. In addition, IGAD will document and disseminate Phase I experiences and tools
                              across the program—such as resilient borehole design guidelines, groundwater data management practices, and
                              contract management training—so they are available to DJIRESA, and will support targeted capacity building for
                              Djibouti’s Directorate of Rural Hydraulics (DHR) through trainings, exchange visits, and technical assistance to
                              strengthen groundwater stewardship and O&M oversight.

                              through two PIUs—one housed in DHR and one at IGAD. It finances day-to-day coordination, procurement, financial

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts




                          16. The ESRC rating is Substantial. Environmental risk is Moderate and social risk is Substantial. On environmental risks,




_For Official Use Only_
                              there is the potential for localized but potentially significant impacts, particularly related to groundwater resources
                              and waste management from desalination, and the need for detailed site-specific assessments during preparation.
                              Key potential environmental risks associated with the project include those typically linked to construction activities
                              (drilling, excavation, building) and operation of water infrastructure in arid and semi-arid environments. These
                              encompass pollution, resource consumption, waste generation, potential impacts on biodiversity and habitats,
                              occupational and community health and safety. The magnitude and spatial extent of these risks are anticipated to
                              be primarily site-specific and localized, associated with individual infrastructure development sites (boreholes, wells,
                              surface water harvesting structures, desalination units, regional center construction/rehabilitation). Most potential
                              impacts, such as dust, noise, and construction waste, are expected to be temporary and reversible with the
                              implementation of standard mitigation measures. However, the potential for groundwater contamination or
                              improper brine disposal from desalination units could have more significant and longer-term consequences if not
                              adequately managed. The project's focus on rural and borderland areas, some of which may be ecologically sensitive
                              or support nomadic populations, necessitates careful site selection and environmental screening. Component 2 will
                              provide technical assistance to enhance the MAEP-RH's capacity in water resource management with a new Master
                              Plan, and strengthen Djibouti water resource knowledge base.

                          17. The social risk rating is Substantial. Activities under Component 1 (groundwater infrastructure and small-scale
                              irrigation development), could lead to a range of social risks and impacts. Key social risks include: conflict and social
                              cohesion risks of both endogenous and exogenous communities due to natural resource access and which can
                              impact traditional livelihoods (nomadic herders in Djibouti and those of border communities); land acquisition

                              physical and/or economic displacement especially of vulnerable groups who rely on traditional access to land and
                              water for their livelihoods and cultural identity; the potential presence of Indigenous peoples in the border areas
                              with Ethiopia; exclusion of vulnerable groups including migrants from other countries (both in search of water and
                              accessing traditional customary land use systems), women, youth and persons with disabilities, and internally

                           E. Implementation

                           Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

                          18. The Ministry of Agriculture, Water, Fisheries, Livestock, and Halieutic Resources (MAEP-RH) will be responsible for
                              the overall implementation of the Project. Within the ministry, the Rural Water Resources Directorate (DHR) will
                              be responsible for technical oversight of the project. A dedicated Project Implementation Unit (PIU) within DHR will
                              be responsible for the day-to-day management of the project and will serve as the primary liaison with external
                              stakeholders, including the World Bank and other relevant ministries. Strategic direction and oversight will be
                              provided by a Project Steering Committee chaired by MAEP-RH and comprising key ministries—including the
                              Ministry of Economy and Finance, the Ministry of National Budget, the Ministry of Social Affairs and Solidarity, the
                              Ministry of Women and Family, and the Ministry of the Environment to ensure alignment with the Financing
                              Agreement and the Project Operations Manual. MAEP-RH and DHR will not be responsible for activities
                              implemented by IGAD.


                          19. The existing PIU in the Water Unit of the Agriculture and Environment Division of IGAD will implement the regional
                              activities under DJIRESA, overseeing technical coordination and day-to-day management with a specialized project
                              team. It will coordinate closely with DHR, work with the Climate Prediction and Applications Centre and other divisions
                              to integrate hydro-climate data and strengthen the regional Groundwater Information System, convene technical
                              working groups with member states and partners to guide joint studies and harmonize methodologies, and lead
                              regional knowledge exchange and capacity building aligned with broader water security and resilience objectives.
