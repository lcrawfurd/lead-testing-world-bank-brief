 B. Introduction and Context


     Country Context

1.        Panama is an upper-middle-income country that has achieved strong economic growth over recent decades,
driven largely by services, logistics, and trade. However, this growth has been accompanied by structural fiscal and
institutional challenges. Public revenues remain relatively low, with one of the lowest tax-to-GDP ratios in the region,
while public spending persistently exceeds revenues, resulting in fiscal deficits and rising public debt. These constraints
limit fiscal space for public investment and heighten vulnerability to external shocks, underscoring the need to strengthen
domestic revenue mobilization and improve the efficiency of public administration.

     Sectoral and Institutional Context

2.       Within this context, the land administration and cadaster sector plays a critical role in Panama’s socio-economic
development. Effective land administration underpins secure property rights, which are essential for private investment,
access to credit, and the functioning of land and real estate markets. In Panama, fragmentation between cadastral, legal,
and fiscal institutions has reduced the efficiency, accuracy, and economic use of land information, constraining
investment, slowing property transactions, and limiting the fiscal potential of property taxation. The cadaster has
historically focused on mass titling and fiscal objectives rather than operating as a continuously updated, multipurpose
system capable of supporting territorial planning, service delivery, and environmental management.

3.       Modernizing the cadaster into an integrated, multipurpose system is therefore a strategic lever for economic
development. An updated and interoperable cadaster can improve equity and efficiency in property taxation, expand the
tax base, and support fiscal sustainability without increasing tax rates. At the same time, it strengthens land tenure security
for households, women, and Indigenous communities; reduces transaction costs for businesses; and provides reliable
geospatial data for infrastructure planning, climate resilience, and sustainable land use. By linking land governance
reforms with fiscal, investment, and environmental objectives, the cadaster sector becomes a foundational enabler of
inclusive growth, job creation, and long-term economic resilience in Panama

     PforR Program Scope

4.       The proposed Program-for-Results (PforR) operation supports a clearly defined slice of the Government of
Panama’s National Multipurpose Cadaster Program (2025–2035), focusing on the first phase of implementation during
2026–2031. While the Government program has nationwide scope and a ten-year horizon, the PforR finances priority
activities in selected Program areas that are critical to establishing the institutional, technical, and operational foundations
of a modern multipurpose cadaster. The Program supports two core Results Areas: (i) development of the national
multipurpose cadaster management system, and (ii) improved access to cadaster and land tenure services in selected
areas.

5.      Under Results Area 1, the PforR supports activities that build the national geospatial and institutional backbone
of the cadaster, including densification and modernization of the national geodetic network through Continuously
Operating Reference Stations (CORS); acquisition of updated base cartography using ortho-imagery and digital terrain
models; formal adoption of the LADM Panamá cadaster model and the national cartographic model; development of the
Integrated Cadaster–Registry Information System (SICAR); strengthening of ICT infrastructure in ANATI and the National
Geographic Institute “Tommy Guardia” (IGNTG); and preparation of a strategic business plan for ANATI and IGNTG to
support long-term operational and financial sustainability. These activities contribute directly to improved
interoperability, data accuracy, service efficiency, and institutional capacity.

6.      Under Results Area 2, the PforR finances activities that expand the availability and use of updated cadastral and
land tenure information in prioritized municipalities. Indicative activities include digitalization of legacy cadastral records;
systematic cadastral surveys; digitization of parcels under the LADM model; integration of cadastral and registry
information into SICAR; mass land tenure regularization to reduce titling backlogs; incorporation of newly surveyed parcels
into the fiscal database of the General Directorate of Revenue; and pilot initiatives to support culturally appropriate
cadastral processes in Indigenous territories. Together, these interventions improve tenure security, reduce transaction
times, support municipal land management, and strengthen the fiscal and economic use of land information.

7.       In line with the World Bank’s Policy and Directive on Program-for-Results Financing, the Program excludes
activities that are not eligible for PforR financing, including land titling within Natural Protected Areas, buffer zones,
mangroves, and wetlands, as well as activities that could involve large-scale civil works or high environmental risk. Minor
renovations of existing facilities and installation of geodetic and IT equipment are included, as they are low-risk,
site-specific, and manageable under national systems. The Program does not include any contracts classified as high-value
in relation to the overall Program envelope; while several contracts for geospatial imagery, ICT systems, and CORS
installation are critical to Program integrity, their individual monetary value remains modest relative to total Program
financing, and they are embedded within the results-based framework.

8.       Program beneficiaries include both direct and indirect groups. Direct beneficiaries comprise individuals whose
properties are surveyed, registered, or formalized under the Program, including women, Indigenous peoples, and other
vulnerable populations; the Results Framework explicitly tracks women’s access to land tenure and cadastral services
through sex-disaggregated indicators. Indirect beneficiaries include municipalities, national government institutions, the
private sector, and the general public, who benefit from improved land information, increased legal certainty, more
efficient property markets, enhanced property tax administration, and better data for territorial planning, climate
resilience, and infrastructure investment.

9.      The Program is implemented solely by the National Land Administration Authority (ANATI), with coordination
across key public institutions, including the Public Registry and the Ministry of Economy and Finance.

 C. Proposed Program Development Objective(s)

 Program Development Objective(s)

 10.     The Program Development Objectives are to develop the national multipurpose cadaster management system
 and improve access to cadaster and land tenure services in selected Program areas. The PDO reflects the Government’s
 objective of transforming Panama’s cadaster into a modern, integrated, and interoperable system that strengthens land
 tenure security, improves service delivery, and enables the effective economic, fiscal, and territorial use of land
 information.

 11.      Achievement of the PDO will be measured through a focused set of PDO-level results indicators that are
 specific, measurable, realistic, and directly linked to the Program’s scope and results chain. These indicators capture
 improvements in system functionality, service efficiency, coverage of updated cadastral information, and access to land
 tenure services, with explicit tracking of gender outcomes where relevant. The PDO-level indicators are:
     I.   Property subdivisions process with reduced response times for users (days), measuring improvements in
          efficiency and service delivery resulting from system modernization and streamlined cadastral processes.
    II.   People with property titles issued (number), capturing expanded access to formal land tenure services
          through mass regularization and backlog reduction in selected Program areas.
               o Women with property titles issued (individual or joint) (number), tracking progress toward more
                   inclusive and equitable land tenure outcomes.
   III.   People using SICAR’s digital services (number), measuring effective access to and use of the Integrated
          Cadaster–Registry Information System enabled by the Program.
               o Women using SICAR’s digital services (number), ensuring that digital access gains are inclusive and
                   gender-responsive.
  IV.     Prioritized municipalities with at least 50 percent of their area with updated cadastral and registry
          information integrated into SICAR – Core System (number), reflecting the expansion of interoperable,
          up-to-date cadastral and legal land information at the local level.

 12.      Together, these indicators provide a balanced measure of success in achieving the PDO by capturing both
 institutional and system-level transformation and tangible improvements in access to services and tenure security
 for beneficiaries, including women, in the Program’s prioritized areas.

 D. Environmental and Social Effects

13.      The environmental and social effects of the Program have been assessed through the Environmental and Social
Systems Assessment (ESSA), which concludes that the overall risk is Substantial, reflecting the scale of institutional
reform and the social sensitivity of land tenure issues. The Program does not finance activities with significant adverse
impacts that are sensitive, diverse, or unprecedented. Its design focuses on institutional strengthening, geospatial
infrastructure, digital systems, and land administration processes, which are expected to generate predominantly positive
and long‑term environmental and social benefits.

14.      From an environmental perspective, the Program is expected to have mainly indirect and positive effects by
strengthening land‑use planning, environmental zoning, climate resilience, and disaster risk management through
improved cadastral and geospatial information. Potential environmental risks are limited, localized, and manageable, and
relate primarily to the installation of geodetic stations and IT equipment, minor rehabilitation of existing facilities, and the
management of non‑hazardous waste and e‑waste. To prevent significant impacts, the Program explicitly excludes land
titling or regularization activities in Natural Protected Areas, buffer zones, mangroves, and wetlands, and incorporates
screening mechanisms and protocols to ensure compliance with environmental regulations. Improved interoperability
with environmental information systems further supports risk mitigation.

15.     From a social perspective, the Program has strong positive impacts, including enhanced land tenure security,
improved access to services, reduced transaction times, and better protection of land rights for women, Indigenous
peoples, and other vulnerable groups. Key social risks identified in the ESSA include potential boundary and tenure
disputes during cadastral surveys, barriers to access for rural and Indigenous populations, risks of exclusion of women if
gender‑sensitive procedures are not applied, and limited institutional capacity for grievance management and conflict
resolution. These risks are addressed through Program design and the Program Action Plan, which includes strengthened
environmental and social staffing within ANATI, standardized environmental, health, safety, and social protocols, culturally
appropriate consultation and communication mechanisms, operational grievance redress and dispute‑resolution systems,
and mandatory sex‑disaggregated data and joint‑titling procedures.

16.    Overall, the ESSA confirms that Program screening and design exclude activities with significant adverse.
environmental or social impacts, and that Panama’s national systems, reinforced by targeted capacity‑building and
procedural measures under the Program, are adequate to manage identified risks and support equitable, inclusive, and
environmentally sound implementation of the PforR.
