 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

 A. Project Strategic Context
 1.      The State of Tocantins (TO), established in 1988, has emerged as one of Brazil’s fastest-growing subnational
 economies, with GDP reaching R$65.9 billion in 2024 and growth averaging over 6 percent since 2020. Spanning
 277,620 km², the state has a population of 1.5 million distributed unevenly across urban hubs and remote rural areas.
 Tocantins is a major grain and cattle producer, accounting for 2.6 percent of Brazil’s grain harvest and 5 percent of its
 cattle herd. The state benefits from 13.8 million hectares of arable land and an expanding agro-industrial base,
 increasingly oriented toward bioenergy, meat-processing, and cellulose, in addition to hosting a major logistics corridor
 for Brazil’s broader agricultural operations – the North-South Railroad (FNS). Yet, lagging regions face persistent
 infrastructure, service, and opportunity deficits, especially among family farmers and vulnerable groups. The state’s rapid
 economic expansion and strategic logistics position underscore the need for investments that promote inclusive and
 resilient development.

 2.      Despite its economic dynamism, Tocantins faces deep-rooted challenges of qualified job creation, rural poverty,
 low productivity, and climate vulnerability. Infrastructure bottlenecks hinder connectivity and reinforce regional
 inequalities: 20 percent of paved roads are in poor condition, limiting mobility, inflating transport costs, and hindering
 access to markets, services and jobs. These challenges affect disproportionately rural and low-income populations. In
 Tocantins, nearly 300,000 people live in rural areas, with over 40 percent relying on Bolsa Família transfers to meet basic
 needs – while job opportunities are scarce. Family farming is the main source of livelihood for most rural households, yet
 productivity remains low and unevenly distributed: although family farms represent more than 70 percent of rural
 establishments, they generate only 16.9 percent of farm output.1 Limited access to credit (13.2 percent), technical
 assistance (10 percent), and market certifications (accessed by less than 20 percent) constrains their capacity to invest,
 expand, or adopt climate-smart practices (latest agricultural census, IBGE 2017). Women farmers are especially
 disadvantaged, facing structural barriers to land, finance, and participation in producer organizations. These challenges
 contribute to persistent exclusion and have accelerated rural-to-urban migration, shrinking the rural population share
 from 25.7 to 14.6 percent between 2000 and 2022 – nearly double the national rate. Meanwhile, domestic tourism,
 particularly in the Jalapão region, offers growing potential for income generation in rural areas, but remains
 underdeveloped, with limited investment, narrow product offerings, and low participation by vulnerable communities
 such as the Quilombolas. In addition, poorly planned tourism puts pressure on sensitive ecosystems, threatening the
 sustainability of the very assets that attract visitors. By contrast, more sustainable and inclusive models, such as
 community-based ecotourism, can help turn tourism into a vehicle for conservation and resilience, making local
 livelihoods and environmental stewardship mutually reinforcing.

 3.      Climate vulnerability is a central development challenge in Tocantins, reinforcing the urgency of building
 resilience across sectors. Situated between the Amazon and Cerrado biomes – both ecologically sensitive and climate-
 exposed – the state is already experiencing shifts in rainfall and temperature patterns that affect agricultural productivity,
 infrastructure durability, and the long-term viability of nature-based tourism. Nearly 30 percent of Tocantins’ native
 vegetation has been lost since 1985, with deforestation and land degradation contributing to rising greenhouse gas (GHG)
 emissions. Rural populations, especially family farmers, face compounding pressures from degraded pastures, irregular

 4.      Gender disparities significantly affect Tocantins' agriculture, transport and tourism sectors, particularly
 disadvantaging women family farmers. Women run only 18.1 percentage of family agriculture units (and just of 11.8
 percent conventional farms), reflecting persistent inequalities in access opportunities. In the transport sector, women
 represent just 15.7 percent of the active workforce in logistics-related positions2 and are notably absent from operational
 roles in road maintenance or heavy vehicle operation. Nationally, only 6.5 percent of truck drivers are women, and similar
 patterns are found in Tocantins, where female participation in logistics and construction remains minimal. In tourism,
 women make up 53.8 percent of the formal workforce (5,667 jobs in 2023), although gender disparities persist: on
 average, women earn R$1,386 per month – 27.6 percent less than their male counterparts – and their average contract
 duration is nearly six months shorter (36.6 months for men).3 In agriculture, women face structural barriers, including
 lower access to productive inputs, credit, and land compared to men, as well as insufficient access to information,
 technical assistance, and extension services. Social norms and the unequal division of domestic labor further restrict
 women’s mobility, autonomy, and agency, limiting their participation in producer organizations and access to markets.
 These challenges are compounded by the need to balance household responsibilities, which reduces the time available
 for productive activities and applying for financing. Moreover, pervasive domestic violence undermines women’s well-
 being and agency, exacerbating their economic vulnerability.

 5.      A multi-sectoral investment approach aligns with the strategic priorities of the State of Tocantins and the World
 Bank to generate more and better jobs, promoting inclusive and resilient development in line with the AgriConnect
 strategy. The project builds on the experience of a recently closed World Bank-financed project, the Tocantins Integrated
 Sustainable Regional Development Project (PDRIS, P121495, closed in 2021), and aims to close spatial and sectoral gaps
 through coordinated investments that will focus on job creation by enhancing sustainable transport connectivity,
 promote climate-smart agriculture, and unlock tourism’s inclusive potential – all critical for rural development and
 covering sectors with high potential of job creation. It also reflects Brazil’s evolving investment strategies under two
 ongoing Multiphase Programmatic Approaches (MPAs): the Pro-Roads MPA (P180555), which supports resilient, safe,
 performance-based road rehabilitation and maintenance contracts (CREMAs), and the Inclusive and Sustainable Agrifood
 Systems MPA (P180429), which promotes the dissemination of climate-smart agriculture through productive alliances.
 These approaches are tailored in Tocantins to address persistent challenges across rural infrastructure, agricultural
 productivity, and tourism access, with a focus on climate resilience, social inclusion, and long-term sustainability. The
 operation is also embedded in a broader context of complementary initiatives, including the World Bank-financed
 programs Progestão (P179088) and Biomas (P508202), as well as the forthcoming Inter-American Development Bank
 (IADB)-supported Tocantins Mais Sustentável project. Together, these programs reinforce the state's capacity to
 capitalize on operational synergies and deliver integrated territorial development.

 6.     Tocantins plays a strategic role in Brazil’s logistics landscape, with a state-managed road network that is
 essential for enabling local access to services and supporting broader economic integration. Its 5,841 km paved road

 7.     Public-private partnerships (PPPs) emerge as a promising model to improve road infrastructure delivery and
 sustainability. The state launched a formal PPP program in 2020, managed by a dedicated Secretariat for Partnerships
 and Investment (SPI) and backed by a guarantee fund.5 Tocantins has successfully implemented PPPs in sectors such as
 health and renewable energy, and is now preparing to structure road concessions. Freight-heavy corridors that are used
 intensively by out-of-state agricultural trucks that accelerate road degradation but generate little local tax revenue – are
 being prioritized.6 The project will help AGETO, with support from the SPI, pilot the state’s first road PPPs, building on
 lessons from the World Bank-financed PDRIS project and supporting institutional capacity for structuring, oversight, and
 risk management.

 8.       Road safety represents an urgent concern in the State of Tocantins. Between 2019 and 2024, annual road traffic
 fatalities in Tocantins rose from 490 to 652, with the fatality rate reaching 41.33 per 100,000 inhabitants, more than
 double the national average.7 Rural areas are especially affected due to poor road conditions, excessive speeding, lack of
 safe road designs, and underinvestment in safety infrastructure. Vulnerable road users (pedestrians, cyclists) lack
 adequate protection, especially in urban crossings or nearby road-adjacent dwellings. Despite efforts to implement a
 statewide road safety policy, data systems, institutional coordination, and enforcement capacity remain limited. The
 project will address these gaps through both infrastructure investments and institutional strengthening activities.

 9.      Tocantins' agricultural sector has expanded rapidly over the past decade, but its gains remain highly uneven.
 Agriculture represented 23.7 of the state’s gross value-added in 2022, with soy representing over 70 percent of
 production and exports. The state’s share of Brazil’s agricultural value-added more than doubled from 2011 to 2021,
 driven by a tripling in planted area and strong productivity gains. However, these benefits have been concentrated on
 large commercial farms, deepening inequalities and increasing pressure on the sensitive Cerrado ecosystem. Family
 farms, defined as establishments below four fiscal modules,8 represent over 70 percent of rural establishments and more
 than 60 percent of agricultural employment, yet generated just 16.9 percent of farm output in 2017, according to the last
 agricultural census (IBGE, 2017) Nearly 80 percent of family farmers had limited access to output markets and product
 certifications. Key value chains for family farmers in Tocantins include small-scale livestock (beef, dairy, poultry, swine,
 sheep, goats), staple crops (rice, beans, cassava, corn), and fruits and vegetables. This segment is highly heterogeneous,

 10.     Public rural services and climate-smart agriculture are essential to unlock the productive potential of family
 farming job creation in Tocantins. Yet only 10.9 percent of family farmers receive extension services, and just 13.2
 percent have access to formal credit, both below national averages. The RURALTINS institute, the state’s primary provider
 of technical assistance and rural extension, plays a central role but faces resources and coverage constraints, limiting its
 capacity to promote the adoption of climate-smart agricultural practices (CSA), market-oriented production, and
 environmental compliance. Producer organizations are also weak: nearly 80 percent of family farms had little or no
 participation in cooperatives or associations in 2017, hampering access to inputs, certifications, and economies of scale.
 Addressing these gaps will require support to cooperative development, participatory diagnostics, and tailored extension
 services. The Secretariat of Agriculture and Livestock (SEAGRO) leads agricultural policy and will oversee implementation
 of the project's agriculture component, with technical support from RURALTINS and the Secretariat for Fisheries and
 Aquaculture (SEPEA), which brings expertise in a promising sector for diversification and job creation. Strengthening
 these institutions is key to improving service delivery, coordination, and advancing climate-resilient rural development.

 11.     Tourism represents a growing but underexploited opportunity for job creation, economic diversification and
 rural inclusion in Tocantins. Between 2020 and 2023, domestic travel experienced a strong post-pandemic rebound –
 visitor spending rose by 52 percent and arrivals by 37 percent, while formal employment increased 42 percent, reaching
 over 10,500 jobs, but there is potential for thousands more tourist arrivals and jobs. Those gains have not fully restored
 to pre-pandemic levels: in 2023, the number of domestic trips to Tocantins was 18.5 percent below 2019 figures, and the
 state’s share of domestic tourism at the national level fell from 0.98 to 0.79 percent.9 With less than one percent of
 national revenue and employment, Tocantins still plays only a marginal role in Brazil’s tourism economy.10 Tourism is
 concentrated in sun-and-beach offerings and is highly seasonal, which contributes to labor turnover and informality (36
 percent of jobs are informal). The sector relies almost entirely on domestic visitors (99.5 percent),11 whose spending is
 below the national average and five times lower than that of international tourists in Brazil. Just 17 percent of domestic
 travel to Tocantins is motivated by leisure, with most trips related to family or medical reasons – a mismatch with the
 state’s rich natural and cultural tourism assets. Diversifying tourism offerings, improving infrastructure, and attracting
 new source markets are key to unlocking the sector’s potential, increasing visitor spending, and driving jobs’ rural growth.

 12.    The Jalapão region exemplifies both the potential and challenges of inclusive and sustainable tourism in
 Tocantins. Recognized as one of the state’s leading destinations – accounting for 38.1 percent of total trips motivated
 by personal reasons and enjoying high visibility on social media – it offers striking landscapes, rich biodiversity, and unique
 cultural heritage. However, the region faces pronounced seasonality, with most visits occurring between May and
 September. Hotel occupancy peaks at 85 percent in July but drops to 20 percent during the rainy season, resulting in
 financial volatility for local businesses and high job turnover. Despite strong potential for less seasonal, higher-value
 segments such as ecotourism and adventure tourism, visitation remains focused on a narrow set of sun-and-beach-style
 offerings, and the overvisited dunes of the Jalapão Natural State Park, placing mounting pressure on fragile ecosystems.

 A. Project Development Objective
 To enhance access to sustainable transport infrastructure and increase the resilience of family farmers and community-

 15.     The Project directly benefits rural populations, family farmers, and tourism-dependent communities and
 businesses across Tocantins, creating new jobs and economic opportunities. Approximately 104,000 people will benefit

 the broader population of Tocantins will benefit from reduced climate risks, and enhanced economic opportunities

F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design
21.    The proposed Project design draws lessons from a previous project in Tocantins, the PDRIS, and other active
and recently closed transport and agriculture operations in Brazil, as well as general World Bank’s key agriculture and
transport sector analytical work in Brazil and Latin America. Key lessons reflected in the Project design include:
  a.    Strong synergies between sectors are essential for effective multisector operations. While PDRIS aimed to
        integrate multiple sectors, limited coordination between components diluted its impact and made
        implementation overly complex. In contrast, the current project restricts its scope to three sectors (transport,
        agriculture, and tourism) that share a common territorial and thematic focus. The design promotes convergence
        at the local level, with joint targeting of rural areas and tourism corridors to unlock inclusive growth and
        strengthen service delivery.
  b.    Long-term performance-based contracting has proven to be a fiscally sustainable and technically sound
        approach to road asset management. The PDRIS experience with five-year CREMA contracts demonstrated
        lifecycle efficiency and high-quality results but also exposed the limitations of short contract durations and weak
        institutional frameworks. This project scales up the model with 8–25-year contracts and introduces PPP
        concessions in high-traffic corridors, supported by a Strategic Road Asset Management (RAM) framework and
        the sustainable operation of Tocantins’ Transport Fund (FET). These measures seek to institutionalize the state’s
        commitment to routine maintenance and reduce the fiscal volatility of road spending.
  c.    Resilience and safety must be embedded directly in infrastructure delivery to be effective. Recent Advisory
        Services and Analytics (ASA) work, including the 2019 Federal Network Climate Resilience Assessment and the
        Green Mobility and Logistics for Sustainability ASA, emphasize the importance of designing for climate
        adaptation and universal accessibility. In Tocantins, these lessons are reflected in the technical specifications of

                      Official Use Only                                                                             Page 12
       The World Bank
       Tocantins Productive Resilient and Inclusive Development Project (P509041)         PROJECT APPRAISAL DOCUMENT


         CREMA contracts, the selection of resilience-critical road segments, and the deployment of institutional tools for
         geospatial diagnostics, emergency planning, and climate-informed budgeting.
  d.    Institutional learning has been built into the project from preparation onward. Tocantins actively participates
        in the Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (MEL) agenda coordinated by the Ministry of Transport and SEPPI,
        which provides access to technical guidelines, model terms of reference, and peer-to-peer exchanges on
        performance-based contracting, PPP structuring, and road safety management. The MEL agenda supports
        harmonization of practices and capacity building across states participating in the Roads MPA.
  e.    The agriculture component incorporates lessons from PDRIS and similar Bank-financed rural development
        projects, which highlight the importance of tailored support strategies for family farmers. Experience shows
        that only a subset of POs is ready to integrate into demanding value chains. The Project distinguishes between
        market-oriented cooperatives and vulnerable groups, tailoring instruments such as the Productive Alliances
        model, technical assistance, and business planning support to their respective profiles.
  f.    Adequate preparation, supervision, and evaluation of productive investments is critical to improving the
        outcomes for family farmers. Previous operations revealed frequent weaknesses in business plan quality,
        implementation oversight, and the continuity of technical support. To address these gaps, the Project supports
        the rollout of participatory diagnostics, specialized TA providers, and strengthened oversight by SEAGRO and
        RURALTINS, including through the establishment of a permanent training center for extension agents and
        producer organizations.
  g.    Monitoring capacity is a prerequisite for sustained improvements in the productivity and resilience of family
        farming. The project builds lessons emphasizing the need for structured tracking of PO performance and adaptive
        technical support. Coordination mechanisms between implementing agencies will be reinforced to ensure timely
        identification of bottlenecks and support the delivery of inclusive and climate-smart production models at scale.
  h.    The tourism component integrates lessons gained from the PDRIS and other World Bank-led tourism projects
        which highlight two crucial elements: (i) The importance of prioritizing tourist destinations to avoid scattered,
        low-impact investments. In this context, the program focuses on the Jalapão region — a destination with high
        potential for ecotourism, adventure tourism and experiential community-based tourism; and (ii) Tourism offers
        an opportunity to benefit vulnerable groups, but effective inclusion requires the creation of appropriate
        conditions and incentives. To consolidate the initial results achieved with the previous Tocantins program, a
        combination of targeted support measures has been designed to strengthen the integration of micro businesses,
        entrepreneurs and Quilombola communities into the tourism value chain.

A. Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
22.    The State of Tocantins will be the Borrower, with the Secretariat of Planning and Budget (SEPLAN) acting as the

 A. Technical and Economic Analysis
 Technical Analysis
 29.    A multi-sectoral design that integrates transport, agriculture, and tourism provides the most effective response
 to Tocantins’ uneven development patterns and connectivity gaps. Despite rapid growth, rural communities, family
 farmers, and isolated municipalities remain excluded from opportunities due to poor access to services and markets.
 Addressing these challenges requires a coordinated approach that improves physical connectivity while strengthening
 local production systems. The territorial prioritization of road segments and rural areas was informed by a geospatial
 analysis that combined criteria such as network criticality, family farming density, income levels, tourism relevance, and
 climate vulnerability – ensuring alignment between transport investments and productive inclusion goals.19

 of CREMA contracts improves lifecycle cost efficiency and service quality by tying payments to performance standards,
 while embedding drainage, slope stabilization, and other resilience measures. PPP concessions will be prepared for high-
 volume corridors to attract private investment under risk-sharing arrangements. The paving of the Lizarda access road –
 one of the state’s last unpaved municipal accesses – will bring year-round connectivity to a highly vulnerable area, while
 outstanding PDRIS safeguards commitments will be completed. To address high road fatality rates, the project will
 support the establishment of a Lead Road Safety Agency, develop a Strategic Action Plan which will promote alignment

 Economic Analysis
 33.      The transport component shows robust economic justification, with a calculated net present value (NPV) is
 estimated at US$64.7 million, and the internal rate of return (IRR) at 40 percent.20 The Highway Development and
 Management Model (HDM-4) was used to assess the economic viability of the road works to be financed by the project,
 adopting a 20-year evaluation period and a 12 percent discount rate. Project benefits were estimated based on: (i) vehicle
 operation cost savings; (ii) travel time reductions on improved corridors; (iii) road safety gains from reduced crash
 fatalities and injuries; and (iv) net changes in GHG emissions valued using the Social Price of Carbon. The cost stream
 includes feasibility studies, civil works, supervision, implementation, and maintenance. Lifecycle cost comparisons also
 confirm the long-term efficiency of CREMA contracts: road agency savings under the proactive model are estimated at
 33.6 percent, consistent with benchmarks from other states such as Bahia, where savings reached 38 percent compared
 to traditional procurement. The project’s potential to reduce road traffic fatalities was assessed using the Road Safety
 Screening and Appraisal Tool. The tool was applied to a representative segment21 and yielded a Project Safety Impact
 score of 0.75, corresponding to an estimated 25 percent reduction in annual crash fatalities. These gains are expected to
 be amplified through complementary interventions such as improvements for vulnerable road users in urban areas and
 institutional reforms to strengthen the State’s road safety management capacity.

Gender
39.     The Project prepared a Gender Strategy Analysis to encourage equitable gender participation in the proposed
activities. The analysis covers three strategic objectives of the Gender Strategy (2024-2030): (i) end gender-based
violence and elevate human capital; (ii) expand and enable economic opportunities; and (iii) engage women as leaders.
The assessment identified persistent gender gaps affecting female family farmers and women in the transport and
tourism sectors in Tocantins. In agriculture, women face limited access to land, extension services, credit, and decision-
making spaces. In the transport and infrastructure sectors, women remain significantly underrepresented in operational
and technical roles. In tourism, although women make up most of the workforce, they face considerable disparities in
the labor market, earning almost 30 percent less than their male counterparts and holding employment contracts of

and truck driving) to strengthen their long-term employability beyond the life of the project; (iii) providing gender-
responsive agricultural extension services tailored to women's time availability and safety needs; (iv) integrating gender-
inclusive beneficiary selection criteria for Collective Plans and Individual Investments under Component 2 (minimum 40
percent of investments led by women); (v) promote women’s entrepreneurship and equitable participation in in tourism
through targeted training and technical assistance, and (vi) establishing a gender-sensitive M&E framework, including
disaggregated indicators and regular feedback mechanisms to ensure women's voices are heard. The projects
contributions to narrowing gender gaps will be tracked within the results matrix through the following indicators: (i)
“Number of women trained and certified for technical or operational roles in construction and logistics sector under the
project“; (ii) “Number of female farmers adopting climate-smart agricultural practices as result of project-provided
financing and tailored technical extension services”; and (iii) “Number of women employed after the Project’s
interventions“.

V. KEY RISKS

50.    The overall risk rating for the project is Substantial, reflecting the complexity of a multisector operation in a
limited-capacity setting.

51.     Political and Governance risk is rated Substantial. The State of Tocantins has experienced political volatility in
recent years, including changes in leadership and turnover in senior government positions. This has led, in previous
operations, to periods of uncertainty regarding institutional continuity and to delays in decision-making processes across
implementing agencies. While the current project benefits from broad political support and is anchored in the State’s
development strategy, changes in leadership could affect implementation momentum and ownership. These risks are
mitigated through proactive engagement with a broad set of technical stakeholders across agencies and upstream
institutional capacity-building, particularly during transitions in state leadership.

52.    Technical Design risk is rated Substantial due to the introduction of several innovative elements across sectors.
In transport, the project expands the use of CREMA contracts with longer terms and stricter performance monitoring,
and initiates PPP preparation for the first time in the state. In agriculture, decentralized matching grants require new
delivery systems and stronger engagement with farmer organizations. In tourism, support for community-based
enterprises introduces unfamiliar planning and coordination demands. While grounded in lessons from past operations,
these design features increase complexity. Risks are mitigated through tailored TA and the establishment of clear
implementation protocols.,

53.     Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability is rated Substantial due to the multisector scope
and uneven capacity across core agencies. The PMU at SEPLAN will concurrently manage this Project, Progestão, and
the IADB’s Tocantins Mais Sustentável, heightening coordination risks. Mitigation measures include retention of key staff,
fiduciary and safeguards training, and formal interagency agreements. On sustainability, risks stem from the potential
discontinuation of road maintenance contracts after the project closes, continued reliance on input distribution in
agriculture, and weak maintenance plans for tourism infrastructure. The Project addresses these through the expected
institutional shift of the road asset management system, results-based support to family farming, operational planning
for the training center, and design-stage integration across sectors.

54.    Environmental risk is rated Substantial due to road upgrade and rehabilitation activities located within or near
ecologically sensitive areas of the Jalapão Ecological Corridor. Although works will be confined to existing rights-of-way
and are not expected to result in significant or irreversible impacts, the presence of protected areas elevates
environmental risk. This risk is mitigated through a robust Environmental and Social Management Framework and
associated instruments, which establish clear screening, mitigation, and monitoring requirements for all road
subprojects, complemented by dedicated institutional arrangements and supervision.
