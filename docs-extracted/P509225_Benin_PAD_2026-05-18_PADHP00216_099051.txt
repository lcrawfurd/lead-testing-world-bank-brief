 I.   STRATEGIC CONTEXT
 A. Country Context
1.      Benin, a coastal West African country of about 13 million, has recorded strong and sustained economic growth
in recent years, positioning it among the fastest growing economies in the subregion. The country recorded an average
growth rate of 6.9 percent between 2021 and 2025, rising to 8.1 percent in 2025, marking one of the most notable
economic achievements in West Africa. 1 Benin’s growth performance has been supported by rising private investment;
expanding exports; and continued public investment in transport, energy, and logistics infrastructure, which have helped
strengthen Benin’s role as a regional trade and transit hub.2
2.       Despite this strong macroeconomic performance, economic growth has not translated into rapid poverty
reduction or corresponding improvements in job creation, labor productivity, and human capital accumulation,
particularly in rural and northern regions. The relationship between growth and poverty reduction remains weaker than
the Sub-Saharan Africa average, reflecting persistent structural constraints related to low labor productivity, limited job
creation in higher-value sectors, and uneven access to basic services.3 More than three-quarters of total employment
remains vulnerable and concentrated in low-productivity informal activities, with only marginal improvement over the
past decade.4 Rural areas bear a disproportionate share of this burden, with vulnerable employment particularly affecting
women and youth: Women account for especially high shares of informal and precarious work, while the majority of young
people remain engaged in jobs with limited productivity and earnings potential, especially in agriculture and related
activities where access to inputs, markets, and skills remain insufficient.5
3.      The First 2,000 Days of Life — from conception to a child's second birthday — represent the most critical and
irreversible window for human capital formation, and one where accelerating and deepening investment holds the
greatest transformative potential for Benin's development trajectory. A child born in Benin today is estimated to achieve
only about 42 percent6 of their potential productivity under current conditions, a deficit largely sealed before a child ever
sets foot in a classroom. This is driven in significant part by chronic undernutrition: 36.5 percent of children under five
were stunted in 2022,7 suffering irreversible cognitive and physical damage from prolonged nutritional deprivation
concentrated in these earliest years, with economic costs of undernutrition estimated at up to 16.5 percent of GDP
annually.8 These deficits do not resolve over time — they compound. Benin’s 2025 Human Capital Index+ (HCI+) score of
125 out of 325, below both the Sub-Saharan Africa regional average of 126 and the lower-middle-income country average
of 153,9 confirms that gaps rooted in the first 2,000 days persist through primary school, tertiary education and working
life, where a pronounced gender gap leaves women’s earning potential roughly 13 percent lower than that of men.
Micronutrient deficiencies not only undermine learning outcomes and contribute to retention and progression challenges
across all levels of schooling, but also diminish adult labor productivity.
4.      Compounding these structural human capital deficits is the growing insecurity in the wider Sahel region, which
has begun to spill into northern Benin, further weakening communities’ resilience, disrupting access to services, and
worsening nutritional outcomes. This instability has disrupted livelihoods, strained basic services, and threatened the
recent development gains in Benin and neighboring countries. Conflict and displacement are also well-documented drivers

of Gender-Based Violence (GBV): as community protection mechanisms break down and household economic stress
intensifies, women and girls face heightened risks of intimate partner violence, sexual violence, and early marriage — risks
that compound their nutritional vulnerability and further constrain their access to health and nutrition services. Moreover,
the share of the population facing severe food insecurity has risen from 10.4 percent in 2014–2016 to 15.8 percent in
2021–2023,10 disproportionately affecting northern regions and forcibly displaced populations. This deterioration is driven
by the convergence of household’s inability to afford nutritious foods due to insecurity, unemployment, low and unstable
informal sector wages, high food prices, climate shocks, and structurally weak food systems. Together, these compounding
stressors push households to reduce meal frequency and dietary diversity, especially during lean seasons, heat waves, and
crises, directly worsening nutritional outcomes and labor productivity.11
5.      Recognizing the central role of nutrition and Early Childhood Development (ECD) in building human capital,
productivity, and resilience, the Government of Benin (GoB) has elevated nutrition as a national development priority.
This commitment is reflected in the National Food and Nutrition Policy (La Politique nationale de l’alimentation et de la
nutrition, PNAN, 2024–2033), which articulates a multi-sectoral, life cycle approach spanning pregnancy, early childhood,
school age, and adolescence. Nutrition objectives are embedded within the GoB’s broader development vision, including
Benin Vision 206012 and the Government Action Program,13 which emphasize human capital development as a cornerstone
of economic transformation, social inclusion, and resilience, alongside the GoB’s 1,000 Days+ Agenda.14 Past interventions
have been constrained by fragmented financing arrangements, uneven service quality, limited coordination across sectors,
and persistent capacity constraints at decentralized levels, limiting effectiveness, coverage, and sustainability.15
6.        The ALAFIA:16 Nourishing Benin’s Future MPA provides a framework to support Benin’s long‑term nutrition,
human capital, and jobs agenda, through a One WBG approach. ALAFIA is designed to break new ground: while the GoB
has made important strides in addressing human capital deficits in the school-age population, most notably through its
Integrated National School Feeding Program (Programme nationale d’alimentation scolaire intégré, PNASI), which has
expanded access to food for children above five, the critical First 2,000 Days has remained comparatively underserved.
ALAFIA and its first phase brings multi-sectoral nutrition interventions with the support of other WBG practices and
development partners, community resilience, and private sector engagement into a single, coherent program — a regional
first. Intervening effectively in the First 2,000 Days requires testing and refining approaches that are not yet fully
established at scale in the Beninese context and the MPA is well suited to this challenge as it allows the GoB to learn as
they go — systematically capturing implementation evidence, adjusting course across phases, and building a knowledge
base that informs scale-up and replication. Consistent with the subnational stunting profile, Phase I prioritizes communes
in regions with the highest stunting burden and service delivery gaps, while Phase II will progressively expand coverage
nationwide, informed by accumulated learning.

8.       Malnutrition, in particular high stunting rates, in Benin is driven by four interrelated and binding constraints.
Climate change is a key risk multiplier, deepening and accelerating malnutrition burden in Benin (see Annex 6 for details).
First, service delivery remains uneven and insufficient, particularly in rural and northern areas. Geographic and financial
barriers, shortages of skilled frontline health personnel and incentive structures, and variable service readiness—including
persistent medication and supplementation stockouts—limit access to high-quality Reproductive, Maternal, Neonatal,
Child, Adolescent Health and Nutrition (RMNCAH-N) services.

diversity. Moreover, anemia remains pervasive, affecting 72 percent of children ages 6–59 months and 58 percent of
women of reproductive age, with increasing rates among adolescent girls, a trajectory that signals a deepening

14.     The MPA was selected over alternative instruments — including a standalone operation and a Series of Projects
(SOP) — after careful consideration of what Benin's nutrition challenge uniquely requires. A standalone operation would
have provided financing for a fixed period but could not credibly commit to the 10-year horizon that sustained stunting
reduction and institutional development demand, nor could it embed the structured learning loops needed to
progressively refine and scale interventions. A SOP was considered but rejected since this instrument lacks the pre-
committed financing architecture and the explicit performance gateway between phases that make programmatic
engagement meaningfully different from two sequential standalone projects. The MPA offers: (i) long-term, predictable
financing that allows behavior change interventions — which typically require multiple project cycles to materialize — to
be sustained at scale; (ii) a structured maturation pathway for ANAN, supporting them across its full development curve
from early capacity building through to a fully functional national coordination and delivery system; and (iii) a pre-
committed Phase II envelope that is contingent on demonstrated Phase I performance, creating real accountability while
signaling to the GoB a credible, long-term partnership. To operationalize this accountability, Phase II preparation and
financing will be contingent on the achievement of defined performance thresholds at Phase I midterm review (Box 1).

 A. Government Program
19.       The SNAN 2026–2030 is Benin’s primary operational framework for translating the country’s long-term

equitable, and nutrition-sensitive food system by tackling the triple burden of malnutrition—undernutrition,

holistic vision and detailed plan but also the human resources, high-level political commitment, and partner engagement

22.     The proposed ALAFIA: Nourishing Benin’s Future MPA (P512948) and its first phase (P509225) aim to improve
nutrition and human capital outcomes in Benin, driving a long-term transformation for the well-being of its population.

Drawing on the WBG’s Investment Framework for Nutrition 2024,27 the Program is anchored in high-priority, evidence-
based ‘best-buy’ interventions across four target groups: (a) antenatal interventions through micronutrient
supplementation; (b) perinatal interventions such as skilled birth attendance and early initiation and support for exclusive
breastfeeding; (c) infancy and childhood interventions including micronutrient supplementation, growth monitoring, and
IYCF counseling; and (d) cross-cutting interventions for the general population such as cash transfers, staple food
fortification, and interventions to reduce obesity and unhealthy diets. Through two interconnected pillars, the Program
will scale up these best-buys focusing on a multi-sectoral convergence approach, strengthening community resilience, and
catalyzing private sector engagement, while building the institutional foundations for sustained impact across the country.
23.      The Program is transformational not only in its holistic, life-course design—spanning preconception through
early childhood and beyond—but also in its embodiment of the One WBG approach, bringing together the World Bank,
the IFC, and a range of complementary sectoral programs under a single, coherent framework. It builds on over a decade
of World Bank engagement with the GoB through multi-sectoral nutrition and ECD operations—including the Benin
Multisectoral Food Health Nutrition Project (P143652, 2014–2019) and the Early Years Nutrition and Child Development
Project (EYNCDP, P166211, 2019–2024)—which demonstrated that the effectiveness and sustainability of a life-course
continuum depend on several interrelated factors: scaling up evidence-based interventions, fostering community
ownership, building decentralized capacity, and strengthening multi-sectoral coordination at the institutional level.

 E. Program Development Objectives (PDO) and PDO Level Results Indicators
 33.    The PDO for Phase I is to improve utilization of quality integrated nutrition, health and child development
 services in targeted areas.
 34.    Definitions. Quality refers to the consistent delivery of evidence‑based, standards‑aligned, client‑centered
 services that meet minimum readiness, safety, and competency thresholds, as well as reliable access to nutritious foods
 enabled through an enabling environment for microenterprises operating in local food systems. Integrated nutrition,
 health, and child development services include the coordinated package of interventions spanning RMNCAH‑N, WASH,
 ECD, school health, and nutrition, delivered coherently to women, adolescents, and children across the life cycle.
 Integration therefore encompasses both service delivery convergence at the community and facility levels and
 system‑level coherence across planning, financing, supply chains—including food supply and market linkages—and
 accountability mechanisms.

RA1: Increase utilization of quality integrated nutrition, health, and child development services
36.     RA1 is about scaling the proven, high‑impact nutrition, health (maternal, child, and adolescent health), and
child development package within the first 2,000 days. The Government’s strategy prioritizes the critical window from
preconception through 6–23 months (first 1,000 days), which is well-supported by global evidence and the WBG’s
Nutrition Investment Framework’s ‘best-buys’.29 With the Government’s Flagship PSN launched in 2024, exclusive
breastfeeding is promoted for 0–6 months, while Small-Quantity Lipid-Based Nutrient Supplements (SQ-LNS) are provided
to pregnant and breastfeeding women and to children 6–23 months to prevent irreversible deficits in growth and
neurodevelopment. Other interventions that will be scaled up include IYCF, growth monitoring, rehabilitation centers,
deworming and Vitamin A, sexual and reproductive health, and ANC/PNC consultations.

37.      The GoB will use the FARN, a community-based model, anchored by Model Mothers (Mamans Lumière) and
CRs, that facilitates cooking demonstrations and awareness sessions for parents of malnourished children. Together,
they cover the preparation of balanced meals using locally available foods, complementary feeding practices, and good
hygiene behaviors. Beyond their core nutrition activities, FARNs also support ECD by encouraging caregivers to engage
with young children through play, communication, and responsive caregiving, recognizing that healthy early development
requires both adequate nutrition and cognitive, social, and emotional stimulation. To improve access to nutritious and
safe foods and reduce disease risk, this RA will pilot a conditional cash transfer scheme providing coupons for enriched
flour, fruits, and vegetables, with a prioritization of locally available products as their supply chains are less vulnerable to
climate shocks than imported products. This RA also harmonizes nutrition curricula in pre-service training with specific
modules on climate change and strengthens SBCC tools, ensuring consistent, evidence-based messaging and practices
across community and institutional settings. For instance, systematic prevention messages for GBV, Sexual Exploitation
and Abuse (SEA), and Sexual Harassment (SH) will be embedded in school and community services.
38.      RA1 will also support Prior Action No. 7 under Pillar 2 (Investing in building human capital) of the First Benin
Accelerated Economic Transformation and Jobs Development Policy Financing (DPF) (P510638, 2025–2028). A decree
on the modalities for implementing mandatory health insurance (Decree No. 2023-327 of June 21, 2023) has been revised
through Decree Nos. 2025-672 and 2025-530 to institutionalize financial coverage of ANC fees, reducing out-of-pocket
costs, which is expected to increase utilization of health and nutrition services. Through these decrees, micronutrient
supplementation is also free of charge for pregnant women and children 6–23 months in 13 high-need communes, which
will help reduce maternal mortality and stunting rates. The follow-on DPFs in the series will expand this micronutrient
supplementation gratuity nationally. The DPF establishes the enabling policy shift (coverage of ANC fees and micronutrient
supplementation), whereas the PforR disburses based on verified improvements in utilization and quality of ANC, PNC,
and micronutrient supplementation in targeted areas (DLIs 2, 3 and 4).
39.     Building on lessons from previous operations, as well as collaborative governance and social accountability, the
Program places institutionalization at the center of its design. This means integrating nutrition and child development
services into commune-level planning, budgeting, and monitoring frameworks while ensuring that decentralized technical
services from across health, agriculture, education, and social protection sectors are equipped and incentivized to sustain
delivery. The appointment of nutrition focal points by communal decree and the integration of nutrition into commune
development plans will guide the coherent integration of health, nutrition, and education services from early childhood
through secondary school, anchored in systems that can endure beyond the Program timeline.
40.      For RA1, the PforR finances the delivery and scale-up of these integrated services, disbursing against verified
results through its DLIs (see below). All DLIs are scalable and disbursements confirmed by the Independent Verification
Agent (IVA). The IPF component provides upstream TA, in curricula development, M&E systems, communal governance
capacity, and supply chain management, that creates the enabling conditions for scaled, sustained delivery, as described
in the IPF component section below.
41.     DLI 1: Communes with a functional CCLS-TP providing multisectoral coordination of community-based
interventions in accordance with the PNSC30 (Percentage). The Program will support the operationalization of CCLS-TP
platforms as the primary mechanism for coordinating nutrition, health, WASH, social protection, and food systems
interventions at the commune level, addressing the persistent challenge of overreliance on Nongovernmental
Organization (NGO) driven coordination that proved fragile beyond project timelines in previous operations. CCLS-TP work
plans will explicitly incorporate nutrition-sensitive actions, including community screening and referral and nutrition
counseling integrated into disease prevention pathways. Outreach and community health delivery will be carried out
through the Local Component of the Health System (Composante locale du systeme de santé, CoLoSS), the flagship strategy
of the PNSC leveraging CRs and skilled Community Health Worker (CHWs) to reach households at the village level.

42.     DLI 2: Beneficiaries newly reached with Small Quantity Lipid-Based Nutrient Supplements (SQ-LNS) (Number).
The DLI is structured around three Disbursement-Linked Results (DLRs): (a) children newly reached with SQ-LNS (6–23
months); (b) pregnant women newly reached with SQ-LNS; and (c) breastfeeding women newly reached with SQ-LNS. The

new beneficiaries in targeted communes, integrating SQ-LNS distribution into existing health and nutrition service

knowledge to identify and refer GBV survivors to appropriate services, the Program will train them on the use of a
validated and culturally adapted GBV screening tool to be integrated into ANC visits, as well as on the application of a
standardized confidential referral protocol, development and dissemination of reference material and through
supervision and coaching. This training will equip frontline workers with the skills to safely and effectively connect women
experiencing or at risk of GBV to appropriate support services.
43.      DLI 3: Women in post-partum receiving at least one post-natal care (PNC) visit within six (6) weeks of delivery
at a health facility (Percentage). Despite progress in facility-based delivery rates in Benin, access to PNC remains
significantly constrained by gender-specific barriers. On the demand side, women's limited decision-making autonomy
within the household is a primary constraint, as the decision to seek care often rests with the husband. Cultural practices
— including the tradition of mothers and newborns remaining indoors for an extended period postpartum — low levels
of education, and lack of awareness further deter women from seeking care, particularly in rural areas and among the
poorest wealth quintiles.31 On the supply side, geographic distance, transportation difficulties, cost of services, and
inadequate staffing disproportionately affect women with fewer resources. These overlapping constraints contribute to a
persistent gender gap in PNC utilization. The Program will support the strengthening of PNC services as a critical but
chronically underutilized contact point for delivering integrated health and nutrition interventions to mothers and
newborns. The Program will finance both demand-side and supply-side measures to increase PNC attendance, including
community mobilization, training of health workers, skilled CRs and CHWs on PNC protocols, and improvements in the
quality and completeness of PNC service packages at health facilities and community outreach points in targeted areas.
This includes the provision of structured postpartum counselling on maternal nutrition, breastfeeding practices, family
planning, newborn care, and early identification of danger signs.
44.     DLI 4: Skilled Frontline Professionals trained and actively delivering integrated nutrition services (Number). The
Program will finance structured training on integrated nutrition interventions — including growth monitoring and
promotion, nutrition counseling, IYCF practices, micronutrient supplementation, basic vision screening and referral, and
early identification and referral of acute malnutrition — for two complementary cadres. This training addresses a core
structural challenge in Benin: the institutional separation between the health and nutrition sectors has historically
excluded nutrition competencies from the health workforce, making their integration into CHW and CR practice a critical
reform rather than a routine training exercise. The standardized curriculum will include dedicated modules on GBV risk
recognition, prevention, and safe referral, as well as on climate change. DLR 4.1 targets skilled CHWs — specifically nurses
operating within the formal health system who are responsible for clinical services and supervision of CRs. A skilled CHW
is considered to be actively delivering integrated nutrition services if they have conducted an on-site supervision visit,
including observation of a CR in a work situation and follow-up with at least 10 households, and a monthly group follow-
up session with CRs in the preceding three months, as specified in the POM. DLR 4.2 targets CRs, deployed at a ratio of
1:200 households. A CR is considered to be actively delivering integrated nutrition services if they have visited all
households in their cluster once per month, or a minimum of 150 households per month, and recorded at least one

RA2: Enhance community resilience and access to quality, nutritious food supply through local engagement and
private sector partnerships
45.      This RA will strengthen community nutrition and resilience by empowering women, engaging local
stakeholders, and catalyzing private sector participation. It will establish climate-resilient nutrition gardens and promote
small-scale livestock across households and schools. With IFC’s support, upstream advisory services will strengthen the
capacity of microenterprises engaged in agri-food production, processing, and distribution—including in biofortification,
fortification of climate-resilient crops, and local livestock value chains. Local livestock producers will also benefit from
support to improve feed quality and ensure adoption of the One Health approach, including vaccination, deworming, and
enhanced biosecurity measures. Clean cooking technologies, such as modern stoves and solar cookers, will be promoted
at the household and school levels to reduce health risks and environmental pressures. In parallel, the Program will link
schools that are unable to establish gardens to reliable supplies of nutritious fresh foods through digital sourcing
platforms, helping address a key challenge around meal quality and micronutrient deficiencies. Community risk
management and early warning and early action systems will be strengthened to enhance preparedness for climate and
food security shocks. These systems—tracking rainfall, crop conditions, prices, disease outbreaks, and malnutrition
trends—will enable rapid, targeted responses such as timely distribution of supplements and therapeutic foods, support
to local producers with drought-tolerant seeds or livestock feed, and pre-positioning of supplies.

integration into local food supply networks, disbursing against verified results through its DLIs (see below). The IPF
component provides the complementary TA, including market readiness assessments, IFC advisory support to women-
and youth-led microenterprises, digital platform operationalization, and commune-level capacity to sustain climate-
resilient food systems—as described in the IPF component section below.
47.      DLI 5: Women- and youth-led agri-food microenterprises that have adopted improved production, processing,
or distribution practices in line with national food safety guidelines (Number). The Program will support the
development of local, nutrition-sensitive agri-food microenterprises as a market-based mechanism for improving
community access to diverse, safe, and nutritious food products. IFC's Phase I advisory support will cover: (i) business
diagnostics and market readiness assessments to identify gaps in capacity to meet food quality, safety, and nutritional
standards required to access institutional markets such as health facilities and schools; (ii) structured capacity building in
financial literacy, record-keeping, aggregation and logistics, and basic business planning; (iii) dedicated modules on food
safety, hygienic processing practices, nutritional quality requirements, food waste reduction, and fortification practices
aligned with Benin's national food safety regulatory framework; and (iv) support to improve aggregation and strengthen
market linkages to households and schools. This engagement builds on and complements IFC's ongoing work under
Women Entrepreneurship Development and Access to Finance Program (WEDAF, P500561), whose financial infrastructure
— including credit guarantee mechanisms and gender-smart financial products — provides an enabling environment for
agri-food microenterprises as they progress toward bankability in Phase II. A women- or youth-led agri-food
microenterprise is defined as one where women or youth hold a majority ownership stake or occupy the primary decision-
making role. Adoption of improved practices is verified through a structured assessment confirming that the
microenterprise has implemented documented changes in at least one of the three areas — production, processing, or
distribution — that demonstrably align with national food safety guidelines.

52.      Exclusion. Certain activities and expenditures are explicitly excluded from the PforR financing boundary. First,

boundary. Third, land acquisition, resettlement, and any activities that would trigger involuntary resettlement are

Bank standards, or procurement of goods and works that would have significant adverse environmental impacts, are
excluded. Excluded activities are not financed under this operation, financed under parallel arrangements by development
partners, or managed under the IPF component where applicable World Bank investment project policies apply.

III. PROGRAM IMPLEMENTATION
 A. Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
59.    The ALAFIA MPA will be implemented through a government‑led, multi-sectoral institutional arrangement,
anchored at the center of government and progressively strengthened at decentralized levels. This implementation
model reflects both the multi-sectoral nature of nutrition and ECD and incorporates lessons from prior World Bank-
supported operations in Benin, which demonstrated the importance of strong coordination, clear accountability, and
decentralized delivery for sustained impact.

implementation progress and results, alignment of sectoral annual work plans with Program priorities, and identification
of corrective actions as needed. Through this structured coordination mechanism, ANAN will strengthen accountability

62.     Line ministries will retain responsibility for the technical execution of sector‑specific interventions within their

project units, to strengthen institutional capacity and sustainability.
63.      Program implementation will be decentralized to departmental and municipal levels, where services are
delivered and results are realized. At the subnational level, through deconcentrated government structures at the
departmental and communal levels, implementation will rely on existing coordination and service delivery structures such
as the Departmental Coordination Committees, Communal Unit of the Territorial Agricultural Development Agency and
Commune Coordination Committees. This will also include the CCLS-TP providing multi-sectoral coordination of
community-based interventions in accordance with the PNSC, Social Protection Centers (Guichets Uniques de Protection
Sociale), and CRs and skilled CHWs to ensure territorial anchoring. These structures, together with sector service delivery
platforms and community-level actors, will play a central role in delivering integrated nutrition, health, WASH, and ECD
services, particularly in poor, rural, and climate-vulnerable areas. The Program will support subnational planning,
budgeting, supervision, and use of data, enabling these actors to adapt implementation to local conditions while remaining
accountable for results.

 B. Results Monitoring and Evaluation
64.    The Program’s M&E arrangements are designed to support results‑based disbursement, program oversight, and
adaptive management while remaining proportionate to system capacity and data quality constraints. The M&E
framework is anchored in the Program’s Results Framework and includes (a) PrDO‑level indicators; (b) RA-specific
intermediate indicators; and (c) DLIs with clearly defined baselines, targets, and verification protocols. The Results
Framework prioritizes a limited number of indicators that capture effective service utilization, system functionality, and
coverage expansion rather than inputs or activity counts. Indicators are selected to reflect results that are attributable to
Program‑supported interventions and aligned with the Program’s Theory of Change.




                                                                                                                       Page 22

65.      The Program relies on a combination of administrative data, program monitoring systems, and independent
verification, recognizing persistent limitations in routine data systems for results verification. Administrative data from
sector ministries, implementing agencies, and Program monitoring systems will be used for program management and
reporting, while independent verification mechanisms will be used to confirm achievement of DLIs and trigger
disbursements. Primary data sources include: (a) Program monitoring and administrative records maintained by
implementing entities and the PCU; (b) Routine sector information systems (health, nutrition, social protection), where
data quality is adequate for monitoring purposes; (c) Financial and budget execution data produced by relevant ministries
for DLIs linked to governance and financing; and (d) Independent verification reports produced by an IVA.
66.     Responsibility for data compilation and reporting rests with the PCU, working in close coordination with
implementing ministries and agencies. Designated focal points within each implementing entity will be responsible for
providing timely and complete data to support monitoring and verification. Monitoring data will be consolidated by the
PCU and reviewed periodically through Program implementation support missions and internal coordination mechanisms.
Where appropriate, indicators will be disaggregated by sex, age, or beneficiary group, in line with the Results Framework.
67.      Disbursements under the Program are linked exclusively to the achievement of results. Each DLI is supported by
a detailed verification protocol that specifies the definition of the result, data sources, verification methodology, and roles
and responsibilities. Verification of DLIs will be conducted by an IVA recruited under the IPF component, with qualifications
and terms of reference acceptable to the Association. The IVA will verify results using a combination of document review,
administrative data validation, field visits, and other methods as specified in the verification protocols. As for the GFF
Grant, the IVA shall determine: (i) the results achieved as prior results and their corresponding amounts up to the
maximum ceiling included in the Verification Protocol, and (ii) the results achieved as of the date of signature of the GFF
Grant Agreement and their corresponding amounts. The IVA will submit verification reports to the World Bank, which will
form the basis for disbursement decisions. To ensure credibility and transparency, verification arrangements prioritize (a)
independence from implementing agencies; (b) clear and objective verification criteria; (c) traceability of data and
documentation; and (d) consistency in verification methods across reporting periods.
68.     Beyond disbursement, the M&E system is designed to support adaptive Program management. Monitoring
information will be used by the PCU and implementing entities to identify implementation bottlenecks, adjust delivery
strategies, and strengthen coordination across sectors and levels of government.
69.      The IPF component supports targeted investments to strengthen M&E capacity, including enhancements to
improvements in data systems, analytical work, and learning activities. This includes support for periodic assessments
and analytical studies to inform Program refinement and Phase II of the MPA. The PCU will prepare periodic progress
reports summarizing performance against the Results Framework, DLI achievement status, and key implementation
issues. These reports will be shared with the Association and relevant government oversight bodies and used to support
implementation support missions and policy dialogue.

D. Capacity Building
74.      Achieving the PforR objectives under ALAFIA I requires targeted capacity building and institutional
strengthening across technical, fiduciary, and E&S systems. These efforts respond directly to constraints identified
during Program preparation and reflect lessons from previous operations, notably the importance of strong
decentralized and multi-sectoral coordination, reinforced public institutional capacity, and reduced reliance on parallel
implementation arrangements to enhance sustainability and resilience. They are designed to support effective
implementation of multi-sectoral nutrition interventions at the national and decentralized levels. Capacity-building and
systems strengthening activities are primarily supported through the IPF component, ensuring that TA, learning, and
implementation support are adequately resourced and clearly separated from results‑based incentives.

75.       Given the Program’s multi-sectoral nature, the central focus of capacity building is strengthening
coordination, convergence, and delivery capacity at the communal level. The Program supports the functionality of
communal multi-sectoral coordination platforms (CCLS‑TP) as the primary mechanism for planning, coordination, and
accountability across nutrition‑relevant sectors. Capacity-building efforts focus on clarifying institutional roles,
strengthening joint planning and coordination processes, and improving the use of monitoring information to support
local decision‑making. At the service delivery level, capacity strengthening targets frontline workers and service
platforms involved in delivering integrated nutrition, health, and ECD services. This includes strengthening competencies
related to preventive nutrition interventions (including SQ‑LNS delivery across the mother–child continuum), PNC, early
childhood stimulation, and SBCC. These efforts emphasize learning by doing, standardized tools, and supportive
supervision, rather than stand‑alone training. The Program also supports institutional capacity related to
nutrition‑sensitive food systems, including strengthening the ability of relevant ministries and agencies to engage with
microenterprises. This includes support for food safety, quality, and fortification standards, as well as institutional
arrangements to facilitate private sector engagement and strengthen local supply chains for nutritious foods.

 80.     Economic rationale. The cost-benefit analysis yields a Benefit-cost Ratio (BCR) of 3.18, a net present value of
 US$339.5 million (at 3 percent), and a cost per Disability-Adjusted Life Year (DALY) averted of approximately US$403—
 well below Benin’s ‘highly cost-effective’ threshold of approximately US$1,400 (equivalent to 1 × GDP per capita). Under
 a conservative scenario (3 percentage point stunting reduction rather than 4.5), the BCR remains positive at
 approximately 2.13, confirming robustness. Malnutrition costs countries in Sub-Saharan Africa up to 16.5 percent of GDP
 annually. In Benin, investing in the first 2,000 days is the most effective lever for translating strong GDP growth into
 inclusive poverty reduction, closing the human capital gap, and building the productive workforce of tomorrow.
 81.     Paris Alignment. The Program, including Phase 1, is aligned with both the adaptation and mitigation goals of the
 Paris Agreement and consistent with Benin’s Updated Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC, 2021) and the National
 Climate Change Adaptation Plan (2022).34 The NDC targets a 20.15 percent reduction in Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions
 relative to the business-as-usual scenario by 2030.35 The Program, including Phase 1, directly addresses CCDR
 recommendations, which emphasize the need for investing in food security for populations at risk of malnutrition,
 especially women of reproductive age and children. Furthermore, the CCDR advocates for enhancing overall health and
 well-being by reducing vulnerabilities, strengthening adaptive capacities, and improving climate resilience.36
 82.    Assessment and reduction of adaptation and mitigation risks. Benin is highly vulnerable to risks from climate
 hazards (droughts, extreme heat, floods, and storms) and Phase 1 activities are designed to reduce these risks. Under
 RAs 1 and 2, to protect micronutrient supplements and other supplies (deworming tablets, Vitamin A, iron, folic acid)
 from damage by floods and extreme heat, water-resilient and temperature-regulated storage (proper ventilation,
 elevated platforms) will be used. Health and nutrition commodities supported under RA1 and perishable foods supported
 under RA2 will also be stored in climate-resilient storage both in schools and households. Furthermore, CRs and skilled
 CHWs and teachers who will deliver the SBCC through community awareness, parental education, and school education
 services will be trained on the relationship between climate change and health and climate-sensitive diseases, particularly
 proper nutrition, climate hazards, and emergency response. Skilled CHWs will use this information to educate the
 Program beneficiaries on cultivation of nutritious foods and nutritious feeding, proper storage of crop harvests, and self-
 protection measures from high heat during gardening (RAs 1 and 2). On mitigation, Phase 1 interventions are universally
 aligned and will not increase GHG emissions or create persistent barriers in the transition to low-GHG emissions
 development pathways. Therefore, any adaptation and mitigation risks for Phase 1 activities are respectively reduced to
 acceptable and low levels. Future operations prepared under the Program will undertake a Paris Alignment assessment
 following relevant methods and guidance as applicable.

C. Environmental and Social
87.    An Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) was conducted, which identified the potential E&S risks
and impacts associated with the activities of the Program. It assessed the provisions and potential gaps in the GoB’s
systems related to the Program, regarding institutional capacities and performance, policies and regulations, consultation
mechanisms, and the GRM for managing the risks and impacts of the Program. The Program is assessed as having an
overall moderate E&S risk level, with a substantial risk specifically for GBV/SEA/SH. Since the Program centers on service
strengthening and training rather than large-scale physical work, direct environmental impacts are limited, but several
indirect risks have been identified. See further details in Annex 4.
88.     Environmental risks and impacts are related to the following: (a) waste management is the most prominent
environmental concern: The distribution of SQ-LNS creates a substantial risk of plastic waste accumulation, expanded
PNC services are expected to generate more biomedical waste (gloves, syringes, and so on), and agri-food processing
activities risk producing poorly managed organic residues; (b) natural resources face indirect pressure as well: Increased
water use linked to climate-smart gardens and potential overexploitation of wood energy in water-stressed areas are
flagged as concerns; (c) climate and biodiversity risks include GHG emissions from team travel for training activities and
potential destruction of plant cover during the creation of family and school gardens; and (d) pollution from pesticide
use in gardens and uncontrolled agri-food processing practices is also noted.
89.    Social risks and impacts are related to the following: (a) GBV/SEA/SH risk is rated as Substantial, arising from
community activities, school interactions, and distribution networks; (b) equity and inclusion risks are significant:
Isolated, very poor, or geographically hard-to-reach households risk being excluded from Program benefits, and women
face additional barriers due to cultural, linguistic, and geographic constraints; (c) governance and local capture risks
include favoritism and conflicts of interest in beneficiary selection, as well as potential tensions in agri-food value chains
and over the management of school gardens, and vulnerable groups including persons with disabilities and minorities
may be marginalized within governance structures such as the CCLS-TP bodies; and (d) labor and safety concerns center
on the increased workload and high turnover of CHWs and CRs, as well as physical safety risks for community workers
(for example, motorcycle accidents during fieldwork).
90.   ESSA concluded that the E&S management of risks and impacts associated with the Program could be
implemented within the framework of legislation and regulations, as well as by national institutions. ANAN is the main

93.     For the IPF component, the TA finances analytical work, policy/regulatory support, and capacity building with no

Standards (ESS) 1 applies for proportional risk management across the TA. Key relevant standards and risks include ESS
2 for direct and contracted workers, addressing fair terms, nondiscrimination, Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) for

engagement is embedded throughout the Program through multiple channels. CRs and skilled CHWs serve as the primary
interface between the health system and communities, facilitating two-way communication and mobilizing demand for
services. Community feedback will be systematically captured through beneficiary satisfaction surveys, complemented
by a Program-specific GRM that provides communities with a structured avenue to raise concerns. To close the feedback
loop, communities will be informed of how their inputs and perspectives have been considered and reflected in Program
implementation.
94.    All TA activities will be subject to the application of ESS 1 as well as the guidance provided in the Operations

Commitment Plan was prepared for the IPF/TA, which includes measures for stakeholder engagement and addresses
potential labor risks associated with the recruitment of consultants. The IPF component will be implemented by ANAN.

required for ANAN, ABE, and other relevant agencies and implementing partners to effectively manage E&S risks and
impacts under the Program, which addresses the sensitive domain of nutrition.

   V.    RISK

 96.   The overall Program risk and Phase 1 risk is Substantial. Given the Program’s design and reliance on country
 systems, the most significant risks relate to technical and institutional capacity, fiduciary management, and security risks.

 97.     Political and Governance Risks are assessed as Substantial. Benin remains politically stable, although this stability
 is fragile, as underscored by the recent coup attempt in December 2025. Benin faces heightened risks from regional
 insecurity and spillovers from crises affecting the Sahel, especially in the northern border areas. Benin has the highest
 number of attacks and fatalities among Gulf of Guinea states since violent groups reach coastal states in 2019. Domestic
 political uncertainties, institutional instability, and the volatility of international funding can affect program
 implementation. The establishment of ANAN leading multisectoral coordination is a positive step but requires continued
 strengthening. Mitigation measures include having the Program support strategies to anticipate exogenous shocks,
 reinforce sector coordination, and ensure that political decisions consider nutrition impacts. Enhanced stakeholder
 engagement and capacity building for ANAN and local authorities will be prioritized. Other mitigation measures include
 the GoB’s PREP, which is making progress in addressing regional disparities and structural drivers of fragility, alongside
 continued efforts to reinforce institutional capacities and maintain social cohesion in vulnerable areas.
 98.     Macroeconomic Risks are assessed as Substantial. Benin’s macroeconomic position in 2025 is robust, with GDP
 growth in 2025 of 8.1 percent and inflation contained at 1.1 percent in 2025. The fiscal deficit is below the WAEMU target
 of 3 percent by 2027 (2.9 percent in 2025), and public debt is estimated at 56.5 percent of GDP in 2025.37 However, risks
 remain due to regional instability, climate shocks, trade disruptions, and the high dependence on external financing for
 nutrition programs. Public health and nutrition expenditures remain below international targets, and food insecurity
 persists, with 6.9 percent of the population facing critical levels or worse of food insecurity as of November 2025.38 Some
 mitigation measures include improved revenue collection and efforts to increase efficiency in public expenditure. The
 Program will support measures to strengthen the financial sustainability of the nutrition sectors, including resource
 mobilization for ANAN and the implementation of the SNAN.
 99.     Technical and Institutional Capacity Risks are assessed as Substantial. The Program’s design combines features
 that individually would each warrant careful risk management and in combination represent significant complexity: a 10-
 year MPA structure; a hybrid PforR/IPF instrument; six DLIs spanning two RAs across health, WASH, ECD, agriculture, and
 livelihoods; the operationalization of a newly established PCU in ANAN; and a learning agenda with multiple pilots whose
 results must inform Phase II design within tight timelines. At the institutional level, while the national-level policy and
 coordination arrangements are well established, implementation capacity at the decentralized levels varies, particularly
 with respect to results monitoring, inter-sectoral coordination, and adaptive management, as evidenced by prior
 operations where commune-level coordination platforms underperformed in the absence of sustained technical support.
 Several design features deliberately mitigate these risks: A phased geographic rollout concentrates implementation in a
 manageable set of communes before national scale-up; DLIs are structured to allow recalibration based on

movements that could delay DLI achievement and disbursement. Mitigation measures include a Security Management
Plan to be developed before activities commence in affected communes; reliance on community-based delivery
mechanisms (CRs, CHWs, CCLS-TP platforms) less exposed to access constraints; flexible IVA verification arrangements
allowing use of remote and administrative data sources when field access is limited; pre-positioning of nutrition
commodities in high-risk communes; and close coordination with the COSO Project (P175043) and other partners
operating in the same areas. Early warning systems supported under the IPF component will track security-related
deteriorations alongside nutrition indicators to enable adaptive responses.

                                                 ANNEX 2. SUMMARY TECHNICAL ASSESSMENT39

Country Context and Problem Analysis

1.        Benin faces a deepening and multidimensional nutrition crisis. Stunting among children under five increased from
34.0 percent in 2014 to 36.5 percent in 2022— above the Sub-Saharan African average of 32 percent — while anemia
affects 72 percent of children aged 6–59 months and 58 percent of women of reproductive age. 40 A child born in Benin
today is estimated to reach only 40 percent of their full productive potential,41 and undernutrition costs the country up to
16.5 percent of GDP42 annually. These figures reflect a structural failure across multiple dimensions: only 42 percent of
infants are exclusively breastfed; only 15 percent of children aged 6–23 months have an acceptable diet; 47 percent of
the population practices open defecation; and 36 percent rely on unsafe water sources, generating chronic enteric
infections that prevent adequate nutrient absorption.43 Benin also faces a triple burden of malnutrition, with rising obesity
(27.6 percent) and hypertension (19.2 percent) coexisting with persistent undernutrition.44 Gender-specific barriers
further compound the problem: limited female decision-making autonomy, social norms restricting postpartum mobility,
and inadequate access to health services — particularly in rural and northern areas — undermine women's and children's
utilization of essential nutrition and health services.45

Technical Soundness of the Intervention Package
2.       The Program design is grounded in the WBG Investment Framework for Nutrition 202446 and draws on
international evidence from Indonesia, Peru, Brazil, Senegal, and Rwanda, all of which demonstrate that sustained
reductions in stunting require integrated, multisectoral approaches linking health, WASH, and food systems. The Program
is structured around two Results Areas. RA1 delivers a high-impact continuum of care across the first 2,000 days through
community platforms and primary health care facilities, encompassing ANC and PNC strengthening, free provision of SQ-
LNS for children aged 6–23 months and pregnant and breastfeeding women, IYCF promotion, micronutrient
supplementation, GBV screening and referral embedded in ANC visits, and financial protection for pregnant and
postpartum women. The selection of SQ-LNS is grounded in the strongest available evidence for reducing stunting and
anemia in this age group, and its free provision addresses a key access barrier. PNC is targeted because it remains severely
underutilized — a critical missed contact point for delivering integrated maternal and newborn interventions. RA2 targets
food availability and dietary diversity through climate-resilient household and school gardens, small livestock promotion,
biofortified crop cultivation, and business development support for women- and youth-led agri-food microenterprises,
addressing the supply-side drivers of dietary inadequacy.

Delivery Platforms and System Readiness
3.      A central design decision is the shift from NGO-led delivery — which characterized prior operations such as
EYNCDP 2019-2024— to institutionalized delivery through commune-level government structures (CCLS-TP platforms).

Institutional Capacity and Fiduciary Risk
4.       The fiduciary risk is rated Substantial, primarily because ANAN — the designated PCU operating under the
Presidency — lacks prior experience with the PforR instrument and operates under private law accounting rules that
require alignment with public accountability standards. ANSSP faces staffing and fiduciary capacity shortfalls, particularly
in high-fragility northern communes in Atacora and Alibori where insecurity further limits supervision and oversight. These
risks are mitigated through the PAP, which mandates the designation of dedicated financial officers across ANAN, ANSSP,
and MAEP, the establishment of a PCU with World Bank-experienced staff, procurement compliance with the Bank's
debarment requirements, and a structured external audit framework. An IVA will provide additional assurance over DLI
achievement.

Results Framework and DLI Rationale
5.      The six DLIs are designed to incentivize verified delivery rather than inputs alone. DLI 1 tracks the functionality of
CCLS-TP multisectoral platforms, directly incentivizing the institutional transition at the commune level. DLI 2 tracks
beneficiaries newly reached with SQ-LNS across the three target groups. DLI 3 tracks PNC utilization within six weeks of
delivery. DLI 4 tracks CHWs and CRs trained and actively delivering services — the "actively delivering" criterion being a
deliberate design choice to go beyond training counts and capture actual service reach. DLIs 5 and 6 track the adoption of
improved practices by agri-food microenterprises and the establishment of climate-resilient gardens and livestock systems
at the household- and school-level respectively. The ten-year MPA horizon is technically justified by the time-lagged nature
of nutrition outcomes: linear growth changes and human capital gains cannot be credibly measured within a standard
three-to-five-year project cycle.

Economic Justification
6.      The Program generates strong value for money, with a Benefit-Cost Ratio of 3.18 — every dollar invested yields
approximately US$3.18 in health-related economic benefits — and a cost-effectiveness of US$403 per DALY averted, well
below Benin's highly cost-effective threshold of approximately US$1,400. The Program is projected to generate
approximately 68,000 more and better jobs, spanning direct employment during implementation, long-term labor income
gains for women and children, and broader economic spillover effects. These figures underscore the cost-effectiveness of
investing in nutrition at scale, given the magnitude of human capital losses attributable to undernutrition in Benin.

Mitigation Measures and Program Action Plan
9.       The PAP includes a structured set of legally binding mitigation measures. Each implementing agency is required
to designate dedicated and qualified financial officers, with ANAN consolidating program financial reports. ANAN will
configure its SUCCESS PRO accounting system to record program transactions and will open two Designated Accounts at
the BCEAO — one for IDA and one for GFF — replenished through Statements of Expenditure. Semiannual unaudited IFRs
will be submitted to the World Bank within 45 days of each semester end. Annual consolidated program financial
statements will be audited by Commissaires aux Comptes with the Court of Accounts participating under an existing MOU,
submitted within nine months of fiscal year-end. Quarterly and biannual internal and external audit cycles respectively
are required, with annual procurement audits included in the external auditor's terms of reference. Mandatory debarment
list checks are required prior to any contract award. Semiannual fraud and corruption reports will be submitted to the
World Bank by the HCPC. The PAP also requires gradual increases in government budget allocations aligned with annual
work plans, and the IPF component provides targeted TA — including nutrition budget tagging and procurement capacity
building — to address systemic gaps across implementing agencies.

Disbursement Arrangements and High-Value Contract Exclusions
10.     World Bank disbursements of US$140 million (IDA) and US$15 million (GFF) will be made against verified DLI
achievement by the IVA. The GoB may request advances of up to 25 percent of IDA financing (US$35 million) and 11.2
percent of GFF financing (US$1.68 million), with combined ceilings for prior results and advances not to exceed 30 percent
of financing per source. Advances will be deducted upon DLI achievement. All withdrawal applications will be submitted
by ANAN. High-value contracts exceeding OPRC thresholds are explicitly excluded from the PforR, and no such contracts
are anticipated.

Conclusion
11.     The FSA concludes that, with the proposed mitigation measures and PAP actions in place, the fiduciary systems of
the four implementing entities provide reasonable assurance that Program funds will be used for their intended purposes
with due attention to economy, efficiency, transparency, and accountability. The Substantial fiduciary risk rating does not
disqualify the Program but requires sustained and active oversight — particularly during the early implementation period
— as ANAN and the broader implementation architecture develop their operational track record under the PforR
instrument.

                            ANNEX 4. SUMMARY ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL SYSTEMS ASSESSMENT48

Purpose, Scope, and Risk Ratings
1.      The ESSA was conducted in accordance with the World Bank's PforR policy to assess the extent to which Benin's
national E&S systems are consistent with the World Bank's six Core Principles.49 The assessment covered all four
implementing entities — ANAN, ANSSP, MAEP, and CNLS-TP — and examined the legal, institutional, and operational
dimensions of E&S risk management across the Program's two RAs. The overall E&S risk is rated Moderate, reflecting the
Program's focus on community-level interventions, nutrition services, and small-scale agriculture. However, the risk
associated with GBV/SEA/SH is rated Substantial, given the nature of community-level interactions, beneficiary-worker
dynamics, and the distribution of inputs to women and girls.

Environmental Risks and Assessment Findings
2.       The assessment identifies three primary categories of environmental risk. The most significant is the accumulation
of plastic waste from SQ-LNS packaging — given the scale of distribution across 46 communes and large numbers of
beneficiaries — alongside organic and plastic waste generated by agri-food processing activities under RA2. This risk is
compounded by the absence of a formalized waste management plan and uneven compliance with national public hygiene
standards at the facility and community level. A second risk category relates to small-scale agricultural activities, including
household and school gardens and small livestock systems, which may generate organic waste, place pressure on local
water and wood resources, and create localized biodiversity impacts, particularly through unguided pesticide use. Third,
poor biomedical waste management in health centers — including gloves, syringes, and other consumables associated
with nutrition and health service delivery — poses contamination risks at facility level, particularly outside urban centers
where waste segregation and disposal infrastructure is weakest. The assessment finds that these environmental risks are
manageable and proportionate to the Program's scope, provided that simplified waste management plans are developed
and operationalized as a condition of Program implementation.

Social Risks and Assessment Findings
3.      The most significant social risk is GBV/SEA/SH. Community-level interactions — including patient-health worker
contact during ANC and PNC visits, distribution of nutrition inputs to women and girls, and engagement with skilled CHWs
and CRs — carry a substantial potential for exploitation and abuse, particularly given power asymmetries and the absence
of formalized safeguard protocols. At the time of assessment, ANAN had not yet adopted a formal Code of Conduct for
partners and service providers, and confidential referral pathways for survivors were not yet in place.

4.      A second social risk is exclusion and inequity in access to Program benefits. Isolated rural populations, persons
with disabilities, ethnic minorities, and illiterate households face structural barriers to participation in community
platforms and benefit distribution mechanisms. The assessment finds that the Program's targeting mechanisms and CCLS-
TP platforms require explicit inclusion criteria and accessible GRM channels to prevent marginalization of the most
vulnerable. Third, the governance of CCLS-TP platforms at the commune level carries a moderate risk of elite capture,
whereby influential local actors benefit disproportionately from Program resources. This risk is to be mitigated through
revised communal by-laws requiring women and persons with disability representation quotas, transparency measures,
and strengthened citizen engagement. Fourth, CHWs and CRs face OHS risks, particularly road accidents from motorcycle
use during outreach activities and exposure to biomedical waste. Structured OHS training will be required.

Legal Framework and Core Principles Alignment
5.       Benin's national E&S legal framework is broadly sound and aligned with the World Bank's PforR Core Principles.
Key instruments include the 1990 Constitution guaranteeing the right to a healthy environment and gender equality, the
Environmental Framework Law (Law 98-030) governing environmental assessments, and the 2021 Law on the Repression
of Gender-Based Offenses, which strengthens legal protections against GBV. The Program is assessed as consistent with
Core Principles 1 (general E&S management), 2 (natural habitats and physical cultural resources), 3 (public and worker
safety), and 5 (social conflict and GBV). Core Principle 4 (involuntary land acquisition and resettlement) is not applicable,
as the Program explicitly excludes activities requiring resettlement or new land acquisition. Core Principle 6 on social
conflict is particularly relevant given the insecurity in Atacora and Alibori in the north, which requires adapted safety
planning for supervision missions in these departments.

Institutional Gaps and Capacity Constraints
6.       The most critical institutional gap identified is ANAN's absence of dedicated E&S specialists and a formalized ESMS
at the time of assessment. ANAN's procedures manual does not yet account for E&S aspects, and its code of conduct had
not been finalized at the time of the assessment. E&S monitoring capacity at the communal level is variable and generally
insufficient, with limited technical capacity for managing organic residues and inadequate personnel trained in OHS. The
GRM is nonexistent or nonfunctional in many decentralized areas, and toll-free grievance reporting lines are not
consistently operational. A further structural risk identified is the over-reliance on NGO-led monitoring in prior operations
such as EYNCDP 2019-2024: when NGO contracts lapsed, E&S monitoring and service continuity broke down at the
community level. The Program's shift to government-anchored delivery mitigates this risk over time but requires parallel
and urgent investment in institutional E&S capacity.

Program Exclusions
7.       In accordance with World Bank PforR policy, the following high-risk activities are explicitly excluded from Program
financing and must be reflected in the POM: activities involving significant involuntary land acquisition or forced evictions;
activities causing significant conversion or degradation of critical natural habitats or cultural heritage sites; working
conditions exposing workers to significant health and safety risks; activities involving forced or child labor; and direct
financing of foodstuff purchases for the PNASI.

PAP Actions and Mitigation Measures
8.       The most critical near-term PAP actions are the recruitment of two dedicated E&S specialists (one environmental,
one social) at ANAN; the development of a formal E&S Management Manual and activity screening sheets; the
operationalization of the GRM including a dedicated, confidential GBV/SEA/SH channel and a functional toll-free number;
and the formalization of a Code of Conduct for all Program partners and service providers. Within twelve months of
effectiveness, simplified waste management plans covering plastic, biomedical, and organic waste must be developed. A
formal cooperation agreement between ANAN and the ABE is mandated to ensure regulatory oversight. Capacity building
for sector actors on PforR fundamentals, exclusion criteria, and OHS practices is required throughout implementation. The
IPF component provides targeted TA to finance the E&S capacity-building activities that the PforR results structure cannot
directly fund.
Conclusion
9.       The ESSA concludes that the E&S risks of the ALAFIA I Program are manageable, provided that PAP actions are
implemented in a timely and effective manner. The most urgent priorities are the recruitment of E&S specialists, the
operationalization of a GBV-sensitive GRM, and the development of the ESMS. ANAN's institutional strengthening in these
areas is central to the Program's long-term social compliance and sustainability.

                                                                 ANNEX 6. CLIMATE CONTENT

1.       The Program has been screened for short- and long-term climate disasters and risks and has been found to be
highly exposed, while the potential risk to Program activities is moderate. Between 1982 and 2022, Benin experienced
20 major floods (3.3 million people affected) and 1 severe drought (2.1 million people affected).50 Those events are
expected to increase in frequency and intensity in Benin because of the result of climate change.51 The country is highly
exposed to rising temperatures, increasing rainfall variability (including floods and droughts), sea level rise, and heat
stress, with impacts most pronounced in northern regions where poverty, food insecurity, and malnutrition are already
concentrated. Temperatures have increased by 0.78°C between 1971 and 2020 and are expected to increase by 0.28°C
per decade by mid-century, with higher impacts in the northern regions.52 Seasonal shifts and climate shocks directly affect
agricultural productivity, food availability, and household incomes, while also placing additional pressure on health,
nutrition, and social protection systems.53 For instance, climate-induced harvest failures and prolonged lean seasons are
closely associated with acute spikes in wasting among children under five, while repeated shocks over time, which deplete
household coping strategies, reduce dietary diversity, and increase food insecurity, also entrench stunting, reinforcing
intergenerational cycles of malnutrition. Benin ranks 150 out of 187 on climate vulnerability and readiness, in part due to
low scores in projected change of cereal yields and agricultural capacity,54 reflecting how deeply climate change is
undermining the food and nutrition security of its population.
2. Climate change is a main driver of the rise of malnutrition in Benin, disproportionately affecting women and children.
With 70 percent of its population dependent on agriculture, Benin faces a severe and growing threat to food security as
climate change intensifies the frequency and impact of floods and droughts.55 Rising temperatures, reduced rainfall, and
more frequent extreme events lower soil fertility, reduce crop yields, destroy harvests, and worsen food availability.56,57
Climate change also compromises food safety and storage in Benin due to inadequate temperature regulation, and flood-
related supply disruptions.58,59 In 2022–2023, flood and heat disruptions to cotton production led to fresh food inflation.60
Women working in agriculture are more vulnerable to climate change than men due to differences in access to land and
education.61 Climate change is a direct contributor for the degradation of nutritional outcomes in Benin, with stunting
affecting one-third of children under five and anemia over half of the women.62,63,64 In West Africa, a 2°C rise in
temperature is estimated to increase stunting prevalence among children by 7.4 percent.65

3. Climate change also poses significant threats to health and the health system in Benin. It poses a risk to mangroves,
which serve as a natural barrier against vector-borne and waterborne disease outbreaks (malaria, diarrheal diseases
including cholera).66,67 Evidence increasingly shows that tuberculosis and HIV/AIDS are climate-sensitive, as their
transmission pathways are directly influenced by environmental changes.68,69 In Benin, where hemorrhage and
hypertensive disorders are already leading causes of maternal death, climate change compounds these risks by
intensifying heat exposure during pregnancy — worsening physiological stress and conditions such as preeclampsia, and
contributing to poor neonatal outcomes.70,71 A multi-country study including Benin found that extreme heat in the final
week before birth raises the risk of perinatal death by 34 percent, underscoring the urgent need for strengthened post-
natal care in the face of a changing climate.72 As extreme weather events threaten electricity supply and the vaccine cold
chain, climate change disrupts the capacity of the health care system in Benin, undermining its capacity to respond to that
additional burden of disease.73 The project intends to implement measures to adapt to the impacts of climate change in
Benin while employing measures to mitigate GHG emissions as outlined below.74

                                  ANNEX 7. INVESTMENT PROJECT FINANCING COMPONENT

1.       The IPF component provides targeted TA to drive the institutional reforms and innovations that the PforR results
cannot finance directly but that are nonetheless essential to its success. The IPF does not finance service delivery—that
is the domain of the PforR—but rather the upstream capacity, systems, and governance structures that make the PforR’s
results-based investments feasible, verifiable, and sustainable beyond the Program cycle. Its design responds to a
fundamental lesson from prior operations, that programmatic gains achieved through project-financed activities erode
quickly when the institutions responsible for sustaining them at both the central and local levels lack the governance
structures, systems, and capacities to do so independently. To that end, the IPF will prioritize five mutually reinforcing
areas.

2.       First, and most foundationally, it will invest in decentralized governance as the primary vehicle for sustainable
nutrition service delivery. This means financing TA to communal administrations, CCLS-TP platforms, and decentralized
sector services—including facilitation tools, training for communal administrators, and the development of nutrition-
inclusive planning and accountability templates—that create the operational foundations for local government to plan,
budget, coordinate, and oversee nutrition and health interventions as a core function, not as a project activity. Support will
cover the development of commune-level multi-sectoral nutrition plans, integration of nutrition targets into communal
development plans and budgets, and the progressive transfer of coordination and accountability functions from central
structures and NGO intermediaries to local institutions. This directly addresses the sustainability gap identified in prior
operations, where the absence of institutionalized local ownership meant that results were tied to the lifespan of external
partners rather than to the strength of local systems.

3.      Second, the IPF will strengthen central-level multi-sectoral coordination to ensure that national policy, financing,
and technical guidance effectively support and do not undermine decentralized implementation. This includes structured,
regular engagement across sector ministries tracked through annual and quarterly coordination meetings, as well as
alignment of sector-specific plans with the SNAN’s objectives and timelines.

4.        Third, it will build the financial stewardship and accountability systems necessary for sustainable nutrition financing
at all levels, including training staff across sector ministries in nutrition budget tagging (including specific tagging for climate
and nutrition expenses), evidence-based advocacy for domestic resource mobilization, and climate-responsive multi-
sectoral operational planning from the central to the communal level.

5.      Fourth, the IPF will drive the digitalization of M&E systems to enable real-time tracking of results across
decentralized implementation sites, support operational research, and generate the evidence base needed to adapt and
scale what works. As part of this, it will support the design and operationalization of early warning and response systems
to detect and address deteriorations in food and nutrition security—including acute malnutrition crises and supply
disruptions in the event of climate-related shocks—before they escalate.

6.       Fifth, the IPF will strengthen supply chains for last-mile delivery of essential nutrition and health commodities, with
particular attention to sexual and reproductive health products, including contraceptives, micronutrient supplements for
women of reproductive age, and maternal health commodities, where supply chain fragility at the subnational level
frequently undermines service continuity. The IPF will finance TA to map bottlenecks, strengthen logistics MISs, and build
the procurement and distribution capacities of decentralized health structures to ensure reliable, quality commodity
availability at the community level.

7.      These five areas map directly onto the Program’s two RAs. In support of RA1, the IPF finances the development
and harmonization of training curricula and SBCC tools for frontline workers—including climate-sensitive modules
integrated into nutrition and health protocols—alongside the digitalized M&E and beneficiary tracking systems required to
verify DLI progress and generate real-time management information and the supply chain strengthening that reduces the
commodity stockouts that have historically undermined service continuity. In support of RA2, the IPF finances market
readiness assessments to identify and profile women- and youth-led agri-food MSMEs eligible for Program support,
providing the evidence base for targeting and sequencing capacity-building investments. It supports IFC advisory services
to these enterprises, covering business development, food safety compliance pathways, and linkages to buyers and input
suppliers. It operationalizes digital sourcing platforms connecting schools to reliable supplies of nutritious fresh foods and
strengthens the market information and aggregation systems underpinning last-mile distribution of nutritious and fortified
foods, ensuring that RA2 investments are embedded in governance structures that endure beyond the Program timeline.

8.       Together, the PforR and IPF instruments are deliberately sequenced: IPF-financed TA and systems development
create the enabling conditions—governance, capacity, and data—that the PforR’s results-based financing then incentivizes,
verifies, and scales. Across both RAs, the IPF also finances nutrition budget tagging and domestic resource mobilization
support to strengthen the Government’s capacity to sustain financing for the SNAN from its own resources over time,
reducing dependence on external financing as the Program matures.
