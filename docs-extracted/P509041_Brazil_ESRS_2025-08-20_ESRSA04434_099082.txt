                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     Tocantins, Brazil’s youngest state, spans 277,620 km² and is home to 1.6 million people. Despite strong growth in
                     agribusiness and transport connectivity, the state faces persistent regional disparities, rural poverty, limited productivity
                     in family farming, and increasing vulnerability to climate change. Over half of its municipalities are classified as highly
                     vulnerable, and infrastructure deficits contribute to economic exclusion and rural-to-urban migration. The paved state
                     road network (5,841 km), which connects agricultural production zones to the BR-153 highway and the North-South
                     Railway, suffers from chronic maintenance gaps, safety risks, and climate-related degradation. Tocantins is seeking to
                     modernize road governance through long-term performance-based maintenance contracts (CREMAs and PPPs),

                     supported by institutional reforms and targeted investments in road safety and resilience. Family agriculture represents
                     over 70% of farms in Tocantins but contributes only 17% of agricultural output, constrained by low productivity, limited
                     access to markets and finance, and weak farmer organizations. Public extension services are under-resourced, and
                     climate-resilient practices are not widely adopted. Meanwhile, tourism—especially in the Jalapão region—holds
                     untapped potential to diversify rural incomes and promote local development but remains underdeveloped and highly
                     seasonal. The Tocantins Productive, Resilient, and Inclusive Development Project will promote (i) sustainable, safe, and
                     resilient transport infrastructure; (ii) improved livelihoods and climate resilience for family farmers; and (iii) sustainable
                     tourism in priority regions. The project adopts a multisectoral approach to reduce structural inequality and enhance
                     climate adaptation, while strengthening institutional capacity and leveraging private sector participation. Component 1:
                     Proactive maintenance, resilient and safe road infrastructure • Subcomponent 1.1: Climate-resilient road improvement,
                     rehabilitation, and maintenance through CREMA and PPP • Subcomponent 1.2: Road safety management capacity and
                     infrastructure • Subcomponent 1.3: Institutional strengthening of AGETO Component 2: Strengthening support to family
                     agriculture • Subcomponent 2.1: Rural services for climate-smart and inclusive agriculture • Subcomponent 2.2: Direct
                     productive support to family farmers • Subcomponent 2.3: Strengthening family farming organizations Component 3:
                     Inclusive and sustainable tourism • Subcomponent 3.1: Strengthening local tourism governance • Subcomponent 3.2:
                     Support to tourism enterprises and Quilombola communities • Subcomponent 3.3: Investments in tourism-related
                     public goods Component 4: Project management and implementation support


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     This is a state-wide project. The State of Tocantins is located in the northern region of Brazil. It has borders with the



Public Disclosure
                     states of Bahia and Piauí to the east, Maranhão to the north, Goiás and Mato Grosso to the south and southwest, and
                     Pará to the west. It has an extension of 277,423.627 km², representing 3.26% of the national territory.
                     The state is part of the Legal Amazon Region, although only a small part of its territory is within the Amazon biome.
                     Most of the state (87%) is within the Cerrado biome. The Cerrado has a diverse fauna and flora that can be affected,
                     severely, by environmental changes, such as fires, fragmentation, replacement of native vegetation by monocultures,
                     among others, disturbances.
                     Although around 15% of the State territory is covered by conservation units, agricultural yields, infrastructure durability,
                     and the appeal of ecotourism destinations are already under pressure. Nearly 30 percent of the state’s native
                     vegetation has been lost since 1985, with land degradation and deforestation contributing to rising greenhouse gas
                     emissions. The protected areas located at Tocantins include ten environmental protection areas (APAs) (IUCN Category
                     V), five Parks (IUCN Category II), one Ecological Station (IUCN Category Ia), and two natural monuments (IUCN Category
                     III).
                     Two regions deserve special attention with regards to environmental features, the Bananal Island and the Jalapão
                     Region. The Bananal Island is the largest fluvial island in the world (23,000 sq.km.), encompasses wetlands of
                     international importance and includes the Bananal APA and the Cantão State Park.
                     The Jalapão region is a semi-desert region, formed by mountains, plateaus and isolated hills that mixes sand dunes in
                     the middle of the Cerrado, including water springs, lagoons, fields of flowers and Golden Grass. The Jalapão region has
                     unique environmental and sociocultural characteristics and is part of the Jalapão Region Ecological Corridor Project,
                     which covers 17 municipalities located between the states of Bahia, Maranhão, Piauí and Tocantins, making a total area
                     of 81,546.93km². The Ecological Corridor covers nine conservation units, three of which are federal, (including the
                     Ecological Station of Serra Geral do Tocantins); two state and one municipal in Tocantins (Jalapão Park, Jalapão APA and
                     Corredeiras Natural Monument/Rio do Sono Canyon); and one Private Natural Protection Reserve (RPPN) in Tocantins,

                     the Jalapão Cathedral. It comprises 10 municipalities in the Tocantins State: Almas, Lagoa do Tocantins, Lizarda,
                     Mateiros, Novo Acordo, Ponte Alta do Tocantins, Rio da Conceição, Rio Sono, Santa Tereza do Tocantins and São Félix

                     The objective of the Jalapão Ecological Corridor is to reinforce the conservation of the ecosystems of the Jalapão region
                     through the integration between federal, state, municipal and private conservation units and the community, with
                     strategic actions to maintain or reestablish ecological connectivity between protected areas and preserve the
                     ecosystem services provided by nature to the population of Jalapão. The Ecological Corridor is an instrument for
                     territorial planning, defined by the National System of Nature Conservation Units – SNUC (Law 9.985 of July 18, 2000).
                     The state has a population of 1.5 million people and its HDI level remains below the national average (0.731 vs. 0.766).
                     Rural poverty remains persistent and lagging regions face persistent infrastructure, service, and opportunity deficits,
                     especially among family farmers and vulnerable groups like women and traditional communities. Although rural-to-
                     urban migration reduced Tocantins’ rural population share from 25.7 to 14.6 percent between 2000 and 2022, nearly
                     300,000 people live in rural areas, with over 40 percent relying on conditional cash-transfer programs to meet basic
                     needs
                     The state has emerged as one of Brazil’s fastest-growing subnational economies and its agricultural sector has expanded
                     rapidly over the past decade with agriculture representing 23.7 of the state’s gross value-added in 2022, as the state
                     benefits from 13.8 million hectares of arable land. Nevertheless, its gains remain highly uneven as these benefits have
                     disproportionately accrued to large-scale commercial farms, deepening structural inequalities while placing increased
                     pressure on the Cerrado ecosystem. Family farming remained largely excluded from these gains as nearly 80% percent
                     of the nearly 45,000 family farmers have limited access to output markets and product certifications, only 10% of
                     farmers receive extension services, and fewer than one in five access formal credit.

                     Holding 19% of the area under agriculture, family farming accounts for 70% of the farms in the state, 40% of the state’s
                     agricultural production and more than 60% of agricultural employment but just. Women comprise only 18% of the
                     holders of family farms. Its productivity remains low due to limited access to credit, inputs, markets, and technical
                     assistance. Family farming is especially vulnerable to climate change impacts that threaten to exacerbate poverty and
                     exclusion, given its dependence on fragile ecosystems and climate-sensitive livelihoods and their limited coping and
                     adaptive capacity.
                     Gender disparities significantly affect Tocantins' agriculture, transport and tourism sectors, particularly disadvantaging
                     women family farmers, who account for only 18% of the holders of family farms and face still lower access to productive
                     inputs, credit, and land compared to men, as well as insufficient access to information, technical assistance, and
                     extension services. Social norms and the unequal division of domestic labor further restrict women’s mobility,
                     autonomy, and agency, limiting their participation in producer organizations and access to markets. Moreover,
                     pervasive domestic violence undermines women’s well-being and agency, exacerbating their economic vulnerability.
                     It is estimated that the interventions in the road, agriculture and tourism sectors will create thousands of direct and
                     indirect jobs. Approximately over half of the population will benefit from road rehabilitation and maintenance works in
                     the North and East of the State, which will improve conditions on approximately 15% of the state-managed paved road
                     network, enhancing access to markets, public services, and economic opportunities for rural residents while also
                     reducing transport costs and travel times. Component 2 will benefit around 28% of the family farmers in the state, who
                     will receive technical assistance and support to adopt climate-smart agricultural practices. This component may directly
                     benefit communities of nine Indigenous Peoples who lived in 10 demarcated Indigenous Lands (a total area of 147,570
                     sq. km). Indigenous Peoples comprise 1.32% of the state population and 76% of them (15,213 people) live in the
                     Indigenous Lands. Components 2 may also directly benefit Quilombola communities, who occupy 42 territories at the
                     state and Component 3 will specifically target 247 Quilombola families in the Jalapão region.

                     The quilombola communities in Tocantins are fundamental to the region's cultural heritage and are increasingly
                     becoming focal points for sustainable tourism development. These communities will benefit from initiatives that
                     improve tourism infrastructure and promote cultural preservation. Investments in tourism aim to empower and equip
                     quilombola communities by providing economic opportunities. The focus on ecotourism and cultural tourism not only
                     attracts visitors but also contributes to the socio-economic development of these communities. These efforts are part of
                     a broader strategy to integrate sustainable practices into the tourism sector, ensuring the equitable sharing of tourism
                     benefits.
                     Tocantins is particularly vulnerable to climate change. An increase of 3 degrees Celsius in temperature, annual
                     precipitation decreases by 23%, and changes in rainfall patterns are projected by 2050, leading to increased biodiversity
                     loss, ecosystem degradation, and water scarcity. Climate change will also affect agricultural yields and infrastructure
                     resilience.

                      ESS8 - Cultural Heritage                                                                                        Relevant




Public Disclosure
                      Although it is not possible to determine the presence of cultural heritage in the project’s area at this stage, ESS8 is
                      considered relevant because a) construction works envisaged under Component 1 construction works involve
                      excavations, demolition, and earthmoving and b) Component 3 may promote (at least indirectly) the commercial use
                      of intangible cultural heritage held by traditional and quilombola communities. The state of Tocantins is known for
                      the richness of its archaeological heritage. Up to 2014, 862 archaeological sites (open-air lithic and ceramic sites) have
                      been registered, particularly at the Jalapão and Lajedo regions. Human occupation in these areas dates to 12,000
                      years before the present and formed archaeological sites throughout prehistory. Structures related to the
                      archaeology of the historical period can also be found, significant of the occupation of this area of contact between
                      the Amazon Rainforest and the Cerrado biome. More recently, identification missions carried by the National
                      Institute of Historical and Artistic Heritage (IPHAN) between 2022 and 2023 identified 16 new archaeological sites
                      (cave paintings comprising panels with pre-colonial rock art made by human groups probably two thousand years
                      ago) in Jalapão. It is also known by forms of expression of intangible cultural heritage, including the suça dance and
                      buriti viola of quilombola communities, the golden grass (capim dourado) crafts associated with the Xerente people
                      and the Ritxòkò ceramic dolls of the Karajá People. The Brazilian regulatory framework on the protection of cultural
                      heritage is robust and allows the achievement of outcomes materially consistent with the objectives of ESS 8. In this
                      framework, the responsibility for recognizing and protecting cultural heritage is an attribution of IPHAN. It
                      acknowledges both tangible and intangible cultural heritage and aims to avoid, minimize, or mitigate impacts on
                      cultural heritage by: • Implementing recognized practices for field-based studies, documentation, and protection of
                      cultural heritage in connection with project activities (Articles 20, 23, 215, and 21 of the Federal Constitution, Decree-
                      Law 25/1937, Law 3,924/1961, Law 9,605/1998, Law 3,551/2000, Law 11,483/2007, Law 140/2011, and Law
                      13,123/2015). • Defining the rights of traditional peoples to be consulted and stipulating the requirements for free,

                              The World Bank
                              Tocantins Productive Resilient And Inclusive Development Project (P509041)



                      prior, and informed consent (Decree 10,088/2019, ratifying ILO Convention 169). • Defining procedures for the
                      environmental licensing of projects located in or near recognized cultural heritage sites (Law 6,983/1981, Law
                      10,165/2000, Law 10,257/2001, Law 12,324/2010, CONAMA Resolution 237/1997, Interministerial Ordinance
                      60/2015, and IPHAN Normative Instruction No. 01/2015). • Following a chance finds procedure for previously
                      unknown cultural heritage encountered during works involving excavations, demolition, earth movement, flooding,
                      or other changes in the physical environment (Law 3,924/1961 and IPHAN Normative Instruction No. 01/2015).
                      Furthermore, the environmental licensing process in Brazil requires that studies necessary for Environmental
                      Licensing include aspects related to the impact assessment and protection of cultural heritage safeguarded at the
                      federal, state and municipal levels. Inter-Ministerial Ordinance 60/2015 defines a Specific Term of Reference (ToR)
                      that sets the minimum requirements related to impact assessment, based on the identification and characterization
                      of cultural heritage in areas directly influenced by the project, aiming to propose measures to control, mitigate, and
                      compensate for any damage caused to these assets. It mandates that entrepreneurs must submit to IPHAN an Impact
                      Assessment Report containing: georeferenced location of the safeguarded tangible and intangible cultural heritage;
                      characterization and evaluation of the existing tangible and intangible cultural heritage; assessment of threats or
                      impacts on the safeguarded tangible and intangible cultural heritage; measures to preserve and safeguard the
                      tangible and intangible heritage, control and mitigate impacts caused by the project; an integrated Heritage
                      Education Project. The entrepreneurs must submit an Archaeological Follow-up Report, or an Archaeological Heritage
                      Impact Assessment Report, and/or an Archaeological Heritage Potential Impact Assessment Report. All studies and
                      reports must: a) be authorized by IPHAN through the publication of an Ordinance in the Federal Official Gazette; b) be
                      coordinated and conducted by a technically qualified team led by an Archaeologist and a Field Archaeologist,
                      approved by IPHAN; c) include recommendations for future activities related to the production and dissemination of

                      scientific knowledge about cultural heritage; and d) register identified archaeological sites according to a form
                      available on IPHAN's website. IPHAN is also responsible for the indication of Custody and Research Institutions for the
                      custody and conservation of the archaeological material and has cleared and widely disseminated chance Finds
                      Procedures, that apply to projects located in altered areas and not coinciding with registered archaeological sites. It
                      mandates that private or public entrepreneurs sign a term committing to: immediately suspend works or activities in
                      the event of a chance discovery of archaeological assets; report the occurrence to IPHAN; halt work in the sections or
                      areas where the heritage is identified; await IPHAN's deliberation and pronouncement on the actions to be carried
                      out (within a maximum period of fifteen days); and be responsible for the management costs arising from the need to
                      rescue archaeological material in the case of low-risk cultural heritage developments. Finally, Federal Decree
                      3,551/2000 established the Registry of Cultural Assets of Intangible Nature and defined a program specifically aimed
                      at the preservation, recognition, and appreciation of Brazil's intangible heritage. To deal with the potential
