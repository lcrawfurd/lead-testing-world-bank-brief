 B. Introduction and Context
  Sectoral and Institutional Context
1.      The state of Haryana makes a significant contribution to India’s economy and food security. With per-capita
income of about INR 319,000 (US$3,520), Haryana ranks well above the national average. Despite covering only 1.3
percent of India’s land area, it produces over 6 percent of national food grains, supplies 11 percent of rice and wheat
under the Public Distribution System, and accounts for 40 percent of basmati rice exports. This performance is supported
by an extensive irrigation network, with 92 percent of its 3.69 million ha of cultivable land equipped for irrigation.
2.       Haryana’s scarce, largely inter-state water resources are under growing water stress, threatening agricultural
output and productivity. The state’s arid climate, extreme temperatures, and highly variable rainfall are compounded by
rising water demand, aging infrastructure, and inefficient irrigation practices. Surface irrigation systems, including the
Bhakra and Western Yamuna canal systems, do not consistently provide reliable supply across the state. As a result,
farmers increasingly depend on groundwater, intensifying pressure on already declining aquifers. Haryana’s bowl-shaped
topography further contributes to persistent waterlogging and salinity in central districts, limiting productivity and land
quality. Addressing these escalating water-related challenges is critical for sustaining the state’s role in national food
security.
3.      Over the past few decades, the demand-supply gap for water has steadily widened with the expansion of
irrigated agriculture, economic and urban growth, and declining rainfall reliability. In 2023, total gross water demand
was estimated at 42 billion cubic metres (bcm), of which agriculture accounted for 89 percent. In contrast, total annual
renewable water availability, drawn from surface sources, groundwater recharge, and effective rainfall, is only about 30
bcm, leaving a gap of 12 bcm. Part of this reflects unmet demand, while a significant share is met through unsustainable
groundwater extraction. With population growth, urbanization, and rising industrial needs, the deficit is expected to
deepen unless technical, institutional, and climate-adaptive reforms improve water-use efficiency and service delivery.

4.       Despite high productivity, Haryana’s agricultural strength is built on an unsustainable foundation of intensive
water use. The wheat-rice rotation dominates nearly three-fifths of farmland. While yields exceed national averages,
inefficient practices such as paddy pre-ponding and absence of irrigation charges have driven excessive water use.
Groundwater levels in several districts are declining by up to 0.5 m annually. The state has introduced incentives to
promote crop diversification toward less water-intensive crops. In parallel, it is promoting micro-irrigation (MI), but MI
coverage remains limited at about 13 percent of the cultivable command area, indicating significant untapped potential
for improving water-use efficiency.
5.       Haryana’s unsustainable water use is driven in part by an ageing and inefficient irrigation system whose
weaknesses have amplified losses and groundwater dependence. The state’s canal network spans over 14,000 km, much
of it deteriorated, resulting in high conveyance losses and uneven service delivery. Operation and maintenance allocations
remain constrained relative to system needs. Surface water supply is further affected by variability in inter-state river
allocations, with actual receipts fluctuating below allocated shares in some years. As canal reliability has declined, tubewell
irrigation has expanded significantly; groundwater now accounts for about 63 percent of irrigated area, while canal
irrigation has declined. Pump and electricity subsidies have further incentivized groundwater extraction.
6.       These structural and performance challenges are reinforced by how irrigation is financed, planned, and
managed in Haryana. Public expenditure remains skewed toward capital-intensive expansion rather than sustained
maintenance, contributing to asset deterioration and unreliable service. Planning and service delivery are fragmented
across departments, limiting convergence between surface water, groundwater, agriculture, and reuse planning. The
absence of interoperable data systems constrains basin-level / command-level water budgeting and performance
monitoring. Haryana also lacks dedicated Participatory Irrigation Management (PIM) or Water User Association (WUA)
legislation that clearly defines service delivery roles, financial responsibilities, and user contributions toward O&M.
7.       Growing dependence on groundwater has resulted in severe and worsening aquifer stress across the state. Of
143 assessment units, nearly two-thirds are classified as over-exploited or critical, and the number of over-exploited blocks
has increased sharply over the past decade. Over 60 percent of the recharge-worthy area falls under over-exploited zones.
Between 2000 and 2024, average depth to water table declined from 10.75 meters to 22.76 meters. In the short term,
this increases farmer costs through deeper wells and higher pumping requirements; in the long term, it signals a serious
sustainability risk for agriculture and water security.
8.      Waterlogging and salinity in the central districts further constrain Haryana’s agricultural potential.
Approximately 7 percent of the state’s geographical area is affected by waterlogging, with a water table within 3 meters
of ground level, and about 7 percent is moderately or highly salt-affected. Poor drainage, canal seepage, and topographic
conditions reduce soil fertility, impair aeration, and diminish crop yields, in some cases leading to land abandonment.
Addressing these challenges requires improved drainage infrastructure and promotion of salinity-tolerant crops.
9.     Climate change impacts are already amplifying Haryana’s complex water management challenges. Rising
temperatures, more frequent heatwaves, erratic monsoons, and increasing rainfall variability are intensifying
evapotranspiration, groundwater depletion, and waterlogging. Irrigated wheat and rice yields could decline by 15–17
percent by mid-century without improvements in water-use efficiency and climate-resilient practices.
10.     Recognizing the urgency of water challenges, the Government of Haryana (GoH) has elevated Integrated Water
Resources Management (IWRM) and water security as strategic priorities across its planning and institutional reforms.
The Haryana Water Resources Authority (HWRA) was established in 2020 to integrate surface and groundwater
management. In 2023, the Integrated Water Resources Action Plan (IWRAP) was issued, providing block-level diagnostics
and coordinated district planning through District Water Resources Plans for the period 2023-25. In 2025, an updated plan
for 2025-27 was issued.

11.     The IWRAP provides an important platform for advancing IWRM in Haryana by translating technical diagnostics
into coordinated action. It prioritizes investments in high-stress areas and supports canal rehabilitation, groundwater
recharge, treated wastewater (TWW) reuse, MI expansion, and climate-resilient agriculture such as direct seeded rice and
crop diversification. A state- and district-level monitoring framework tracks performance, laying the foundation for more
integrated, data-driven water resources management.

  PforR Program Scope
12.      Summary of the Government Program. The GoH’s IWRAP constitutes the state’s medium-term strategy to reduce
the water demand-availability gap and strengthen water security through successive two-year planning cycles led by the
HWRA and implemented by 11 state agencies. Since 2023, the broader government program – estimated at about US$1.5
billion – has addressed water stress across sectors through a combination of supply- and demand-side interventions,
including canal and watercourse rehabilitation, groundwater recharge, drainage improvements, treated wastewater
reuse, promotion of MI, and climate-resilient, water-efficient agricultural practices. The current IWRAP cycle (2025-27)
continues implementation of these measures across the state.
13.      Program Boundaries and Financing. The proposed PforR operation supports a focused subset of IWRAP aimed at
improving the efficiency, reliability, and sustainability of agricultural water use. It will support implementation by three
key agencies – Irrigation and Water Resources Department (IWRD), Micro Irrigation and Command Area Development
Authority (MICADA), and Department of Agriculture and Farmers Welfare (DAFW) – and will concentrate on strengthening
institutions and water governance (including WUAs), enhancing irrigation and drainage service delivery (both gravity and
pressurized systems), mobilizing non-conventional water resources, expanding MI, and promoting reduced agricultural
water demand. The Program will be implemented over six years (2026–2032) with total financing of US$749 million,
comprising US$500 million from IBRD, US$214 million from the GoH, and US$35 million in commercial financing.
14.       The Program will support the GoH through the following five Result Areas (RA):
      a. RA1: Strengthen Institutions for Planning, Management, and Financing of Sustainable Water Resources
         Management: RA1 will enhance Haryana’s institutional and financial capacity for integrated, data-driven, and
         resilient water resources management. RA1 will lay the foundational blocks for demand-site management and
         water allocation reforms by strengthening planning, monitoring and management under the State’s IWRM
         framework. RA1 will incentivize:
          (i)   statewide data and analytical systems for integrated planning, including sub-basin-level water resource
                databases, improvements to the state’s irrigation system asset inventory, and preparation and public
                disclosure of annual water budgets;
          (ii) operationalized institutional arrangements for IWRM, including enforced inter-agency roles and coordination
                mechanisms across HWRA, IWRD, and DAFW, and functioning multi-stakeholder participatory committees
                within IWRD units;
          (iii) improved planning instruments aligned with IWRM principles, including an IWRM 2047 Vision, and upgraded
                IWRAP with basin-level planning and enhanced results monitoring and feedback protocols;
          (iv) adoption of sectoral protocols and standard operating procedures (SOPs) for sustainable water management,
                including conjunctive use, TWW reuse,1 vertical and sub-surface drainage, WUA-based MI co-management,
                integration of environmental and social risk screening into project preparation and contracting, and water-
                efficient and diversified cropping systems;
          (v) development of draft bylaws for PIM through WUAs;

     b. RA2: Enhance Efficiency and Quality of Irrigation Services: RA2 will improve access to reliable, equitable, and
        climate-resilient irrigation services in up to 15 irrigation commands, ensuring that water reaches farms when and
        where it is needed. RA2 will incentivize: (i) modernization of canal systems through refurbishment of canal
        networks and water courses; (ii) deployment of supervisory control and data acquisition systems, real-time data
        acquisition systems, and digital dashboards for real-time monitoring and decision-making; (iii) operationalization
        of performance-based irrigation service delivery by private ISP(s) in selected commands; and (iv) stabilization of
        groundwater levels in selected groundwater assessment blocks through the implementation of conjunctive-use
        management, aligned with RA4 and RA5 interventions to promote sustainable MI and less water-intensive
        cropping systems.
     c. RA3: Mobilize Non-conventional Water Resources for Irrigation: RA3 will increase irrigation water supply and
        enhance drought resilience through the mobilization of non-conventional water sources. RA3 will incentivize: (i)
        creation of additional offline water storage in low-lying areas to harness monsoon surplus, excess canal water,
        and drainage water to supplement irrigation and groundwater recharge; (ii) recovery of drainage water through
        sub-surface and vertical drainage measures, augmenting irrigation supply and reclaiming waterlogged and saline
        land for productive cultivation, with the drainage systems subsequently transferred to drainage user groups for
        continued maintenance; and (iii) development of TWW reuse systems – implemented in line with the reuse
        protocols established under RA1 – by connecting sewage treatment plants, managed by Public Health Engineering
        Department and Gurugram Metropolitan Development Authority, with irrigation command areas, and integrating
        them into MICADA and district-level irrigation service delivery plans.
     d. RA4: Expand Micro-Irrigation through Farmer-managed and Sustainable O&M: RA4 will enable farmers to adopt
        efficient, solar powered, WUA-managed irrigation systems and assume greater technical and financial
        responsibility for their O&M. RA4 will incentivize: (i) scale-up of co-management models that progressively
        transfer O&M responsibility of sections of irrigation infrastructure up to farm outlets to WUAs, supported by
        performance-based partnerships with contractors; and (ii) expansion of on-farm MI (including sprinkler and drip
        systems and pilots with center pivots) through WUA clusters, supported by vendor engagement for equipment,
        AI-enabled digital cluster initiatives, and clear arrangements for O&M. These measures will improve on-farm

       e. RA5: Reduce Agricultural Water Demand: RA5 will support agricultural water demand management as a
          complement to basin- and command-level water resources management. It will promote less water-intensive and
          climate-resilient cropping systems as demand management instruments to reduce irrigation water use and relieve

15.      Program Beneficiaries and Coverage. The Program is expected to benefit approximately 4.5 million people
through a combination of statewide institutional reforms and targeted investments in selected irrigation clusters.
Statewide interventions will strengthen water governance, planning, and irrigation performance management, while
cluster-level investments will improve irrigation service reliability, expand MI, promote water-efficient cropping practices
such as direct seeded rice and crop diversification, and address drainage and waterlogging challenges. The integrated
approach is designed to generate strong demonstration effects and enable scaling of water-efficient irrigation and
agricultural practices across the state.

18.     Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA). ESSA for the Program has been prepared, in accordance
with World Bank’s PforR policy and directives. ESSA assesses the adequacy of Program’s environmental and social (E&S)
management systems, identifies key gaps with respect to PforR core E&S principles, and recommends actions to
strengthen E&S performance and impact during Program implementation. ESSA specifically examined Program’s legal and
regulatory frameworks, institutional arrangements and capacity, systems and practices and past performance in mitigating
and managing E&S risks/impacts associated with Program activities. The assessment relied on: (i) desk review of
regulatory, institutional and operational documents; (ii) in-depth discussions with officials of IWRD, MICADA, DAFW,
HWRA, and other related agencies; (iii) field visits to proposed Program areas; and (iv) stakeholder consultations with
WUAs, farmers, elected representatives and contractors in Sonipat, Karnal, Kurukshetra, Rohtak, and Jhajjar districts.
19.      Benefits. The Program is expected to deliver significant positive E&S benefits. Rehabilitating and modernizing
canals and watercourses will reduce water losses and groundwater extraction and improve access and reliability of
irrigation services. Reclaiming waterlogged/saline lands will reduce salinity impacts, restore soil productivity and bring
back underutilized agriculture areas into cultivation. Expanding micro-irrigation will generate water and financial savings
for farmers with lower labor and operating costs. Reuse of TWW, with suitable safeguards, will augment water availability
and farm productivity in deficit areas and reduce dependence on freshwater sources. Demand-side measures such as crop
diversification and DSR will reduce agricultural water demand and support broader adoption of climate-resilient farming
practices. Strengthening the institutional capacity of IWRD, MICADA, and DAFW as well as empowering WUAs/drainage
societies and farmers is expected to make water governance and agriculture service delivery more inclusive, participatory,
and transparent with more stable farm incomes and resilient livelihoods.
20.     Risks. The E&S risks of the Program are Substantial. Potential environmental risks and impacts primarily relate to
canal rehabilitation/modernization and civil construction activities, covering: (i) occupational health and safety (OHS) risks
such as accidents; (ii) localized pollution from noise and dust; (iii) improper disposal of waste and saline water; and (iv)
potential contamination of water sources if TWW systems are not adequately managed. Given low adoption of Integrated
Pest Management (IPM) in the state, Program interventions may lead to increased use of pesticides. While the Program
does not involve land acquisition or physical and/or economic displacement, construction works may cause temporary
impacts on adjoining private lands and access routes. A small number of canal segments may involve legacy
disputes/claims. The use of Government or Panchayat land for storage structures and TWW-related tanks/pipelines will
not cause any displacement but may cause temporary impacts on private lands and access routes. OHS risks may also arise
from inadequate implementation of required provisions for labor practices, working conditions and health and safety
measures. Community health and safety concerns, especially related to construction, traffic, waste handling, and reuse of
treated wastewater, are also relevant. Low participation of women and vulnerable farmers (tenant/sharecroppers) in
WUAs and Program benefits may increase risks of inequitable benefit sharing and elite capture. This could be further
exacerbated by weak stakeholder engagement.
21.     Gaps. Haryana’s E&S framework for IWRM and agriculture sectors is supported by comprehensive national and
state laws, policies, and guidelines. However, these are not fully institutionalized, operationalized, or applied consistently
across Program interventions. Program Implementation Units (PIUs) lack designated E&S officials with appropriate
expertise, and structured training programs are limited. Standardized procedures/templates for E&S screening, chance
find procedures, and site-specific environmental and social management plans (ESMPs) with adequate budgets and
enforceable contractor obligations are not in place. Dedicated E&S focal points and regular training for staff and
WUAs/drainage societies, especially for O&M activities including treated wastewater, are needed. While frameworks for
habitats and cultural resources exist, the uptake of IPM and Integrated Nutrient Management (INM), as well as monitoring
of other contaminants, remains limited. Worker and community health and safety requirements need stronger
implementation. Systems for land-related due diligence, beneficiary inclusion, equitable water sharing, stakeholder
engagement and grievance redressal also need strengthening. The state has taken proactive measures, such as adopting

22.      Recommendations. The ESSA’s priority recommendations link key E&S actions to DLIs/DLRs and time-bound
measures under the Program Action Plan (PAP). E&S results are supported through DLRs that require: (i) establishing and
applying TWW quality standards and safeguards, including monitoring arrangements, safety protocols, and disclosure at
TWW sites in alignment with Central Pollution Control Board norms and TWW reuse protocols adopted under the
Program; (ii) development of WUA bylaws that clarify roles and responsibilities, financial powers and accountability, and
provisions for women’s participation in executive committees; (iii) development of SOPs for WUAs and drainage societies
covering participatory water management, TWW reuse, and O&M; and (iv) embedding E&S clauses, screening
requirements, and ESMP obligations into Detailed Project Reports, bidding documents, and construction contracts.
Complementing these, the PAP commits to: (i) appointing/deputing/designating E&S focal points (Executive Engineer
level) across IWRD, MICADA, and DAFW; (ii) institutionalizing citizen feedback and disclosure protocols; (iii) developing
E&S training modules and rolling out training programs on PIM, O&M, TWW reuse, IPM/INM, natural resource
management, including crop diversification, and DSR field implementation. Additional ESSA actions will be operationalized
through the Program Operations Manual, including standardized screening checklists and ESMP templates, toll-free
Grievance Redress Mechanism access and analytics, WUA capacity strengthening, IPM/INM targets and monitoring, IEC
and demonstrations, and enhanced water quality monitoring and disclosure. Stakeholder consultations have been
undertaken, and a multi-stakeholder workshop along with staged disclosure of the draft and final ESSA are planned.
