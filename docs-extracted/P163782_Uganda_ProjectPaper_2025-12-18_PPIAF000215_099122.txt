                            I.   BACKGROUND AND RATIONALE FOR ADDITIONAL FINANCING

                        A.       Introduction and context

                        1.        This Project Paper seeks the approval of the Regional Vice President to provide additional financing (AF) in the
                        amount of US$65 million1 to the Republic of Uganda for the Integrated Water Management and Development Project
                        (IWMDP) (P163782). The Parent project was approved by the Board on June 14, 2018, for US$280 million equivalent and
                        became effective on June 27, 2019. The current Closing Date of the project is January 31, 2026, with a proposed extension
                        to January 31, 2028. The proposed AF aims to address a portion of a cost overrun which would otherwise have resulted in
                        a list of water supply and sanitation (WSS) infrastructure scoped under the project not being constructed, thus enabling
                        the completion of all originally planned activities and the achievement of the Project Development Objective (PDO).




For Official Use Only
                        2.     The IWMDP was designed with four components: Component 1: WSS in Small Towns and Rural Growth Centers
                        (RGCs) and Support to Districts Hosting Refugees (US$161.5 million: US$114.5M national IDA, US$43.0M IDA 18 Sub-
                        Window for RHC, US$4.0M counterpart). Component 2: WSS in Large Towns and Support to Districts Hosting Refugees
                        (US$120.5 million: US$77.5M national IDA, US$15.0M IDA 18 Sub-Window for Refugees and Host Communities, US$3.0M
                        counterpart, US$25.0M parallel KfW financing). Component 3: Water Resources Management (US$25.5 million:
                        US$25.0M national IDA, US$0.5M counterpart) and Component 4: Project Implementation and Institutional Strengthening
                        (US$5.5 million: US$5.0M national IDA, US$0.5M counterpart).

                        3.      The project has two implementing agencies (IAs) - the Ministry of Water and Environment (MWE) and the National
                        Water and Sewerage Corporation (NWSC). NWSC is responsible for Component 2 and MWE is responsible for the rest of
                        the project components. Each pertinent department within these IAs is responsible for operational tasks such as planning,
                        coordination, financial management (FM), monitoring, and supervision of their respective component activities.
                        Furthermore, the existing decentralized and MWE deconcentrated management structures, including Water Management
                        Zones (WMZs), NWSC town offices, and Urban Water Authorities (UWAs), as well as local governments, provide support
                        to ensure the successful delivery of project outputs in each area.

                        4.      The proposed AF will be used to finance a cost overrun resulting from costs that are higher than estimated
                        during project preparation, contract price adjustments and exchange rate losses. No new activities, other than those
                        already planned at appraisal, will be undertaken. No additional safeguard policies are triggered and there are no new
                        safeguard risks. The cost overrun was caused mainly by (i) exchange rate losses which reduced the US Dollar value of the
                        financing by about US$20 million since the appraisal of the original financing, and (ii) cost escalation of materials and
                        services from the resulting post-COVID-19 pandemic effects on the global economy, which contributed to higher than
                        estimated costs and price adjustments to ongoing contracts. The AF will enable the project to achieve its PDO. Activities
                        to be financed are part of the original project design and the PDO, Project Components, and PDO Indicators remain
                        unchanged.

                        5.       This Project Paper seeks approval to extend the closing date by 24 months from January 31, 2026, to January
                        31, 2028. Based on a revised implementation schedule for all activities, appraised by the Bank, a Closing Date extension
                        of 24 additional months (for a total cumulative extension of 38 months) is proposed, to ensure the full works completion,
                        including realization of project results. The Project Paper also seeks to confirm the alignment with new Corporate Results
                        Indicator (CRI) done during the blanket change forWorld Bank-financed projects in July 2024 and revise the final targets
                        of the indicators, building on the first project restructuring approved in September 2024. The project is complying with its
                        legal covenants, including audit and FM reporting requirements. The project’s overall risk rating is Moderate.

                        6.       The AF scope is aligned with the draft Uganda Country Partnership Framework (CPF) FY26-35 (under
                        preparation), especially High-Level Outcomes 3 and 4 focusing on Healthier and better skilled people and on Sustainable
                        and resilient natural resources respectively. It specifically supports Objective 3.1 on Expanded Health, Nutrition, and
                        Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) services and Objective 4.2 on Improved sustainable management of natural




For Official Use Only
                        resources. The high-level climate and disaster risk screening (CDRS) and assessments conducted for the parent project
                        have been updated. The risks and risk management measures remain valid, the latter as confirmed in the recent 2025
                        Uganda Country Climate and Development Report (CCDR). Per the CCDR, Uganda is the 14th most vulnerable country to
                        climate change and ranks 163rd in readiness to cope with climate impacts. The projected rising temperatures and
                        increasingly variable precipitation could have extensive social and economic impacts, especially considering the exposure
                        of the country’s productive assets to climate risks. Over the past two decades, heavy rainfall, flash and slow onset floods,
                        and droughts have affected an average of 200,000 Ugandans annually. Both the flood and drought risk are addressed by
                        this project. The AF also targets the most vulnerable northern regions that are highly exposed to weather shocks and have
                        inadequate coping strategies. Relatedly, the proposed water supply infrastructure and catchment management activities
                        remain as important as they were at original project design, as adaptation measures to address current and anticipated
                        future climate risks. Given that this AF addresses a cost overrun and will not finance new activities, it did not undergo a
                        formal Paris Alignment process. However, the do-no-harm principle applies and the AF is aligned with Uganda’s Updated
                        Nationally Determined Contribution (submitted on September 12, 2022), which recognizes WSS as a priority sector, with
                        specific actions to “Ensure resilient access to water supply for domestic and productive purposes, Increase to sanitation
                        and wastewater treatment and Scale-up Integrated Water Resources Management approach and use efficiency.”

                        7.       Refugee-hosting districts (RHDs) are increasingly experiencing pressures. As of October 31 2025, Uganda hosts
                        1,961,518 registered refugees and asylum-seekers settled across 12 refugee settlements in 13 districts including Kampala.
                        Of this number, 133,783 (119,525 new arrivals, 14,258 new births) were registered between January and June 20252. 97%
                        of the new arrivals fled conflicts in Democratic Republic of Congo (51%), South Sudan (29%) and Sudan (17%). The World
                        Bank, following consultation with UNHCR, confirms that the protection framework for refugees continues to be adequate
                        in Uganda. Uganda's refugee protection environment has recently faced significant challenges due to the rapid exit of
                        NGOs and the reduced institutional presence following unprecedented cuts to UNHCR’s operational budget. Protection
                        services, education, health, and livelihood have been severely affected, leading to increased school dropouts, child labour,
                        deteriorating health outcomes, and growing economic hardship among adults. Rising food insecurity has further
                        heightened the risk of gender-based violence (GBV) affecting forcibly displaced women and girls. These challenges

                        B.       Original Financing Objectives, Design, and Performance

                        8.      The original project aims to improve access to WSS services, integrated water resources management, and
                        operational performance of WSS service providers in Project areas. The performance by project component towards this
                        objective is below:

                             •   Component 1: WSS in Small Towns and RGCs and Support to Districts Hosting Refugees: This component aims to
                                 improve WSS services in Uganda's small towns, rural communities, and refugee settlements across 44 locations.
                                 Improved access to water supply services is increasing communities’ resilience to droughts and rising




For Official Use Only
                                 temperatures. Improved sanitation services (through improved on-site facilities and increased collection and
                                 treatment of fecal sludge) furthers both adaptation and mitigation. The reduction in the volume of untreated fecal
                                 sludge discharged into the environment is contributing to adaptation by improving water quality.3 The improved
                                 water quality is lowering the incidence of waterborne diseases and reducing the potential impact of floods, as
                                 flood waters will become less contaminated (thus raising communities' resilience to floods). The contribution of
                                 improved fecal sludge management to mitigation will be substantiated with a GHG accounting analysis.
                                 Construction activities are currently in various stages of completion, with commitments worth US$140.28 million
                                 (100%) and ten activities already completed, delivering WSS services to about 540,000 people. However, several
                                 contracts under this component, particularly those targeting rural and refugee communities, have faced delays
                                 due to site access encumbraces resulting from delayed compensation payments and, contractor cash flow
                                 constraints - exacerbated by delays in processing payments to contractors. Environmental and social risk
                                 management activities are ongoing, conducted by external service providers. Additionally, utility turnaround
                                 activities that included network expansions, and capacity support for Umbrella Authorities (UAs) are complete,
                                 leading to improvement plans that require funding as part of the utilities’ transformation roadmap into
                                 creditworthy and efficient entities.
                             •   Component 2: WSS in Large Towns and Support to Refugee Hosting Districts: This component supports WSS activities
                                 in 3 select large towns including one in a refugee hosting district (Adjumani), as well as select water supply system
                                 source protection activities in areas under the mandate of the NWSC. The protection of water sources is increasing
                                 available water and improving public health outcomes, thereby contributing to the communities’ adaptation. For
                                 the contribution of improved sanitation to adaptation and mitigation, see discussion on Component 1 above. Total
                                 commitments under this component amount to US$86.4 million and works are underway in Adjumani and Mbale
                                 while the Gulu system was completed and is delivering water to an additional over 46,000 people. The Gulu and
                                 Mbale works contracts have experienced cost over-runs, totaling US$6.0 million due to price adjustments for Gulu
                                 and design changes to accommodate a change in the water treatment plant site location for Mbale - occasioned
                                 by land disputes. Overall, there are sufficient funds within the NWSC allocation under Component 2 to

                                  officials with a solid governance structure. This risk is also mitigated since a majority of the project activities are
                                  in the construction stage with minimal risks of being stopped. Additionally, the provision of water and sanitation
                                  services is an uncontroversial and popular demand.

                                  works due to design modifications / variations arising from inadequate geotechnical and water quality studies as

                        42.      Gender: The AF includes activities from the gender tagged parent project that were assessed to contribute to
                        narrowing the gender gap. A 2017 study by MWE on the Gender Impact of the Water and Sanitation Sub-Sector highlights
                        the socioeconomic impacts of insufficient water supply and sanitation (WSS) facilities on women and girls and their
                        livelihoods. Specifically, by improving access to safe water and sanitation services, project interventions can address the
                        impacts and reduce the rates of gender-based violence (GBV) through measures such as reducing the time spent collecting
                        water and freeing up time for productive use, as well as providing opportunities to close the earning and asset gaps.

                        43.       Adult women and teenage girls spend more time fetching water than other groups—about 4 hours per week
                        versus 1 hour for adult men and 3.5 hours for boys. During droughts, this increases to 4.7 hours for women and over 5.7
                        for girls, with no change for males (Kamei 2022). Water collection mainly falls to women in rural areas (65%) compared to
                        urban (58.9%). These gender gaps in time use contribute to other human capital deficiencies like lower education and a
                        10% gap in labor force participation between women (76.5%) and men (85.4%; ILO 2025). Travelling to fetch water also




For Official Use Only
                        exposes women to increased risks of sexual violence and harassment. To address the gender gaps, the provision of WSS
                        including household-level connections reduces the distance to water points, saving women time for other productive
                        activities. The project through the Catchment management component also includes livelihood and income components,
                        skill training, and job opportunities that contribute to closing the earning and asset gaps, especially for women and youth.
                        Project construction activities create further opportunities for enhancing women’s and youth employment. Additionally,
                        a water and environment information system has been developed to support data availability on service delivery gaps and
                        address the gender gaps. Finally, the project seeks to counteract counterproductive social norms by fostering women’s
                        leadership through stakeholder engagement and focused training on gender and social development, encouraging
                        inclusive service delivery and women’s participation in governance structures, such as the management of public toilets.
