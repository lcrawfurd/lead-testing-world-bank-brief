                          B. Introduction and Context

                          Country Context
                          1. Morocco, a lower-middle-income country with a population of 37.7 million, has been on a steady economic growth
                             path over the past two decades while still facing development challenges. Supported by large public investments,
                             political, institutional and sector reforms, and strong commitment to macroeconomic stability, Morocco has made
                             significant socio-economic progress over the past 15 years, which led to notable progress in reducing extreme
                             poverty, increasing life expectancy, and expanding access to basic public services and infrastructure. Nonetheless,
                             growth remains constrained by structural bottlenecks, including limited job creation and exposure to external shocks,
                             underscoring the need for productivity-enhancing investments in strategic sectors such as energy.

                          2. After the shock-induced volatility of previous years, Morocco’s economic recovery strengthened in 2025. Since
                             2020, Morocco has faced a series of global and domestic shocks, including the COVID-19 pandemic, volatile
                             commodity prices, inflationary pressures, subdued rainfall, and the 2023 Haouz earthquake. Despite these
                             challenges, the country's strong macroeconomic fundamentals, such as adequate foreign exchange reserves, have
                             helped absorb the shocks. At the same time, volatility in international fuel prices in recent years has increased
                             Morocco’s energy import bill and put upward pressure on domestic energy costs and public finances, reinforcing the
                             importance of reducing exposure to fossil fuel markets through accelerated deployment of renewable energy (RE)
                             and related investments.
                          3. Morocco’s energy sector is central to economic growth, competitiveness and employment, and the proposed
                             Project will support the integration of private sector-led RE generation. Electricity demand in Morocco has grown
                             steadily at 4 percent annually since 2010, reaching 45 terawatt-hours (TWh) in 2024. Meeting this rising demand
                             while advancing climate commitments requires scaling clean energy and strengthening power system flexibility. The
                             proposed Ifahsa Pumped Hydropower Storage (PHS) Project is a strategic investment supporting Morocco’s transition
                             toward a more diversified, reliable, and resilient energy system, with direct implications for national energy security,
                             industrial competitiveness, and clean job creation. The Project will provide essential flexibility and system resilience
                             to enable the integration of at least 1 gigawatt (GW) of new private sector led RE generation projects. The Project is
                             supported by a substantial package of concessional and blended World Bank financing—combining long-tenor IBRD
                             resources, climate concessional funds, and financial incentive grants—which materially enhances its economic




_For Official Use Only_
                             attractiveness while supporting Morocco’s low-carbon and climate resilience objectives. Public financing is essential
                             given that storage provides system-wide public good services that are not yet fully remunerated under current
                             market and tariff arrangements. While sector reforms and tariff adjustments are underway, ONEE’s financial
                             constraints and the absence of flexibility remuneration mechanisms currently limit commercial viability. The Project
                             ensures affordability while laying the groundwork for future market-based solutions. The Project is aligned with
                             broader World Bank Group (WBG) engagements in the energy sector in Morocco aimed at supporting the sector
                             institutional and market reforms, infrastructure resilience, RE development by private sector, and clean energy jobs
                             and employment. It is also expected to enable the mobilization of approximately US$1 billion in private capital
                             through the RE capacity enabled by the Project, and will deepen partnerships with development institutions,
                             including the African Development Bank (AfDB), co-financing the proposed Project.

                          Sectoral and Institutional Context

                          4. Morocco has made strong progress in expanding electricity access and managing growing demand, achieving near-
                             universal coverage nationwide. Installed capacity reached 12,017 megawatts (MW) in 2024, while electricity
                             consumption continued to rise steadily reaching 45 TWh. The peak power demand grew to 8,200 MW in 2025, up by
                             8 percent from 2024, and is projected to reach 11,300 MW by 2030, driven by the country's economic growth,
                             desalination projects, and the rise of energy-intensive industrial gigafactories. Since the adoption of its energy
                             strategy in 2009, Morocco made significant headway in RE development, but remains heavily reliant on fossil fuel,
                             notably coal for its electricity generation. Fossil fuel imports still account for approximately 87 percent of the
                             country’s energy needs, a slight drop from 97 percent in 2009. This dependency exposes the power sector to price
                             volatility and financial pressures, while contributing significantly to Morocco’s carbon footprint.
                          5. Morocco’s electricity sector is undergoing structural changes to better attract private sector investments.
                             Historically, ONEE was the sole responsible for electricity generation, transmission, and part of the distribution.
                             Recently, Law 83-21 enacted in 2023 resulted in electricity and water distribution having fully transitioned to 12
                             newly created Regional Multiservice Companies (SRMs), with ONEE completely ceasing distribution as of November
                             1st, 2025. This transition marks a step towards sector unbundling, with ONEE expected to evolve into a transmission

                                system operator (TSO) while retaining a limited share of generation and continuing to act as the single buyer for
                                Independent Power Producers (IPPs). Currently, ONEE manages, operates, and responsible for maintaining reliability
                                of an electricity transmission network of 29,806 kilometers, including submarine cables to Spain and inactive
                                interconnection lines with Algeria as of 2021. Since the opening of the power generation to the private sector in
                                1994, most power generation capacity was developed by IPPs, with ONEE remaining the sole electricity buyer. While
                                private sector involvement has enhanced efficiency and financing, the current structure, characterized by an open
                                market limited to only RE since the adoption of law 13-09 in 2010, has yet to be made fully operational and requires
                                significant market reforms and investments in network flexibility.
                           6.   Morocco has abundant RE resources that could support the country’s growth and decarbonization objectives. The
                                enactment of Law 13-09 in 2010 opened the RE market to third-party producers for the first time. Subsequently, Law
                                40-19 approved in February 2023, amending Laws 13-09 and 82-211, establishes a framework for self-generation.
                                However, continued delays in their full operationalization have hindered the pace of RE projects in the open market.
                                As of 2025, Morocco’s installed RE capacity reached 5,471 MW (2,390 MW wind; 2,120 MW hydro; and 961 MW
                                solar), accounting for 45 percent of total installed capacity and only but supplying only 26 percent of national
                                electricity generation. By 2030, Morocco aims to raise its RE installed capacity to 64 percent and its share of electricity
                                generation to 59 percent, highlighting the growing need for flexibility solutions to help bridge the gap between
                                capacity and effective energy delivery. Achieving these targets will require significant investment in grid flexibility
                                and energy storage solutions to manage variability in solar and wind generation.




_For Official Use Only_
                           7.   Enhancing power system flexibility and resilience through strategic investments in grid infrastructure is necessary
                                to support higher RE penetration. To meet RE targets while maintaining the reliability and flexibility of the power
                                system, ONEE is prioritizing investment in flexibility solutions, including battery energy storage systems (BESS) and
                                PHS. Based on ONEE’s Master Plan for 2026–2030, developed using OptGen modeling software, PHS was identified
                                as the least cost option to integrate growing solar and wind capacity and meet peak demand. Accordingly, ONEE has
                                launched a program to develop 1,000 MW of PHS capacity by 2030. This program includes the recently commissioned
                                350 MW Abdelmoumen PHS plant near Agadir, a 350 MW PHS plant under development at El Menzel, and the
                                planned 300 MW Ifahsa PHS plant under the proposed Project, which will facilitate the gradual displacement of fuel-
                                based peaking and balancing generation. In parallel, ONEE is procuring 800 MW/1,600 MWh of BESS for deployment
                                across various locations, including existing substations and plants, also informed by the 2026–2030 Master Plan.
                                Together, these investments will strengthen storage capacity, enhance operational efficiency, and support increased
                                integration of RE into the Moroccan power system.
                           8.   Morocco has experience in developing and implementing PHS solutions. Morocco’s PHS role in the electricity
                                system has evolved with its renewable ambitions. Afourer PHS (460 MW, 2005) was built for energy arbitrage—
                                storing off‑peak electricity and generating at peak—while providing stability and reserves in a thermal‑dominated
                                system. Following the 2009 RE strategy, ONEE developed Abdelmoumen PHS (350 MW, 2024) to explicitly address
                                solar and wind intermittency and enhance grid flexibility. Looking ahead, the forthcoming Ifahsa PHS continues this
                                trajectory to increase RE integration. In selecting the site, ONEE applied a multi‑criteria analysis—hydrology and head
                                availability, proximity to existing grid infrastructure, geotechnical suitability, environmental and social constraints,
                                and construction logistics—confirming its feasibility and least‑cost contribution to system flexibility. This shift—from
                                supporting a thermal system to enabling RE integration—underscores the rising importance of flexible storage in
                                Morocco’s power sector.

                                country’s full RE potential. The sectoral reforms engaged by Morocco, which includes the unbundling of the

                          15. The primary beneficiaries of the Project are Moroccan electricity consumers – including households and
                              businesses, who will benefit from a more reliable, affordable, and cleaner supply of electricity. By strengthening
                              the power system’s flexibility and enabling the integration of additional RE, the project will contribute to enhancing

                              Moroccan consumers. Delivering 300 MW of dispatchable capacity with five hours of storage will provide firm, on-

                              employment opportunities for local workers and contractors, while also stimulating demand for goods and services

                            D. Project Description

                           18. The project, with a preliminary estimated total cost of US$500 million, will comprise the following components:
                              •    Component 1: Construction of the Ifahsa PHS Plant (US$450 million): This component will finance the full
                                   development and commissioning of the Ifahsa PHS facility. The scope includes: i) major civil works to construct
                                   the upper and lower reservoirs, each with an average depth of 20 meters, the water conveyance system , and an
                                   underground powerhouse; ii) procurement and installation of electromechanical equipment—including reversible
                                   pump-turbine units, generator-motors, transformers, control and protection systems, and auxiliary equipment—
                                   to deliver an installed capacity of 300 MW; iii) Supply and installation of hydromechanical equipment at the dams,
                                   including dam-safety measurement equipment compliant with national regulations, ESS4 Annex 1 on Dam safety,




_For Official Use Only_
                                   and international good practice, seismic and hydrological monitoring systems; and iv) project preparatory works
                                   such as, installation of an outdoor 400 kV switchyard and a power supply during construction, temporary access
                                   roads and base camps.
                              •    Component 2: Construction of the Transmission Line and Grid Integration Facilities (US$30 million): This
                                   component will finance the construction and commissioning of the transmission infrastructure required to
                                   connect the Ifahsa PHS plant to the national grid. It includes i) the construction of two double-circuit 400 kV
                                   overhead transmission lines, spanning 62 km, to connect the plant to the existing 400 kV line between the
                                   Melloussa and Oualili substations; ii) expansion of the two substations and switching facilities to connect the plant;
                                   iii) all associated works and equipment compliant with national grid codes to enable the plant’s connection to the
                                   national grid.
                              •    Component 3: Technical Assistance (TA), Capacity Building, and Project Management Support (US$20 million):
                                   This component will provide technical, institutional, and implementation support to ensure the efficient and
                                   sustainable operation of the Ifahsa PHS plant, while laying the legal, financial, technical and regulatory foundations
                                   necessary to assess future private sector participation in large-scale storage projects, including through PPP
                                   structures. It will also explore potential climate and carbon finance mechanisms to enhance the long-term
                                   financial sustainability of such investments. The technical design studies, financed outside this component, are
                                   supported under the US$1.5 million Grant Facility for Project Preparation (GFPP), signed on June 30, 2025. It will
                                   finance the following activities:
                                   •   Sub-Component 3.1: Owner’s Engineer and technical oversight. This sub-component will finance: i) the
                                       services of an Owner’s Engineer to supervise the PHS plant works, ii) the implementation of dam‑safety
                                       instruments—including the preparation and implementation of Dam Safety Management Plan, Emergency
                                       Preparedness Plan, and operation and maintenance (O&M) manuals—and iii)            the recruitment of
                                       independent panels of experts covering dam safety, environmental management, and social safeguards.
