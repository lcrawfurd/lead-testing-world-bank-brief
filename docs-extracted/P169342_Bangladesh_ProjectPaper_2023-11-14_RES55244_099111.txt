 I. PROJECT STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING

 1. Background: The Bangladesh Rural WASH for Human Capital Development Project was approved by the World Bank
    Board on September 25, 2020, and by the Executive Committee for National Economic Council (ECNEC) of Bangladesh
    on December 22, 2020. The total cost of the Project is USD 550.5 million. The Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank
    (AIIB) and World Bank are jointly financing USD 400 million in equal shares, and the Government of Bangladesh is
    providing US$150.5 million in counterpart funding. The Project become effective on 10 May 2021. The Project is
    executed by two implementing agencies: Department of Public Health Engineering (DPHE) and Palli Karma-Sahayak
    Foundation (PKSF), with the objective to (i) improve access to ‘safely managed’ water supply and sanitation in
    selected areas of rural Bangladesh and (ii) strengthen sector institutional capacity for water and sanitation.

 2. Project status. Progress towards the Project Development Objective is moderately satisfactory, and the
    Implementation Progress rate is also moderately satisfactory. While both agencies have made progress on activities,
    disbursement remains low, with challenges related to completion of small piped water schemes due to poor
    contractor performance in several areas (component 1), PKSF sanitation loans that remain below target because of
    drop out of two microfinance finance (MFI) partners and delay in implementation activities related to behavior
    change campaign (component 2), as further detailed below. Since effectiveness on May 10, 2021, the Project has
    disbursed US$57.43 million (30%) IDA financing and an equal amount from AIIB finance. There is no significant audit
    observation pending resolution until FY 2022. Observation related to detail recording of expenditure in the
    Management Book has been resolved. Overall, the environmental and social compliance status is rated as
    satisfactory, and the implenting agencies prepared monitoring tools for field level data collection on complaiance
    related to environmental safeguards. The Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) established at the central, district,
    upazila, and union levels is receiving, evaluating and responding to concerns and complaints related to the project's
    environmental and social performance.

(a)   Component 1: Investments in water supply: A total of 3,364 small pipe water schemes will be constructed, of which
      contracts have been awarded for 29 packages, covering 3,008 schemes, in which 1,595 tube wells have been installed
      and 654 structures for overhead tanks have been completed. Construction of 145 pipelines is currently underway
      Community consultation is ongoing to select the site for remaining 614 schemes. A technical assistance (TA) firm is
      on board for the feasibility study of large pipe water schemes in 78 subdistricts to identify the locations, availability
      of land and quality of ground water for construction of large pipe water schemes. The firm has already conducted
      the feasibility study for 55 large schemes and bidding documents have been prepared for 23 schemes. However,
      there are delays in the completion and hand over of small piped due to poor performance of local contractors. There
      are about 600schemes affected, and DPHE is closely monitoring the contractor's performance and providing technical
      assistance to expedite work progress.

(b)   Component 2: Investments in sanitation and hygiene: The Component is financing, among other actions, the
      delivery of safely managed sanitation and hygiene facilities for households. It is supporting the deployment of twin-
      offset pit latrines (for safe management of fecal sludge) through micro-financing to local households. The PKSF
      planned to provide US$ 235.4 million WASH loans to one million households of which US$135.32 million will be
      provided by the Government of Bangladesh as counterpart funding. 57 MFI partner organizations (PO) of PKSF along
      with their 1,255 branch offices are now active in 78 subdistricts. They provided 27,373 water loans to households and
      sanitation loans to 93,157 households, in a total of US$ 37.93 million. Out of 352 public toilets planned under the
      project, construction of 284 public toilets has been completed, and the remainder toilets are under construction.
      However, there are issues related to the drop out of MFI partners and the related geographical coverage and WASH
      revolving fund of the component (see further below). Activities on behavior change campaign are also behind due to
      delay in procuring firm for the capacity building of MFI staff. Four percent of the installed toilets and three percent
      of the water facilities constructed from MFI loans were verified by independent verification consultants. Key findings
      include confirmation that 98 percent of constructed toilets were twin pits as designed, including distance between
      two pits and locked door facility. Moreover, 27 percent households were highly satisfied with their toilets and 71
      percent were satisfied. Two percent of respondents were not satisfied due to the location of the installed toilet, which
      was felt to be unsafe for females and persons with physical challenges.

(c)   Component 3: Institutional strengthening: Two firms has been hired for capacity development of local government
      officials. DPHE and PKSF hired IT firms to provide technical assistance on developing the information management
      and visualization system for project monitoring.

(d)   Component 4: Project implementation and management: Both DPHE and PKSF have set up their Project
      Management Units (PMU), headed by the respective Project Director and Project Coordinator and supported by
      deputed and recruited staff. The respective Project Steering Committees have also been formed and holding
      meetings.

(e)   Component 5: COVID-19 emergency response: This component provided quick, just-in-time WASH services where
      needed in the COVID-19 pandemic hotspots. Construction of 631 hand washing stations (out of 882 planned) has
      been completed, and the remainder stations are under construction.

 3. Rationale for restructuring. The proposed restructuring would be to (i) expand geographical coverage to include 104
    additional sub-districts to support the targeted number of beneficiaries; (ii) revise the percentage of counterpart
    funding; and (iii) revision of disbursement estimates.

Expansion of project areas
 4. PKSF activities in component 2 are implemented on the ground by Partner Organizations (PO) in charge of supporting
    poor and non-poor households to access micro-credit to install toilets and introduce safely managed sanitation
    facilities in selected geographical locations. PKSF is working with 57 partners organizations; of which, two major POs
    – Bangladesh Rural Advancement Committee (BRAC) and Society for Social Services - that were originally included in
    implementation - have dropped out of the program. This leaves households in 414 branch locations (comprising 25
    percent of planned branches) across the original 78 sub-districts unattended by any PO and therefore are unable to
    access micro-credit for improved sanitation. This gap in household coverage for improved sanitation provision will
    negatively impact the number of project beneficiaries and the achievement of the PDO.

 5. PKSF has proposed to address this coverage gap left by expanding the geographical coverage of the project to include
    104 sub-districts in addition to the original 78 sub-districts of 18 districts. PKSF already has 57 partner organizations

    with around 2,000 branch offices and organized credit members in the newly proposed 104 sub-districts. These
    identified PKSF partners have the capacity to support project activities.


6. The inclusion of additional 104 sub-districts would enable the project to achieve the target of one million household
   toilets construction. No additional costs or extended implementation period are envisaged since the proposed sub-
   districts are located within the districts already supported by the Project and both PKSF POs and DPHE have been
   implementing other projects in these locations for a long time. The LEs are already trained in all districts, if additional
   trained LEs required, PKSF will build their capacity by using their own resources. The proposed expansion of
   geographical coverage would also enable the government to complete the implementation of project activities on
   time.


   Revision of counterpart funding
7. The total financing for PKSF is US$328.94 million, which includes the IDA and AIIB contribution of US$184.48 million
   and counterpart funding of US$144.46 million. According to the Financing Agreement for the subcomponent 1.3 (MFI
   loan for households for water facility improvement), the financing ratios from years 1–5, 25 percent by IDA, 25
   percent by the AIIB, and 50 percent by the PKSF. For subcomponent 2.2.a (MFI loan to households for installation
   sanitation and hygiene facilities), the financing ratios in years 1–2 is 40.5 percent by IDA, 40.5 percent by the AIIB,
   and 19.0 percent by the PKSF while those in years 3–5 would be 22.7 percent, 22.7 percent, and 54.7 percent
   respectively. In last two years, PKSF could not provide household level WASH loans to even 50% of their targeted
   beneficiaries due the delay in field level implementation which interrupted the generation of WASH revolving funds
   according to plan, the main source of counterpart funding. The revision of percentage of counterpart funding will be
   supportive to use the approved funding for PKSF from IDA and AIIB as well as enable PKSF to contribute counterpart
   funding according to agreement.

   Revision of disbursement estimates
8. The estimates will be revised to account for unprecedented challenges in operating during the COVID-19 pandemic,
   which delayed Project signing, effectiveness and begin of field level activities. Moreover, over the course of
   implementation, the country has witnessed high rates of inflation and significant depreciation of the Bangladeshi
   Taka against the US dollar, which also resulted in slower disbursement than originally anticipated.




II. DESCRIPTION OF PROPOSED CHANGES

9. Expand geographical coverage: PKSF is currently working in 78 sub-districts and propose to cover an additional 104
   sub-districts, for a total number of 182 sub-districts. PKSF already has 57 POs with around 2,000 branch offices and
   organized credit members in the newly proposed 104 sub-districts, with the capacity to support Project activities.
   The expanded coverage will require an amendment to the Financing Agreement, specifically on paragraph 48 of

      this amendment through a letter dated February 23, 2023.

 10. Revise the percentage of counterpart funding: Around US$90.34 million was planned to be disbursed to PKSF from
     IDA and AIIB financing in the first two years under Category 3(a) and 3(b) of the Financing Agreement, but actual
     disbursement was only US$39.88 million due to the slow uptake of loans at the household level. First two years PKSF
     contribution was according to the agreement (US$12.96 million)1. As PKSF counterpart funding was expected to be
     financed by revolving the loan financing, it has not been able to generate the necessary resources to contribute as
     per the original ratio described in the Financing Agreement for next three years. The Government of Bangladesh,
     through ERD, has officially requested an amendment to the financing ratio through a letter dated September 24,
     2023, as detailed in Table 1. The proposed financing percentages would be for year 3 onwards. So far there haven’t
     been any disbursements relating to year 3 and hence the changes proposed in the financing percentage would apply
     for all disbursements from year 3 onwards. For Year 1 and Year 2 financing percentages included in the existing
     financing agreement would apply. Corresponding changes will also be made under AIIB financing.
