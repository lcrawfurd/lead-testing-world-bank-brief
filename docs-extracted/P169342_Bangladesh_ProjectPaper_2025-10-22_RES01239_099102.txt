I. PROJECT STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING

  1. Background: The Bangladesh Rural WASH for Human Capital Development Project (the Project)
     was approved by the World Bank Board on September 25, 2020, and became effective on May
     10, 2021.The total cost of the Project is US$550.5 million. The Asian Infrastructure Investment
     Bank (AIIB) and World Bank are jointly financing US$400 million in equal shares, and the
     Government of Bangladesh (GoB) committed to provide US$150.5 million in counterpart funding.
     The Project is being executed by two implementing agencies: the Department of Public Health
     Engineering (DPHE) and the Palli Karma-Sahayak Foundation (PKSF). The project development
     objectives (PDO) are to (i) improve access to ‘safely managed’ water supply and sanitation in
     selected areas of rural Bangladesh; and (ii) strengthen sector institutional capacity for water and
     sanitation.

  2. Restructuring request: Requested by the GoB, the proposed restructuring involves an extension
     of the Project's timeline by 14 months, till December 31, 2026. In addition to the timeline
     extension, there will be an adjustment to the disbursement arrangements. Specifically, the AIIB
     will no longer be part of the financing, and instead, the IDA will jointly Co-Finance with PKSF the
     project implementation costs for the extended period from November 21, 2025 to December 31,
     2026 and a re-calibration of the results framework targets to account for additional beneficiaries
     planned to be covered during the extension period. This is the third project restructuring, and the
     first proposed extension. The first restructuring, approved on May 14, 2023, extended the
     geographical location; and the second, which was approved on June 03, 2025, involved the
     cancellation of counterpart funding.

  3. Project status: Progress towards the PDO and the implementation progress rating is “Moderately
     Satisfactory,” reflecting positive advancement across all components. Construction of both large
     and small water supply schemes is progressing well, and the uptake of household sanitation loans
     for improved toilets has also continued at a rapid pace. However, considering start-up delays and
     construction timelines, it has now become clear that piped water connections to households will
     not be achieved in full within the remaining implementation period, until October 31, 2025.
     Further, the provision of toilets to the extreme poor has been slower than planned, but with the
     identification of extreme poor households is now complete, progress under this activity is
     expected to accelerate.

  4. Implementation progress:
     a) Component 1: Investments in water supply
        • Development of large-piped water schemes by the DPHE. After some initial delays,
           contracts have been awarded for all 54 schemes in 22 packages, and implementation is
           ongoing in all schemes. Physical progress is around 20 percent as of June 30, 2025. All
           schemes are expected to be completed by October 2026. These schemes serve 85,050
           beneficiaries (or 14 percent of the target).
        • Development of small-piped water schemes by the DPHE. Physical progress is 84 percent
           as of May 30, 2025. However, 418 out of the total 3,271 schemes are not expected to be
           complete by October 2025. So far, a total of 1,287 schemes (including 228 schemes under
           Component 5 on COVID-19 emergency response) have been completed and handed over

    b)   Component 2: Investments in sanitation and hygiene
         • Public toilets and toilets in community health clinics by DPHE. Construction of public
             toilets in areas with high pedestrian traffic, including markets, bus stations, and
             community health clinics, is well proceeding. Out of 852 public toilets, 740 are completed
             and handed over to the relevant committees. Renovation of 640 out of 780 sanitation
             and hygiene facilities in community health clinics has been completed, with the remaining
             140 facilities ongoing.
         • Household toilets by the DPHE. Out of the targeted 220,874 extreme poor households,
             204,351 households have been identified by two firms hired by the DPHE. Among these
             identified households, latrines have been constructed for 77,306 households.
         • Microfinance loans by PKSF. PKSF partners have provided a total of 441,170 loans,
             amounting to US$119.43 million, to households for the installation of sanitation and
             hygiene facilities. In addition, the PKSF has also been working with select local
             entrepreneurs (LE), providing them with training for the installation of latrines and
             US$2.25 million worth of loans for business expansion where needed.
    c)   Component 3: Institutional strengthening. A firm has been procured by the DPHE to work
         with UNICEF and the Policy Support Unit of Bangladesh (PSB) on developing a Sector
         Investment Plan. The firm will conduct a detailed financial analysis as part of the Sector
         Development Plan (2025–2040) for the Water Supply and Sanitation (WSS) Sector in
         Bangladesh. A software application for the Information Management and Visualization
         System under the Project has been developed. Its mobile and web applications are both
         operational at present.
    d)   Component 4: Project implementation and management. Both the DPHE and PKSF have set
         up their Project Management Units (PMU) headed by the respective Project Director and
         Project Coordinator and supported by deputed and recruited staff—and are functioning
         smoothly. In addition, the respective Project Steering Committees have been formed and are
         holding regular meetings.
    e)   Component 5: COVID-19 emergency response. This component has provided rapid WASH
         services where needed in the COVID-19 pandemic hotspots. Construction of 829 hand
         washing stations (out of 882) has been completed, and the remaining stations are under
         construction.

5. Financial Management: The Project consistently submitted Interim Unaudited Financial Reports
   (IUFRs), withdrawal applications, and other related disclosures in a timely manner. A desk review
   indicates that all recent submissions up to December 2024 for both PIUs were acceptable, with a
   few exceptions from the Financial Management perspective regarding reporting requirements
   and financial disclosure. Both PMUs have submitted the project audit report for the fiscal year
   2023–2024, with the auditor expressing an unqualified opinion on the project financial
   statements. The DPHE is actively implementing measures to address the observations noted in
   the audit report.

  6. Rationale for Restructuring: The DPHE and PKSF have shown remarkable progress despite a
     seven-month lag in implementation due to the delay in effectiveness and other implementation
     challenges caused by COVID-19 disruptions, the recent political turmoil, and serious flooding in
     July-August 2024. Investments in small-piped water schemes under Components 1 and 5 and
     institutional strengthening under Component 3 are on track and expected to be completed by the
     current project closing. Investments in sanitation and hygiene under Components 2 and 5 have
     also made tremendous progress, especially those related to public sanitation and hygiene
     facilities in high pedestrian traffic locations and community clinics. However, more time will be
     required to complete the large-piped water schemes under Component 1—although the tenders
     for all 54 schemes have been awarded and implementation is underway, the large-piped water
     schemes are scheduled to be completed by the end of 2026. In addition, out of 3,271 small piped
     water schemes, 418 will not meet the completion deadline of October 2025, designed to serve
     160,290 beneficiaries (or 27 percent of the total target), the completion of these schemes is
     critical to the achievement of development outcomes.

  7. This is the first request for extension of the Project, which has shown satisfactory performance
     with no pending fiduciary issues. The principal justifications and rationale for the extension are
     the following:
         (i) The requested extension will allow the GoB to complete the remaining works, thus
               enabling the Project to reach the full potential of its development impact, by providing
               access to safe water and sanitation to all targeted beneficiaries. Besides the ongoing
               contract activities, no new major contract activities are expected to be undertaken. With
               the demonstrated commitment to accelerating project implementation since the Mid-
               Term Review, the remaining construction works are expected to be completed within the
               extension period.
         (ii) The proposed change in the disbursement arrangements for the extended project period
               will allow Joint Co-Financing between IDA and PKSF for the extended period. This change
               is necessary because the extension period will not include co-financing from AIIB. AIIB has
               confirmed the closing of AIIB financing on the original closing date, November 20, 2025.
         (iii) Furthermore, under Component 2 through PKSF loans, the Project will be also able to add
               more than 1 million additional beneficiaries beyond its original target. This makes a
               measurable difference to the achievement of SDGs, as it adds 1 percent to the rural
               population who have access to safely managed sanitation facilities. These changes will be
               reflected in an adjustment to the results framework.



II. DESCRIPTION OF PROPOSED CHANGES

  8. Changes requested. The GoB, via two ERD letters dated April 17, 2025, and June 23, 2025, has
     officially requested to extend the Project for around 14 months. The proposed and subsequent
     changes involve the following:
     a) Extension of the project duration to December 31, 2026. This adjustment is crucial to ensure
          the Project meets the development objectives and maximizes potential outcomes.
     b) Change in the financing arrangements for the period from November 21, 2025 to December
          31, 2026. The proposed restructuring includes a change to financing arrangement where IDA
          will jointly Co-Finance with PKSF the project implementation costs for the extended period
