 I. COUNTRY CONTEXT AND OPERATION SUMMARY

1.      Zambia’s economy is recovering after the severe drought of 2024. Improved rainfall in 2025 has supported a
rebound in agriculture, with maize output reaching 3.5 million metric tons during the last agricultural season, compared
to 1.5 million MT harvested in 2023/2024. Mining production, driven by copper, grew by 8.8 percent in 2024 and 13.4
percent in the first half (H1) of 2025 year-on-year (y/y), supported by higher international prices and investment in
brownfield expansion. Real GDP growth is projected at 5.2 percent in 2025, up from 3.8 percent in 2024 and closer to the
5.7 percent average achieved between 2021 and 2023. Inflation fell from over 16 percent by the end of 2024 to 9.4 percent
in January 2026, while the kwacha has started to appreciate after years of sharp weakening.
2.      While the immediate drought-induced humanitarian pressures have eased, Zambia’s high vulnerability to
climate shocks remains. Despite these positive trends, poverty remains widespread, with an estimated 59 percent of the
population still below the national poverty line, and food insecurity lingers in areas hardest hit by the recent drought.
Variable rainfall continues to threaten agricultural output, water security, and hydropower generation, which still supplies
over 80 percent of electricity. The 2023/2024 drought created a generation deficit of more than 1,000MW, leading to
nationwide load-shedding, with an average daily residential power supply of four hours until last December. Although
hydrological conditions have improved since 2025, the energy sector remains exposed to climate variability. Zambia ranks
132 out of 185 countries in the Notre Dame Global Adaptation Initiative Index (2021), reflecting high vulnerability and low
adaptive capacity. Rural households that depend on rainfed farming remain particularly exposed to recurring drought and
flood cycles.
3.       The country has nearly completed its debt restructuring and restored fiscal policy credibility, but pressures
remain elevated. Agreements with bilateral creditors, bondholders, and major private lenders have resolved over 94
percent of eligible external debt under the Common Framework (CF) for debt treatments. Fiscal consolidation has led to
primary budget surpluses and a significant reduction in the debt-to-GDP ratio, now well under 100 percent. Still, debt
service is expected to average around 24.5 percent of domestic revenues over the medium term. Domestic arrears to
suppliers continue to accumulate, estimated at over 7.9 percent of GDP in 2025H1, while public investment remains
subdued. Financing needs are largely met through concessional support, as Zambia remains locked out of international
markets. State-owned enterprises (SOEs) pose significant fiscal risks due to their weak financial performance, high
dependence on government support, and inconsistent enforcement of financial reporting standards.
4.       Zambia has significant opportunities in natural resources and the global expansion and transformation of energy
supply. The country’s substantial reserves of copper, cobalt, and manganese are in high demand for green technologies,
positioning Zambia as a key global supplier. At the same time, the country’s vast forest cover and land endowments provide
potential for carbon credit generation, which could be mobilized through well-designed market and institutional
frameworks. Effective management of these resources could help build fiscal buffers, generate foreign exchange, and
support inclusive growth. Yet, these opportunities are not automatic and require stronger policies to manage mineral
revenue volatility, reforms to optimize state investments through SOEs that compete on a level playing field with the private
sector, and adequate frameworks for public–private partnerships (PPPs).
5.      The private sector faces considerable structural constraints that hurt the country’s growth and job prospects.
Zambia’s infrastructure gaps remain significant in the energy, water, and transport sectors, and combined with high
regulatory barriers and preferential treatment of SOEs, weigh on competitiveness. Load-shedding has eased this year, but
household electricity access remains just above 50 percent, limiting output and productivity growth. Non-mining sectors—
such as manufacturing and agro-processing—continue to lag, slowing economic diversification. The share of formal

employment in Zambia’s labor markets decreased, from about 37 percent in 2017 to 27 percent in 2021. Historically
correlated with growth, private investment will be crucial for sustaining Zambia’s recovery. Based on population growth
projections, Zambia needs to create over 10 million new jobs by 2050 to prevent its labor force participation and
employment rates from declining.
6.       The Government of the Republic of Zambia (GRZ) has pursued ambitious reforms to restore macroeconomic
stability, drive economic growth, and create better jobs. Following the 2021 elections, the administration strengthened
debt management, turned primary deficits into surpluses, and cut inflation nearly in half. Key actions included boosting
private investment, protecting social sector spending, canceling non-performing projects, and reducing state intervention
in the energy sector. While initial reforms focused on stabilization, Zambia is now shifting toward structural reforms that
encourage private sector-led growth and job creation. The government actively supports women’s climate-smart,
productive enterprises. Investments under the Integrated Resource Plan and Energy Compact are expanding energy access,
enabling income generation and job opportunities. These efforts were supported by the recently concluded IMF Extended
Credit Facility (ECF) program, which aimed to restore stability and foster resilient, inclusive growth. However, recent shocks
and political uncertainty ahead of the presidential elections highlight the urgent need for continued reforms to support
recovery and strengthen resilience, particularly to climate change.
7.      The proposed Development Policy Financing (DPF) operation of US$45 million provides targeted support to
Zambia’s ongoing reform program. It is the second of two operations in a programmatic series, the previous one with
US$100 million comprising two instruments, US$25 million as a regular DPF and US$75 million as a Catastrophe Deferred
Drawdown Option (Cat-DDO). The operation is part of the World Bank’s continuous multi-sectoral support to help Zambia
emerge from the debt crisis and shift to a more resilient, sustainable, and inclusive economic growth path. It supports
foundational reforms and serves as a platform for policy dialogue aligned with the broader reform effort in Zambia,
including the IMF’s ECF. The Program Development Objective (PDO) will be achieved through reforms aimed at (i) enhancing
fiscal management and resilience, (ii) promoting private-sector investment and resilience in enabling sectors, and (iii)
strengthening disaster risk management and climate resilience. The program aligns with Zambia’s Eighth National
Development Plan for 2022–2026 (8NDP) and IDA21 policy priorities, including private investment, job creation, crisis
preparedness, climate action, and gender equality. Additionally, the DPF supports Zambia’s efforts to secure debt
restructuring under the CF.
8.        The operation is informed by recent analytics and the World Bank Group’s identified priorities in its ongoing
partnership with the GRZ while assisting the country in responding to various shocks from 2020. It draws on recent
findings from core WBG analytics, including a country economic memorandum (2024), a public finance review (2024), and
a country private sector diagnostic (2025), as well as an energy transition minerals roadmap (2025) and groundwork under
Mission-300 and Zambia’s Energy Compact (2025), among others. Since 2021, IDA commitments to Zambia have exceeded
US$2.1 billion, with all new commitments since 2023 delivered as grants to support the country’s emergence from debt
distress. This year’s DPF builds on that engagement, complementing previous operations and the IMF-supported program,
AfDB, and EU budget support. This operation underscores the World Bank’s continued support for reforms critical to
consolidating Zambia’s progress and ensuring it translates into sustained, inclusive, and climate-resilient growth.

 II. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK

9.         Zambia’s macroeconomic policy framework, anchored to fiscal and monetary targets of the IMF’s ECF program,
is deemed adequate for the proposed operation. The macroeconomic framework reflects a full post-debt restructuring
baseline aligned with IMF program parameters and the Joint IMF-World Bank Debt Sustainability Analysis (DSA) (section
II.C). It captures the memorandum of understanding (MoU) agreed between the authorities and the official creditors in
October 2023, delivering a 40 percent net present value reduction through delaying principal repayments and reducing

of overall and external debt distress (Figure 1).1
10.     Meanwhile, GRZ has initiated and carried out vital reforms, establishing a strong base for economic recovery and
faster growth, while also earning the trust of creditors. These reforms tackle previous macro-fiscal issues that caused
external debt default during the global “polycrisis.” Since 2021, the administration has canceled non-performing projects
and cut inefficient subsidies in the energy sector, freeing fiscal space for human development and social protection. It is
advancing major reforms in the hard-hit energy sector under Zambia’s Energy Compact, supported by Mission-300.2 The
GRZ is enhancing the legal environment in which SOEs and PPPs operate to reduce fiscal risks and encourage greater private-
sector participation. Monetary policy focuses on reducing inflation, while increased foreign direct investment and mineral
export earnings have begun to stabilize the exchange rate. Lastly, the authorities are enhancing business regulations to
foster private investment and international competitiveness, including through reforms in key sectors supported by this
operation.

worst drought the country experienced in the last 40 years, GDP growth remained resilient. The momentum continued in
2025, with GDP growing by 4.9 percent in 2025H1 y/y, compared to 2.0 percent in the same period of 2024. Agriculture,

12.       Inflationary pressures are reducing, driven by tight monetary policy, low international fuel prices, an appreciating
kwacha, and a bumper agricultural harvest. Headline inflation fell from its peak of 16.8 percent in February 2025 to 9.4
percent in January 2026, while food inflation dropped from 20.6 percent to 10.9 percent. As of November 1, new electricity
tariffs replaced emergency rates, though they still do not cover full costs. In November 2025, the Bank of Zambia (BOZ)
reduced the policy rate by 25 basis points to 14.25 percent, marking its first rate cut in five years in response to falling
inflation. Higher metal exports improved foreign currency inflows and appreciated the kwacha (17.3 percent in 2025 YTD).
The decline in banks’ exposure to government securities—from 20.2 percent of banking sector assets at end-2024 to 18.2
percent in August 2025—has supported private sector credit growth, which rose to 21.4 percent y/y in September 2025
from 17 percent in 2024.
13.     Zambia’s current account deficit (CAD) expanded in 2025 despite robust copper and agricultural exports. The CAD
narrowed to 1.9 percent of GDP in 2024, following a 13.3 percent increase in copper exports, which was underpinned by
rising metal production. In 2025H1, Zambia’s CAD reached 2.0 percent of GDP, from a 0.2 percent surplus in 2024H1 due
to higher imports amid improved domestic demand, continued electricity imports in response to electricity shortages, and
unwinding of drought-related official grants. The CAD in the six months to June was financed by foreign direct investment
(1.8 percent of GDP, slightly below the 2024H1 level) and non-resident domestic debt purchases (0.5 percent of GDP). Still,
gross official reserves rose from US$4.3 billion at the end of 2024 to US$4.7 billion (4.7 months of imports) by end-June.
14.       The Government continues its prudent fiscal trajectory, with budget balances projected to remain on track in
2025. Zambia’s budgetary performance has improved under the IMF ECF program. In 2024, increased revenues—although
still below potential—paired with reductions in spending, particularly in public investment, enabled the authorities to
achieve a primary surplus of 3.1 percent of GDP. However, this surplus also reflected tight domestic financing conditions.
The authorities revised the 2025 budget to account for arrears repayments and additional interest expenses, resulting in
an estimated increase in the overall deficit by 1.1 percentage points to 4.6 percent of GDP (Table 2). Still, fiscal discipline
persisted into 2025, as the overall deficit stood at 1.0 percent of GDP, while the primary balance reached 1.7 percent of
GDP in 2025H1—significantly surpassing the 0.9 percent target set by the IMF program—due to higher-than-projected
revenues and under-execution of investment, particularly foreign-financed projects. Public investments were under-
executed in 2025H1 (50.2 percent below target), also partly due to a shortfall in financing; however, social spending and
debt service targets were met. Net domestic financing amounted to 1.6 percent of GDP, as external disbursements lagged.
As Zambia proceeds with debt restructuring, it remains dependent on external concessional financing to address the fiscal
deficit.
15.     Zambia's external public and publicly guaranteed (PPG) debt on a contractual basis declined to US$20.9 billion
by the end of 2024, representing 88.2 percent of GDP (Table 3). The decrease reflects the haircut from the completed
Eurobond exchange and a principal repayment of US$336.3 million on the restructured bond in June 2024. Fuel arrears to
non-resident suppliers and ZESCO’s arrears to external Independent Power Producers (IPP) slightly declined as the GRZ paid
US$221 million of the outstanding amounts. The debt stock in 2024 reflects an increase of approximately US$1.02 billion in
new foreign-currency disbursements from the IMF and the World Bank. Meanwhile, holdings of domestic-currency debt by
nonresidents fell in dollar terms to US$2.1 billion by end-2024, partly due to limits on primary market purchases and
exchange rate depreciation. Total domestic debt decreased by 3.1 percent and reached 48.4 percent of GDP in 2024 (Table
3), owing to financing constraints.

their deposits, reflecting structural constraints on intermediation. Sector stability remains, supported by robust capital
buffers and enhanced asset quality. As of August 2025, the capital adequacy ratio stood at 25 percent and the common

22.       The macroeconomic outlook faces considerable downside risks. Climate change and global trade uncertainties
remain major risks, threatening agricultural output, hydroelectric power supply, and mining investment prospects. While
the mining sector will continue to support FX inflows and tax revenues, Zambia’s overreliance on it poses significant
volatility risks. Inflation risks driven by food and fuel prices, coupled with exchange rate pressures, could reduce domestic
demand and economic growth. Fiscal revenue could underperform because the narrow tax base exacerbates revenue
volatility during negative shocks. Higher-than-anticipated government spending and associated financing needs may arise
from social spending triggered by climate shocks or unforeseen election-related spending. Upside risks include higher
copper prices and renewed mining investments, which might enhance confidence and support growth.
23.      Zambia’s public debt is assessed as sustainable but remains at high risk of overall and external debt distress. The
latest available DSA (January 2026) is based on a full post-restructuring macro-framework and includes official creditors'
treated debt, the completed Eurobond exchange, and the agreements in principle reached with four major external
commercial creditors. It assumes that remaining claims from the rest of the creditors will align with the authorities'
restructuring strategy and are considered resolved for DSA purposes due to their minor share and ongoing negotiations.
These measures are projected to yield favorable outcomes. Under the baseline scenario with weak debt-carrying capacity,
by 2027, the present value of the external debt-to-exports ratio is expected to fall below the 84 percent threshold necessary
to provide “substantial space to absorb shocks.”5 However, the debt service-to-revenue ratio is projected to breach the 14
percent threshold in 2025, while the 2026-31 average ratio is projected at 13.4 percent. Despite a prolonged breach of the
present value of PPG external debt-to-GDP ratio until 2036, Zambia’s debt indicators are projected to improve, consistent
with achieving a moderate risk of external debt distress in the medium term (Figure 1). Shocks to exports, revenue, and
growth would present downside risks to the debt outlook.

 C. IMF Relations

24.      In January 2026, the Fund’s Executive Board approved the sixth and final review of the ECF, allowing for
immediate disbursement of US$190 million and US$1.7 billion cumulatively since its approval in August 2022. The
program’s performance has been satisfactory. All end-June 2025 quantitative performance criteria (QPC) and indicative
targets (ITs) were met, except for the QPC on net international reserves and the IT on spending arrears clearance. Eight out
of nineteen structural benchmarks were met, and six additional ones were completed with delays. The Executive Board also
granted a waiver for the nonobservance of the QPC on net international reserves at end-June 2025. The World Bank and
the IMF continue to collaborate closely, coordinating policy dialogue, analysis, and technical assistance. The reforms
proposed in this DPF complement and reinforce measures under the completed IMF program, the latter focusing on
revenue mobilization, PFM, transparency, and financial stability. The authorities have requested a successor ECF
arrangement and plan to initiate discussions with the Fund soon. The IMF and the World Bank emphasize supporting the
authorities in maintaining macroeconomic stability, building resilience to climate shocks, and promoting job-rich, private-
sector-led growth.

nearly 200,000 jobs for women by 2028. These efforts are supported and informed by the GEWEL 2 (P181391) and Scaling-
Up Shock Responsive Social Protection (P179095) projects.
     Policy Area: Climate-resilient water supply

 DPF 2 Prior Action #9: To ensure the water sector financial viability and a stable and climate-resilient water supply, the Recipient
 has, through its Ministry of Water Development and Sanitation, submitted to Parliament the Water Supply and Sanitation Services
 Bill, which establishes: (i) the objective of maintaining cost-reflective tariffs for all water supply and sanitation utilities; and (ii)
 NWASCO’s regulatory autonomy in determining the criteria for setting and approving tariffs

58.      Rationale and DPF1 Progress: Since 2016, Zambia’s Commercial Water Utilities (CUs) have faced financial strain
due to problems with tariff reviews and rising electricity costs. Revenue and cost recovery have fallen sharply, leaving CUs
reliant on public funds through concessional loans and limited grants for both operational and capital needs. Tariffs haven't
changed since 2019, creating an unsustainable situation as operational costs—including fuel, chemicals, and electricity—
have surged.36 From 2019 to 2024, O&M costs nearly doubled while total billing rose only 21 percent, and CU debt increased
fivefold (US$95 million by end-2024). In 2024, water provision averaged 17.55 Zambian Kwacha (ZMW)/m³, but was sold at
only ZMW 7.93/m³, worsening fiscal shortfalls. Unsustainable operating cost-coverage ratios lead to insufficient
maintenance funding, which shortens asset life, increases future expenses, and heightens climate vulnerability. Combined,
these issues delay water and sanitation improvements and negatively affect health, economic growth, and gender equity.
59.      Substance: The GRZ has presented to Parliament amendments to the Water Supply and Sanitation Act, outlining
principles, criteria, and procedures for establishing a new water tariff structure, as well as processes for review and
determination (DPF2 Prior Action #9). The proposed legislation specifies the responsibilities and independence of the
National Water and Sanitation Council (NWASCO), including its role in developing tariff-setting guidelines, vetting tariff
applications, and granting approvals. A cost of services study is planned for 2026, funded through the Water Supply and
Sanitation Services in Growth Centers Program for Results (P509550), which will supply NWASCO with data, analytical
resources, and information to create a multi-year tariff framework.
60.      Expected results: The reforms are designed to boost NWASCO’s autonomy and efficiency, enabling improved water
and sanitation pricing and predictable revenue for CUs. A multi-year tariff framework will ensure sufficient cost-coverage
ratios and financial sustainability of CUs, enabling greater investment in infrastructure and climate resilience. These changes
will promote energy efficiency, reduce non-revenue water, expand service coverage, and lower greenhouse gas emissions
by decreasing reliance on charcoal. The overall objective is to achieve more equitable and climate-resilient access to water
and sanitation, thereby creating direct and indirect employment opportunities and boosting economic growth. The Water
Supply and Sanitation Services in Growth Centers Program for Results (P509550) supports these efforts. Finally, the reforms
are expected to make the water sector more attractive for PPP arrangements, reinforcing reforms under Pillar B.

 IV. OTHER DESIGN AND APPRAISAL ISSUES
 A. Poverty and Social Impacts

63.      The four proposed policy reforms under Pillar A are expected to yield indirect but meaningful poverty and social
impacts. The first reform (PA#1) enhances fiscal transparency and predictability, foundational to establishing fiscal rules
that generate budgetary room for pro-poor and pro-jobs fiscal policy. PA#2 and PA#3 strengthen fiscal risk management
related to SOEs: the credit risk assessment framework for loans and sovereign guarantees (PA#2) and limiting preferential
financing for SOEs (PA#3) together promote more disciplined and efficient public resource allocation, potentially freeing up
funds for critical social sectors, especially since high-risk SOEs are not concentrated in these areas. Taken together, these
measures strengthen oversight and discipline across the SOE portfolio, though some adjustment channels—such as shifts
in investment plans, employment, or SME linkages—may merit monitoring. Lastly, PA#4 aims to mobilize non-debt
financing and generate carbon revenues, with the potential to support local development if benefit-sharing mechanisms
and safeguards are effectively implemented. The four reforms in the pillar contribute to a more equitable and sustainable
fiscal environment, which, in turn, can improve poverty and inequality outcomes.
64.      Pillar B reforms are not expected to have direct negative impacts in the short term and can yield positive
distributional and poverty impacts in the medium term, primarily through indirect channels. The PPP reform (PA#5)
supports private capital inflows and has the potential to create jobs and stimulate economic growth, with distributional
effects depending on the sectoral composition of jobs created and the affordability of services for lower-income
households. Monitoring inclusion and affordability risks will be important as new PPP investments are implemented. The
renewable energy reform (PA#6) is anticipated to deliver direct benefits through job creation and improved access to
reliable, climate-resilient energy. These gains can enhance productivity and living conditions, particularly for underserved
communities, contributing to long-term poverty reduction and more equitable development outcomes. Impact could be
stronger if the reforms also help close existing household-level access gaps.
65.     The three reforms under pillar C are expected to deliver important poverty and distributional benefits,
particularly for vulnerable and hazard-prone populations. The Disaster Risk Management Amendment Bill (PA#7)
promotes a shift from reactive to proactive disaster response, improving the timeliness and quality of interventions and
reducing welfare losses for households frequently affected by climate-induced hazards. The reform includes details on
targeting strategy, institutional capacity, and coordination, all essential to ensuring that support reaches the most
vulnerable communities. The interoperable social registry reform (PA#8) strengthens adaptive social protection by enabling
rapid and accurate targeting of assistance during climate shocks. Once functional, it can yield significant poverty-reduction
impacts by minimizing income and consumption losses and protecting jobs—especially for women—provided that
coordination, data governance, and community-level delivery mechanisms are effectively established.
66.    The water sector reform offers a largely positive outlook for poverty and distributional impacts (PA#9) with some
nuances. By increasing the regulator's (NWASCO's) autonomy and adopting cost-reflective tariffs, the reform supports

design to ensure affordability for vulnerable groups. Overall, the reform is likely to strengthen sector viability and service
quality with minimal harm to the poor.

 B. Environmental, Forests, and other Natural Resources Aspects

67.     The policies supported under Pillar A of this operation are not expected to have significant adverse impacts on
Zambia’s environment, forests, or other natural resources. Instead, they will positively contribute to climate resilience,
lower carbon emissions, and sustainable natural resource management. The environmental and social impacts of the
proposed prior actions are broadly positive, reinforcing fiscal stability, climate resilience, and social protection. Under Pillar
A, the reporting of non-mining fiscal balances (PA#1) strengthens fiscal discipline and transparency, indirectly improving
environmental governance and ensuring predictable funding for sustainable development and social programs. The
issuance of a credit risk assessment circular (PA#2) enhances oversight of loans and guarantees to SOEs, reducing fiscal
exposure to poorly governed projects that could otherwise generate negative environmental or social impacts. Similarly,
the submission of an SOE Bill to Parliament (PA#3) will promote corporatization, transparency, and competitive neutrality,
discouraging preferential treatment and subsidization of environmentally or socially harmful enterprises. The issuance of
carbon market regulations (PA#4) will create a robust framework for trading carbon credits, incentivizing emission
reductions and climate-friendly investments that yield positive environmental outcomes and support green livelihoods.
68.     Pillar B prior actions are not expected to harm the natural environment. Adopting new PPP regulations and
climate-resilient guidelines (PA#5) supports sustainable, climate-adaptive infrastructure by integrating environmental
measures and reducing social impacts. Approving a competitive procurement framework for renewable energy and
updating the national grid code (PA#6) encourages private investment in clean energy, diversifies power sources, reduces
hydropower dependence, and promotes climate resilience, green jobs, and energy access. These policies do not directly
fund or procure renewable energy infrastructure, so supply-chain risks are not involved. Any future renewable energy
projects must comply with Zambia’s legal standards and World Bank ESF requirements, including labor, working conditions,
and supply-chain due diligence.
69.      Pillar C reforms advance Zambia’s green growth agenda, enhance resilience to climate shocks, and strengthen
the capacity of public institutions to deliver environmentally sustainable and socially inclusive outcomes. The
amendment of the DRM Act (PA#7) establishes a multi-hazard early warning system that enhances institutional and
community preparedness, reducing vulnerability and losses from climate-induced disasters. The approval of an
interoperable social registry (PA#8) improves the efficiency and accuracy of social protection targeting, enabling rapid
support to poor and vulnerable households during climate shocks and strengthening social resilience. PA#9 supports cost-
reflective tariffs and regulatory autonomy, strengthening water utilities’ finances and enabling more sustainable, climate-
resilient, and inclusive water and sanitation services.

 C. PFM, Disbursement, and Auditing aspects

70.     The authorities are improving fiduciary, procurement, and budgeting. While there has been progress in tax
administration, budgeting, treasury, and cash management reforms, challenges remain in internal controls, external audit,
and financial reporting. Zambia ranks well in public finance transparency (central government), debt management, budget
preparation, and legislative and audit scrutiny, as noted in recent 2025 PEFA.
71.     GRZ has implemented several procurement reforms supported by a comprehensive legal and regulatory
framework, including the Public Procurement Act No. 8 of 2020 (as amended) and the Public Procurement Regulations of
2022. These establish the Zambia Public Procurement Authority (ZPPA) as an independent regulator with oversight
responsibilities and mandate the use of the e-GP system for public procurement. The World Bank collaborates with GRZ to
monitor procurement performance, enhance public investment management efficiency, and streamline administrative
procedures to reduce processing times. Additionally, the ZPPA has launched a data analytics portal that leverages e-GP data
to improve transparency, integrity, and evidence-based reforms in public procurement.
72.     MOFNP timely publishes the annual budget on its website.37 It publishes a green paper summarizing the proposed
medium-term budget plan and annual national budget a month before submitting the annual budget to Parliament. The
authorities also issue a white paper on the medium-term budget plan after Parliament has approved the annual national
budget.
73.      PFM reforms have continued. MoFNP, in liaison with Smart Zambia Institute, launched an initiative to build the
national digital ecosystem comprising (i) a National Service Bus - Government Service Bus, (ii) a payment gateway, and (iii)
a public service portal as part of the revenue reforms. The digital ecosystem addresses the following challenges: weak
internal controls and financial reporting, delayed banking, loss of revenue, misapplication and misappropriation of funds,
and failure to monitor in real time the remittance of revenues. This ongoing PFM reform is being supported by development
partners.
74.       Disbursements will follow the World Bank’s procedures for DPF. The single tranche DPF for US$45 million will be
disbursed once the operation is approved, and the Financing Agreement has become effective. The grant proceeds will be
made available to the Recipient upon fulfilling the withdrawal tranche release condition to be indicated in the Financing
Agreement. The DPF proceeds from the World Bank will be transferred to a US$ BOZ account that forms part of the foreign
exchange reserves. The Recipient is expected to report to the IDA within 30 days after the withdrawal of the financing on
(i) the exact sum received into the US$ BOZ account, (ii) the details of the account to which the Kwacha equivalent of the
financing proceeds will be credited, (iii) the record that an equivalent amount has been accounted for in the Recipient’s
budget management systems, and (iv) the statement of receipts and disbursement of the foreign reserve account.
Disbursements of the credit will not be linked to any specific purchases, and no procurement requirements must be
satisfied.
75.     The central bank undertakes to maintain a flexible exchange rate as a primary shock absorber while mitigating

 V. SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION

77.      The overall residual risk rating for the operation is substantial. Table 5 shows that substantial macroeconomic,
governance, technical design, and institutional capacity risks challenge the achievement of the PDO. While mitigation
measures such as fiscal reforms, technical assistance, and stakeholder engagement are in place, significant vulnerabilities
persist. In addition, DPF1 included a Cat-DDO that requires progress in the reform program to remain eligible for
disbursement, which will be regularly supervised by the Bank.
78.     Political and governance risk is substantial. Zambia continues to face political and governance risks stemming from
constraints in public-sector management, institutional capacity, and the legal framework. These factors can affect
accountability and the pace and effectiveness of policy implementation. In 2024, Zambia’s Country Policy and Institutional
Assessment (CPIA) score was 3.3, slightly above the Sub-Saharan Africa IDA average of 3.1. The governance cluster—
covering public sector management and institutions—scored 2.9, in line with the regional average, though below higher-
performing peers such as Rwanda (3.9) and Kenya (3.6). Within this cluster, transparency, accountability, and corruption in
the public sector received the lowest score (2.5), indicating areas that require further progress. Challenges related to public
procurement and the judiciary persist. The electoral cycle this year may add uncertainty and affect reform sequencing.
Three prior actions include bills submitted to Parliament, and the intended reforms may take time to materialize. The
government has demonstrated a commitment to transparency and fiscal sustainability, but progress remains constrained
by administrative capacity. Broad stakeholder consultations, increasing public engagement around reforms, and continued
World Bank support help mitigate these risks.
79.       Macroeconomic risk is substantial. The GRZ has demonstrated a strong commitment to economic reform, with a
credible track record in implementing challenging policy measures. Fiscal consolidation has led to primary budget surpluses,
and economic activity is robust, aided by effective monetary tightening that has gradually eased inflation. Following ongoing
debt treatment, Zambia’s debt-to-GDP ratio is projected to decline over the medium term, indicating improved debt
sustainability. However, significant macroeconomic risks persist, particularly from external shocks such as commodity price
volatility and adverse climatic events, which could disrupt the country’s economic trajectory and program objectives. Delays
in implementing debt restructuring could add interest costs. The continued support of multilateral development banks
through highly concessional net financial flows is expected to be critical for Zambia’s debt restructuring and risk mitigation,
but ongoing vigilance and prudent macroeconomic management remain essential to preserve recent gains and ensure

80.       Technical design risk of the program is rated substantial. Achieving certain reforms involves significant technical

tariffs, and designing robust social registry and DRM systems. These challenges are partially addressed through planned

81.     Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability risk is rated as substantial. Capacity gaps, rules, and

technical assistance from the World Bank and other development partners, as well as support for the objectives and policy
actions of the DPO through other projects and technical assistance, further help address these challenges.

 Fund Relations Note

IMF Executive Board Completes Sixth Review Under the Extended Credit Facility with Zambia
January 28, 2026

    •   The IMF Executive Board completed the sixth and final review under the 38-month Extended Credit Facility
        Arrangement, which has supported Zambia’s reform agenda aimed at restoring macroeconomic stability, building
        economic resilience, and promoting sustainable and inclusive growth.
    •   Zambia’s economy has shown economic strength amid external and domestic shocks. Continued reforms are
        essential to safeguard macroeconomic stability, and debt and fiscal sustainability.
    •   With the ECF arrangement having been successfully completed, policy focus in 2026 remains on maintaining fiscal
        discipline and policy credibility to consolidate the hard-earned gains.

Washington, DC: The Executive Board of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) completed today the sixth and final review
of Zambia’s 38-month Extended Credit Facility (ECF) Arrangement, approved on August 31, 2022. The completion of this
review allows for an immediate disbursement of SDR 138.9 million (about US$190 million), bringing Zambia’s total
disbursement under the ECF-supported program to SDR 1,271.66 million (about US$1.7 billion).

Program performance has been broadly satisfactory, albeit with some delays on structural conditionality. All end-June 2025
quantitative performance criteria (QPC) and indicative targets (ITs) were met, except for the QPC on net international
reserves and the IT on spending arrears clearance. Eight out of nineteen structural benchmarks (SBs) were met, and six
additional SBs were completed with delays. The submission to Parliament of the revised Banking and Financial Services Act
(BFSA), broadly aligned with international standards, satisfied the prior action set for this review. The Executive Board also
granted a waiver for the nonobservance of the QPC on net international reserves at end-June 2025.

Zambia’s economic outlook remains positive. Real GDP growth is estimated at 5.2 percent in 2025, underpinned by strong
mining activities and record-high maize production. Real GDP growth in 2026 is projected at 5.8 percent on the back of
continued recovery in electricity generation and strong performance in mining and services. Inflation is projected to
converge gradually toward the 6-8 percent target band by 2027. Notwithstanding heightened global uncertainty, the
medium-term outlook remains favorable and hinges on scaling up mining investment, robust agriculture production,
improved electricity generation, and sustained fiscal discipline. Continued efforts are needed to promote private sector
participation, economic diversification, and more inclusive growth.

Zambia’s public debt is assessed as sustainable but remains at high risk of overall and external debt distress. External debt
restructuring continues to advance as five bilateral agreements with official creditors have been signed, and progress with
commercial creditors is advancing.       Provided that the authorities maintain the projected fiscal consolidation path,
Zambia is expected to reach a moderate risk of external debt distress over the medium term.

Following the Executive Board discussion on Zambia, Mr. Nigel Clarke, Deputy Managing Director and Acting Chair, issued
the following statement:

“Despite external and domestic shocks, Zambia has significantly reduced macroeconomic imbalances, made considerable
progress on debt restructuring, and undertaken sustained fiscal consolidation while safeguarding social spending. The
performance under the program has been broadly satisfactory and the authorities should remain focused on maintaining

prudent macroeconomic policies and advancing reforms to foster inclusive and private-sector-led growth. Continued
engagement with the Fund and development partners would support these policy endeavors.

“Fiscal performance in 2025 remained strong. Fiscal consolidation driven by revenue mobilization remains essential to
consolidate macroeconomic gains, while protecting social spending, and create fiscal space to meet development needs.
The authorities’ focus on strengthening revenue mobilization and enhancing public financial management is appropriate.

“Careful monetary policy calibration to gradually bring inflation toward the target band will be key to anchoring inflation

of the deposit insurance scheme are welcome steps to strengthen financial stability.

“Governance and structural reforms remain vital for promoting private sector activity and supporting diversification and
inclusive growth. The revised Agricultural Acts will enhance predictability and support private sector investment. Continued
efforts to strengthen governance and enhance transparency in the energy sector will improve the business climate and
support sustainable growth. Measures to enhance climate resilience are important to help to safeguard development
gains.”
