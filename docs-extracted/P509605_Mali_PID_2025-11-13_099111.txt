                         B. Introduction & Context
                         Country Context
                         Mali, a Sahelian country, faces significant challenges due to security and political instability, including two military
                         coups between August 2020 and May 2021, leading to a recession in 2020. The six-month Economic Community of
                         West African States sanctions from January to July 2022 further dampened growth and the macro-fiscal outlook. The

                         under-diversified economy remains vulnerable to external shocks, including commodity price volatilities and adverse
                         weather conditions. Mali ranks 184 out of 189 on the 2022 Human Development Index, with more than 40 percent of
                         the population living in poverty, primarily in rural areas. Political tensions and insecurity have increased since 2012,
                         leading to a surge of internally displaced persons (IDPs) and high costs to public finances and resources for delivering
                         public services. In addition, Mali ranks 158 out of 162 on the Gender Inequality Index, with high rates of gender-based
                         violence, early marriage, and childbirth.
                         Mali is among the top seven most vulnerable countries to climate change impacts. By 2100, average temperatures
                         may rise up to 3°C with more rainfall variability. Climate change effects are clear in education: schools reduced hours
                         from April to May 2024 due to extreme heat, and the 2024/25 school year start was delayed from October to November
                         because floods displaced over 500 schools. Nearly 400 schools remained underwater or heavily damaged at the
                         beginning of the school year. The country's demographic, socioeconomic, and environmental factors, including
                         settlements in floodplains and migration towards urban areas, further exacerbate physical vulnerability.

                         Sectoral and Institutional Context
                         Mali has made significant strides in expanding access to education over recent decades, yet it remains one of the




Public Disclosure copy
                         lower-performing countries in Sub-Saharan Africa. The Human Capital Index (HCI) for Mali is among the lowest
                         globally, with a score of 0.32, indicating that a child born today will only be 32 percent as productive as they could be
                         with complete education and full health. The education sector faces substantial challenges, with the average child
                         expected to receive only 5.24 years of schooling, and harmonized test scores significantly lower than those of
                         neighboring countries. Additionally, only 73 percent of children under the age of five are not stunted, impacting their
                         cognitive and physical development. Learning outcomes are among the lowest in the world, with a Learning Poverty
                         rate of 94 percent in 2022, indicating that less than 6 out of 100 children can read and understand a simple age-
                         appropriate text at age 10.
                         Community schools have played a pivotal role in expanding education in Mali, particularly in rural areas. Established
                         and managed by school-based management committees (SBMCs) consisting of parents, teachers, and local
                         government representatives, these schools mobilize resources for construction, maintenance, and teacher
                         recruitment. Despite their success, sustaining this model is challenging due to funding shortages for teacher salaries.
                         Government support often falls short of community needs, weakening the model.
                         Access to and completion of primary education remain low compared to neighboring countries as well. In 2023, the
                         Gross Enrollment Rate (GER) at the national level was 81.3 percent, with a gender disparity where the GER was 83.9
                         percent for boys and 78.5 percent for girls, resulting in a girl-to-boy parity index of 0.94. Regional disparities are
                         significant, with regions like Segou and Mopti experiencing declines in GER from 2019 to 2023. The learning crisis in
                         Mali is deeply rooted in significant accessibility challenges, particularly in rural areas where children often must walk
                         long distances, sometimes up to 7 kilometers, to reach the nearest school. Upon arrival, they frequently encounter
                         overcrowded classrooms with insufficient resources, which hampers their ability to learn effectively. These hardships
                         disproportionately affect girls, who are often excluded from educational opportunities.
                         Rapid population growth is expected to continue straining the education system, highlighting the urgent need for
                         cost-effective school infrastructure programs. According to the UN World Population Prospects (2022), Mali's school-
                         age population is projected to grow by an additional 200,000 to 300,000 children annually until 2050. Many schools
                         built between 1995 and 2005 are nearing the end of their functional lifespan and will soon need to be replaced. On
                         the other hand, school construction projects often face significant challenges, including delays, cost overruns, and
                         ongoing concerns about quality. Issues such as the absence of personal protective equipment, inadequate protective
                         barriers at construction sites, and difficulties in implementing the Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP)
                         have been reported, further complicating the effective oversight and quality assurance of these structures. The
                         absence of key stakeholders, such as community leaders and parents, in the decision-making and supervision process

                         Relationship to CPF
                         The proposed project is fully aligned with both national and regional priorities. It directly supports the third strategic
                         objective, "Develop Human Capital," from the latest Country Partnership Framework (CPF) for FY16–FY19 (report
                         number 94005-ML) and the second pathway, "Investing in Human Capital Accumulation for All Citizens," from the FY22
                         Systematic Country Diagnostic (SCD[1]). At the regional level, the project aligns with the second pillar, "Expanding
                         Opportunities for Learning," from the Western and Central Africa Education Strategy for FY22. Within Mali's education
                         sector, it specifically aligns with the second pillar, "Inclusive Education," as outlined during the national education forum
                         in January 2024.
                         Both the CPF and CSD emphasize community engagement as a cross-cutting theme for interventions. Community-driven
                         initiatives and targeted resource transfers are identified as the most effective methods for delivering infrastructure and
                         services efficiently. By aligning with these priorities, the project demonstrates a collective commitment to building

                         strategic orientations, ensuring that the needs of local communities are at the forefront of the development process.
                         This approach not only fosters ownership at the grassroots level but also enhances value for money and long-term
                         sustainability.



                         C. Development Objective
                         Development Objective
                         The Project Development Objective is: (i) strengthen community capacity to manage school construction process; and
                         (ii) improve the climate resilience and sustainability of school infrastructure.
                         Key Results
                             •     Communities successfully complete a school construction project (Number)
                             •     Households in project areas involved in participatory decision-making (Number)
                             •     New construction incorporating at least 3 climate-resilient/sustainable factors[1] (Percentage)
                             •     Communities developing a maintenance plan (Percentage)

                         [1] The five criteria are as follows: (a) natural ventilation; (b) locally sourced/sustainable building material; (c) water
                         storage system; (d) flood prevention/adaptation; (e) WASH facility



                         D. Preliminary Description
                         Activities/Components
                         Component 1. Piloting community-based construction models in selected regions (US$2.5 million)
                         Sub-component 1.1. Community-based school construction in 50 villages (US$2.09 million).
                         This subcomponent would fund the construction and equipment of classrooms across 50 villages. To increase the
                         number of potential beneficiaries, the project could use selection criteria such as: (i) existing schools needing
                         additional classrooms due to overcrowding; (ii) extension of primary schools having the potential to become lower


                                                                                                                                                       Page 3
                               The World Bank

                         secondary; and (iii) rehabilitation of existing schools posing a hazard to student and teacher safety. The
                         implementation will be phased. First, 10 sites in the regions of Koulikoro and Bamako will benefit from project
                         activities to test interventions and fine tune the operations manual before scale-up to more remote locations. Given
                         the large number of internally displaced in and around urban centers, this first phase would target areas of
                         resettlement for displaced population. Then, the model will be tested in more remote locations in the regions of
                         Segou and Mopti.
                         Sub-component 1.2. Matching for community fundraising (diaspora) (US$0.41million).
                         This subcomponent will fund a model of school construction that leverages community funds raised from the
                         diaspora, combined with a matching fund from the project, representing a sustainable and collaborative approach to
                         educational development. In this model, members of the diaspora contribute financially to the construction of
                         classrooms in their home communities, fostering a sense of ownership and direct involvement in local development.
                         These contributions are then matched by funds from the project, effectively doubling the resources available for
                         construction. This matching fund mechanism not only incentivizes diaspora contributions but also ensures that the
                         project has a vested interest in the successful completion and quality of the construction. The project will also fund
                         capacity building at the community level for successful fundraising.
                         Component 2. Identifying sustainable school construction approaches (US$1.24 million)




Public Disclosure copy
                         Sub-component 2.1. Resilient school infrastructure design (US$0.23 million).
                         The component will finance an architecture competition for innovative school designs that: (a) prioritize the use of
                         local materials; (b) refurbish existing items such as furniture and building materials. Two criteria will be cross-cutting
                         and guide the selection of the winning designs: (a) climate resilience and reducing the carbon footprint; and (b)
                         fostering community engagement and supporting the local economy. The competition aims to promote creativity and
                         sustainability by utilizing local talent and knowledge. Winning designs will receive an award.
                         Sub-component 2.2. Community-led construction for education transformation (US$1.01 million).
                         The project will finance the implementation of the winning design to transform educational infrastructure, resulting in
                         enhanced facilities that meet modern standards and foster a better learning environment. It will also support training
                         programs for local communities in construction techniques and sustainable practices, empowering them with the
                         skills and knowledge to actively participate in the construction process and maintain the infrastructure sustainably.
                         Additionally, the project will explore partnerships with the national school of engineers, integrating academic
                         expertise with community needs to ensure the project's success and sustainability. Aspiring engineers will receive
                         additional training from the winning architectural team to support community construction efforts and get deployed
                         to respective construction sites to ensure high quality standards and adherence to safety procedures. The project will
                         finance the necessary resources, including construction materials, labor costs, training programs, administrative
                         support, stipends, transportation, accommodation, quality control measures, safety equipment, and regular
                         inspections, to achieve these outcomes.
                         Component 3. Project Management and Administration, Monitoring and Evaluation, and Knowledge
                         Dissemination (US$0.36 million)
                         The MIQRA/PCU will be responsible for the day-to-day implementation of the project activities and may need to
                         procure a small amount of equipment, and consultant services to conduct on-field assessments. Notably, the project
                         will contract experienced technicians for site supervision and technical assistance to be hired in the region of
                         operations. Additionally, the project will fund the position of a communication/community mobilization specialist
                         who would support and/or coordinate community capacity building and needs assessment, as well as a monitoring
                         and evaluation specialist for monitoring the project achievement including setting Kobo toolbox with community, and
                         timely transfer of resources. The project will rely on the fiduciary, environment and social specialists from the
                         MIQRA/PCU. This sub-component will also support the establishment of a grievance mechanism and establish a
                         feedback mechanism to ensure that community input is being considered during implementation.

                         Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                         The project's environmental and social risks are rated as moderate and can be effectively managed through
                         appropriate measures. Environmentally, risks related to air pollution, construction waste generation, and traffic
                         accidents can be minimized by adopting environmentally friendly construction techniques, implementing effective
                         waste management systems, and establishing enhanced safety protocols for transportation. While these risks are
                         present, they are localized and temporary, allowing for effective management. Socially, risks related to insecurity in
                         certain regions, forced labor, child labor, and health and safety at work are also moderate. These risks can be mitigated
                         by implementing security mechanisms in the affected areas, closely monitoring child labor, and providing worker safety
                         and well-being training. Additionally, preventive measures will be put in place to address gender-based violence, sexual
                         abuse, and sexual harassment. In summary, although risks exist, their effective management and mitigation are
                         entirely possible through tailored strategies and rigorous monitoring.
