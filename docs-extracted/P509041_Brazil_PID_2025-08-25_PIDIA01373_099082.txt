                          B. Introduction and Context

                          Project Context

                           The State of Tocantins, established in 1988, has emerged as one of Brazil’s fastest-growing subnational economies,
                           with GDP reaching R$ 65.9 billion in 2024 and growth averaging over 6 percent since 2020. Spanning 277,620 km², the
                           state has a population of 1.5 million distributed unevenly across urban hubs and remote rural areas. Tocantins is a major
                           grain and cattle producer, accounting for 2.6 percent of Brazil’s grain harvest and 5 percent of its cattle herd. The state
                           benefits from 13.8 million hectares of arable land and an expanding agro-industrial base, increasingly oriented toward
                           bioenergy, meat-processing, and cellulose, in addition to hosting a major logistics corridor for Brazil’s broader
                           agricultural operations – the North-South Railroad (FNS). Yet, lagging regions face persistent infrastructure, service, and
                           opportunity deficits, especially among family farmers and vulnerable groups. The state’s rapid economic expansion and
                           strategic logistics position underscore the need for investments that promote inclusive and resilient development.

                            Despite its economic dynamism, Tocantins faces deep-rooted challenges of rural poverty, low productivity, and
                            climate vulnerability. Infrastructure bottlenecks hinder connectivity and reinforce regional inequalities: 20 percent of
                            paved roads are in poor condition, limiting mobility, inflating transport costs, and hindering access to markets and
                            services. These challenges affect disproportionately rural and low-income populations. In Tocantins, nearly 300,000
                            people live in rural areas, with over 40 percent relying on Bolsa Família transfers to meet basic needs.1 Family farming
                            is the main source of livelihood for most rural households, yet productivity remains low and unevenly distributed:
                            although family farms represent more than 70 percent of rural establishments, they generate only 16.9 percent of farm
                            output.2 Limited access to credit (13.2 percent), technical assistance (10 percent), and market certifications (accessed
                            by less than 20 percent) constrains their capacity to invest, expand, or adopt climate-smart practices.3 Women farmers
                            are especially disadvantaged, facing structural barriers to land, finance, and participation in producer organizations.
                            These challenges contribute to persistent exclusion and have accelerated rural-to-urban migration, shrinking the rural
                            population share from 25.7 to 14.6 percent between 2000 and 2022 – nearly double the national rate. Meanwhile,
                            domestic tourism, particularly in the Jalapão region, offers growing potential for income generation in rural areas,4 but
                            remains underdeveloped, with limited investment, narrow product offerings, and low participation by vulnerable
                            communities such as the Quilombolas. In addition, poorly planned tourism puts pressure on sensitive ecosystems,
                            threatening the sustainability of the very assets that attract visitors. By contrast, more sustainable and inclusive models,
                            such as community-based ecotourism, can help turn tourism into a vehicle for conservation and resilience, making local




_For Official Use Only_
                            livelihoods and environmental stewardship mutually reinforcing.5

                            Climate vulnerability is a central development challenge in Tocantins, reinforcing the urgency of building resilience
                            across sectors. Situated between the Amazon and Cerrado biomes – both ecologically sensitive and climate-exposed –
                            the state is already experiencing shifts6 in rainfall and temperature patterns that affect agricultural productivity,
                            infrastructure durability, and the long-term viability of nature-based tourism. Nearly 30 percent of Tocantins’ native
                            vegetation has been lost since 1985, with deforestation and land degradation contributing to rising GHG emissions.
                            Rural populations, especially family farmers, face compounding pressures from degraded pastures, irregular
                            environmental registration, and limited adaptive capacity.7 At the same time, road infrastructure is also exposed to
                            erosion and flooding, especially for settlements that are still not connected to the paved network, as in the case of the
                            isolated municipality of Lizarda. More generally, the national pattern of climate-related infrastructure losses is also
                            observed in Tocantins.8

                            A multi-sectoral investment approach aligns with the strategic priorities of the State of Tocantins and the World
                            Bank’s commitment to inclusive and resilient development. The project builds on the experience of a recently closed
                            World Bank project, the PDRIS,9 and aims to close spatial and sectoral gaps through coordinated investments that
                            enhance sustainable transport connectivity, promote climate-smart agriculture, and unlock tourism’s inclusive
                            potential. It also reflects Brazil’s evolving investment strategies under two ongoing Multiphase Programmatic
                            Approaches (MPAs): the Pro-Roads MPA (P180555), which supports resilient, safe, performance-based road
                            rehabilitation and maintenance contracts (CREMAs), and the Inclusive and Sustainable Agrifood Systems MPA
                            (P180429), which promotes the dissemination of climate-smart agriculture through productive alliances.10 These
                            approaches are adapted in Tocantins to address persistent challenges across rural infrastructure, agricultural
                            productivity, and tourism access, with a focus on climate resilience, social inclusion, and long-term sustainability. The
                            operation is also embedded in a broader context of complementary initiatives, including the World Bank-financed
                            programs Progestão11 and Biomas,12 as well as the forthcoming IADB-supported Tocantins Mais Sustentável project.
                            Together, these programs reinforce the state's capacity to capitalize on operational synergies and deliver integrated
                            territorial development.

                            Tocantins plays a strategic role in Brazil’s logistics landscape, with a state-managed road network that is essential for
                            enabling local access to services and supporting broader economic integration. Its 5,841 km paved road system13 –
                            under jurisdiction of the Tocantins Transport and Infrastructure Agency (AGETO) – connects agricultural production
                            zones in Brazil’s Midwest to national and export markets via key logistics corridors, including the BR-153 highway and
                            the North-South Railway (FNS). Yet, 20 percent of this network is classified as being in poor or very poor condition,14
                            reflecting chronic underfunding of routine maintenance and the cumulative effects of climate-related hazards such as
                            erosion and climate-related hazards. Despite recent investment efforts, a pattern of deferred maintenance followed by
                            costly reconstruction persists,15 undermining infrastructure resilience and driving up logistics costs – especially for rural
                            and low-income populations. State road investments are guided by the State Logistics and Transport Plan (PELT-TO),
                            which was last updated in 2016.

                            sustainability. The state launched a formal PPP program in 2020, managed by a dedicated secretariat (SPI) and backed
                            by a guarantee fund.16 Tocantins has successfully implemented PPPs in sectors such as health and renewable energy,
                            and is now preparing to structure road concessions. Freight-heavy corridors that are used intensively by out-of-state

                            Road safety represents an urgent concern in the State of Tocantins. Between 2019 and 2023, annual road traffic
                            fatalities in Tocantins rose from 490 to 572, with the fatality rate reaching 34.8 per 100,000 inhabitants—more than
                            double the national average.18 Rural areas are especially affected due to poor road conditions, excessive speeds, lack of
                            safe road designs, and underinvestment in safety infrastructure. Vulnerable road users (pedestrians, cyclists) lack
                            adequate protection, especially in urban crossings or nearby road-adjacent dwellings. Despite efforts to implement a
                            statewide road safety policy, data systems, institutional coordination, and enforcement capacity remain limited. The
                            project will address these gaps through both infrastructure and institutional strengthening.

                            Tocantins' agricultural sector has expanded rapidly over the past decade, but its gains remain highly uneven.
                            Agriculture represented 23.7 of the state’s gross value-added in 2022, with soy representing over 70 percent of
                            production and exports. The state’s share of Brazil’s agricultural value-added more than doubled from 2011 to 2021,
                            driven by a tripling in planted area and strong productivity gains. However, these benefits have been concentrated in
                            large commercial farms, deepening inequalities and increasing pressure on the sensitive Cerrado ecosystem. Family

                            farms, defined as establishments below four fiscal modules,19 represent over 70 percent of rural establishments and
                            more than 60 percent of agricultural employment, yet generated just 16.9 percent of farm output in 2017.20 Nearly 80
                            percent of family farmers had limited access to output markets and product certifications. Key value chains for family
                            farmers in Tocantins include small-scale livestock (beef, dairy, poultry, swine, sheep, goats), staple crops (rice, beans,
                            cassava, corn), and fruits and vegetables. This segment is highly heterogeneous, ranging from subsistence producers to
                            more dynamic, commercially oriented small and medium-sized family producers who still require targeted support to
                            expand and upgrade.

                            Public rural services and climate-smart agriculture are essential to unlock the productive potential of family farming
                            in Tocantins. Yet only 10.9 percent of family farmers receive extension services, and just 13.2 percent have access to
                            formal credit – both below national averages.21 The RURALTINS institute, the state’s primary provider of technical
                            assistance and rural extension, plays a central role but faces resources and coverage constraints, limiting its capacity to
                            promote the adoption of climate-smart agricultural practices (CSA), market-oriented production, and environmental
                            compliance. Producer organizations are also weak: nearly 80 percent of family farms had little or no participation in
                            cooperatives or associations in 2017, hampering access to inputs, certifications, and economies of scale. Addressing
                            these gaps will require support to cooperative development, participatory diagnostics, and tailored extension services.

                            D. Project Description

                            The proposed project will be implemented over eight years and is supported by a US$120 million IBRD Investment
                            Project Financing (IPF), complemented by US$30 million in counterpart financing from the State of Tocantins and an
                            estimated US$7.7 million in private capital mobilization (PCM). The longer implementation period follows established
                            good practice in similar CREMA projects across Brazil, aligning with the full duration of long-term performance-based
                            contracts and their associated payment schedules. The Government has also expressed interest in seeking additional
                            financing to scale up activities once implementation is underway. A detailed cost breakdown is presented in subsequent
                            paragraphs. Annex 2 presents a map of Tocantins with the geographic distribution of selected interventions.

                            Component 1 – Proactive Maintenance, Resilient and Safe Road Infrastructure (US$111.1 million, of which US$ 80
                            million IBRD, US$25 million counterpart financing, and an estimated US$6.1 million PCM). Component 1 aims to
                            strengthen the climate resilience, safety, and sustainability of Tocantins’ state road network, ensuring reliable year-
                            round access to markets, services, and economic opportunities. It will support the upgrade, restoration and long-term
                            maintenance of key state road corridors through performance-based contracts, including both long-term CREMA and
                            pioneering PPP modalities. Complementary investments will enhance road safety in urban and rural areas, increase




_For Official Use Only_
                            climate resilience in critical network segments, and address environmental liabilities. Institutional strengthening
                            activities will equip AGETO with tools, systems, and skills for better road asset management, road safety management,
                            planning, and climate adaptation.

                            Subcomponent 1.1 – Climate-resilient road improvement, rehabilitation, and maintenance based on performance-based,
                            long-term (8-10 years) CREMA contracts (US$ 104.2 million, of which US$ 73.1 million IBRD, US$ 25 million counterpart
                            financing, and an estimated US$ 6.1 million PCM). This subcomponent will finance road rehabilitation and performance-
                            based maintenance contracts (CREMA) across approximately 671 km of Tocantins’ paved road network, divided into
                            three lots.30 It also includes 88 km of road upgrading works to connect the Municipality of Lizarda – one of the poorest
                            municipalities and the only one not connected by paved road. These investments aim to restore road quality, enhance
                            year-round connectivity, and improve user safety, while transferring responsibility for upkeep to contractors through
                            long-term, performance-based contracts. Climate adaptation measures, such as improved drainage, slope stabilization,
                            and erosion control, will be integrated into designs. The project will finance detailed engineering studies, safeguard
                            documentation, and supervision of works.

                            Subcomponent 1.2 – Road safety management capacity and infrastructure (US$ 4.4 million IBRD). This subcomponent
                            will finance targeted interventions to improve road safety conditions across the State. Activities include (i) civil works to
                            implement safer pedestrian infrastructure, cycling paths, traffic calming measures, and drainage improvements in
                            selected urban areas; (ii) infrastructure upgrades along high-risk corridors; and (iii) leasing of road safety equipment
                            such as radars, variable message signs, and mobile weigh stations. It will also support the establishment of a Lead Road
                            Safety Agency informed by a Strategic Action Plan (financed under Component 4) to improve road safety management

                            capacity building for key agencies, particularly AGETO and SPI, building on the institutional experience gained under the

                            Component 2 – Strengthening support to family agriculture (US$ 30.6 million, of which US$ 26 million IBRD and US$
                            3 million counterpart financing, and US$ 1.6 million PCM). Component 2 aims to improve the productivity, climate
                            resilience, and market access of family farmers across Tocantins through a multi-pronged intervention strategy.
                            Activities will be statewide in scope and implemented by SEAGRO in close coordination with RURALTINS, Tocantins’
                            decentralized public agency for technical assistance and rural extension. The component will promote the adoption of
                            climate-smart agriculture (CSA), the productive inclusion of vulnerable groups, and the development of sustainable
                            value chains. Key interventions include: (i) expansion of rural public services; (ii) training and capacity-building for
                            producers and technicians; (iii) matching grants for on-farm investments; (iv) deployment of Technological and

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts


                           The environmental risk of the Project is rated as Substantial. The precise location, scope and magnitude of diverse
                           subprojects have not been defined, and the risks and impacts cannot be fully determined until the program and
                           subproject details have been concluded. These impacts are expected to be site-specific, few if any of them irreversible,
                           and in most cases mitigatory measures can be readily designed and implemented.




_For Official Use Only_
                           Component 1 will support the maintenance of 350 km of state highways, with long-term performance-based road
                           management contracts and the development of private public partnerships of state highway sections, in different
                           parts of the State. The roads’ maintenance works may pose significant environmental impacts and risks, inherent to
                           heavy construction works, (e.g. excavation, earth movement, and paving). The road rehabilitation and performance-
                           based maintenance contracts pose potential risks to biodiversity and habitats. Vegetation removal may be necessary
                           for the widening and implementation of legally required ROWs (20m buffer zone each side of the road), as well as for
                           the upgrading/paving of the access road to the Municipality of Lizarda. However, the project is not expected to result in
                           significant conversion of habitats or major impacts to local biodiversity as most roads already have well stablished ROWs
                           and vegetation clearing been not expected to be significant.

                           The CREMA contracts in the Jalapão Ecological Corridor do not include construction works within full protection
                           conservation units. Only one road segment selected for the CREMA maintenance program crosses a sustainable use
                           conservation unit (IUCN class V), namely, the Jalapão Protection Area, (APA). This road was developed prior to the
                           creation of the APA and the Jalapão State Park, and is recognized by the Jalapão Park Management Plan, as the main
                           access road to the Jalapão region. The rehabilitation/maintenance works of the Jalapão road system, to be funded by
                           the Project, shall not result in significant negative impacts to the environment, as they will be limited to rights of way.
                           In fact, the rehabilitation/maintenance works may result in environmental benefits, reducing the risk of soil erosion
                           (e.g., gullies), caused by the degradation of existing roads. The improvement of the road system in the Jalapão may
                           result in indirect impacts to the environment, increasing the number of visitors to the region, but this action is in line
                           with the Jalapão Ecological Corridor objectives, considering the social and economic aspects and acknowledging the
                           importance of the ecological tourism in the region.

                           The social risk classification is Moderate. Direct and indirect benefits are expected from the implementation of the
                           Project, especially by increasing the earnings and improving the livelihoods of 12,000 beneficiary family farmers
                           (including Indigenous Peoples, Quilombola and Traditional Communities) and by improving the state's logistical capacity
