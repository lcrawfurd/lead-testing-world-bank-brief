    I.    STRATEGIC CONTEXT
A. Country Context
1.        Poverty has increased slightly amid recent shocks, despite some economic stabilization.
Pakistan made significant progress towards reducing poverty between 2001 and 2018 with the expansion
of off-farm economic opportunities and increased external remittances. However, this has not fully
translated into improved socio-economic conditions: over one-third of school-age children across Pakistan
were found to be out of school; nearly two-thirds of those in school in fiscal year (FY)24 were learning
deprived; and alarmingly high rates of stunting (40 percent in FY23) persist. Critical constraints, including
recurrent fiscal and current account deficits, protectionist trade policies, unproductive agriculture, a
difficult business environment, a heavy state presence in the economy, and a financially unsustainable
energy sector, have remained largely unaddressed, leading to slow and volatile growth. Amid the COVID-
19 pandemic, the catastrophic 2022 floods and macroeconomic volatility, poverty has increased. The
estimated lower-middle income poverty rate is 40.5 percent (US$3.65/day 2017 purchasing power parity)
for FY24 with an additional 2.6 million Pakistanis falling below the poverty line from the year before.
2.      Pakistan experienced heavy monsoon rains in 2022 leading to catastrophic and unprecedented
flooding with enormous human and economic impacts. Roughly 33 million people were impacted, and
many permanently displaced. More than 13,000 kilometers (km) of roads were destroyed, 2.2 million
houses damaged, around 3.8 million hectares of crops were flooded, and an estimated 1.2 million
livestock were killed. Limited access to input and output markets and temporary disruptions to supply
chains subsequently drove up food prices and added to existing price pressures resulting from reduced
agricultural yields and the global rise of food prices. The government’s Post-Disaster Needs Assessment 1
estimated that the need for rehabilitation and reconstruction is at US$16.3 billion.
3.       Pakistan has made recent progress towards macroeconomic stabilization, but risks remain
extremely high and faster sustained growth will require substantial reform. At the beginning of FY24,
Pakistan's economy faced a potential economic crisis in the face of political uncertainty, global monetary
policy tightening, and fiscal and external imbalances, that led to pressures on domestic prices and foreign
reserves. To preserve reserves, measures to manage imports and capital outflows were introduced, which
disrupted local supply chains, economic activity and exacerbated inflationary pressures. Under the interim
government, an International Monetary Fund (IMF) Stand-By Arrangement was approved in July 2023.
Consequently, exchange rate flexibility was restored, import controls were relaxed, and steps were taken
to contain the fiscal deficit. Political uncertainty also diminished with the successful conduct of the general
elections. Coupled with favorable weather conditions and easing external conditions, the economy began
recovering in FY24. Consequently, growth of real gross domestic product (GDP) at factor cost is estimated
to have risen to 2.5 percent year on year in FY24, after contracting by 0.2 percent in FY23. Downside risks
remain high, with the outlook predicated on the new IMF Extended Fund Facility program remaining on
track, continued fiscal restraint, and additional external financing. Heavy banking sector exposure to the
sovereign, domestic policy uncertainty, and geopolitical instability pose significant risks to the outlook.
Robust economic recovery over the medium term will require the steadfast implementation of much
broader fiscal and economic reforms.

4.     According to the 2022 Country Climate and Development Report (CCDR), Pakistan’s high
vulnerability to climate change is a risk multiplier, compounding its human and economic development
challenges. Pakistan consistently ranks among the top 10 countries most affected by climate change
worldwide. 2 More frequent and extreme weather events adversely impact ecosystems, people, and their
settlements and infrastructure. Droughts, flooding, and heat waves are prevalent risks. Coastal cities such
as Karachi are also vulnerable to climate change-induced sea level rise, cyclones, saltwater intrusion, and
coastal erosion. The risks from climate change and environmental degradation could further aggravate
Pakistan’s economic fragility and ultimately reduce annual GDP by 18 to 20 percent per year by 2050.
5.      Pakistan also faces an unfinished agenda on human development, which compounds its
structural macro fragility and creates a drag on the economy. The country currently ranks 141 out of 174
on the Human Capital Index, scoring 41 out of 100. The stunting rate of children under five remains among
the highest in the world at 40 percent. Evidence suggests that the lack of safely managed water supply
and sanitation and basic hygiene (WASH) services is partly responsible for this staggering loss of human
potential. 3
B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
6.      Safely managed WASH services are the foundation of public health and quality of life and are
central to addressing the stunting crisis in Pakistan. However, the country lags on WASH service
indicators because of decades of underinvestment. Close to 126 million people (half of the population)
do not receive safely managed water services, 166 million (three-quarters of the population) do not have
access to safely managed sanitation services, and 36 million lack basic hygiene services. Data from the
Pakistan Council of Research in Water Resources showed that 61 percent of drinking water samples tested
nationwide failed to meet national drinking water quality standards in 2020. 4
7.      WASH is a provincial mandate, and provinces are at different stages in addressing service gaps.
Punjab and Sindh, the two largest provinces, have made commitments to address service gaps and have
enabling policies and plans in place, such as the Punjab Water Policy 2018, Punjab WASH Sector
Development Plan (2014-2024), Sindh Water Policy 2023, and Sindh WASH Sector Development Plan
(2016-2026). Both provinces have also passed laws to enable service providers to become financially
sustainable. In Punjab’s secondary cities, WASH services are the mandate of local governments. The
Punjab Water Act (2019) strengthened the regulatory framework for water service delivery. Similarly, the
recently enacted Karachi Water and Sewerage Corporation Act, 2023 converted the Karachi Water and
Sewerage Board into a corporate entity with enhanced financial and managerial autonomy. However,
massive capital injection is needed to address infrastructure gaps in all provinces.
8.       Traditional investment approaches have been too small and fragmented. The 2014 Punjab
WASH Sector Development Plan identified the need for annual spending of PKR 40 billion to provide two-
thirds of the population with safely managed WASH services by 2024. However, actual spending was
insufficient and focused mainly on infrastructure, neglecting operation and maintenance (O&M) and
service delivery. Sindh did not sufficiently finance its WASH Sector Development Plan (2016-2026), and
service indicators have continued to worsen. Low and unpredictable budget have led to ad hoc
engagements. The absence of systematic monitoring undermined accountability and limited the ability of

to address service delivery issues, including WASH, by investing in infrastructure and transforming the
performance of service providers such as the Karachi Water and Sewerage Corporation (KWSC).
10.     The KWSC is the largest water utility in Pakistan, and, given the size and role of Karachi as
Pakistan’s economic and financial hub, its performance is of national significance. The KWSC provides
WASH services to about 17 million people, almost 84 percent of Karachi’s population. Service hours are
intermittent and range from two hours every two days to four hours per day with widespread rationing.
Many Katchi Abadis 6 are unconnected and rely on informal water sources. Non-revenue water (NRW) is
around 64 percent in part due to insufficient hydraulic capacity and leakages. The sewer network covers
only about 75 percent of the city, with aging pipes, insufficient capacity, malfunctioning pumping facilities,
and a lack of treatment capacity. The city’s three wastewater treatment plants operate at one-third of
capacity, treating 50 million gallons per day (MGD) of the estimated 472 MGD generated.
11.     Karachi and the KWSC’s water supply challenges are compounded by high and increasing
competition for water (especially for Indus River water), a deteriorated and complex bulk water supply
system, and the lack of regional water resources planning. Karachi’s primary water source is the Indus
River, which also supplies the province’s extensive irrigation system and drinking water outside of Karachi.
Currently, Karachi faces a water supply deficit of about 580 MGD, 7 but increasing supply requires
investment and coordination. However, there is no water resources management plan or allocation
mechanism within Sindh, nor are there formal operating rules for critical infrastructure conveying water
to Karachi, which is mostly managed by the Sindh Irrigation Department.
12.      From a financial and human resources standpoint, KWSC performs well below expectations for
a water utility serving a major commercial hub. The utility has 1.07 million registered customers
compared to Karachi Electric’s 3.5 million in the same area. No household has a meter, and only 75 percent
receive bills. Billing efficiency is about 36 percent, and O&M cost recovery is around 73 percent. The total
number of staff is almost 10,000, but staff productivity is low at 9.5 per 1,000 connections. At the same
time, the utility has been unable to recruit new talent due to a GoS hiring moratorium from 2008 to 2024.
A lack of diversity in the workforce undermines the KWSC’s potential. Women comprise only 4 percent of
the workforce and only 10 women hold technical and managerial positions compared to 630 men. Due to
the water sector being traditionally male-dominated and perceived as not supportive of women, as well
as a lack of skills training, it is difficult for women to break into the sector. Recently, the KWSC has been
making efforts to address its gender gap by establishing a Women’s Development Centre that includes a
daycare and increasing training opportunities for women employees.

D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach
20.     An MPA is conducive to the sustained focus and coordinated investments over a prolonged
period at the province level that are needed to address urban WASH services gaps. It offers several
advantages over a standalone Investment Project Financing (IPF) or Program-for-Results (PforR)
operation. The MPA assures governments and stakeholders of the World Bank’s long-term commitment,
offering more predictability to other development partners, including multilateral development banks
(MDBs) and the private sector, and providing a platform to align partners, evaluate impact, and share
lessons learned across provinces. At the same time, it offers sufficient flexibility to course-correct and
adjust the program and its ambition based on results achieved, evolving government priorities, and
finances. This Project Appraisal Document introduces the MPA and focuses on KWSSIP-2 as the first phase,
signaling the commitment by the World Bank to support critical WASH outcomes over the long term.
21.     The MPA Program Development Objective (PrDO) is to provide safely managed WASH services
in urban areas in Pakistan. The PrDO result indicator is “people provided with safely managed WASH
services,” and the target is 33.5 million by 2035. The people who count against this indicator are those
receiving the three services of safely managed water, safely managed sanitation, and basic hygiene
services as defined in the World Bank Corporate Scorecard. Safely managed water means drinking water
from an improved water source accessible on premises, available when needed (a minimum of 12 hours
per day), and free from fecal and hazardous chemical contaminants (including fluoride, arsenic, and lead).
Safely managed sanitation means using an improved sanitation facility not shared with other households
and where excreta is safely disposed of in situ or removed and treated offsite. Basic hygiene service means
a handwashing facility with soap and water is available at home. The MPA’s theory of change is shown in
Table 2. Participating provinces will have the flexibility to select from the menu of activities based on their
needs, priorities, and capabilities, provided they are aligned with the PrDO and results chain.

26.      The MPA includes a robust learning agenda to enable learning across provinces on WSS service
provider reforms and hygiene behavioral change. The MPA will facilitate knowledge sharing, drawing on

are needed. For instance, lessons from utility corporatization reforms in Karachi under Phase 1 will inform
the design of similar reforms for water service providers in secondary cities in Punjab under Phase 2. In
addition, lessons from PPP transactions and utility turnaround strategies in Phase 1 will inform similar
initiatives in Phases 2 and 3. Moreover, the MPA will invest in building institutional capacity for capturing
and sharing lessons at the municipal and utility levels so that the learning could, over time, benefit other
regions as well. Phase 3 will be informed by the lessons learned from Phases 1 and 2 as well as KWSSIP-1.
27.     The learning agenda will generate evidence to inform context-specific hygiene behavioral
change interventions to maximize public health gains from WASH services. Robust monitoring and
evaluation (M&E) of MPA activities will help assess the impact of such interventions on outcomes like
reduced exposure to water-borne diseases and child stunting. Program assessments will answer short-run
behavior questions, such as the effective framing of hygiene messages to encourage the adoption of new
behaviors and the uptake of services. In contrast, more traditional impact evaluations will answer longer-
term questions, such as the impact of program interventions on reducing child stunting.

 II. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

A. Project Development Objective
30.     The Project Development Objective (PDO) of Phase 1 is to provide safely managed water and
sanitation services in Karachi and increase KWSC’s financial and operational performance. The PDO
indicators are: (i) People provided with safely managed water (including female and youth) (number); (ii)
People provided with safely managed sanitation (including female and youth) (number); and (iii) KWSC
operating cost coverage ratio improved (percent). The first two are WBG Corporate Scorecard indicators.
B. Project Components
31.     The project scope consists of three components that contribute to the PDO and a Contingent
Emergency Response Component (CERC) with zero allocation, providing flexibility to the GoP/GoS to
reallocate funds to support emergency response activities in case of an eligible emergency. 16
32.      Component 1 – Capacity Building and Reform of KWSC (US$14 million, of which IBRD US$5.6
million, AIIB US$5.6 million, GoS US$2.8 million). This component will finance capacity building and
reform support activities to enable KWSC’s evolution into a well-functioning utility that is resilient to
climate change. The component will finance: (i) technical assistance and capacity building related to
technical and commercial operations, including asset management, geographic information systems (GIS)
and data systems, hydraulic modeling, NRW reduction, billing and revenue management, financial
management, communications, and customer service; (ii) implementation of organizational structure
reforms to enhance efficiency, accountability and commercial orientation in service delivery; (iii)
implementation of a human resource reform plan, including a gender-inclusive recruitment and training
strategy; (iv) preparation and implementation of water safety plans, including the establishment of a
centralized water quality testing laboratory capable of carrying out biological and key chemical tests
(including lead and other heavy metals); (v) rehabilitation and upgrade of office spaces and establishing
an innovation, research, development, and learning center; (vi) technical assistance on PPPs, their
outsourcing and management; (vii) preparation of all required rules, regulations and guidelines to enable
full operationalization of the KWSC Act and their adoption as standard operating procedures for
operationalizing the utility; and (viii) any other capacity-building activities necessary to strengthen KWSC’s
corporate governance and increase its operational and financial performance and adaptive capacity, such
as technical trainings for women in the KWSC, support to the newly established Environmental and Social
(E&S) cell at KWSC , and support to improve groundwater management.
33.      Component 2 –Safely Managed Water Supply and Sanitation Services (US$958.56 million, of
which IBRD US$223.824 million, AIIB US$223.824 million, GoS US$241.912 million, private capital
US$269 million). The component will finance investments to enhance Karachi’s water supply and
sanitation infrastructure. The medium-term investment plan addresses five interlinked structural
problems in Karachi’s water and sanitation system: a bulk water supply shortfall, a leaky water distribution
network, an intermittent supply that does not meet drinking water quality standards, a lack of wastewater
collection and treatment capacity, and persistent poor service levels, especially in Katchi Abadis.

residual chlorine levels. The sub-component will also finance network expansion to areas of Karachi that

reduction program to reduce physical and commercial water losses, prioritizing physical water losses by
improving system maintenance, active leakage control, speed and quality of leak repairs, and pressure
management. Reducing physical water losses will help reduce pressure on water sources and build
resilience to droughts. An initial set of areas for rehabilitation has been established based on the potential
for NRW reduction and energy-saving benefits relative to E&S complexity and costs. District Metered
Areas financed under this sub-component, complemented by the installation of customer meters from
the KWSC metering program, will support the implementation of NRW reduction plan.
37.      Sub-component 2.4: Improvement of Energy Efficiency (US$19.90 million, of which IBRD US$7.96

plants as well as the utilization of feasible renewable energy resources, and thus reduce energy costs and
KWSC’s carbon footprint. The KWSC will partner with competitively selected Energy Service Companies

other major facilities. ESCOs will function as project developers for comprehensive energy conservation
measures and assume the technical and performance risks associated with the selected interventions.
ESCOs will be engaged on performance-based contracts with payments linked to actual energy cost
savings. If needed, about US$6 million is allocated for viability gap funding (VGF).

E. Lessons Learned
46.      The design of the MPA draws on lessons and evidence from analytical work and more than five
decades of WBG experience supporting WASH operations worldwide. The project draws on a wealth of
evidence that shows that lack of safe drinking water and sanitation and fecal contamination of the
environment are the primary factors underlying the stunting crisis in Pakistan. 22 The project scope
includes investments to reduce the risk of fecal-oral transmission of pathogens through the provision of
safe drinking water, safe wastewater collection and treatment, and hygiene behavior change.
47.      Lessons from global experience show that the World Bank is more impactful when it supports
credible government programs that include infrastructure investments and institutional capacity
strengthening. This MPA shifts the default approach from ad hoc, opportunistic, and short-term
engagements typically emphasizing infrastructure to a long-term programmatic approach combining
critical infrastructure investments with reforms and capacity strengthening of service providers to
maximize chances of achieving and sustaining safely managed services.
48.     Public utility reform initiatives in a complex socio-political context must deliver quick wins to
maintain the support of all stakeholders. The scope of Phase 1 includes short-term investments, such as
ILC units, to enable the KWSC to immediately start providing safe drinking water in selected parts of its
service area, support for increasing revenues rapidly and reducing costs through energy efficiency
interventions and leveraging technology in billing and revenue collection.
49.    Finally, institutional reform requires sustained commitment from borrowers over the long term.
The project uses legal covenants to maintain focus and attention on the most critical reforms relevant to
improving the KWSC’s operational and financial performance. The project scope also includes
complementary technical assistance and change management support to achieve results.

Economic Analysis
63.      The economic rationale for this project is strong, with a positive net present value (NPV) of
US$1,140 million and an economic internal rate of return (IRR) of 35 percent. The benefits considered
include reduced cost of using alternative water sources, reduced time spent collecting water, and health
benefits from reducing waterborne diseases. However, the direct environmental benefits of reducing
pollution cannot be estimated reliably. Sensitivity analyses showed that the project is robust and that
even significant changes in critical variables will not render the project unviable. For instance, a one-year
delay in completing the K-IV Augmentation subproject critical to water service improvements only reduces
the economic IRR of the subproject from 22 percent to 17 percent.
64.      Greenhouse gas (GHG) ERs—monetized by applying a low and high shadow price of carbon—
further add to the project's economic benefits. The project is expected to generate a net ER of 17,558,840
tons of carbon dioxide (CO2) equivalent over its economic lifetime. The annual GHG reduction is estimated
at 877,942 tons of CO2 equivalent. The ERs are driven by energy efficiency gains, reduced tanker water
deliveries, and improved wastewater collection and treatment 28.
Financial Analysis
65.     The project is financially viable for KWSC with an NPV of US$470 million and IRR of 20 percent,
assuming no cost of financing to KWSC. Although the KWSC Act enables the KWSC to operate on a
commercial basis and to borrow to meet its capital expenditure and recover all its costs (including
financing costs) through tariffs, the KWSC is still a long way from achieving full cost recovery. It will
continue to depend on the GoS for capital financing for some time, especially for wastewater operations.
66.     The KWSC has not produced complete audited financial statements, so the World Bank could
not reliably ascertain its financial position. For the FY08-20 statements, the auditors have provided
disclaimers due to insufficient audit evidence. An issue of concern is the ability of the KWSC to operate as
a solvent and financially viable entity during and after the project, with reduced dependency on
operational GoS subsidies. Operating cashflows, excluding subsidies, have remained negative for the past
years. The KWSC faces the challenge of increasing revenue from its water sales due to an inaccurate and
incomplete customer database, lack of customer metering, and archaic billing and collection practices.

D. Environmental and Social
74.      The World Bank has assessed the project’s potential E&S risks and impacts. The project will
provide safely managed WASH services and reduce environmental pollution from untreated wastewater.
For the PPP activities in component 2, OP 4.03 (Performance Standards for Private Sector Activities) will
be applied. The overall E&S risk is High due to social risks related to legacy resettlement issues in the
associated facility and potential economic resettlement and livelihood impacts on non-titled people on
public land and right of way during project implementation.
75.      The project’s environmental risk is Substantial. Risks and impacts during construction include: (i)
an increase in air and noise pollution; (ii) loss of trees and vegetation; (iii) generation of construction
wastes and wastewater; (iv) quarrying activities and associated soil erosion; and (v) occupational health
and safety (OHS) and community health and safety (CHS) issues, including traffic disruptions due to the
activities in densely populated areas. During project operation, risks and impacts include the generation
of sludge from wastewater and water treatment, OHS hazards related to chemicals management,
potential soil, groundwater, and water distribution contamination due to any leakages of the sewerage
pipes, and over-abstraction from Keenjhar Lake without offsetting or regulating additional withdrawals
for K-IV Phase 1. Additionally, components of the project may also be exposed to flash floods, which will
be considered while finalizing the project designs.
76.     The project design includes actions to mitigate potential adverse environmental impacts on the
Keenjhar Lake due to increased withdrawals. A Keenjhar Lake Water Balance Study and an Ecological
Assessment for Keenjhar Lake and Downstream Indus Impacts confirmed that the most significant
mitigation action for Keenjhar Lake is the rehabilitation of KBFU, which is ongoing. As per the ESCP, the
Keenjhar Lake water balance will be regularly updated, and increased water delivery to the lake will be
realized before an equal or lesser amount is withdrawn for K-IV Phase 1. The ESCP also requires
implementation of the Keenjhar Lake Conservation Action Plan to begin before award the civil works
under subcomponent 2.5(ii) and to be maintained throughout project implementation. The K-IV
Coordination and E&S Implementation Committee will coordinate and report on ESCP compliance. The
Ecological Assessment found that the increases in withdrawals from the Indus at Kotri Barrage to offset
K-IV withdrawals at Keenjhar Lake represent no more than 3 percent of historical withdrawals at Kotri.
The contribution of the project to the cumulative impacts of multiple withdrawals and degradation of the
Indus watershed is therefore assessed as negligible. The planned irrigation improvements and improved
water resources planning under the ongoing World Bank-financed Sindh Water and Agriculture
Transformation Project (P167596) and Sindh Barrages Improvement Project (P131324, and its Additional
Financing P179051) are expected to mitigate regional issues of overallocation and competition for water.

populated areas. The World Bank, in close collaboration with the KWSC and the PIU, has confirmed that
no Anti-Encroachment Drives resulting in forced evictions have taken place on the direct footprint of the
project. In addition, the project does not anticipate private land acquisition as the required land and Rights
of Ways, including of transmission lines, are already owned by the KWSC or the GoS. Additionally, the GoS

 VI. KEY RISKS

86.      The overall risk rating for the MPA is Substantial considering the risks inherent in program
activities in all phases, including network infrastructure investments in dense urban contexts. The Phase
2 E&S risk is rated substantial, while for phase 3 it is rated high. Macroeconomic risk is also rated High for
phases 2 and 3. The Phase 2 and 3 risks on institutional capacity, fiduciary, and stakeholders are expected
to reduce as a result of KWSSIP-2 risk mitigation measures. Moreover, the Program learning agenda will
help strengthen measures for the MPA to reduce potential risks that would be revealed.
87.     The overall risk rating for KWSSIP-2 is Substantial due to the following substantial and high risks.
88.       Political and Governance risk is rated High. The project’s political context is complex, with
multiple public and private actors. The risk of political disagreements affecting implementation is high,
especially on contentious topics such as project target areas, tariff revisions, and PPPs. To mitigate these
risks, the preparation process has emphasized a collaborative approach between the GoS, KWSC, and
Karachi Municipal Corporation. This approach will continue through the PSC, which brings diverse political
actors together. While the extent to which project-specific measures can mitigate political risks is limited,
the project includes specific actions to build public support through broader stakeholder participation,
strategic communication, and outreach. Governance risks will be mitigated through: (i) implementation
of the institutional strengthening activities under the project; (ii) dialogue and close collaboration with
the GoS and KWSC, as well as proactive engagement by the World Bank team, and management as issues
arise; (iii) use of legal covenants to maintain focus on critical reforms and the outcomes of the reforms;
and (vii) leveraging digital technologies tools and geospatial information systems in M&E.
89.      Macroeconomic risk is rated High. While Pakistan’s economy has stabilized over the past 12
months due to new external inflows and improvements in macroeconomic management, debt and
external financing needs remain elevated, and risks remain very high. Significant risks include
international price or interest rate shocks; natural disasters that may impact domestic production,
revenue collection, or expenditure needs; reversal of recent reforms to constrain expenditures or to
ensure exchange rate flexibility; and lack of progress in further structural reforms in the fiscal, state-
owned enterprise, and energy areas. Realization of risks may lead to macroeconomic instability, with
major impacts on economic activities, prices, and household incomes, thereby impeding achievement of
project results. Risks will be partially mitigated by ongoing World Bank and other partners’ support to
structural policy reforms in the fiscal, energy, and state-owned enterprise reform sectors. Mitigation
measures also include the financing provided by the project through the IPF instrument and the
monitoring by the PSC that resources will remain available during the implementation of the project. Risks
will also be mitigated in the following ways: (i) project costing includes substantial price contingencies to
mitigate the risk of an increase in the price of imported goods; and (ii) counterpart financing is limited to
about one-quarter of the total project cost. However, the GoS has related obligations of approximately

US$28 million for the K-IV Phase 1 (associated facility) and US$71 million for the financing of the KBFU
Rehabilitation, therefore, even with these aforementioned measures which can be further exacerbated
by the inability of GoS to secure funds to meet its financial obligations, the residual risk remains high.
90.      Environment and Social risk is rated High. The combined E&S risks are rated high due to social
risks associated with legacy resettlement at the associated facility. A Corrective Action Plan has been
developed to mitigate these risks. Additional social risks from the potential economic displacement and
livelihood impacts due to works in densely populated neighborhoods will be mitigated by implementing
the merged Resettlement Plan and Abbreviated Resettlement Plan for Katchi Abadis. Substantial
environmental risks include the potential for ecological impacts, mainly on mangroves at the mouth of
the Malir River and on Keenjhar Lake due to water extraction for K-IV Phase 1. This risk is being mitigated
by the ongoing rehabilitation of KBFU canal and ESCP actions requiring regular water balance studies of
Keenjhar Lake and implementation of the Keenjhar Lake Conservation Action Plan, as well as increased
water delivery to Keenjhar Lake before an equal or lesser amount is withdrawn for K-IV Phase 1. Before
contract award for civil works for K-IV Augmentation, adequate funds for the KBFU rehabilitation and K-
IV Phase 1 works must be secured to avoid material delay and ensure their timely completion. Failure may
lead holding disbursement to pursue financing the K-IV Augmentation works. Additionally, the risk to
mangroves at the mouth of the Malir River will be mitigated by controlling access to the mangroves from
the TP-IV site, ensuring that no mangrove cutting takes place.
91.       Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability risk is rated Substantial. The KWSC
suffers from high staff turnover and struggles with M&E and management of project interdependences.
These risks will be mitigated by: (i) hiring additional technical project management staff from the market;
(ii) training PIU management and staff; (iii) improving PIU management and administrative systems; and
(iv) engaging a PMC to provide support in managing coordination and project interdependences.
92.      Fiduciary risk is rated Substantial. Procurement risk is rated as substantial due to the size of the
K-IV Augmentation works contracts and the PPP interventions. The procurement risk will be mitigated
through proper staffing of the PIU with procurement and contract management specialists, training and
capacity building in procurement and contract management, and engagement of a PMC which will, inter
alia, support the PIU in procurement and contract management.
93.     Stakeholders risk is rated Substantial. The sector includes many stakeholders whose interests are
often difficult to reconcile. A wide array of formal and informal organizations, private companies, non-
governmental organizations, unions, religious institutions, and local government structures can be
expected to vie for influence on project decisions and contracts. The project is financing a sustained,
professional communications and outreach effort. The selection of Katchi Abadis could lead to
controversy and opposition from local interest groups (e.g., current water vendors) and delay
implementation. The KWSC will work with local councilors and engage with residents through the KWSC’s
Katchi Abadi Cell. The KWSC will also establish a small local office in each of the selected Katchi Abadis
and a special Katchi Abadi counter in its service centers.
94.      Coordination risks are rated Substantial due to interdependencies that may cause delays and
cost overruns. The interdependencies are: (i) technical dependency between K-IV Phase 1 (including its
transmission line) and K-IV Augmentation; (ii) schedule dependency between KBFU and all K-IV projects,
as the former affects the full operation of the latter; (iii) resource dependency between all K-IV projects,
given limited public finance; and (iv) information dependency between all K-IV projects. To mitigate these
risks, the GoS will notify a K-IV Coordination and E&S Implementation Committee and the KWSC will
engage a PMC firm experienced in managing complex capital programs to support program coordination.

A. Project Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
1.      The Implementing Agency of this project will be the GoS through the KWSC and the PIU
established within the KWSC to manage KWSSIP-1 and subsequent KWSSIP projects. The PIU will report
to KWSC management represented by the Chief Executive Officer/Managing Director and maintain liaison
with the Board of Directors. The PIU and associated consultants are expected to reach 50 percent
women’s participation over the project life.
2.       Performance Agreement Assessment. The KWSC will select and manage a firm tasked with
independent verification of the KWSC’s Performance Agreement, following terms of reference
satisfactory to the Bank. The firm will verify achievement of targets, and in doing so shall examine
secondary data, collect primary data, and conduct other measurements annually or periodically as
needed. The firm will share the findings with the KWSC, who will, in turn, share them with the GoP and
the Bank.
3.      A PSC has been established to provide oversight and high-level coordination. The PSC includes
high-level representatives of all agencies involved in the PSC. It provides planning and strategic guidance
and facilitates multi-stakeholder cooperation for the development and implementation of a vision, policy
reforms, and investment program towards the improvement of Karachi’s WASH services. The PSC has
defined responsibilities to minimize overlaps, mitigate potential disagreements with the KWSC, and will
be empowered to evolve its membership in case of changing circumstances.
4.      Pursuant to a Project Co-Lenders’ Agreement with the AIIB, (which will be very similar to the
one for KWSSIP-1), the World Bank will supervise the project and administer both the IBRD and the AIIB
loans in accordance with the World Bank’s policies and procedures. The co-lender agreement will be
signed by the World Bank and the AIIB in accordance with the 2016 Co-Financing Framework Agreement
between World Bank and the AIIB (as amended in 2018). The AIIB and the World Bank will jointly finance
the project in equal shares, with all components financed jointly by the partners.
B. Financial Management and Disbursements
5.        The project has a residual FM risk rating of Moderate (subject to timely implementation of the
recommended mitigation measures and Action Plan) and has met the minimum FM requirement in
accordance with the requirements of Bank Policy for Investment Project Financing effective October 1,
2018. The FM arrangements provide reasonable assurance that the financing proceeds will be used for
the intended purposes, with due attention to the principles of economy, efficiency, effectiveness,
transparency, and accountability to support implementation and achieve the desired results. This
objective will be sustained by ensuring that strong FM systems are maintained for the project throughout
implementation. Detailed FM reviews will be conducted regularly, either within the proposed supervision
plan or on a more frequent schedule if needed, to ensure that expenditures incurred by the project remain
eligible.
6.      Disbursements will be report-based with advance equivalent to six months forecast of expected

implementing the project.

the same will be refunded to the World Bank. In addition to the advance method, other methods of
disbursement, which include direct payment and reimbursement, will be used as and when needed.
15.       Auditing. Project financial statement will be audited by the Auditor General of Pakistan through
the Director General Audit Sindh. Audited financial statements would be submitted to the Bank within six
months of the close of each FY.
16.       Implementation Support Plan. Two annual field supervision missions will review the FM
arrangements at the PIU. The internal and external auditors' reports, as well as the project’s supervision
reports, would be the primary sources of information about the FM arrangements at KWSSIP-2.
