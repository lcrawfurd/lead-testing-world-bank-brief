      EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Program background
The proposed Uganda Cities and Municipalities Infrastructure Development (UCMID)
Program focuses on interventions meant to address the urban challenges outlined in the Urban
policy of 2017. The Program is aligned to the Government of Uganda GoU Vision 2040, and
the fourth National Development Plan (NDP) IV (2025/2026 – 2029/2030), with the goal of
“achieving higher household incomes and employment for sustainable socio-economic
transformation.”

The UCMID seeks to build on the progress and successes registered under Uganda Support to
Municipal Infrastructure Development (USMID) by extending infrastructure to the beneficiary
entities including other Cities/Municipalities and Refugee Hosting Districts (RHD). The
objective of the program is to enhance the institutional performance of participating cities and
municipalities for urban service delivery and management; and increase access to improved
and climate resilient infrastructure.

Program Development Objectives
The Program Development Objective (PDO) is to enhance the institutional performance of
selected urban local governments and ministries for urban planning and management and
improve access to climate smart infrastructure, services and jobs for urban residents and
displaced populations in refugee-hosting areas.

The Program will focus on three Results Areas (RAs): (i) RA1: Enhanced local and national
level government institutional capacity and performance for sustainable urban management,
(ii) RA2: Increased access to climate smart urban infrastructure, services and jobs, and (iii)
RA3: Improved livelihoods and local economic opportunities for the host communities and
refugees.

Environmental and social benefits of USMID
USMID Cities and Municipalities have seen notable environmental and socio-economic
improvements, including better roads, drainage, waste management, public cleanliness, and
restored green spaces. These upgrades have enhanced air quality, reduced congestion, and
flooding, and improved urban service delivery. Socio-economic gains include modernized
markets, improved access to schools and health centers, increased safety through lighting and
walkways, job creation, boosted local investments, and rising land values and a boost in own
source received which contributes to improved city/municipal services.

Methodology for Environment and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA)
The assessment employed a multi-pronged methodology, including a review of national
policies, legislation, and key program documents such as ESSA and implementation
frameworks for USMID, USMID AF, UgIFT-AF, and GKMA-UDP. It incorporated
institutional analysis to evaluate mandates, capacities, and interests of relevant entities,
particularly in USMID-implemented Cities, Municipalities, and Refugee Hosting Districts.
Field visits and stakeholder consultations were conducted with local authorities and central
government agencies (e.g., MoLHUD, MoLG, NEMA, MoFPED, MLGSD, MoWE, and NPA)
to validate findings.

Findings
Uganda and the implementing entities have robust legal, regulatory and institutional systems
to assess and manage Environment, Social, Health and Safety (ESHS) risks. However,
implementation and enforcement remain weak due to capacity and staffing constraints,
particularly in the cities, municipal councils, and refugee hosting districts (RHDs).
    • Uganda has laws addressing environmental, social, health, safety, inclusion, and
        accountability issues, as well as a comprehensive legal and institutional framework for
        managing ESHS risks. However, challenges persist in implementation, which is limited
        by personnel, equipment, and budgetary constraints.
    • Several cities, municipal councils, and refugee-hosting districts have some key
        Environmental and Social (E&S) staff already in place. Others are actively recruiting
        substantive roles in FY 2024/25 in preparation for the UCMID Program. The existing
        E&S staff in Municipalities previously benefited by USMID are adequate. In addition,
        new Cities/Municipalities and RHDs staff need to be equipped with skills and tools to
        implement UCMID effectively.
    • Some of the USMID cities and municipalities staff have benefited from capacity-
        building initiatives, including training, equipment provision, and improved workspaces.
        They are now familiar with Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) tools,
        E&S integration in bidding documents, and project monitoring using Environmental
        and Social Management Plans (ESMPs). However, E&S staff lack budget to perform
        supervision and enforcement activities effectively.
    • Early stakeholder engagement is recognized as a key tool for program buy-in,
        particularly through the involvement of City and Municipal Development Forums
        (MDFs).
    • Grievance Redress Committees (GRCs) have been formed and trained, with monthly
        monitoring meetings conducted to manage complaints. However, entities lacked
        adequate funding for facilitating GRCs and there was weak coordination with Central
        GRM systems, such as the Ministry of Gender, Labor and Social Development, to
        oversee the processing and for support and potential closure of some of the persistent
        grievances.

Challenges identified

   •   Delays and system inefficiencies: The process for obtaining ESIA approval certificates
       from NEMA, along with the functionality of the Environment License Management
       System (ELMIS), has been hampered by significant delays. These setbacks have
       negatively impacted the timely execution of projects.
   •   Institutional development: ESHS staff within local governments and ministries, such
       as MoGLSD, need enhanced authority and resources to effectively oversee, inspect,
       monitor, and certify project activities. Additionally, local governments should be given
       greater empowerment to enforce contractor adherence to E&S staffing and compliance
       requirements.
   •   Design and audit shortcomings: Limited involvement of local staff in project design
       has led to costly errors.
   •   Budgetary constraints: E&S components are often underbudgeted in the Bills of
       Quantities.
   •   Environmental audits: Audits are conducted too late to inform corrective actions,
       highlighting the need for earlier engagement of local governments in design and timely
       audit processes.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background
The Uganda Cities and Municipalities Infrastructure Development (UCMID) Program is well
aligned with the Government’s National Urban Policy, which positions cities and urban centres
as “drivers of change” for development, and aims to “promote liveable urban areas that are
organized, inclusive, productive, and sustainable.” The proposed Program focuses on
interventions designed to address the urban challenges identified in the urban policy. The
UCMID Program aligns with the Government of Uganda’s (GoU) Vision 2040 and the Fourth
National Development Plan (NDP IV) for the period 2025–2030. Some of its main objectives
are: i) develop and maintain urban infrastructure in line with physical development plans, ii)
develop sustainable waste management systems, iii) strengthen land use management, iv)
increase economic opportunities in urban areas, v) strengthen the policy, legal, institutional,
and coordination framework. The proposed Program scope also aligns fully with the draft
Country Partnership Framework (CPF) for 2023–2028. The Program will promote more
effective and sustainable decentralization in Uganda by enabling urban Cities/Municipalities
and RHDs to improve their capacity in urban governance for service delivery.

The GoU views urban development as essential for achieving middle-income status by 2040,
emphasizing it as a driver for economic growth, employment, and wealth creation. This is
reflected in the Draft NDP IV (2025–2030) and the National Resistance Movement (NRM)
manifesto (2021–2026), which set out important commitments, particularly regarding physical
planning and infrastructure investments. The Sustainable Urbanization Housing and Land
Management Program (SUHLM) is one of the eighteen programs anchored within NDP IV.
The SUHLM contributes to Objective 4 of NDP IV, which is to build and maintain strategic
sustainable infrastructure in transport, housing, energy, water, industry, and Information and
Communication Technology (ICT).

1.2 Description of the UCMID Program
The UCMID Program seeks to build on the progress, successes, and lessons learned under the
USMID Program by extending infrastructure interventions to additional beneficiary entities,
including other Cities/Municipalities and Refugee Hosting Districts (RHDs). The objective of
the Program is to enhance the institutional performance of selected urban local governments
and ministries for urban planning and management and improve access to climate smart
infrastructure, services and jobs for urban residents and displaced populations in refugee-
hosting areas.

In addition to the 10 Cities of Jinja, Mbarara, Gulu, Arua, Masaka, Hoima, Mbale, Soroti, Lira,
and Fort Portal, and 11 municipalities under the USMID-AF program (Kabale, Moroto, Tororo,
Kitgum, Kasese, Kamuli, Mubende, Busia, Ntugamo, Apac, Lugazi, Entebbe), the Program
scope will be extended to 15 municipalities, including Masindi, Rukungiri, Bushenyi-Ishaka,
Mityana, Kumi, Nebbi, Koboko, Kisoro, Kapchorwa, Ibanda Iganga, Njeru, Bugiri, Sheema,
and Kotido Municipal Councils, as well as all 11 RHDs of Arua, Isingiro, Kamwenge,
Kiryandongo, Lamwo, Maddi-Okollo, Moyo, Obongi, Terego, and Yumbe.

The Program will include improvements to road networks, solar street lighting, stormwater
drainage, urban greening, development of green parks and belts, innovation and incubation
centres, urban transport facilities (bus, taxi, and lorry parks), non-motorized infrastructure,
economic infrastructure, and solid waste management.

The World Bank (WB) has substantial experience in Uganda with PforR programs, including
the USMID Program (2013), USMID Additional Financing (2018), Uganda Intergovernmental
Fiscal Transfers (UGIFT) Program (2017), and the Greater Kampala Metropolitan Area Urban
Development Program (GKMA-UDP) (2022). Beyond these programs with sizable
investments, the WB has been engaged in policy dialogue on urban development and
decentralization in Uganda for nearly three decades. Uganda has therefore established a strong
foundation for a programmatic approach to enhancing government systems along multiple
dimensions utilizing PforR arrangements.

Over the last ten years, the Government has adopted and enhanced a programmatic approach
and coordination between Ministries, Departments, and Agencies (MDAs) through the inter-
ministerial Program Technical Committee (PTC), which coordinates Program implementation
and addresses technical issues that may affect Program execution. The use of PforR
investments has entrenched a culture of performance-based access to development funds.
Citizen consultations have become an integral element of PforR Programs, enabling
participating entities to achieve improved performance and contribute to visible impacts on the
ground.

The UCMID Program will build on (i) the lessons learned to leverage results achievement; (ii)
consolidate achievements and experiences registered under the previous programs of USMID;
(iii) adopt innovative approaches to enhance economic development, (iv) focus more on
climate resilience and disaster risk management in the design and implementation, (v) focus on
transformative investments which can be delivered within a realistic time frame of 4 -5 years
of the Program period; (vi) ensure there is strategic alignment to the Government priorities in
the NDP IV; (vii) leverage funding from other Development Partners (DPs) and private sector
through Public Private Partnership (PPP) with support from International Finance Corporation
(IFC) , and (viii) effective management of social and environmental risks.

1.3 Program Development Objectives

The PDO is achored at the outcome results and linked to the Disbursement Disbursement
Link Results (DLRs) and indicators to be achieved under the Program’s results areas.
The Program will focus on three Results Areas (RAs), namely: (i) RA1: Enhanced local and
national level government institutional capacity and performance for sustainable urban
management, (ii) RA2: Increased access to climate smart urban infrastructure, services and
jobs, and (iii) RA3: Improved livelihoods and local economic opportunities for the host
communities and refugees.

1.4 PforR Program Boundary
The GoU program is the Sustainable Urbanization and Housing (SUH) Program, covering all
urban areas nationally. It is one of eighteen programs under NDP IV (2025–2030) and aims to
achieve “higher household incomes and employment for sustainable socio-economic
transformation.” The SUH program includes five strategic objectives covering urban
infrastructure, physical planning, drainage and waste management, economic opportunities,
affordable housing, and policy, legal, and institutional strengthening.

The UCMID Program will cover a subset of the SUH program and select investment areas
eligible for PforR funding. It will include ten secondary cities, eleven USMID-supported
municipalities, fifteen new municipalities, and eleven refugee-hosting districts. New
municipalities can only be admitted if they demonstrate the required minimum access capacity
and have a plan for physical development. The Program will focus on economic integration of
host communities and refugees in urban areas. Urban areas in RHDs will be selected if their
plans and prioritized infrastructure demonstrate a focus on improving livelihoods for both host
communities and refugees.

Intervention areas considered high risk for implementation under PforR arrangements will be
excluded. These include public transport projects such as Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) and Light
Rail Transit (LRT), large-scale Solid Waste Management (SWM) infrastructure (particularly
“brownfield” projects), and housing development services and institutions.

1.6 Program Implementation Arrangements
The UCMID Program will be implemented through the Government of Uganda’s existing
intergovernmental structures, building on the institutional experience of the Uganda Support to
Municipal Infrastructure Development (USMID) Program. UCMID strengthens coordination,
integrates climate considerations, and promotes performance-based delivery, with roles
distributed across national ministries, agencies, and local governments.

1.7 Program Steering Committee
Strategic oversight will be provided by the Sustainable Urbanization and Housing Programme
Working Group, which will serve as the Program Steering Committee (PSC). The PSC will be
chaired by the Permanent Secretary (PS) of the Ministry of Lands, Housing and Urban
Development (MoLHUD). Members will include PSs from the Ministry of Local Government
(MoLG), Ministry of Finance, Planning and Economic Development (MoFPED), Ministry of
Works and Transport (MoWT), Ministry of Water and Environment (MoWE), Ministry for
Kampala Capital City and Metropolitan Affairs (MoKCC&MA), and Ministry of Trade,
Industry and Cooperatives (MoTIC). Executive Directors of the National Planning Authority
(NPA) and National Environment Management Authority (NEMA) will also be members.
Development partners, including the World Bank, will serve as ex-officio members. The PSC
will meet as needed to address strategic and policy matters.

1.8 Program Technical Committee
The Program Technical Committee (PTC), chaired by the Commissioner of MoLHUD, will
serve as the core technical coordination platform for UCMID. It will include senior
representatives from MoFPED (Budget Policy and Evaluation, and Development Assistance
and Regional Cooperation), MoLG, MoWT, the Public Procurement and Disposal of Public

MoWE (Climate Change Department), the Ministry of Gender, Labour and Social

1.9 Coordinating Ministry – MoLHUD
MoLHUD will serve as the lead implementing agency for UCMID, with overall responsibility
for program coordination, technical leadership, fiduciary oversight, and results reporting to
government and development partners. Day-to-day implementation will be mainstreamed
within the Ministry’s existing departmental structure, ensuring ownership and sustainability.

To address immediate capacity gaps and ensure timely delivery of program results, MoLHUD
will be supported by a lean Program Support Team (PST) embedded within the institution. The
PST will include specialists in engineering, procurement, financial management, urban
planning, monitoring and evaluation (M&E), social and environmental safeguards,
occupational health and safety (OHS), climate resilience, communications, and digital systems.
These professionals will work alongside departmental counterparts, provide technical
backstopping to Cities/Municipalities and RHDs, and support MoLHUD in fulfilling its
coordination and oversight functions. The PST’s role will be transitional, with functions
gradually absorbed by MoLHUD staff as institutional capacity is strengthened through training,
mentoring, and staffing adjustments. PST staff will operate under performance contracts.

In addition to coordination, MoLHUD will lead policy and regulatory reforms to modernize
Uganda’s urban planning and development framework. This includes reviewing and updating
physical planning laws, regulations, and technical standards to incorporate climate resilience,
low-carbon development, and inclusive spatial planning approaches. MoLHUD will also
oversee the implementation of the Physical Planning Act, ensuring that national and local
physical development plans reflect UCMID priorities and resilience goals.

Furthermore, in partnership with the Uganda Bureau of Statistics (UBoS) and the National
Planning Authority (NPA), MoLHUD will spearhead the creation of a national urban data
platform equipped with Geographic Information System (GIS) and remote sensing capabilities.
This platform will enable evidence-based planning, real-time monitoring of urban growth and
infrastructure development, and better alignment of investment decisions with climate and
development objectives.

1.10 Ministry of Local Government (MoLG)

and accountability. MoLG will also ensure conducive policies are in place, support LGs in
enhancing own-source revenue (OSR), and coordinate the development of standardized
guidelines for procurement, contract management, and operations and maintenance.

2. THE ENVIRONMENT AND SOCIAL SYSTEM ASSESSMENT (ESSA) PROCESS
2.1 Objective and Scope of ESSA
The ESSA was undertaken by the World Bank team for the UCMID Program in accordance
with the World Bank requirements of the Program-for-Results (PforR) financing instrument,
including para 9 of the PforR Policy and para 30 of the PforR Directive. The primary aim of
the ESSA is to identify the environmental and social risks associated with program
implementation, assess the capacity of existing government systems at both national and local
levels, identify gaps where they exist, and propose effective measures to address these gaps.
The overall goal is to strengthen the existing systems for environmental and social risk
management.

The ESSA process involved a detailed assessment of the environmental and social systems in
place, including an evaluation of human and financial resources, institutional competencies,
and gaps or weaknesses, as well as the nature of necessary improvements that would form the
basis for a program action plan. The assessment also considered the environment and social
performance and lessons learned from the original Uganda Support to Municipalities
Infrastructure Development (USMID) Program of 2013, USMID Additional Financing
(USMID-AF) of 2018, and other PforR projects such as Uganda Intergovernmental Fiscal
Transfer (UgIFT-AF) and the Greater Kampala Metropolitan Area – Urban Development
Program (GKMA-UDP).

2.2 Context of the ESSA
The ESSA was conducted to ensure consistency with the six “core principles” outlined in
paragraph 9 of the World Bank’s policy on Program-for-Results financing and in line with
paragraph 30 of the World Bank’s Program-for-Results Directive These principles are designed
to manage program risks effectively and promote sustainable development. The six core
principles are detailed in Annex 8.

2.3 ESSA Methodology
The assessment involved a comprehensive review of national development plans, policies,
legislation, institutional capacities, and program procedures at national and local levels to
evaluate the readiness of selected Cities, Municipalities, and Refugee Hosting Districts to
implement environmental and social systems aligned with Bank PforR financing. The sampling
deliberately prioritized regional balance and included entities that participated in USMID/AF,
Refugee Hosting Districts, and newly established Municipalities, as indicated in Table 3. The
objective was to assess preparedness for the UCMID Program, gather lessons learned, and
obtain suggestions for more effective implementation.

Structured questionnaires were used to collect data from urban agencies to better understand
institutional roles, stakeholder perspectives, and existing feedback mechanisms for managing
social and environmental risks. The assessment also focused on identifying improvement
strategies, strengthening institutional capacity, enhancing system performance, and developing
an action plan to be incorporated into the Program Action Plan and enforced through
Disbursement-Linked Indicators (DLIs).

deeper understanding of the UCMID Program. The review examined national development

3.1 Program environmental and social benefits
The UCMID Program is expected to deliver multiple environmental and social benefits through
improved infrastructure. Upgraded roads, drainage systems, economic projects, bus and taxi
parks, street lighting, and greening of municipal roads will enhance aesthetics, transportation
efficiency, and reduce pollution. Roads in Refugee-Hosting Districts (RHDs) will have
improved mobility and access to markets, while paved surfaces and reduced emissions
contribute to better air quality. Improved drainage mitigates flooding, erosion, and disease
vector breeding, enhancing public health. Waste management capacity will be strengthened
through improved collection systems, community sensitization on fee-based disposal, and
infrastructure improvements. Tree planting and landscaping will further enhance green cover
and urban aesthetics. Environmental monitoring will be reinforced through new equipment,
staff training, and increased local awareness and participation in waste management initiatives.

Urban infrastructure improvements will also contribute significantly to community well-being.
Enhanced access to social services, reduced travel costs, and the creation of clean, organized,
and safer urban environments—including named streets, improved drainage, and solid waste
management—will improve quality of life. Road safety and public health are expected to
improve, while recreational spaces and green areas provide additional social benefits.
Economic gains include job creation, extended business hours, and increased local government
revenue from leisure parks, property rates, and other sources. Staff capacity will be
strengthened through targeted training and retooling with modern equipment, enabling better
management of environmental and social impacts.

Furthermore, the program will bring transformative benefits to slum areas where roads will be
constructed by improving access to essential services, enhancing mobility, and enabling faster
emergency response. It will stimulate local economic activity by attracting businesses and
creating jobs, while also integrating the community into the broader urban network. Roads raise
land value, encourage housing upgrades, and support public health through better sanitation
and waste management. Additionally, they improve safety and social cohesion by increasing
visibility, enabling patrols, and creating shared public spaces that foster community interaction.

Local Economic Development (LED) projects, including markets, resource centers, and bus
parks, will improve livelihoods, support youth and women employment through new markets
and transport hubs, contributing to a vibrant, secure, inclusive urban economy and improve
revenue collection in municipalities, cities, and RHDs.

3.2 Environmental and Social Risks
3.2.1 Risks during project preparation
Potential risks during project preparation include site selection that may encroach on
ecologically sensitive areas due to inadequate early screening; incomplete or delayed safeguard
documentation, which may result in overlooked impacts or weak mitigation strategies;
insufficient budgetary provisions in Bills of Quantities (BoQs), which may limit effective
mitigation and monitoring; weak contractor compliance due to poorly developed or enforced
Contractor Environmental and Social Management Plans (ESMPs), leading to non-adherence
to environmental standards; temporary facilities such as camps, borrow pits, and quarries,
which may cause land degradation, pollution, and biodiversity loss if not properly managed;

3.2.2 Construction related environmental and social risks
Construction activities pose several environmental and social risks, including dust, noise,
erosion, sedimentation of surface water, traffic disruptions, restricted pedestrian access, waste
pollution, visual impacts, and increased flood vulnerability. Additional risks arise from poorly
managed worker camps, interference with local businesses, and accidental damage to utilities
such as water, electricity, and telecom lines.

While these impacts are generally manageable through standard procedures and sound
construction practices, experience from the USMID Program shows that mitigation measures
often fall short when contractor quality is poor or oversight capacity is weak. Communities
may be exposed to safety risks such as open excavated pits for utilities or drainage installation,
unbarricaded and lacking warning signage. These risks are heightened at night due to poor
visibility.

3.2.3 Occupational health and safety risks (OHS)
OHS risks arise during site clearance, construction, decommissioning of temporary facilities,
and operation and maintenance of developed facilities. Workers may face ergonomic hazards
from lifting, bending, reaching overhead, pushing, and pulling heavy loads. Electrical hazards,
machine hazards, falls from elevated platforms, slips, trips, and falls are common and may
result in minor to severe injuries. Fires and explosions from equipment malfunction, electrical
sparks, or gas leaks may cause severe injuries or fatalities. Vehicle and equipment malfunctions
can result in severe injuries from unexpected movements, falling objects, or accidents. Noise-
induced hearing loss may occur with prolonged exposure to excessive noise without hearing
protection. Repetitive tasks may lead to stress, fatigue, and decreased concentration, increasing
accident risks.

3.2.4 Community health and safety risks (CHS)
Community health and safety risks include accidents, exposure to hazardous materials during
construction, and air pollution, among others. For example, there is also the risk of failure to
control boda-boda riders, which increases the likelihood of accidents. Mitigation measures to
protect the public from the impacts and safety hazards associated with ongoing civil works
should be considered from the mobilization stage by the contractor, with some measures also
embedded within the designs. Such measures include creating pedestrian walkways away from
traffic routes and designated access routes for host communities; providing proper notices of
hazard locations or barricading such sites, including construction sites; deploying flag persons
to assist with crossing and controlling traffic; and sensitizing the community on project
activities.

Inadequate or lack of prior communication regarding scheduled road or drainage works, and at
times traffic diversions due to road closures, negatively affect businesses and increases travel
time. Delays or long periods of abandonment make these challenges even more difficult to
overcome. There is also a need to ensure adequate safety signage at, approaching, and
throughout the alignment of projects.

Cities and municipalities have, in recent years, expanded their boundaries, which has resulted

project designs, including building Cities/Municipalities and RHDs’ capacity to manage these

data collection and promoting community sensitization.

3.2.6 Impacts on biodiversity and fragile environments
The cities and municipalities contain areas of biological sensitivity, including protected areas,
wetlands, and forest reserves within their boundaries, as well as other fragile environments
such as hilly areas and riverbanks. While these areas may not always be designated as natural
habitats, they nonetheless serve important functions, such as flood retention, which may be at
risk due to urban development. The situation is especially urgent regarding peri-urban forests,
many of which have already been converted to other uses, while others are heavily encroached
upon. It is possible that some municipalities, particularly newly engaged, will have such areas
that can be potentially affected by the UCMID subprojects.

Such sensitive areas can be excluded by using the program screening tool where project
activities may have significant adverse impacts on natural habitats. Further guidance should be
provided in the POM on approaches to minimize risks to non-critical resources. Cities and
municipalities can also invest in protecting or restoring these fragile environments.

3.2.8 Land acquisition and resettlement

and/or resettlement of a scale or nature that would have significant adverse impacts on affected

light of lessons learned from past urban operations, the acquisition of land and assets for linear
or fixed location type of investments, including roads upgrades, drainage, for example, will
require compensation at replacement cost to all Program affected persons.

3.2.9 Exclusion of vulnerable or marginalised groups
The unintended negative effects of development projects do not impact all individuals equally.
Certain vulnerable or marginalized groups are particularly at risk of suffering from these
impacts and may take years, even decades, to recover. Vulnerable or marginalized groups
include women, children and youth, the elderly, the very poor, people with disabilities,
refugees, ethnic minorities, and other disadvantaged or historically underserved communities.
These groups may face more severe disadvantages from project impacts because even small
changes in their circumstances can lead to significant additional hardships.

The UCMID program aims to improve the quality of life for urban residents. However, if new
or improved services—such as transport, drainage, or solid waste management—are not
affordable or accessible, or if services previously provided by the informal sector as income-
generating activities are institutionalized, the poor and vulnerable groups (including women,
the elderly, and persons with disabilities) may not benefit, or may even be left worse off.
Similarly, if new markets or abattoirs introduce higher rental or usage costs, small traders may
face higher operating costs and become worse off. Therefore, it will be critical that
considerations targeted to vulnerable and marginalized populations are incorporated into the
environmental and social assessments of subprojects, with corresponding mitigation measures
developed.

3.2.10 Labor and working conditions
Given the relatively small scale of subprojects and their location in urban areas with substantial
labor pools, it is not expected that UCMID will result in large numbers of external workers
being recruited. However, experience shows that contracted workers can pose social risks and
impacts, including social tensions with host communities, gender-based violence (GBV), and
the spread of HIV/AIDS and other sexually transmitted infections due to potential risks
associated with commercial sex.

Other labor-related concerns include:
   • Occupational Health and Safety Hazards: Workers are exposed to dangerous
       machinery, dust, noise, and hazardous chemicals, often without access to proper
       protective equipment. There is frequently a lack of comprehensive safety training or

   • Discrimination and Unequal Treatment: Gender-based discrimination persists, with
       women being excluded from certain roles or receiving lower pay. Workers from
       marginalized ethnic or regional backgrounds may experience exclusion or harassment.
       Access to opportunities continues to be restricted for young individuals, persons with
       disabilities, and other vulnerable groups.

3.2.11 Gender-Based Violence (GBV) and Violence Against Children (VAC)
Gender-related risks include the lack of consideration of working mothers’ interests and needs,
such as sanitation facilities, shelters for protection, and breastfeeding facilities. There is also
insufficient provision of appropriate and adequate health and welfare facilities, such as toilets,
washing facilities, and changing rooms that are separate for men and women and sensitive to
the needs of persons with disabilities. At the same time, communities have inadequate
information concerning their civic and human rights, in relation to sexual related violence
including sexual exploitation and abuse and, sexual harassment. Mechanisms to support
survivors of GBV are inadequate. Economic hardships, especially among refugees, combined
with limited livelihood options, may force women and girls into transactional sex as a means
of survival. Contractor labour may also exacerbate and/or expose project communities to
incidences of GBV and VAC and family and marriage breakages as well as social conflict.
Increased household incomes resulting from employment of community members in the
proposed project may cause or increase GBV in families.

3.2.12 Community engagement and GRM
While there is little risk of the program causing major social conflict, some grievances may
arise during project implementation that could create social tensions. These include disputes
over access to land and natural resources, resettlement challenges, community health and safety
concerns, or gender-related issues tied to construction work. Such risks may be compounded
by a lack of understanding of the program and poor involvement of community members, who
are the intended beneficiaries.

executing Cities/Municipalities and RHDs, there was no clear guidance on how grievances and
stakeholder engagement would be managed—particularly regarding reporting and follow-up

administrative systems, including Local Councils, the Police, and Courts of Law. To strengthen

                  Uganda's current approach to land acquisition and resettlement primarily uses cash
                  compensation with very few instances of in-kind compensation. This focus on cash rather than
                  restoring livelihoods can worsen the adverse effects of displacement, both physical and
                  economic, causing lasting harm to communities unfamiliar with managing large sums of
                  money. Moreover, cash compensation can disrupt family cohesion, gender dynamics, and
                  negatively affect the poor and vulnerable.
                  A key weakness in Uganda’s land acquisition framework is its narrow focus on physical assets,
                  often neglecting economic displacement and loss of access. Although the Constitution permits
                  compulsory acquisition for public use, its provisions are vague and prone to misinterpretation.
                  Compensation practices appear to have unclear standards on fairness, timeliness, and coverage
                  of livelihood impacts. Other weaknesses include:
                     •   Project works starting before displaced or affected people have been resettled or
                         compensated or had their titles transferred,
                     •   Non-payment or delayed payment of compensation for land acquisition and involuntary
                         resettlement,
                     •   Livelihood restoration programs not planned, or planned but not implemented,
                     •   Weak systems for managing undocumented or disputed property ownership (such as
                         multiple claimants for the same land, polygamous households, succession disputes,
                         multiple forms of title covering a single property),
                     •   Lack of consideration of the different needs of vulnerable communities (including
                         ethnic minorities) and vulnerable groups (which may include disabled, elderly, or
                         young people),
                     •   Failure to effectively mitigate the impacts on farmers or workers in the informal sector,
                     •   Inadequate attention to intangible assets, social capital, and other social assets disrupted
                         by displacement,
                     •   Weakness of regulations and systems governing land acquisition and resettlement
                         (valuation, title registration, payment of compensation) provides opportunities for
                         abuse by affected people (extortion, speculation, corruption and vexatious claims, for
                         example),
                     •   Extensive reliance of the Government of Uganda on voluntary land donations to secure
                         rights of way, particularly in urban settings.

                  Bank’s Directive for PforR Financing: highlights avoidance exacerbating social conflict,

                  distributional equity and cultural sensitivities. While there is little risk that the program will

                  6.1 Policy, legal and institutional framework
                  The existing policy, legal, and institutional framework addresses Environmental & Social
                  (E&S) issues related to program activities, but its implementation and enforcement are uneven.
                  Environment and social systems are well legislated and institutionalized in Uganda with over
                  40 related legislations and regulations. Environment and Social systems are grounded in the
                  Constitution, which commits government to protecting natural resources on behalf of the
                  people. It explicitly encompasses the concept of sustainability, including meeting the needs of
                  present and future generations. Uganda´s institutions have defined mandates and adequate
                  enabling legislation, albeit with some gaps, overlaps and weaknesses. However, the
                  implementation of the existing policy, legal, and institutional framework remains a challenge
                  that UCMID will need to address to comply with Bank policy.

                  Institutional Capacity has also grown over the USMID programme implementation period,
                  especially for Cities and Municipalities that have been supported under USMID. The MoWE
                  and NEMA have since established up to 4 regional offices – in Mbale, Lira, Hoima and Mbarara
                  to provide support to Cities/Municipalities and RHDs in all regions. MoLG structure provides
                  for USMID critical staff, including Environment Officers and Community Development
                  Officers at Districts, Cities, City Divisions and Municipalities. All Local Governments
                  (Districts, Cities, Municipalities) have in place Councils, standing committees including
                  Environment Committees, Building Committees.

                  6.2 E&S risk management processes
                  ESIA, Environmental Audits have established processes with enabling policies, laws and
                  regulations, and clear procedures. The ESIA and Environment Audit practice is regulated by
                  NEMA with regulations for registration of practitioners and for the practice in place, and an
                  association of Impact Assessors UAIA (Uganda Association for Impact Assessment) regulated
                  by NEMA. The ESIA practitioners have also been taken through ELMIS, including the ESIA
                  process, certification and practice renewal processes, submission of ESIA and Audit reports,
                  permits and license applications.

                  However, more needs to be done in terms of growing the practice, including training in various

                  preparation of responsive ESIAs/PBs, ESMPs, Environment Management System (EMS) and
                  Health and Safety Management System (HSMS), and undertaking timely environmental audits.

                  6.4 Right of Way Acquisition and Resettlement
                  Land acquisition on projects where their footprint is mainly limited to the acquisition of right
                  of ways with minor impacts has, in the past, been managed primarily through voluntary land
                  donations, supported by stakeholder engagement and signed consent forms. Compensation is
                  provided for any significant impacts that involve acquisition of an affected structure or loss of
                  livelihood. In cases of severely affected PAPs compensation at full replacement cost is
                  provided. Voluntary land donation is an appropriate means to acquire land for certain type of
                  investments (such as small community driven development operations, for example) only when
                  certain criteria are met (and this process is well documented), namely: (i) informed consent
                  (information provided to PAP – in a documented manner – that the PAP is able to refuse and
                  receive compensation); (ii) power of choice (there has to be alternative sitings to the project
                  and, hence, in linear infrastructure projects by definition this does not exist); (iii) minimal
                  impact on PAP, i.e. the amount of land being donated is minor and will not reduce the donor’s
                  remaining land area below that required to maintain the donor’s livelihood at current levels;
                  (iv) no household relocation is involved; (v) the donor is expected to benefit directly from the
                  project (so typically this is for Community-Driven Development type of operations). In the
                  case of UCMID program, investments that are linear or fixed infrastructure, such as roads or
                  drainage, as the land and asset acquisition for those investments shall require compensation at
                  replacement cost to Program affected persons.
                  This ESSA guides that process of land acquisition must be aligned with the World Bank’s policy
                  on land acquisition and resettlement as per the Bank Directive and Core Principle 4 for PforR
                  Financing which requires minimizing land acquisition and involuntary resettlement during
                  implementation, while ensuring economic and social impacts—especially on those without
                  formal land rights—are identified and mitigated. It mandates timely, adequate compensation
                  and livelihood restoration, along with the replacement of any disrupted public infrastructure or
                  services. The lack of sufficient funds for timely and adequate compensation is a risk under
                  UCMID, as similar Programs has encountered this issue in the past.

                  6.7 Gaps Identified
                  6.7.1 Capacity for ESHS Supervision
                  The sampled Cities, Municipalities, and RHDs indicated limited capacity for E&S staff to
                  supervise projects from start to closure, including decommissioning. Only core staff have been
                  considered in most cases (Environmental Officers and CDOs, with no assistants). Various local

                  environmental and social requirements in their work plans. According to the District Local
                  Government planning guidelines, 5% of the total budget for project activities is supposed to be
                  allocated to M&E including E&S aspects. However, this cost is inadequate and, in some cases,
                  used entirely for M&E.
                  6.7.2 Operation and Maintenance
                  Cities/Municipalities and RHDs lack resources for maintaining completed works and have
                  insufficient local revenue, previously reliant on Uganda Road Fund (URF) and other sources
                  that are no longer available. Operation and maintenance (O&M) are significant issues, with
                  non-functional streetlights, challenges in acquiring new equipment like survey machines and
                  cartridges and maintaining streets and drainage due to limited budgets. Some infrastructure
                  requires specialized, expensive maintenance equipment and staff not available in the Public
                  Service structure. Some cases include solar lights, drainages, trucks etc.

                  6.7.7 Stakeholder Engagement and Communication Strategy
                  Stakeholder engagement is recognized as a key tool for managing social risks and ensuring that
                  communities and affected individuals actively participate in project selection, design, and
                  implementation. Engagements should begin early in the program design and preparation stages
                  to introduce the program to communities and other stakeholders. They should also be
                  conducted in a systematic manner, guided by the Stakeholder Engagement and Communication
                  Strategy that MLHUD is in the process of preparing.

                  The City/Municipal Development Forum has played a major role in stakeholder engagement.
                  Forum members attend all meetings and participate in radio programs together with the project
                  implementation team to update communities on program progress and explain the reasons for
                  delays. In addition, the project implementation team holds management meetings with
                  accounting officers to discuss issues as they arise.

                  To be effective, stakeholder engagement must start early in program design and preparation
                  and follow a systematic approach including keeping all records of engagements.

                  6.7.8 Waste Management
                  Cities and Municipalities have expanded boundaries and now face volumes of waste due to
                  increased volumes of waste with growing populations and businesses. Cities/Municipalities
                  and RHDs have limited infrastructure for waste collection and disposal, including insufficient
                  temporary storage and land for waste sites. While USMID did not support urban liquid and
                  solid waste management works, critical waste management issues persist, affecting long-term
                  sustainability and public health.

                  Waste accumulation was evident in some municipalities due to lack of transportation or funds
                  for collection. Most Cities/Municipalities and RHDs have passed ordinances to enforce
                  polluter pays policy, formed garbage task forces, and engaged private garbage collectors,
                  charging a fee approved by the councils. Under USMID, up to twenty-three Garbage trucks
                  were provided and nine weigh-bridges developed. Others have since initiated Public-Private
                  Partnerships (PPP) for waste collection, where the community pays for private collectors,
                  reducing the burden on Cities/Municipalities and RHDs.

                  Some Cities/Municipalities and RHDs have waste composting sites to produce compost
                  manure but lack clear markets or strategy for sale of the product. The Cities/Municipalities
                  and RHDs have implemented the Polluter Pays Principle and are still facing challenges with
                  where to dump or make compost because most of the sites are full and no longer generate
                  fertilizers. To address this, most Cities/Municipalities and RHDs have procured land for waste
                  management, as outlined in the Physical Development Plan.

                  6.7.9 Maintaining equipment and Tools
                  The re-tooling efforts included procuring engineering and survey equipment, laptops, scanners,
                  photocopiers, RTK Total Station survey machines, plotters, GPS devices, environmental
                  monitoring equipment, various software (GIS, AutoCAD), office furniture, motorcycles, and
                  work supervision vehicles (pickups). Renovation and rehabilitation of administrative blocks
                  and offices were also part of retooling. Cities, Municipalities, and RHDs appreciated these
                  components for enhancing their capacities and improving the work environment. Motorcycles
                  contributed to revenue collection. Several challenges were encountered, including insufficient
                  consultation with city and municipal staff regarding their specific equipment needs, inadequate
                  training for staff on the use of newly acquired tools and equipment, inconsistent utilization of
                  the Physical Planning Urban Management Information System (PPUMIS), and a limited
                  budget for equipment maintenance, among other issues.

                   The National Land Policy, 2013: The land policy addresses the contemporary land issues and conflicts facing
                   the Country. The vision of the policy is: “Sustainable and optimal use of land and land-based resources for
                   transformation of Ugandan society and the economy” while the goal of the policy is: “to ensure efficient,
                   equitable and sustainable utilization and management of Uganda’s land and land-based resources for poverty
                   reduction, wealth creation and overall socio-economic development”. The Program will utilize the policy in
                   dealing with land issues and conflicts that may arise during sub-projects implementation.

                   The Land Use Policy, 2004: The National Land Use Policy’s main goal is “to achieve sustainable and equitable
                   social and economic development through land utilization in Uganda”. The specific objectives of the policy
                   include among others to: (i) Promote land use activities that ensure sustainable utilization of natural resources
                   for national socio-economic development. (ii) To ensure planned, environmentally friendly, affordable, and
                   well-distributed human settlements for both rural and urban areas. (iii) To reverse and alleviate adverse
                   environmental effects at local, national, regional and global levels. The policy requires planned development
                   and social and environmental protection. The program will thus ensure environmental and social sustainability
                   in its operations especially during preparations of the Physical Development Plans which must be done in
                   conformity with this policy.

                   National Gender Policy, 2007: The GoU adopted a National Gender Policy as a tool to guide and direct the

                   The Land Act, Cap. 236: Section 78 of the Land Act (Cap 227) principally addresses four major issues
                   namely, holding, control, management, and land disputes. Regarding tenure, the Act repeats in Section 3, the
                   provisions of Article 237 of the Constitution, which vests all land in the citizens of Uganda, to be held under
                   customary, freehold, Mailo or leasehold tenure systems. However, the Land Act provides for compulsory
                   acquisition of land or rights to use land for the execution of public works. Regarding control of land use, the
                   Land Act reaffirms the statutory power of compulsory acquisition conferred on the government and local
                   authorities under articles 26 (2) and 237(2) (a) of the Constitution (Section 43). The Act also requires that
                   landowners manage and utilize land in accordance with regulatory land use planning (Sections 44 and 46).
                   Land will be required during the rehabilitation, expansion and construction of various urban infrastructure. The
                   Act will guide the UCMID program on matters to deal with land acquisition.

                   The Land (Amendment) Act, 2010: Section 76(1) (a) of the Land (Amendment) Act 2010 states that the
                   jurisdiction of the District Land Tribunal (DLT) shall determine disputes relating to the grant, lease,
                   repossession, transfer, or acquisition of land by individuals, the commission or other authority with
                   responsibility relating to land.    The Act provides the DLGs (DLT) with jurisdiction to resolve issues
                   related to land acquisition where need arises.

                   The Land Acquisition Act, Cap 235: The Land Acquisition Act makes provision for the procedures and
                   method of compulsory acquisition of land for public purposes whether for temporary or permanent use. The
                   Minister responsible for land may authorize any person to enter upon the land and survey the land, dig, or bore
                   the subsoil or any other thing necessary for ascertaining whether the land is suitable for a public purpose. The
                   GoU is mandated to pay compensation to any person who suffers damage because of any action related to land
                   acquisition for public purposes. Any dispute as to the compensation payable is to be referred to the Attorney
                   General or court for decision.

                   For any land that will be acquired for the UCMID program right procedures should be followed as guided by
                   the Act.

                   The Roads Act 1965:          The 1965 Roads Act defines the road reserve “right of way” for a road as the area
                   bounded by imaginary lines and of no more than 50 feet from the center line of the road. This area is declared
                   by statutory instrument as the road reserve. The act also states that no person may build any structure, or plant
                   any tree, plant or crop in the road reserve. The road authorities are allowed to take materials and/or dig within
                   the road reserve for the construction and maintenance of the road. The Act will guide LGs and RHD during
                   implementation of sub-projects especially roads & drainage channels in line with PforR modalities that require
                   implementation of sub-projects within existing the ROW.

                   The Local Governments Act, Cap 243 (as amended in 2010): Local Government Act (LGA), 1997 provides
                   for the system of Local Governments (LGs), which is based on the district. Under the District are lower LGs
                   and administrative units. This system provides for elected Councils that have both legislative and executive
                   powers. Thus, the District Councils play an important role in land administration: land surveying, physical
                   planning, and management of municipal resources that are not the responsibility of the central government.
                   According to the LGA, the Districts are charged with the responsibility of acquiring land for
                   development/construction purposes and in the sensitization and mobilization of the local communities. The
                   chairpersons of the District Councils nominate the Executive Committee of each Council, whose functions
                   include: i) Initiating and formulating policy for approval by council; ii) Overseeing the implementation of
                   Government and Council policies and monitor and coordinate activities of Non-Government Organizations
                   (NGOs) in the district; and iii) Receiving and solving disputes forwarded to it from lower LGs. The Act
                   empowers DLGs to develop and implement District rates upon which compensation for crops and non-
                   permanent structures is based. The UCMID program is going to be implemented by MoLHUD, MoFPED,
                   MoLG and the DLGs. The Act provides the system under which the Local Governments operates including
                   developing District rates upon which compensation for crops and non-permanent structures which are likely to
                   be impacted by the program.

                   The Physical Planning Act amended 2020: National Physical Planning Act Amended 2020 under part one,
                   section 3 declares the entire country a planning area. The Physical Planning Act provides for the formulation
                   of the National Physical Planning Board with the responsibility of taking decisions of approval of physical
                   development plans and land use change decisions for the country. The Minister responsible for Physical
                   Planning appoints nine members (9) of whom five shall be persons employed in the public service to the board.
                   Among these members are representatives of some key government Ministries and departments which are

                   Local Council Courts Act, 2006: The Act establishes Local Council (LC) Courts for the administration of

                   The Children Act CAP 62: The Children’s Act under section 5(2) grants any person having custody of a child
                   with a duty to protect the child from discrimination, violence, abuse, and neglect. Furthermore, section 8
                   provides that “no child shall be employed or engaged in any activity that may be harmful to his or her health,
                   education or mental, physical or moral development”. The Act protects Children with disabilities under section
                   9. It states that it is the duty of the parents of children with disabilities and the State to take appropriate steps
                   to see that those children are assessed as early as possible as to the extent and nature of their disabilities; offered
                   appropriate treatment; and afforded facilities for rehabilitation and equal opportunities to education. The Act
                   protects children from employment and any harmful activities during Program implementation and address
                   issues such as SEA/SH&VAC.

                   The National Council for Disability Act, 2003: This establishes the National Council for Disability. The
                   objective of the National Council for Disability include: i) to promote the implementation and equalization of
                   opportunities for Persons with Disabilities (PWD); and ii) to monitor and evaluate the impact of policies and
                   programs designed for equality and full participation of PWD. This policy will help to guide the construction
                   of urban infrastructure such as roads, drainage channels and slum areas should ensure they meet the
                   requirements for universal accessibility.

                   Access to Information Act, 2005: The Act provides for the right of access to information pursuant to Article
                   41 of the Constitution; prescribes the classes of information referred to in the Article; the procedure for
                   obtaining access to that information, and other related matters. Article 5, Right of access indicates that - Every
                   citizen has a right of access to information and records in the possession of the State or any public body, except
                   where the release of the information is likely to prejudice the security or sovereignty of the State or interfere
                   with the right to the privacy of any other person.      The MoLHUD will ensure that participating LGs and
                   RHDs have information about the program and are trained in meaningful and effective stakeholder engagement
                   techniques.

                   The Workers’ Compensation Act, Cap 233: This law provides for compensation to be paid to workers (or
                   their dependents) for injuries suffered and scheduled diseases incurred in the course of their employment.
                   Section 28 of the Act states that; where a medical practitioner grants a certificate that a worker is suffering
                   from a scheduled disease causing disablement or that the death of a workman was caused by any scheduled
                   disease; and the disease was due to the nature of the worker's employment and was contracted within the
                   twenty-four months immediately previous to the date of such disablement or death, the worker or, if he or she
                   is deceased, his or her dependents shall be entitled to claim and to receive compensation under this Act if such
                   disablement or death had been caused by an accident arising out of and in the course of his or her employment.
                   It also emphasizes the provision of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) to employees to minimize accidents
                   and injuries.      Under the Program specifically under RA 2, the civil work contractors will be required to

                   provision of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) to all workers to minimize accidents and injuries.

                   The Employment Act, CAP 226: The Employment Act is the governing legal statutory instrument for the
                   recruitment, contracting, deployment, remuneration, management, and compensation of workers. The Act is
                   based on the provisions of Article 40 of the 1995 Constitution of Uganda. It is the principal legislation that
                   seeks to harmonize relationships between employees and employers, protect workers’ interests and welfare and
                   safeguard their occupational health and safety through: (a) Prohibiting forced labour, discrimination, and sexual
                   harassment at workplaces (Part II; Part IV). (b) Providing for labour inspection by the relevant ministry (Part
                   III). (c) Stipulating rights and duties in employment (weekly rest, working hours, annual leave, maternity and
                   paternity leaves, sick pay, etc. (Part VI). (d) Continuity of employment (continuous service, seasonal
                   employment, etc. (Part VIII). The Act will thus govern labour types and conditions under which the people
                   hired by the project work. It prohibits Child labour (a condition the construction contractor must also comply
                   with) as well as providing guidance on work rights during the operation phase.

                  Annex 8 : PforR Six Core Principles For ESSA

                  Core Principle 1: General Principle of Environmental and Social Management

                       •      Bank Policy for Program-for-Results Financing: Environmental and social management procedures and
                              processes are designed to (a) promote environmental and social sustainability in Program design; (b)
                              avoid, minimize, or mitigate adverse impacts; and (c) promote informed decision-making relating to a
                              program’s environmental and social effects.

                       •      Bank Directive: Program-for-Results Financing (BP 9.00): Program procedures will:

                       •      Operate within an adequate legal and regulatory framework to guide environmental and social impact
                              assessments at the program level.

                       •      Incorporate recognized elements of environmental and social assessment good practice, including (a) early
                              screening of potential effects; (b) consideration of strategic, technical, and site alternatives (including the
                              “no action” alternative); (c) explicit assessment of potential induced, cumulative, and trans-boundary
                              impacts; (d) identification of measures to mitigate adverse environmental or social impacts that cannot be
                              otherwise avoided or minimized; (e) clear articulation of institutional responsibilities and resources to
                              support implementation of plans; and (f) responsiveness and accountability through stakeholder
                              consultation, timely dissemination of program information, and responsive grievance redress measures.


                  Core Principle 2: Natural Habitats and Physical Cultural Resources

                       •      Bank Policy for Program-for-Results Financing: Environmental and social management procedures and
                              processes are designed to avoid, minimize and mitigate against adverse effects on natural habitats and
                              physical cultural resources resulting from the program.

                       •      Bank Directive: Program-for-Results Financing: As relevant, the program to be supported:
                       •      Includes appropriate measures for early identification and screening of potentially important biodiversity
                              and cultural resource areas.
                       •      Supports and promotes the conservation, maintenance, and rehabilitation of natural habitats; avoids the
                              significant conversion or degradation of critical natural habitats, and if avoiding the significant
                              conversion of natural habitats is not technically feasible, includes measures to mitigate or offset
                              impacts or program activities.
                       •      Considering potential adverse effects on physical cultural property and as warranted, provides adequate
                              measures to avoid, minimize, or mitigate such effects.



                  Core Principle 3: Public and Worker Safety

                       •      Bank Policy for Program-for-Results Financing: Environmental and social management procedures and
                              processes are designed to protect public and worker safety against the potential risks associated with (a)
                              construction and/or operations of facilities or other operational practices developed or promoted under the
                              program; (b) exposure to toxic chemicals, hazardous wastes, and otherwise dangerous materials; and (c)
                              reconstruction or rehabilitation of infrastructure located in areas prone to natural hazards.

                       •      Bank Directive: Program-for-Results Financing:
                       •      promote adequate community, individual, and worker health, safety, and security, through the safe design,
                              construction, operation, and maintenance of Program activities; or, in carrying out activities that may be
                              dependent on existing infrastructure, incorporate safety measures, inspections, or remedial works as
                              appropriate; promote measures to address child and forced labor.
                       •      Promotes use of recognized good practice in the production, management, storage, transport, and
                              disposal of hazardous materials generated through program construction or operations; and promotes

Confidential 70   ANNEXES |
                              use of integrated pest management practices to manage or reduce pests or disease vectors; and provides
                              training for workers involved in the production, procurement, storage, transport, use, and disposal of
                              hazardous chemicals in accordance with international guidelines and conventions.
                       •      Includes measures to avoid, minimize, or mitigate community, individual, and worker risks when
                              program activities are located within areas prone to natural hazards such as floods, hurricanes,
                              earthquakes, or other severe weather or climate events.



                  Core Principle 4: Land Acquisition

                       •      Bank Policy for Program-for-Results Financing: Land acquisition and loss of access to natural resources
                              are managed in a way that avoids or minimizes displacement, and affected people are assisted in improving,
                              or at least restoring, their livelihoods and living standards.

                       •      Bank Directive: Program-for-Results Financing: As relevant, the program to be supported:
                       •      Avoid or minimize land acquisition and related adverse impacts.
                       •      Identifies and addresses economic and social impacts caused by land acquisition or loss of access to
                              natural resources, including those affecting people who may lack full legal rights to assets or resources
                              they use or occupy.
                       •      Provides compensation sufficient to purchase replacement assets of equivalent value and to meet any
                              necessary transitional expenses, paid prior to taking of land or restricting access;
                       •      Provides supplemental livelihood improvement or restoration measures if taking of land causes loss of
                              income-generating opportunity (e.g., loss of crop production or employment); and
                       •      Restores or replaces public infrastructure and community services that may be adversely affected.
                       •      And includes measures for land acquisition and related activities to be planned and implemented with
                              appropriate disclosure of information, consultation, and the informed participation of those affected;

                  Core Principle 5: Indigenous Peoples and Vulnerable Groups

                       •      Bank Policy for Program-for-Results Financing: Due consideration is given to cultural appropriateness of,
                              and equitable access to, program benefits giving special attention to rights and interests of Indigenous
                              Peoples and to the needs or concerns of vulnerable groups.

                       •      Bank Directive: Program-for-Results Financing:
                       •      Undertakes free, prior, and informed consultations if Indigenous Peoples are potentially affected
                              (positively or negatively) to determine whether there is broad community support for the program.
                       •      Ensures that Indigenous Peoples can participate in devising opportunities to benefit from exploitation of
                              customary resources or indigenous knowledge, the latter (indigenous knowledge) to include the
                              consent of the Indigenous Peoples.
                       •      Gives attention to groups vulnerable to hardship or disadvantage, including as relevant the poor, the
                              disabled, women and children, the elderly, or marginalized ethnic groups. If necessary, special measures
                              are taken to promote equitable access to program benefits.



                  Core Principle 6: Social Conflict

                       •      Bank Policy for Program-for-Results Financing: Avoid exacerbating social conflict, especially in fragile
                              states, post-conflict areas, or areas subject to territorial disputes.

                       •      Bank Directive: Program-for-Results Financing: Considers conflict risks, including distributional equity
                              and cultural sensitivities.

             The World Bank Office, Kampala
                1. Introduction: World Bank team led the meeting, which included participants online
                    and physical attendants including representatives from government Ministries,
                    Departments, and Agencies (MDAs), as well as staff from all participating Cities,
                    Municipalities and RHDs (Refugee Hosting Districts). The purpose was to validate the
                    Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) report for the Uganda Cities
                    and Municipalities Infrastructure Development Project (UCMID).
                1.1.Attendance:
                    • MDAs: MoLHUD, MGLSD, MoWE, National Environment Management
                        Authority (NEMA), National Planning Authority (NPA), Public Procurement and
                        Disposal of Public Assets Authority (PPDA), Office of the Prime Minister (OPM).
                    • Ten Cities of: Arua, Fort Portal, Gulu, Jinja, Hoima, Lira, Masaka, Mbale, Mbarara,
                        Soroti.
                    • Municipalities of: Bushenyi-Ishaka, Busia, Bugiri, Ibanda, Iganga, Apac, Kabale,

                1.2.Purpose and Scope of the Meeting: Validation of UCMID ESSA: Stephen Ajalu
                    (Task Team Leader, World Bank) outlined the meeting’s main objective: to validate the
                    Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) report for the UCMID
                    programme, encouraging candid feedback from all participants, including
                    representatives from ministries, agencies, and local governments.
                    ESSA had been compiled through consultations and interviews with various entities,
                    and the feedback would inform finalization of the report and subsequent programme
                    decisions. Participants were encouraged to submit comments and questions via chat or
                    in the meeting with the assurance that all feedback would be considered in refining the
                    ESSA and addressing any inaccuracies or gaps.

                  1.3.Overview of UCMID Programme and ESSA Findings: Moses Otim and Elizabeth
                      Aisu presented the background, objectives, and findings of the UCMID programme’s
                      ESSA, detailing its alignment with national policies, the programme’s hybrid structure,
                      and the assessment of environmental and social risks, with input from World Bank and
                      government representatives.
                  1.4.Programme Background and Objectives: The UCMID programme was described as
                      a response to urban policy challenges, aiming to expand infrastructure support to cities,
                      municipalities, and refugee-hosting districts, with objectives including enhanced
                      institutional performance and increased access to climate-resilient infrastructure.
                  1.5.ESSA Methodology and Core Principles: The ESSA was conducted using
                      stakeholder consultations, policy and legislative reviews, and analysis of previous
                      programmes, assessing alignment with six World Bank core principles covering
                      environmental and social sustainability, risk mitigation, and capacity strengthening.


Strictly Confidential 79   ANNEXES |
                      1.6.Key Risks and Benefits Identified: Findings highlighted both benefits (improved
                          infrastructure, social services, job creation, capacity building) and risks (land
                          acquisition challenges, environmental impacts, social conflicts, under-budgeting, and
                          capacity gaps), with recommendations for mitigation and capacity strengthening.
                      2. Key issues discussed
                      2.1.Strengths: Participants identified some strength of the ESSA report to include;
                      2.1.1. Comprehensive Risk Identification: The report meticulously identifies a wide
                              array of E&S risks across the project lifecycle, from preparation to operation and
                              maintenance. These include site selection concerns, construction impacts,
                              occupational and community health and safety (OSH/CHS), waste management,
                              biodiversity, drainage, land acquisition and resettlement, exclusion of vulnerable
                              groups, labour conditions, Gender-Based Violence (GBV), community
                              engagement, and cultural heritage (pp. 23-28).
                      2.1.2. Detailed Institutional and Policy Analysis: The assessment provides a thorough
                              review of Uganda's policy, legal, and regulatory framework for E&S risk
                              management, highlighting the strengths of the existing system and outlining the
                              mandates of various institutions involved (NEMA, MoLHUD, MGLSD, etc.) (pp.
                              29-32). The alignment with the World Bank's PforR core principles is also well-
                              articulated (pp. 32-34).
                      2.1.3. Lessons Learned Integration: The ESSA effectively incorporates lessons and
                              experiences from previous PforR programs like USMID, USMID-AF, UgIFT-AF,
                              and GKMA-UDP, which adds significant value and practical relevance to the
                              findings and recommendations (pp. 9, 21).
                      2.1.4. Actionable Recommendations: The report concludes with clear and specific
                              recommendations aimed at enhancing program design, strengthening institutional
                              capacity, improving ESHS management, refining land acquisition procedures,
                              strengthening GRM, addressing labor and working conditions, and improving waste
                              and climate risk management (pp. 41-43).
                      2.1.5. Stakeholder Engagement: The methodology emphasizes stakeholder
                              consultations, including various levels of government and technical staff, ensuring
                              a broad perspective on the issues and potential solutions (pp. 21-22).
                      2.1.6. Land Acquisition and Compensation Challenges: Participants indicated
                              persistent challenges around land acquisition, compensation, and voluntary land
                              donation in urban operations. World Bank representatives clarified policy
                              requirements and the need for clear procedures and adequate funding for
                              compensation to Program affected people for land acquisition impacts.
                      2.1.7. Compensation funding issues: Concerns were raised about delays in project
                              implementation due to insufficient government funding for compensation, with
                              participants noting that voluntary donation was sometimes used as a workaround
                              but often lacked proper disclosure and consent procedures. GoU should clarify
                              about the counterpart funding which could be used for compensation.
                      2.1.8. Policy and procedural clarifications: World Bank representatives clarified that
                              voluntary land donation should only be considered in specific cases, primarily for
                              small-scale, community-driven projects. For larger infrastructure initiatives, it is
                              essential to adhere to standard land acquisition procedures, which include proper
                              land valuation and fair compensation. Additionally, it was noted that compensation
                              does not always have to be monetary; alternatives such as in-kind compensation

Confidential 80   ANNEXES |
                              and livelihood restoration should also be considered as best practices within the
                              program. It was emphasized that affected persons must be fully informed of their
                              rights and the value of their land, with the option to refuse donation, and that any
                              compensation (monetary or in-kind) should be transparently documented.
                      2.1.9. Quantitative Risk Assessment: Participants requested more granular, quantitative
                              analysis of risks and impacts, suggesting that future assessments should distinguish
                              between areas with and without issues, and tailor mitigation measures accordingly.
                      2.1.10. Capacity Gaps, staffing, and institutional strengthening: Participants discussed
                              capacity gaps in local governments, updates on staffing, and the need for ongoing
                              training and equipment provision, with recommendations for updating records and
                              prioritizing capacity building in the program’s action plan.
                              Participants clarified that some previously reported staffing gaps had been filled
                              and requested that the ESSA report be updated to reflect current staffing levels, with
                              instructions to submit updated templates to the ministry.
                              Recommendations were made for further capacity building, including training in
                              environmental and social safeguards, provision of equipment, and support for
                              monitoring and enforcement, particularly in areas such as occupational health and
                              safety.

                      2.1.11. Institutional Coordination: Suggestions were made to harmonize grievance
                              redress mechanisms and improve coordination between ministries, local
                              governments, and other stakeholders to ensure effective implementation and
                              compliance.
                      2.1.12. Recommendations were made for managing environmental and social risks,
                              including improved screening, budgeting, stakeholder engagement, and monitoring,
                              with input from local government and ministry representatives.
                      2.1.13. Screening and Mitigation Measures: Recommendations included strengthening
                              screening systems to avoid encroachment on sensitive ecosystems, ensuring timely
                              environmental assessments and approvals, and implementing robust mitigation
                              measures for construction and operational risks.
                      2.1.14. Budgeting and Resource Allocation: Participants highlighted the need for
                              adequate budgeting for environmental and social safeguards, suggesting that the
                              standard 5% allocation may be insufficient and advocating for tailored budgeting
                              based on project-specific needs.
                      2.1.15. Stakeholder Engagement and Grievance Redress: The importance of
                              stakeholder engagement was emphasized, with calls for improved community
                              mobilization, awareness creation, and the establishment of effective grievance
                              redress mechanisms at both local and national levels.
                      2.1.16. Operation and Maintenance Planning: Recommendations were made to include
                              operation and maintenance requirements in project design and budgeting, ensuring
                              sustainability of infrastructure investments beyond the construction phase.
                      2.1.17. Waste Management and Climate Change Considerations: Representatives from
                              the Ministry of Local Government, Ministry of Water, and other entities discussed

                              awareness creation, and the integration of climate change policies and regulations.
                      2.1.18. Waste Management Policy: The Ministry of Local Government reported ongoing
                              development of a national waste management policy, emphasizing the importance
                              of awareness creation, community mobilization, and high-level coordination for
                              effective implementation.
                      2.1.19. Programme Limitations: It was clarified that the UCMID programme would only
                              cover low-risk waste management activities, with more comprehensive
                              interventions to be addressed in a separate, standalone project.
                      2.1.20. Climate Change Integration: Participants noted the need to include recent climate
                              change acts and policies in the ESSA, ensuring alignment with national climate

                      2.1.21. Utility Mapping and Project Design Challenges: Participants discussed
                              challenges in utility mapping, project design, and the need for accurate information
                              from utility firms, with updates on the launch of a national AGIS platform for
                              mapping utility lines.
                      2.1.22. Utility Mapping Initiatives: A national AGIS platform was launched to map utility
                              lines across the country, aiming to improve accuracy and coordination in project
                              design and implementation.
                      2.1.23. Design Stage Budgeting: Recommendations were made to conduct utility mapping
                              and allocate budgets for related aspects at the design stage, reducing delays and cost
                              overruns during project execution.
                      2.1.24. Grievance Redress Mechanisms and Monitoring: Ministry representatives and
                              participants discussed the need for harmonized grievance redress mechanisms,
                              improved monitoring systems, and the development of automated tools to track and

                              efforts under the PIM Plus programme.
                      2.1.26. Automated Monitoring Tools: Plans were outlined to develop software tools for
                              tracking grievances, enabling real-time monitoring and reporting to enhance
                              transparency and responsiveness.
