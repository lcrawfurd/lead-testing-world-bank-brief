                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     Since 2017, the World Bank has supported Yemen’s health sector through large-scale emergency operations that have
                     delivered essential health, nutrition, and water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) services to millions, while gradually
                     investing in local institutions and systems. These efforts have sustained human capital in a protracted crisis, reaching
                     nearly 24 million beneficiaries and training over 18,000 health personnel. Despite achievements, challenges in Yemen
                     persist—including low immunization rates, malnutrition, conflict-related access constraints, declining donor funding,

                     and reliance on external third-party implementation. The upcoming operation will prioritize integration, strengthen
                     local capacity, and revamp health workforce development by aligning community cadres with national strategies,
                     improving training systems, and creating job opportunities for health workers. The project will consist of 5 components:
                     Component 1: Delivery of integrated health, nutrition and WASH services Component 1 will prioritize delivering an
                     integrated minimum service package (MSP) across all levels of care—primary, secondary, and tertiary. Investments will
                     be made to improve water and sanitation infrastructure at primary health centers. A Geographic Accelerator sub-
                     component will tailor area-based interventions to local needs, combining MSP delivery with nutrition, WASH, and social
                     protection interventions. Component 2: Support to prevent and respond to communicable disease outbreaks
                     Component 2 will retain emergency response capacity through disease surveillance, rapid response teams, laboratory
                     support. It will also deliver emergency water trucking and to hospitals and support water quality testing. Component 3:
                     Systems building for sustainable delivery of health and nutrition services Component 3 focuses on building sustainable
                     systems for health and nutrition service delivery by strengthening sub-national institutions and the health workforce. It
                     includes tailored training for ministries and local health offices in planning, monitoring, and financial management,
                     alongside support for refining national health strategies. A dedicated subcomponent will address critical health
                     workforce reforms, aiming to resolve systemic bottlenecks and establish more sustainable funding mechanisms for
                     health worker salaries. These reforms will also contribute to job creation and retention in the health sector, helping
                     transition from volunteer-based service delivery to a more stable and professionalized workforce. Component 4: Project
                     management Component 4 will consist of supervision costs, monitoring and evaluation, third party monitoring and
                     financing of E&S compliance requirements, fiduciary aspects and procurement. Component 5: Contingent emergency
                     response category (CERC) A zero cost CERC component will support the operation in the case of emergencies.

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     Yemen is characterized by a predominantly semi-arid climate with limited rainfall and high temperatures, especially in
                     its coastal and desert regions. Despite these challenging conditions, Yemen has rich biodiversity, shaped by its varied
                     topography, ranging from coastal plains and highlands to plateaus and deserts. This project will be implemented in
                     several governorates in Yemen but specific locations where project sub-components will be implemented have not yet
                     been identified. The project will be implemented in urban as well as rural areas, in different jurisdictions with different
                     environmental, social and institutional settings.

                     Yemen’s overall conditions have deteriorated dramatically over the last decade, leaving the country in a profound state
                     of fragility. Authority in Yemen remains deeply fragmented, with the internationally recognized Internationally
                     Recognized Government (IRG) operating from Aden and the Houthi De Facto Authorities (DFA) controlling Sana'a and
                     large parts of the north and west of the country. The conflict has weakened institutions, eroded the social contract, and
                     created parallel governance structures that complicate project implementation and the equitable delivery of services
                     across the country.

                     Yemen’s poverty rate has soared due to over a decade of protracted conflict, economic collapse, fragmented political
                     authority, breakdown of essential health service delivery (and other basic services), and rising food prices. Almost 80
                     percent of the population now live below the poverty line, with rural areas and larger households disproportionately
                     affected. Severe acute malnutrition remains a critical concern, with over 609,000 children affected in 2024, and Yemen's
                     15 most affected governorates — which this project targets — accounting for 89 percent of the country's stunted

                     children. Massive flooding in 2024 (and recent flooding in March 2026) further damaged health infrastructure and
                     disrupted supply chains for medicines and medical supplies.

                     Many health facilities require upgrades to their water and sanitation systems to reduce losses, expand service coverage,
                     and improve water quality. Latrines need construction or repair using gender-responsive, disability-accessible designs,
                     and additional handwashing stations are needed in health facilities and public areas to strengthen hygiene practices.
                     Medical waste management remains challenging, particularly final disposal due to the lack of specialized landfills. While
                     many facilities use incineration and autoclaving, others lack such technologies and still resort to improper waste
                     disposal.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     Under the proposed project, UNICEF and WHO will serve as grant recipients and implementing agencies (IAs). Both
                     agencies have an established track record implementing World Bank-financed projects under the ESF in Yemen. UNICEF
                     is currently implementing the Emergency Social Protection Emergency Crisis Response Project (ESPECRP, P173582) and
                     the Sustaining Education and Learning Project (SEAL, P509685). WHO and UNICEF are jointly implementing the Yemen
                     Emergency Human Capital Project (YEHCP, P176570), which provides the most directly relevant precedent for this
                     operation given its comparable scope, implementing arrangements, and operating context.

                     Both agencies maintain dedicated environmental and social teams comprising environmental specialists, social
                     specialists, gender specialists, and grievance mechanism (GM) focal points. Under the ongoing YEHCP, both agencies
                     have demonstrated the capacity to manage environmental and social risks and impacts, including strengthening

                     infection prevention and control practices and medical waste management at supported health facilities. Their overall
                     E&S performance has been satisfactory, though the Bank has identified specific areas requiring improvement that will
                     be systematically addressed under this project.

                     Both agencies have established GM systems with multiple channels for receiving and addressing complaints, including
                     dedicated pathways for SEA/SH cases. However, a review of performance under prior operations has revealed that
                     these systems require further strengthening in two areas. First, existing GM channels will need to be assessed and
                     adapted to ensure they are genuinely accessible to the most vulnerable project-affected parties, including women,
                     persons with disabilities, IDPs, and hard-to-reach populations. Second, worker grievance processes have raised concerns
                     regarding confidentiality, risk of retaliation, and discrimination issues that will be explicitly addressed through
                     strengthened provisions in the Labor Management Procedures (LMP) and SEP for this project. Lessons learned from
                     prior operations will be incorporated into the design of all relevant E&S instruments.

                     Environmental and social oversight under this project will be maintained through a combination of quarterly Third-Party
                     Monitoring (TPM) reporting to independently verify implementation of E&S measures across all instruments and the
                     ESCP, biannual E&S progress reporting by each implementing agency, and biannual implementation support missions
                     conducted by the World Bank. Targeted measures to address identified gaps, including the quality and specificity of
                     E&S reporting, grievance and incident management practices, and the comprehensiveness of TPM reports in meeting
                     ESCP requirements will be incorporated into the design of the E&S instruments for this project.

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                             Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                      Substantial
                     The environmental risk classification for this project is assessed as substantial, primarily driven by activities under
                     Component 3 and Sub-component 1.2, which involve enhancement of public health laboratory capabilities, public
                     health campaigns, and rehabilitation of water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) infrastructure at health facilities. The
                     most significant environmental risk relates to healthcare waste management, particularly the safe collection,
                     segregation, storage, transportation, and final disposal of medical and infectious waste. The risk is heightened by
                     inadequate waste treatment and disposal infrastructure, including the absence or insufficiency of operational
                     incinerators or other disposal units, at a number of targeted health facilities. Improper management of medical waste
                     poses direct risks of environmental contamination (soil, water, and air) and creates exposure pathways for healthcare
                     workers, patients, service users, and surrounding communities. Closely linked to this is the risk of inadequate
                     infection prevention and control (IPC) practices at health facilities. Poor IPC protocols can increase the risk of
                     healthcare-associated infections and cross-contamination, with direct implications for the health and safety of both
                     facility staff and the people they serve. Additional environmental and community health and safety risks arise from
                     the transportation, handling, storage, and disposal of vaccines, laboratory reagents and materials, medical
                     consumables, and associated waste generated through project activities. Risks include improper cold chain
                     management for vaccines and biological materials, accidental spills or leakages during transportation, and inadequate
                     disposal of expired or unused medical supplies — all of which can have adverse impacts on community health and the
                     surrounding environment if not properly managed. Furthermore, workers involved in vaccination campaigns,



Public Disclosure
                     laboratory operations, and the handling and disposal of medical supplies and waste face occupational health and
                     safety (OHS) risks, including exposure to biological hazards, sharp medical instruments, and hazardous chemicals.
                     These risks require specific OHS protocols, appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE), and staff training to be
                     systematically addressed throughout project implementation.

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The overall social risk rating has been confirmed as Substantial at appraisal, consistent with the concept stage
                     assessment. This rating reflects Yemen's deeply fragile, conflict-affected operating environment, the complexity of
                     the implementing arrangements involving two UN agencies across 15 governorates, and the nature of the activities
                     supported, including infrastructure rehabilitation, health service delivery, community outreach, and capacity building,
                     each carrying distinct social risk profiles. Labor risks, including child, forced, and informal labor (including within solar
                     equipment and other supply chains), discriminatory and gender-exclusionary labor practices, and worker
                     confidentiality, retaliation, and discrimination concerns, are cross-cutting across all components (1.1, 1.2, 2, 3).
                     SEA/SH risks are similarly pervasive, arising from the presence of civil works/rehabilitation labor near health facilities,
                     power imbalances in training and deployment settings, and the broader dynamics of Yemen's conflict context (1.2, 2,
                     3). Community health and safety risks, including those associated with increased construction traffic, unsafe
                     worksites, and disruption of access to health facilities during rehabilitation works, are primarily concentrated under
                     infrastructure activities [1.2), while the handling, transport, and disposal of hazardous biological materials and
                     laboratory waste also present community health and safety risks, particularly for communities in proximity to WHO-
                     supported laboratories, hospitals, and outbreak response sites where inadequate waste management practices could
                     result in environmental contamination or community exposure to infectious agents (1.1, 2). Exclusion and inequality

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                           Relevant

                      ESS3 is relevant as the project will produce medical waste generated through vaccination campaigns and other
                      project-supported health activities, including contaminated liquids, sharps, chemicals, and hazardous laboratory
                      materials. These medical wastes present risks of environmental contamination and adverse impacts on local
                      communities if not adequately managed. To address these risks, UNICEF and WHO will prepare an Infection Control
                      and Medical Waste Management Plan (ICMWMP), which all beneficiary health facilities will be required to follow. The
                      ICMWMP will establish protocols for the safe segregation, handling, transportation, treatment, and disposal of
                      medical and expired waste, and will promote resource efficiency and the adoption of climate-friendly cold chain
                      systems in line with good international practice. Considering the experience under the ongoing health operation

                      (Yemen Emergency Human Capital Project (YEHCP - P176570)) medical waste management, particularly final disposal,
                      remains a significant challenge in the project context. While some facilities employ incineration and/or autoclaving,
                      others lack access to such technologies and currently resort to disposal methods that do not meet environmental or
                      public health standards. The absence of specialized hazardous waste landfills further constrains safe disposal options.

                      waste. Civil works under Sub-component 1.2 for the rehabilitation of WASH facilities and construction of latrines may

                      through site-specific Environmental and Social Management Plans (ESMPs), which will incorporate waste
                      management measures and ensure compliance with WBG EHS Guidelines and national environmental laws.
