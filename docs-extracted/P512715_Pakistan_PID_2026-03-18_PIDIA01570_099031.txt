                          B. Introduction and Context

                          Country Context

                          1.     Amid an economic crisis at the start of fiscal year (FY) 2024, Pakistan undertook decisive measures to prevent
                          a sovereign default, including sharp fiscal consolidation, exchange rate adjustment, and monetary tightening. An IMF-
                          SBA arrangement helped to bridge financing gaps and restore confidence. Adjustment has been successful, with inflation
                          now falling, the exchange rate stabilizing, and foreign exchange reserves at more comfortable levels, allowing for the
                          easing of import controls. However, fiscal consolidation measures, tight monetary conditions, and recent flooding have
                          continued to weigh on economic activity, with the economy expected to grow by only 3 percent in FY26. Pakistan must
                          now carefully translate stabilization efforts into sustainable, faster growth and reduce poverty from current high levels
                          (estimated at around 22 percent using the national poverty line). This will require reforms to make the tax system more

                           effective and equitable, enhance the efficiency of public spending, reduce the large role of the state in the economy,
                           lower trade barriers, improve the business environment, and improve basic services to address human capital deficits.
                           Reform efforts are underway in critical areas but remain at early stages of implementation. The outlook is predicated on
                           the IMF Extended Fund Facility program remaining on track, continued fiscal restraint, and continued external financing
                           inflows. Significant downside risks arise from heavy banking sector exposure to sovereign debt, geopolitical factors, and
                           further natural disasters and climate change impacts.

                           2.      Pakistan’s high vulnerability to climate change is a risk multiplier, compounding its human, economic
                           development, and jobs challenges. Pakistan is among the top 10 countries worldwide most affected by climate change
                           and is in the top 4 countries affected by recurring extreme events over the past 30-year period.1 More frequent and
                           extreme weather events are adversely affecting ecosystems, infrastructure, people, and their settlements. Economic
                           activity is also disrupted, leading to jobs and livelihoods lost due to destroyed harvests, lost livestock, and inactivity of
                           businesses. Heatwaves, floods, droughts, and cyclones are prevalent risks. Flooding in particular is expected to become
                           the new normal due to warmer climate projections accelerating glacial melting in the higher altitudes whereas
                           downstream regions are expected to experience a ‘higher magnitude of multiday precipitation’ increasing the risk of flash
                           flooding.2 The combined risks from the intensification of climate change and environmental degradation, unless
                           addressed, will further aggravate Pakistan’s economic fragility, and could reduce annual gross domestic product (GDP)
                           by 18 to 20 percent per year by 2050.3




_For Official Use Only_
                           3.     Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (KP) is among the most flood-prone regions in Pakistan. Flood events, including riverine
                           floods, flash floods, and glacial lake outburst floods (GLOFs), have intensified over time due to factors such as climate
                           change, deforestation, land degradation, and increasing population density in flood-prone areas. As per the National
                           Disaster Management Plan 2025, about three-quarters (30.3 million) of the 40.9 million population of KP live in districts
                           vulnerable to flooding.4 KP has in the recent past experienced severe flood events, including in 2010, 2016, 2022 and
                           2025, that resulted in significant loss of life, disruption to livelihoods, and damage to infrastructure and productive assets.

                           Sectoral and Institutional Context

                           4.      KP sustained significant damage and losses from flooding events over the years, with approximately US$2.3
                           billion in cumulative damages from major flood events in 2010, 2022, and 2025 alone.5 More recently, the 2025
                           monsoon triggered localized riverine, glacial, and flash flooding, causing 509 human fatalities. This represents the highest
                           death toll seen in KP in recent years, surpassing the 2022 floods, underscoring the severity of the impact despite the
                           relatively localized nature of the events. Additionally, 5,467 livestock were lost, 514 km of roads and 66 bridges were
                           damaged, and over 3,000 houses were damaged or destroyed.6 The 2022 floods were also devastating for KP, leading to
                           309 lives lost.7 Damages from the 2022 floods were estimated at US$935 million, losses were assessed at US$658 million,

                           5.     Exposure is concentrated in the settlements along rivers, where flood and landslide risks are the highest and
                           most of the population is located. KP is characterized by diverse topography, with nearly 70 percent of its area consisting
                           of mountains and hills ranging from 200 m in the southern plains to peaks above 7,000 m in the north, while the highest
                           density of the population is concentrated in valleys and river basins along the Kabul, Swat, and Indus river systems.10 The
                           population is exposed to a mix of hazard types including flash and riverine floods, GLOFs, mudflows, and landslides
                           precipitated by glacial melting and heavy monsoon rainfall. Rising regional temperatures have accelerated glacial melt in
                           northwestern KP, resulting in the formation of more than 3,000 glacial lakes.11 Further downstream, KP’s exposure to
                           sustained monsoon rainfall is expected to intensify, increasing flood potential.12 Of KP’s 35 districts, the Government
                           currently designates 11 as high flood risk and 14 as medium flood risk.13

                           6.     Building flood resilience is foundational to KP’s long-term development, poverty reduction, and job creation,




_For Official Use Only_
                           yet the scale of flood risk facing KP far exceeds what public finances can absorb through reactive recovery and
                           reconstruction. Recent experience illustrates this imbalance: recovery and reconstruction needs from Pakistan’s 2022
                           floods were estimated at roughly 1.6 times the entire national development budget for FY2023,14 underscoring that post-
                           disaster rebuilding alone is fiscally unsustainable. At the same time, providing universal protection against high-return-
                           period floods (for example, 1-in-100-year events) across all people, assets, and locations in KP would require investment
                           far beyond available capital budgets and would take decades to implement.

                           7.      The KP Irrigation Department (KP ID) currently manages flood protection infrastructure such as embankments,
                           carrier channels, check dams, and gauge stations. While such gray infrastructure is effective in managing flooding, it
                           cannot provide complete protection against increasingly frequent and severe floods. There is a need to complement
                           these interventions with site-specific nature-based solutions (NbS), a robust early warning system, and adequate
                           emergency preparedness and response (EP&R) capabilities. Site-specific NbS at the scheme level complement gray
                           infrastructure, enhancing localized flood resilience. While the KP ID has traditionally focused on gray infrastructure, it
                           increasingly recognizes the potential of NbS, particularly given their alignment with KP’s natural landscapes. However,
                           the department has limited experience and capacity in designing and implementing such initiatives and will rely on the
                           technical leadership of the KP Climate Change, Forestry, Environment and Wildlife Department.

                          8.     KP’s disaster management architecture, led by PDMA and supported by District Disaster Management
                          Authorities (DDMAs), provides the institutional foundation for EP&R. PDMA is housed under the Relief, Rehabilitation
                          and Settlement Department (RRSD). While PDMA plays a central role in multi-hazard preparedness and relief
                          coordination, its systems for early warning dissemination, contingency planning, and risk analytics remain
                          underdeveloped. At the district level, DDMAs exist legally under the National Disaster Management Act (2010) but are
                          often under-resourced or nonfunctional in practice. The absence of operational District Emergency Operation Centers,
                          warehouses, and basic logistics such as vehicles has constrained their ability to manage last-mile preparedness and
                          response. Strengthening PDMA’s systems and fully operationalizing DDMAs through targeted infrastructure and capacity
                          support are therefore critical priorities for enhancing the KP’s disaster readiness.

                          9.     Rescue 1122 is KP’s frontline lifesaving emergency response service but is constrained by limited capacity and
                          geographical coverage. Like PDMA, it is housed under the RRSD. Rescue 1122 is mandated to oversee rescue operations,
                          emergency response, frontline ambulance service, protection, and prevention across all types of emergencies, including
                          health emergencies and water-related hazards. However, many high-risk sites remain underserved due to the absence
                          of rescue centers, while existing facilities lack adequate equipment, trained personnel, and disaster-specific protocols.
                          To strengthen provincial disaster response, the service requires expanded coverage through new centers in hazard-prone
                          areas, upgraded technical capacity and equipment, and tailored training to address the increasingly frequent and
                          complex nature of flood emergencies.

                           D. Project Description

                          10.    Component 1 - Improving Infrastructure and Planning for Resilience (estimated US$193 million). This
                          component will support the design and construction of priority infrastructure investments aimed at reducing KP’s
                          exposure to climate change-exacerbated floods including riverine floods, increased flash flooding due to changing
                          monsoon patterns, and GLOFs from accelerated glacial melt. The component includes both gray and green solutions for
                          flood protection and will be implemented by the KP ID, with technical support provided through a dedicated technical
                          assistance (TA) subcomponent. TA will also pave the way for future engagements and investments.

                          11.     Subcomponent 1.1 - Flood Protection Infrastructure (US$188 million). This subcomponent will finance the
                          construction and rehabilitation of critical infrastructure to mitigate flood risk in KP. Investments under this subcomponent
                          will include a combination of flood protection infrastructure, such as embankments, flood carrier channels, spurs,

                           retaining walls, revetments, gabion works, and check dams15, to attenuate peak flows and reduce downstream impacts.
                           Activities will also include targeted desilting, channelization, dredging, and sediment management in critical river reaches
                           and confluences. Infrastructure, such as embankments, will be designed for up to 1-in-100-year flood considering climate
                           change. NbS will be considered on a site-specific basis to replace or complement gray infrastructural interventions where
                           possible, which will potentially include terracing, slope stabilization using vegetation, and riverbank restoration.

                           12.     Subcomponent 1.2 - Technical Assistance for Development of Long-Term Infrastructure Investment Plan (US$5
                           million). This subcomponent will support the development of a province-wide flood risk assessment and planning
                           framework to guide long-term flood resilience investments in KP. It will include hydrological and risk modeling,
                           preparation of flood hazard and risk maps under current and future climate scenarios, and identification of priority
                           hotspots for intervention. The subcomponent will propose a phased program of structural and nonstructural measures,
                           including gray infrastructure and nature-based solutions, to inform investment prioritization, sequencing, and future
                           feasibility and design studies.

                           13.    Component 2 - Establishing Flood Early Warning System (US$8 million). This component will support the
                           establishment and operationalization of an integrated Flood Early Warning System (FEWS) for KP, managed through
                           collaboration between KP PDMA and KP ID. The FEWS will link hydromet observations, forecasting and modeling, decision
                           support systems (DSSs), and standardized alert dissemination to enable timely and coordinated preparedness and
                           response to climate-exacerbated flood events.




_For Official Use Only_
                           14.    Subcomponent 2.1 - Flood Telemetry, Data Integration, and Forecasting (US$5 million). This subcomponent will
                           strengthen hydrometeorological service delivery in KP by expanding and harmonizing the hydromet observation network
                           operated by KP ID and PDMA, enhancing hydrological and hydraulic modeling capacities within KP ID, and establishing
                           an integrated data management platform to support real-time data access and data sharing across relevant provincial
                           and federal stakeholders.

                           15.    Subcomponent 2.2 - Decision Support Systems (DSSs) and Last-Mile Connectivity (US$3 million). This
                           subcomponent will operationalize the FEWS by establishing integrated decision-support and alert dissemination
                           functions. It will support a Flood Risk Management DSS (FRM-DSS) for KP ID to integrate real-time hydromet data and
                           model outputs for improved reservoir and river operations, an Early Warning and Early Action Decision Support System
                           (EWEA-DSS) for KP PDMA to enable timely and evidence-based preparedness and response actions based on forecasts
                           and impact information, and a Common Alerting Protocol to ensure consistent, standardized dissemination of flood
                           warnings through multiple communication channels to at-risk communities through Subcomponent 3.1.

                           16.    Component 3 - Strengthening Institutional and Community Preparedness (US$39 million). This component will
                           enhance the capacity of provincial institutions and local communities in KP to prepare for and respond to climate-induced
                           multi-hazard disasters, with a particular focus on floods. It will strengthen the operational outreach and institutional
                           capabilities of PDMA and Rescue 1122, improving the application of risk information in development planning and
                           building community-level resilience. The component complements the infrastructure-focused and FEWS investments
                           under Components 1 and 2 and is designed to ensure that both institutional systems and social preparedness mechanisms
                           are adequately equipped to manage and mitigate disaster risks.

                          17.     Subcomponent 3.1 - Strengthening Disaster Preparedness and Community Resilience (US$14 million). Building
                          on the FEWS developed under Component 2, this subcomponent will strengthen the operational capacity of KP PDMA to
                          lead multi-hazard disaster preparedness, coordinate response, and support relief and recovery planning. It will support
                          multi-hazard vulnerability and risk assessments in high-risk districts, establishment of a Provincial Disaster Risk
                          Information System (PDRIS) to consolidate risk information, deployment of digital tools for disaster reporting, impact
                          assessment, and beneficiary registration, modernization of emergency operations centers and response facilities, and
                          implementation of community-based disaster risk management and last-mile early warning dissemination in selected
                          high-risk districts.

                          18.     Subcomponent 3.2 - Enhancing Emergency Response Systems (US$25 million). This subcomponent will
                          strengthen the disaster response capacity of Rescue 1122, with a focus on flood and climate-related emergencies, by
                          expanding service coverage in flood-prone and high-risk areas through the establishment of rescue and highway stations,
                          improving operational logistics, and upgrading command-and-control and communication systems. The subcomponent
                          will finance specialized rescue and evacuation equipment, boats, vehicles, heavy machinery, and personal protective
                          gear, and will support targeted training for Rescue 1122 staff in emergency medical response, swift water rescue, gender-
                          sensitive rescue, GBV response and referral, and coordination with communities and local authorities.

                          Units (PIUs) and operations of the KP ID and RRSD. It will cover project management, procurement, contract

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts




                          21.     The overall Environmental and Social (E&S) of the project is assessed as Substantial. These are associated with
                          the project’s flood risk management investments across KP, including flood protection civil works, NBSs, and the provision
                          of FEWS. Environmental risks include localized hydro-morphological impacts on environmental flows and downstream
                          areas, erosion and sedimentation, construction-related nuisances such as air and noise pollution and waste management,
                          risks from improper handling of fuels and chemicals, and occupational and community health and safety concerns linked
                          to works in or near waterways and in glacial lake outburst flood prone areas. Social risks may be heightened where

                           could result in physical or economic displacement, livelihood impacts, and social tensions. Additional risks stem from
                           local socioeconomic conditions and intradistrict disparities, particularly in newly merged areas, which could lead to
                           unequal distribution of benefits and perceptions of inequity. The project will need to address risks of social exclusion and
                           marginalization, especially for women, religious minorities, and indigenous communities such as the Kalash in Chitral
                           District, with particular attention to inclusive outreach and accessibility of early warning systems and disaster
                           preparedness measures.

                           22.     These E&S risks will be mitigated through the following actions. An exclusion list is embedded into the selection
                           of investment criteria to avoid any high-risk investments, investments in critical habitats/area of high biodiversity value,

                           Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

                          23.     Two PIUs will be established with dedicated resources and enhanced systems to meet the operational, fiduciary,
                          and safeguards standards of the World Bank. The first PIU will be established under the KP ID and the second PIU under
                          the RRSDs. The PIUs will include dedicated technical, fiduciary, and safeguards personnel. Targeted TA and capacity-
                          building support will be provided by the World Bank during preparation and early implementation to ensure readiness
                          and effective delivery while promoting coordination with ongoing resilience and flood management programs in KP. A
                          Project Steering Committee (PSC), chaired by the Chairman of the KP Planning and Development Department (P&DD), will
                          be responsible for overall project-level oversight of implementation and cross-sectoral coordination.

                          24.     The KP ID will implement Component 1. The component will support the planning, design, and implementation
                          of flood protection infrastructure across vulnerable areas of KP. The department holds the statutory mandate for flood
                          management and irrigation service delivery and operates through a decentralized structure with zonal and district-level
                          engineering directorates.

                          25.     The KP ID and RRSD will jointly implement Component 2. Due to the integrated and cross-sectoral nature of the
                          FEWS and coordination required between the KP ID and PDMA, which is under the RRSD, both departments will
                          collaborate to ensure effective implementation and long-term sustainability of assets and systems established under this
                          component. Private sector engagement will potentially be taken up to enhance operations and maintenance.

                          26.      The RRSD will implement Component 3. The RRSD oversees PDMA and Rescue 1122 in KP, and the two agencies
                          will serve as executing partners, with PDMA working on improving multi-hazard disaster preparedness and Rescue 1122
                          supporting the improvement of disaster response. This institutional arrangement will promote coordination, efficiency,
                          and accountability across concerned departments, while ensuring consistency with the project’s objectives and fiduciary
                          standards.
