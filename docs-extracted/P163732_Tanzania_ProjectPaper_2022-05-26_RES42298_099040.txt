I. PROGRAM STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING

A      Program Status

1. The Tanzania Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Program (SRWSSP) is a US$350 million Program-for-
   Results (PforR) operation that is supporting the implementation of Water Sector Development Program Phase 2 in
   Tanzania. The Program was approved on June 26, 2018, with an IDA credit of SDR 243.4 million (US$350 million
   equivalent), and became effective on July 15, 2019. The current closing date is July 31, 2024. To date, the Program
   has disbursed 53 percent (US$182.0 million equivalent). The second-year results have been verified, and a Results
   Achievement Notification Letter was received on April 5, 2022. Since two of the Disbursement-linked Results (DLRs)
   surpassed the allocations, the Government of Tanzania (GoT) requested a restructuring of the Program to reallocate
   funds to these overachieved disbursement-linked indicators (DLIs) with an official letter, dated April 5, 2022.

2. The Program Development Objectives (PDO) of SRWSSP are to increase access to rural water supply and sanitation
   services in participating districts and strengthen the capacity of selected sector institutions to sustain service
   delivery. It covers 86 districts in 17 regions that have low access to improved water supply and sanitation, high
   stunting rates, and high poverty rates. It has three Results Areas and an IPF component. Each result area and
   associated DLIs are summarized as below:
       (i) Results Area 1- Sustainable access to improved water services in rural areas (US$105 million), providing
             increased access to well-designed, properly constructed, and well-managed rural water supply services (both
             new1 and rehabilitated) and sustaining existing water points to ensure they remain functional and provide
             adequate service levels (DLI #1&2).
       (ii) Results Area 2- Increased access to improved sanitation services in rural areas (US$75 million), achieved
             through promotion of hygiene practices and improved sanitation at household level; construction and
             rehabilitation of institutional water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) facilities at primary schools and health
             care facilities; and Community-Wide Sanitation (DLI #3, 4, and 5).
       (iii) Results Area 3 - Strengthening of the capacity of sector institutions to sustain service delivery in rural areas
             (US$150 million), through better data management for reporting and decision-making; strengthening of
             technical and financial capacities of Community-based Water Supply Organizations (CBWSOs); and
             establishment and operationalization of the Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Authority (RUWASA) (DLI #6,
             7, and 8).
       (iv) Investment Project Financing (IPF) component (US$20 million) to provide technical support to address
             capacity gaps and institutional deficiencies.

3. The Program is in its third year of implementation, and both progress towards achieving the PDO and overall
   implementation progress are rated Satisfactory (S). For Results Area 1 (Sustainable access to improved water
   services in rural areas), more than 3.3 million people have gained access to improved water supply with adequate
   pressure and closer to their houses, surpassing the program end target of three million (Table 1). Results of the
   sustainably functioning public water points (WPs) and household connections as of June 30, 2021, are 26,363 and
   25,079, respectively. These numbers are lower than those recorded for the first year because for a local government
   authority (LGA) to be qualified for disbursement under DLI 2, it needs to sustain at least 75 percent of the recorded
   number of WPs at the baseline as well as new WPs verified in year one. While there were over 37,000 functioning
   water points that met all eligible criteria for functioning WPs, 35 out of 86 LGAs did not meet the 75 percent

       surpassed the end program target.

        gained access to improved sanitation facilities3, which surpassed the program end target of four million people.

           Cumulatively, there are now 215 villages that have achieved and sustained community-wide sanitation (CWS),4
           which is slightly below the yearly target (350). The reason for this is lack of coordination of targeting interventions
           between the Implementing Agencies (IAs) at community level, and this will be resolved in future years through
           better intervention planning. Results in school WASH (SWASH) and WASH in Health Care Facilities (HCFs) are also
           making steady progress. To date, 873 HCFs and 644 primary schools have been provided with improved sanitation
           and hand washing facilities through the Program (program end target is 1500 each).5

     5. Result Area 3 (Strengthen the capacity of sector institutions to sustain service delivery in rural areas) has
        continued to progress, albeit slower than anticipated in some DLIs. Cumulatively, 3,475 villages (program end
        target 3,667) were under the management of registered CBWSOs with improved operation and maintenance
        (O&M) capacity for water supply services. RUWASA was established in June 2019 with established and operational
        audit unit and quality assurance and quality control (QA&QC) mechanisms. While all IAs struggle to improve data
        collection and management – only 59 out of 86 districts managed to submit timely, accurate and complete sector
        monitoring and evaluation data, RUWASA was able to spearhead and upgraded a data management system, which
        is expected to result in improved reporting.

     6. The IPF component has been slow to implement with disbursement to date at 19 percent of the allocated
        financing. Eight contracts, representing approximately 24 percent of the IPF funding, have been signed and are
        under implementation. Eight more packages are under advanced preparation (representing approximately 33
        percent of the IPF funding). Many of the activities envisaged to be completed by Strategic Program Support
        Consultant for the Program, which was deemed critical for the successful implementation of the parent Program,
        have been completed through other project activities. Therefore, the support required by this consultancy has
        substantially changed and thus the ToRs are being re-written. As the previous consultancy was at the technical
        evaluation stage, those consultants that bid on the first tender will be requested to submit bids for the revised
        ToR. The ToR for the critical Program support to the National Sanitation Campaign is being reviewed by the World
        Bank. It is envisaged that the procurement for this activity will be completed by October 2022 and the consultants
        onboarded. A reassessment of the IPF component by IAs was recently completed to utilize the support on activities
        to further strengthen institutional capacities and priority studies that are critical to provide sustainable services.
        These include support to: i) creation of joint coordination mechanism and strategy among the IAs on selection and
        implementation of LGA and Village WASH projects (DLI 5); ii) data management and Rural Sector Data
        Management System (RSDMS) (DLI 7); iii) training of regional/ district level IAs to strengthen the accuracy,
        timeliness and completeness of data (DLI 7); iv) consultancy to support FSM service delivery strategy and Program
        pilots (DLI 8); v) support to design and construction of climate resilient water supply infrastructure (DLI 1 and 2);
        vi) improved asset management (RUWASA); vii) feasibility study for the design, installation and operation of pre-
        paid water meters in rural Tanzania under a PPP scheme (DLI 10); and viii) required capacity building to the 8 new
        regions, amongst others. Now that these activities have been agreed the procurement plan will be updated and
        process begin soonest to catch up with the pace of implementation. All activities proposed to be supported under
        the IPF will be completed by the closing date of the parent Program.

    8. Based on disbursement of the year 2 verified results the program will be 95 percent disbursed (total SDR 220.65
       million). A letter of request for Additional Financing was received on February 16, 2022. The letter proposed the
       expansion of the Program to all regions of the country (from current 17 to 8 more regions) and requested US$400
       million of additional IDA financing coupled with US$240 million of counterpart funding from the government. An
       Additional Financing for the program of US$150 million in IDA Financing is currently under preparation with a proposed
       Board Date in FY23. As mentioned above, the activities proposed under the IPF component of the Program is being re-
       evaluated to ensure the activities supported still align to the Program’s critical needs.

B   Rationale for Restructuring

    9. The proposed Level II restructuring was requested by GoT through an official letter dated April 5, 2022. The
       requested restructuring is based on the year 2 verified results whereby DLR 1 and 3 do not have enough funds
       available to fully disburse up to the results achieved. Therefore, it is proposed to reallocate funds from DLR 5 & 6
       to provide the required funding for DLR 1 & 3 up to the results achieved in year 2.

    10. This restructuring paper seeks approval of the reallocation of budget from DLR 5 & 6 to DLR 1 & 3. Once the
        restructuring has been completed a disbursement of SDR 69,211,727 (US$ 95 million equivalent) will be made
        against achievement of all the year 2 verified results (as per Table 2). The proposed additional financing of US$150
        million IDA funds along with US$100 million of government counterpart funds will then top up all DLRs and provide
        required funds for future years of the Program.

    11. This constitutes the first restructuring of the SRWS Program and there are currently no outstanding audit or
        financial reports.


II. DESCRIPTION OF PROPOSED CHANGES


    12. The following change to the Program is proposed:
    a) Reallocation of funds from DLR 6 to DLR 1 and DLR 5 to 3. The proposed restructuring will reallocate budget
       across the DLRs. Funds from DLR 6 which has not been fully utilized will be reallocated to DLR 1 and funds from
       DLR 5 to DLR 3. To facilitate the disbursement for year two, SDR 5,146,001 (approximately US$ 7,029,437) of IDA
       funds will be reallocated from DLI 6 to DLI 1 and SDR 110,780 (US$ 151,325) from DLI 5 to DLI 3. After reallocation,
       DLI 6 will have a balance of SDR 51,762,703 (US$ 70,728,568.7) of which SDR 43,939,584 (US$ 60,039,057) is
       required for the year 2 verified results, leaving SDR 7,823,119 (US$ 10,689,511.51) in this DLR after the year 2
       disbursement (Table 3). DLI 5 will have a balance of SDR 16,487,428 (US$ 22,521,827) of which SDR 2,010,744
       (US$ 2,746,676) is required for the year 2 verified results, leaving SDR 14,476,684 (US$ 19,775,150) in this DLR
       after the year 2 disbursement.
