                                               GLOSSARY

 Project impact          : Any consequence immediately related to the taking of a parcel of land
                           or to restrictions in the use of legally designated parks or protected areas.
                           People directly affected by land acquisition may lose their home,
                           farmland, property, business, or other means of livelihood. In other
                           words, they lose their ownership, occupancy, or use rights, because of
                           land acquisition or restriction of access.

 Associated Facilities   : Means facilities or activities that are not funded as part of the project
                           and, in the judgment of the Bank, are: (a) directly and significantly
                           related to the project; (b) carried out, or planned to be carried out,
                           contemporaneously with the project; and (c) necessary for the project to
                           be viable and would not have been constructed, expanded or conducted
                           if the project did not exist. For facilities or activities to be Associated
                           Facilities, they must meet all three criteria.

 Affected persons        : Individuals, organizations or businesses that are directly affected
 (APs)                     socially and economically by World Bank-funded projects caused by the
                           involuntary taking of land and other assets that results in (i) relocation
                           or loss of shelter; (ii) loss of assets or access to assets; or (iii) loss of
                           income sources or means of livelihood, whether or not the affected
                           persons must move to another location. In addition, affected person is
                           one for whom involuntary restriction of access to legally designated
                           parks and protected areas that result in adverse impacts on livelihoods
                           also.

 Cut-off-date            : The Borrower will establish a cut-off date for eligibility. Information
                           regarding the cut-off date will be well documented and will be
                           disseminated throughout the project area at regular intervals in written
                           and (as appropriate) nonwritten forms and in relevant local languages.
                           This will include posted warnings that persons settling in the project area
                           after the cutoff date may be subject to removal.
                            For the project, the cut-off date is established when the PPC issues the
                            Notification of Land Acquisition (NLA) for the project area (Article 85,
                            Land Law 2024), followed by the detailed measurement survey.
                            Affected persons and local communities will be informed of the cut-off
                            date for each Project’s component, and that anyone moving into the
                            Project areas after that date will not be entitled to compensation and
                            assistance under the Project. The project planning (based on the basic
                            design) has not been approved yet.

                           advantage of a project’s benefits. Such an individual/group is also more
                           likely to be excluded from/unable to participate fully in the mainstream
                           consultation process and as such may require specific measures and/or

 Forced eviction        : Is defined as the permanent or temporary removal against the will of

 Involuntary            : Project-related land acquisition or restrictions on land use may cause

 Land acquisition       : Refers to all methods of obtaining land for project purposes, which may

                          individuals or households; and (c) project impacts that result in land
                          being submerged or otherwise rendered unusable or inaccessible.
                          “Land” includes anything growing on or permanently affixed to land,

 Livelihood             : Refers to the full range of means that individuals, families, and

 Replacement cost       : Is defined as a method of valuation yielding compensation sufficient to
                          replace assets, plus necessary transaction costs associated with asset
                          replacement. Where functioning markets exist, replacement cost is the
                          market value as established through independent and competent real
                          estate valuation, plus transaction costs. Where functioning markets do
                          not exist, replacement cost may be determined through alternative
                          means, such as calculation of output value for land or productive assets,
                          or the undepreciated value of replacement material and labor for
                          construction of structures or other fixed assets, plus transaction costs. In
                          all instances where physical displacement results in loss of shelter,
                          replacement cost must at least be sufficient to enable purchase or
                          construction of housing that meets acceptable minimum community
                          standards of quality and safety. The valuation method for determining
                          replacement cost should be documented and included in relevant
                          resettlement planning documents. Transaction costs include
                          administrative charges, registration or title fees, reasonable moving
                          expenses, and any similar costs imposed on affected persons. To ensure
                          compensation at replacement cost, planned compensation rates may
                          require updating in project areas where inflation is high or the period of
                          time between calculation of compensation rates and delivery of
                          compensation is extensive.

 Restrictions on land   : Refers to limitations or prohibitions on the use of agricultural,
 use                      residential, commercial or other land that are directly introduced and put
                          into effect as part of the project. These may include restrictions on access
                          to legally designated parks and protected areas, restrictions on access to
                          other common property resources, restrictions on land use within utility
                          easements or safety zones.

 Security of Tenure     : Means that resettled individuals or communities are resettled to a site

                          eviction and where the tenure rights provided to them are socially and
                          culturally appropriate. In no event will resettled persons be provided
                          with tenure rights that are in effect weaker than the rights they had to the
                          land or assets from which they have been displaced.

 Stakeholders           : Any and all individuals, groups, organizations, and institutions

                          influence a Project.

1. INTRODUCTION

1.1. Project Overview
 −   Project name: Ca Mau sustainable fisheries development project (SFDP).
 −   Executive agency: Ca Mau PPC, Address: No.2 Hung Vuong, Tan Thanh Ward.
 −   Project owner: ODA and NGO Projects Management Unit, now Construction Project
     Management Unit (PPMU) of Ca Mau province; Address: No. 1, Ta An Khuong, Vinh Trach
     Ward.
 −   Project operating agency: Department of Agriculture and Environment (DAE) and local
     authorities.
 −   Financer and co-financer: World Bank (WB).
 −   Project duration: (i) Preparation: 2021 – 2025; and (ii) Implementation: 2026 – 2029.
1.2. Project objectives
1.2.1. Overall objective
1.    The project aims to develop a sustainable fisheries sector with deep international integration
and climate change adaptation, while protecting the environment through the construction and
upgrading of infrastructure, technological innovation, and the strengthening of management capacity
and production organization.
1.2.2. Specific objectives
2.   The project aims to the following specific objectives:
 −   To increase aquaculture productivity in 03 aquaculture areas, including Tan Duc commune
     (now Tan Thuan commune), Ta An Khuong Dong commune (now Ta An Khuong commune),
     Dam Doi district (before the merger); Dong Thoi commune (now Cai Nuoc commune), Cai
     Nuoc district (before the merger); Phu Tan commune, Phu My commune, Phu Tan district
     (before the merger) by investing in embankments combined with rural roads; improving
     technical production methods, disease control, and environmental management towards
     sustainability and adaptation to climate change; and establishing value chain linkages and
     branding strategies.
 −   To accommodate more boats while ensuring compliance with environmental protection
     regulations by improving the capacity of Rach Goc fishing port
 −   To combat illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing; to manage and protect marine
     protected areas; and to protect fishery resources in Ca Mau province by investing in equipment
     and enforcement mechanisms.
1.3. Project Components
3.   The project consists of 4 components which provide practical solutions to optimize and
improve resource management for fisheries exploitation and aquaculture, as follows:

2.3. Methodologies for Preparation of the RP
20.   The following methods were used to develop the RP:
−     Method of collection of documents and data:
      ▪   Collect land use map, cadastral map of the project area: At this stage, the sources of data were
          taken from (i) Land acquisition boundary of each investment item provided by the FS
          consultant; (ii) Cadastral map of the project area provided by the Land Management Office
          of project districts (before the merger); and (iii) Map extracts managed by a commune
          cadastral officers of project communes or leaders of the residential groups.
      ▪   Gather contracts for the transfer of land use rights and ownership of land-attached assets in
          the project area or its vicinity to determine replacement costs.
−     Map overlaying method: Overlay of land acquisition boundaries (provided by the FS
      consultant) and the latest version of cadastral maps of the project area to establish a list of
      affected households and the level of effect. This method ensures rapid and relatively accurate
      statistics on the numbers of PAHs, numbers of people/households living together in one house,
      area of affected land types and current land use, and households categorized as the vulnerable
      based on a digitalized database. On this basis, the project footprints, scope, and scale of land
      acquisition impacts will be determined. The cadastral maps updated annually also provide a
      legalized basis for land use and land use administration in the project area. On the basis of the
      list of affected households and the level of affected above, the Environmental and social (E&S)
      consultant team worked with leaders of each residential group/hamlet and the cadastral officers
      to determine the inventory of losses (IOL).
−     Quantitative method with a semi-structured questionnaire was used for the interview of affected
      people whose are selected randomly from a list of affected households (household surveys).
      The surveys collected information from selected households related to (i) demographic
      characteristics; (ii) occupation; (iii) living standards (income, expenditure, loans, health
      conditions, environmental sanitation, water supply, etc.); (iv) production conditions; (v) social
      networks; (vii) access to public services, (viii) Vulnerability of affected households; (ix) their
      ability to restore their livelihoods; (x) preferences of physical resettlement option; and (xi) their
      support for project implementation.
−     Qualitative methods used include (i) focus group discussions, (ii) key informant interviews, and
      (iii) field observation. The qualitative techniques aim to obtain an in-depth understanding of
      the potential impacts of the project on specific groups of affected households, such as
      households who need to physically relocate, and/or lose more than 20% of their productive land
      households whose current business are affected, etc.
−     Methods of information disclosure and public consultation were used, such as rapid preliminary
      assessment and consultation with stakeholders, on-site interviews and meetings with
      households, community meetings, group discussions, and socio-economic surveys.
−     Replacement Cost Survey (RCS) method was used to calculate compensation rates for all assets
      affected by the project to meet the requirements of ESS5. The replacement cost was obtained
      through a field survey conducted by an independent appraiser, including (i) Land price is
      determined at the time of resettlement report preparation; (ii) Prices of trees and crops are

may be impacted during the works. The impacted parties include the owners (electricity companies
and FPT, Viettel, and VNPT companies) and users of the infrastructure being relocated in the five
communes of Dong Thoi, Phu Tan, Phu My, Ta An Khuong Dong, and Tan Duc. The quality of
living conditions, the performance of work and study, and the level of annoyance experienced by the
residents will be adversely affected by the temporary disruption of the infrastructure.
3.2.2. Temporary Impacts
37. According to the project’s FS report, during the construction, the contractors primarily use
temporarily affected land areas, about 6,000m2 as material gathering yards, bypass roads, access
roads, etc. The contractors will provide compensation and support for the temporarily affected land,
taking into account the extent of damage and the duration of the construction. Should construction
activities impact any other structures or plants, the project policy will determine the appropriate
compensation. In addition, after completing construction activities, the construction unit must be
responsible for returning the site to its original state as before the project for the temporarily affected
land area.
38. During the construction stage, there may be unexpected consequences such as subsidence,
flooding, or exposure. When this impact happens, the compensation and support policies outlined in
this RP will be used to address it.

3.3. Affected people’s willingness to voluntarily donate land for the project implementation
39. The existing embankments combined with rural roads are narrow, mostly made of dirt or
cement, and have deteriorated over time. They frequently flood during high tides and are only suitable
for two-wheeled vehicles. This has significantly impacted both aquaculture activities and residents’
mobility.
40. During numerous meetings held between local authorities and residents in the five communes
within the project area since 2022, all households expressed a strong commitment to the principle of
“the State and the people working together” to construct the embankments in combination of rural
roads. This collective effort aims to improve transportation and production conditions, thereby
contributing to the local socio-economic development.
41. This commitment is clearly documented in the meeting minutes (see Appendix 7), where
residents affirmed that if the State invests in the construction of the embankment, they would
voluntarily donate the entire portion of land required for the project. In some hamlets, people even
expressed their willingness to cover the cost of backfilling materials. Furthermore, residents are ready
to harvest any remaining crops, dismantle fences, and rebuild house walls on their own without
requesting compensation or support from the government.
42. Moreover, there is currently a high demand for upgrading the embankment combined with
transportation infrastructure in the project area, while government funding remains limited. As a
result, in some locations, local residents have prepared the entire roadbed themselves, with the
government only investing in the concrete surface. Despite this, people are still willing and happy to
cooperate. Therefore, the approach of voluntarily donating land and harvesting remaining crops for
personal use is entirely reasonable and acceptable under the circumstances.
43. Therefore, for this project, the local authority advocates for voluntary land donation. However,
the land donation procedure will be carried out in accordance with the World Bank’s policies and
requirements, ensuring that no household donating land will be worse off or fall into poverty as a
result. Details of the voluntary land donation procedure are presented in Section 5.7 below.

4.2. The World Bank’s Land Acquisition, Restriction on Land Use and Involuntary
     Resettlement (ESS5)
46. The WB has launched ESF with 10 ESSs since October 2018, in which ESS5- Land acquisition,
restrictions on land use and involuntary resettlement recognizes that project-related land acquisition
and restrictions on land use can have adverse impacts on communities and persons. Project-related
land acquisition or restrictions on land use may cause physical displacement (relocation, loss of
residential land, or loss of shelter), economic displacement (loss of land, assets, or access to assets,
leading to loss of income sources or other means of livelihood), or both. The term “resettlement”
refers to these impacts. Resettlement is considered involuntary when affected persons or
communities do not have the right to refuse land acquisition or restrictions on land use that result in
displacement.
47. Experience and research indicate that physical and economic displacement, if unmitigated, may
give rise to severe economic, social and environmental risks: production systems may be dismantled;
people face impoverishment if their productive resources or other income sources are lost; people
may be relocated to environments where their productive skills are less applicable and the
competition for resources greater; community institutions and social networks may be weakened; kin
groups may be dispersed; and cultural identity, traditional authority, and the potential for mutual help

Ca Mau Sustainable Fisheries Development Project                                          27 | P a g e
RESETTLEMENT PLAN
may be diminished or lost. For these reasons, involuntary resettlement should be avoided. Where
involuntary resettlement is unavoidable, it will be minimized and appropriate measures to mitigate
adverse impacts on displaced persons (and on host communities receiving displaced persons) will be
carefully planned and implemented.
48.        As such, ESS5 aims for the following:
    (i)     To avoid involuntary resettlement or, when unavoidable, minimize involuntary resettlement
            by exploring project design alternatives,
    (ii)    To avoid forced eviction,
    (iii) To mitigate unavoidable adverse social and economic impacts from the land acquisition or
          restrictions on land use by (a) providing timely compensation for loss of assets at replacement
          cost and (b) assisting displaced persons in their efforts to improve, or at least restore, their
          livelihoods and living standards, in real terms, to pre-displacement levels or to levels
          prevailing prior to the beginning of project implementation, whichever is higher,
    (iv) To improve living conditions of poor or vulnerable persons who are physically displaced,
         through the provision of adequate housing, access to services and facilities, and security of
         tenure,
    (v)     To conceive and execute resettlement activities as sustainable development programs,
            providing sufficient investment resources to enable displaced persons to benefit directly from
            the project, as the nature of the project may warrant,
    (vi) To ensure that resettlement activities are planned and implemented with appropriate
         disclosure of information, meaningful consultation, and the informed participation of those
         affected.
49. In addition, the following measures for the best resettlement results will be required for the
project:
−          Consultation will be made with affected persons on proposed measures for compensation,
           support, and resettlement plan,
−          Affected persons will be provided with options for resettlement and livelihood restoration,
−          Compensation for the loss of assets other than land will be provided at full replacement costs,
−          New resettlement sites will offer living conditions at least equivalent to those previously
           enjoyed, or consistent with prevailing minimum codes or standards, whichever set of standards
           is higher,
−          PAHs will be provided with allowances, support during relocation process, and support in job
           training and job finding to restore their income,
−          Vulnerable groups will be identified and will be provided with additional support to assist them
           with their effort to restore their livelihoods.
50. Furthermore, methods used for the valuation of losses in WB funding projects are based on full
replacement costs. For the project, the losses consist of damages to land, structures, and other assets
and these replacement costs will be evaluated as follows:

land, residences, other structures, business, assets, or access to resources, specifically:

−    Persons whose residential land/houses will be affected (permanently or temporarily) by the
     Project;
−    Persons whose leased houses will be affected (permanently or temporarily) by the Project;
−    Persons whose businesses, agricultural activities, occupations, or places of work will be
     affected (permanently or temporarily) by the Project;
−    Persons whose crops/ trees (annual and perennial) will be affected in part or in total by the
     Project;
−    Persons whose other assets or access to those assets will be affected in part or in total by the
     Project; and
−    Persons whose livelihoods will be affected (permanently or temporarily) due to restriction of
     access to protected areas by the Project.
−    Communities or state agencies/companies:
      • Community assets and facilities that will be temporarily/permanently affected by the project,
      • Lands, houses, structures of governmental agencies, companies, etc. that will be
        temporarily/permanently affected by the project,
−    Disadvantaged/ vulnerable individuals and groups.

project. According to the initial socioeconomic surveys, the project's vulnerable groups will generally
include:
−    Female-headed households with dependents and economic difficulties (single, widow, disabled
     husband);

5.8. Forced Eviction
74. The Borrower will not resort to forced evictions of affected persons. “Forced eviction” is
defined as the permanent or temporary removal against the will of individuals, families, and/or
communities from the homes and/or land which they occupy without the provision of, and access to,
appropriate forms of legal and other protection, including all applicable procedures and principles in
the ESS5. The exercise of eminent domain, compulsory acquisition or similar powers by local
authorities will not be considered to be forced eviction providing it complies with the requirements
of national law and the provisions of the ESS5 and is conducted in a manner consistent with basic
principles of due process (including provision of adequate advance notice, meaningful opportunities
to lodge grievances and appeals, and avoidance of the use of unnecessary, disproportionate or
excessive force).
75. The prohibition against “forced eviction” does not prevent action by local authorities to remove
a person who continues to occupy land upon completion of the legal process of eminent domain or
compulsory acquisition. Eviction is not considered to be forced eviction if it conforms to national
law, including compliance with, and completion of, all relevant legal and administrative procedures,
including appeals processes; complies with all the relevant requirements of ESS5, and is carried out
in a way that respects basic principles of due process.
76.    Due process is understood to include the following types of procedural protections:
 (a)     an opportunity for genuine consultation with those affected.
 (b)     adequate and reasonable notice for all affected persons prior to the scheduled date of eviction.

 (c)   information on the proposed evictions, and, where applicable, on the alternative purpose for
       which the land or housing is to be used, to be made available in reasonable time to all those
       affected.
 (d)   especially where groups of people are involved, government officials or their representatives
       as well, if requested, impartial observers to be present during an eviction
 (e)   all persons carrying out the eviction are to be properly identified.

5.9. Valuation of Replacement Cost
79. “Replacement cost” is defined as a method of valuation yielding compensation sufficient to
replace assets, plus necessary transaction costs associated with asset replacement. Where functioning
markets exist, replacement cost is the market value as established through independent and competent
real estate valuation, plus transaction costs. Where functioning markets do not exist, replacement
cost may be determined through alternative means, such as calculation of output value for land or
productive assets, or the undepreciated value of replacement material and labor for construction of
structures or other fixed assets, plus transaction costs. In all instances where physical displacement
results in loss of shelter, replacement cost must at least be sufficient to enable purchase or
construction of housing that meets acceptable minimum community standards of quality and safety.
80. The valuation method for determining replacement cost should be documented and included in
relevant resettlement planning documents. Transaction costs include administrative charges,
registration or title fees, reasonable moving expenses, and any similar costs imposed on affected
persons. To ensure compensation at replacement cost, planned compensation rates may require
updating in project areas where inflation is high or the period of time between calculation of
compensation rates and delivery of compensation is extensive.

6.3.5. Compensation/support for tenants
94. Tenants who are residing in leased state houses: (i) will not be compensated for the land area
and houses owned by the state but fully compensated in cash at the replacement costs for the
improved, repaired and upgraded works; (ii) will be entitled to buy a land-plot in the Project
resettlement area to resettle with minimum area; (iii) will be entitled to rent or buy an apartment in
another publicly owned house (if available); and (iv) if the Project has no plot in its resettlement site
and/or apartment in another publicly owned house for them to rent/buy, PAHs will be supported in
cash with not less than 60% of the replacement cost of land and house to be self-resettled.
95. Tenants who are residing in leased private houses will be provided with a transportation
allowance for moving assets to their new residential areas. For the vulnerable tenants as defined in
this RP, in addition to transportation allowance, they will get additional assistance according to PPC
regulations.
6.3.6. Compensation for loss of standing crops, trees, and livestock
96. Regardless of the legal status of the land, cash compensation at full replacement cost will be
made to PAPs at the time of compensation for the crops, trees, livestock planted or raised on the land.
Where affected trees/animals can be relocated, compensation will be paid for the loss of the
trees/animals plus the transportation cost.

a result of land acquisition for the project:

 − Employees with at least a 12-month labor contract with a private or state enterprise or organization

99. Public infrastructure/buildings directly related to the livelihoods and utilization of
communities, such as irrigation canals, water supply, schools, health centers, transportation roads,
electricity poles, telecommunication, and cable lines (except structures with construction permits
requiring relocation when needed) that are damaged by the project will be restored or repaired as

communities will not pay for such costs.
6.3.9. Compensation for relocated graves
100. Compensation for grave removal will include the costs of excavation, relocation, reburial, and
other related costs that are relevant to customary requirements. The removal option and level of
compensation for PAHs will be determined in consultation with them and in accordance with
applicable provincial regulations.
101. When the land for the reburial of graves is available in a cemetery approved by the local
authority, PAHs will be informed of it so they can make their selection. If PAHs want to relocate the
graves on their own, PAHs will be provided cash amount of VND 25,000,000 per grave for the self-
relocation of the affected grave to other locations. Also, they will be compensated for the expense of
construction of the affected grave at the unit price of the province.
102. Futhermore, PAHs will receive additional support for cremation and the storage of ashes, with
the following specific support amounts: (i) Cremation: VND 2,000,000 per body; and (ii) Storage of
ashes at ash storage facilities: VND 2,000,000 per urn.
103. In the event that the owner of the affected graves couldn’t be identified, many public
announcements (e.g., on television and in newspapers) will be made in an effort to reach the owner.
If the owner could not demonstrate, the relocation of graves should be done by the project. The
current location and status of the graves (with photos), their relocation procedure, and their new
locations, as well as other relevant information, will be carefully documented and kept for record
purposes.

6.3.13.1. Allowance for vocational training and job changes

 − Support moving to new residential areas: PAPs requiring relocation inside or outside the province

      new house at a PPC-regulated rental rate, specifically: (i) for households with 4 members or
      fewer, the support is 5 million VND per month per household; (ii) for households with more
      than 4 members, each additional member will receive support of 500,000 VND per member
      per month.
    • PAPs whose main houses are partially acquired and who need to rebuild a new house on the
      remaining land will be supported through temporary resettlement for six (6) months at a PPC-
      regulated rental rate as mentioned above.
    • PAPs who choose self-relocation and provide written commitments to do so will be assisted in

      or housing in rural areas or within Ca Mau city where the land is acquired for the project (and
      are not eligible for resettlement), during the period of waiting for the construction of new
      housing, they will be supported with temporary housing rental fees. The rental support fee is

6.3.14. Requirement on escrow account
115. In certain cases, where repeated efforts to contact absentee owners have failed, where PAPs
have rejected compensation that has been offered to them in accordance with the approved plan, or
where competing claims to the ownership of lands or assets are subject to lengthy legal proceedings.
On an exceptional basis, with prior agreement of the Bank, and where the Borrower demonstrates
that all reasonable efforts to resolve such matters have been taken, the Borrower may deposit
compensation funds as required by the plan (plus a reasonable additional amount for contingencies)
into an interest-bearing escrow or other deposit account and proceed with the relevant project
activities. Compensation placed in escrow will be made available to eligible persons in a timely
manner as issues are resolved.

7. SOCIO-ECONOMIC BASELINE STUDIES

7.1.    Objectives and Methods of The Socio-Economic Survey (SES)
7.1.1. Objectives
117. A socio-economic survey (SES) conducted affected households will provide comprehensive
understanding of the background of the project area and current socio-economic A socio-economic
survey (SES) conducted on impacted homes would offer a comprehensive understanding of the
foundation of the project area and the present socio-economic circumstances of the affected
households. The survey offers valuable information for the development of this RP and suggests
livelihood restoration strategies for the affected households, particularly severely affected
households.
118. Particularly, the SES collected information related to: (i) Demographic characteristics; (ii)
Occupation; (iii) Living standards (income, expenditure, loans, health conditions, environmental
sanitation, water supply, etc.); (iv) social networks, indicative of their participation in local
specialized groups,); (v) production conditions; (vi) Traffic and access to public services; (vii)
Vulnerability of affected households; (viii) their ability to restore their livelihoods, (ix) their
preferences on physical resettlement options, and (x) their support for project implementation. SES
form is attached in Appendix 6.
7.1.2. Survey methods
119. Methods used: A mixed method approach was applied. This aims to enhance the reliability
and the validity of the study findings, see details as follows:
 −     Literature research method: Relevant documents have been collected, reviewed, studied, and
       analyzed/evaluated, including Project proposal, site map, map extracts and socio-economic
       reports, World Bank Environment and Social Framework (ESF), and guidance notes, Vietnam
       Land Law 2024 and related decrees and decisions of Ca Mau PPC. The World Bank's (ESF)
       environmental and social framework, especially the ESS5, has been studied and compared with
       the Land Law 2024 and related decrees to find out the differences and propose measures to
       apply to the project sentence.
 −     Quantitative techniques were used with the socioeconomic survey (SES). The survey collected
       information related to: (i) Demographic characteristics; (ii) Occupation; (iii) Living standards
       (income, expenditure, loans, health conditions, environmental sanitation, water supply, etc.);
       (iv) social networks, indicative of their participation in local specialized groups,); (v)
       production conditions; (vi) Traffic and access to public services; (vii) Vulnerability of affected
       households; (viii) their ability to restore their livelihoods, (ix) their preferences on physical
       resettlement options, and (x) their support for project implementation.
 −     Qualitative techniques used include (i) focus group discussions; (ii) key informant interview,
       and (iii) field observation. The techniques aim to obtain in-depth understanding of the potential
       impacts of the project on certain groups of affected households, such as (i) households requiring
       physical relocation, and/or losing more than 30% of their productive land); (ii) households with
       current business affected; and (iii) households with aquaculture land affected, etc.

economic opportunities. Khmer cultural traditions, such as Chol Chnam Thmay (New Year) and Ok

pressures. Government initiatives like Program 135 focus on ethnic minority development,

7.2.3. Economic Conditions in the project area
131. Crop Cultivation: The total area of annual and perennial crops in the project area slightly
increased compared to previous years, mainly due to the expansion of maize, vegetables, and fruit
trees. Rice-shrimp farming models were implemented across over 1,500 hectares, with an average
yield of 3.5 tons per hectare by 2024. The total area under vegetables and cash crops exceeded 3,000
hectares, with average productivity ranging from 3.5 to 4 tons per hectare in 2024. Some short-term
industrial crops saw a slight decrease in cultivated area.
132. Livestock: The total pig herd across the project area reached nearly 70,000 heads, while poultry
numbers exceeded 1.4 million by 2024. Several localities promoted the farming of salangane birds
and civets (Paradoxurus hermaphroditus), which generated significant income. Animal disease
prevention and control were effective, with notable reductions in infection and mortality rates
compared to previous years.

133. Aquaculture: by 2024, the total aquaculture area across the project area reached over 150,000
hectares, with key farming models including intensive, super-intensive, improved extensive, and
integrated mangrove-shrimp systems. Total aquaculture production in 2024 exceeded 220,000 tons,
with shrimp accounting for more than half. Eco-shrimp farming, blood cockle farming, crab farming,
and biological-product-based models have shown positive results and are being replicated. Two-stage
shrimp farming and integrated aquaculture under forest canopies were notably successful.
134. Capture Fisheries: Capture fisheries production totaled nearly 75,000 tons, showing a slight
increase from 2023. Fishing vessel monitoring and management were strengthened; 100% of eligible
vessels were equipped with Vessel Monitoring Systems (VMS), and no violations of foreign waters
were recorded in 2024.
135. Collective Economy: The collective economy continued to be prioritized and strengthened.
The project area had nearly 130 cooperatives and over 350 farmer groups, primarily operating in
agriculture and aquaculture by 2024. Several new cooperatives were established, while
underperforming ones were dissolved or restructured. Successful cooperative models have been
expanded, including two-stage shrimp farming, blood cockle and crab farming, dry fish processing,
eco-shrimp with biological inputs, and shrimp cracker production.
136. Science, Technology, and Training: More than 150 training sessions, conferences, and
technical consultations were held across the project area in 2024, with thousands of participants.
Effective livelihood models were evaluated and scaled up, contributing to sustainable development
and improved income for local communities.

7.3.8. Gender issues in production, livelihood development and access to resources
151. Gender in production development and decision making: Surveys during the E&S
document compilation reveal that the participation and role of women is still very limited in the
project area. The proportion of women engaged in production activities, including forestry, fishing,
and aquaculture, remains significantly low, whereas men constitute the predominant labor force in
aquaculture (84.1%) and agriculture (83.9%). Women primarily partake in livestock and poultry

Ca Mau Sustainable Fisheries Development Project                                          77 | P a g e
RESETTLEMENT PLAN
rearing at home (39.4%). Nonetheless, the rearing of chickens and ducks to fulfill the family's dietary
requirements is not the primary source of income.
152. In terms of community activities, women make up a disproportionately large portion of
participants. Among meetings in villages and communes, 43.2% of participants are women, while
44.2% are men. This is likely due, in part, to the fact that women represent their families at meetings
invited by local authorities, as men in the fisheries sector are either required to be away from home
frequently or spend very little time at home.
153. Women have a very minor role in choosing to invest or to provide the primary contribution to
the family's income; in fact, just 9.6% of the time do women do either of these things. Of the women
who have the authority to make decisions about their own investments, businesses, and output, just
11.5% are female-headed families, meaning that most of these women must figure out how to make
ends meet on their own.
154. In the aforementioned households, women primarily take care of the sick and aged, as well as
their children and other dependents, which limits their ability to contribute financially and
economically. In low-income professions including agriculture, husbandry, and small trade, women's
share of household income is larger than men's (63.9%). But women are still underrepresented in
high-paying fields including fishing, aquaculture, and hired labor.
155. Gender issues in accessing loans and credits: Survey results show that people, particularly
those in vulnerable groups, are unable to improve their lives due to a shortage of capital that can be
invested in consumption and production. When households do not have enough capital, they are less
able to withstand and recover from shocks such as crop failure caused by diseases and pollution
(shrimp, livestock, poultry), unpredictable weather (fisheries, hired labor), fluctuating prices (input
material prices rise, product prices fall), and health problems.
156. The demand for loans among people is substantial, with a rate of 81.2%. However, the actual
loans provided fulfill just 65.8% of this need. The primary source of loans remains predominantly
private loans at 41.6%, followed by policy banks at 35.3%. The loan is intended for family production
activities. The extensive reliance on private loans by individuals significantly impacts production
efficiency and revenue due to limited borrowing capacity, elevated interest rates, and particularly the
pressure of repayment timelines.
157. The State primarily extends loans via policy banks, with a focus on borrowers who are
predominantly from poor and near-poor demographics. Households report that bank loans remain
insufficient to satisfy their capital requirements. Meeting the need for loans to facilitate business and
production investments presents a significant problem, particularly given the adverse changes in
environmental conditions, such as epidemics and heightened risks of natural disasters.
158. Consequently, it is imperative to establish conditions that enable vulnerable groups to access
small-scale credit or savings funds for investment in livestock, agriculture, or minor commercial

proportion of women than men in the project region own physical capital sources including homes,
land, and other real estate. The traditional norm of the husband being the head of the household and

women own valuable assets in the family, while less than 20% of women own land and houses in
their own identities. The restriction on asset ownership impacts on the decision-making process in
production and the conversion of use purposes, as well as the mobilization of credit (through
collateral) for production. The issue of women's property ownership is relatively prevalent in general.
However, in areas with low agricultural capacity and vulnerability to climate change, the impact on
vulnerable people such as women is significantly larger. As a result, gender equality propaganda
operations should be pushed in order to modify the concept of establishing property and land
ownership in families, thereby improving women's legal rights and status in the family, society, and
the economy.
162. Gender issues in accessing to social capital: The results of the surveys reveal that 35.2% of
households do not join any cooperative or union groups, while only 64.8% feel the need to do so.
Many families join cooperatives and associations in the hopes of finding a job and securing a loan.
The habit of producing on a small and spontaneous scale places individuals at a significant risk in
the current socio-economic context, which is characterized by numerous challenges and difficulties.
The establishment of new farmers’ groups and cooperatives will provide individuals with the
opportunity to share production experiences and receive spiritual and material support when
confronted with business and production risks. The absence of a robust and effective cooperative
group model or the habit of participating still results in a lack of security and a desire to engage.

10. STANDARD OPERATING PROCEDURE FOR VOLUNTARY LAND
    DONATION (SOP)

172. Projects funded by the WB often require temporary or permanent land acquisition to implement
technical infrastructure works. Where land funds are available, priority should be given to acquiring
that land on a voluntary basis (the “willing buyer, willing seller” approach). However, if voluntary
land acquisition is not feasible, the land acquisition must be carried out following ESS5. ESS5 sets
out the conditions and procedures that must be followed when non-voluntary land acquisition results
in social and economic impacts as specified in ESS5. This policy must be adhered to in all cases of
voluntary land donation to address such issues and support project implementation while minimizing
potential risks.
173. The project has been prepared since 2020. At that time, the districts organized consultation
meetings on the issue of land donation and reported the consultation results to the PPMU. The PPMU
was informed that all affected households agreed to donate land for the project implementation. At
that time, there was no standardized procedure for land donation, so each district implemented it
differently, and the consultation minutes and record-keeping also varied. Up to now, documents
related to land donation have not been fully compiled (minutes attached in Appendix 7). Therefore,
the Project Owner will apply the following procedure to carry out land donation for the project.

176. The following principles should be applied when implementing voluntary land donation
activities in the project:

11. GRIEVANCE REDRESS MECHANISM (GRM) RELATED TO LAND
    ACQUISITION AND RESETTLEMENT OF THE PROJECT

11.1. Objectives of the GRM
182. The purpose of a Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) is to set forth procedures, methods,
and process that assist the resolution of a grievance that may arise during the Project implementation.
It offers process that is transparent, credible, and predictable to all parties that could result in
outcomes considered fair, effective, and lasting. More specifically, the GRM aims to:
−    Provide affected people with avenues for making a complaint or resolving any disputes that
     may arise during the course of Project implementation;
−    Ensure that appropriate and mutually acceptable redress actions are identified and implemented
     to the satisfaction of complainants; and
−    Avoid the need to resort to judicial proceedings.
11.2. Grievance redress mechanism (GRM)
183. Grievance redress mechanism of the Project will be conducted in compliance with the Law on
Complaints (Law No. 02/2011/QH13, dated 11 November 2011), and the Law on Denunciation (Law
No. 25/2018/QH14, dated 12 June 2018). The grievances will be redressed in three steps before
escalating to court.
184. PAPs will be entitled to complaints regarding their interests and responsibilities in land
acquisition for the project, including but not limited to land acquisition, resettlement, compensation
policy, entitlements, unit prices, and other entitlements associated with the IRP.
185. Therefore, a GRM is developed for land acquisition and resettlement of the project. It sets forth
procedures, methods, and processes that assist the resolution of a grievance that may arise during the
project implementation. In addition, it offers a process that is transparent, credible, and predictable
to all parties which could result in outcomes considered fair, effective, and lasting. Furthermore, it
should take into account the availability of legal remedies as well as community and traditional
dispute resolution systems.
186. The GRM will be applied to persons or groups that are directly or indirectly affected by the
project, as well as those that may have interests in the project and/or have the ability to influence its
outcome either positively or negatively.
187. The steps of the project GRM are as follows (Figure 4):
−    First Stage - Commune People’s Committee (CPC): An aggrieved PAP may file a written or
     verbal complaint with the one-door faculty of CPC's. The member of the faculty will be
     responsible for notifying the CPC leaders of the complaint for resolution. The Chairman of the
     CPC will meet with the aggrieved PAP and will have 30 days to resolve the complaint from the

Ca Mau Sustainable Fisheries Development Project                                           86 | P a g e
RESETTLEMENT PLAN
     date of receipt. The Secretariat of the CPC will be in charge of documenting and maintaining
     files on all complaints handled by the CPC.
−    The second level – PPC. If after 30 days the aggrieved PAP does not hear from the CPC, or if
     the PAP is not satisfied with the decision taken on his/her complaint, the PAP may bring the
     case, either in writing or verbally, to any member of the PPC or lodge an administrative case to
     the Regional People’s Court for solution. The PPC has 45 days within which to resolve the

12.2.2. Consultation during project implementation
196. After the project is approved, the Ca Mau PPMU will coordinate with local authorities to
disseminate project information, including objectives, locations, components, etc. and to conduct
consultations with PAHs continuously during the project implementation. Furthermore, the PPMU,
with the assistance of the consultant, will be responsible for the following tasks:
−    Providing information to relevant agencies at all levels through consultation workshops,
     including detailed information on the project policies and implementation procedures on land
     acquisition.
−    Organizing information dissemination and consultation with all PAPs.
−    Coordinating with the project resettlement committee to conduct the DMS, update the
     compensation rates in the updated PR, reconfirm the scale of land acquisition and impacts on
     properties based on the results of consultation with PAPs, and develop and complete the
     compensation plan for each PAH.
−    Finalizing the compensation plan with affected assets and compensation entitlements, which
     must be signed off by PAPs to demonstrate their agreement with the evaluated results and
     recording any questions of PAPs about the content of the compensation plan.
−    Distributing a questionnaire about resettlement options to all PAHs eligible for relocation (a)
     to inform them about resettlement options (a clear explanation of the consequences of choosing
     each option will be provided), and (b) to request that PAHs confirm their choice of resettlement
     option and preliminary confirmation of resettlement site location, and (c) to propose the PAHs
     to clarify services that they are using such as education/health/market and distance of access to
     those services to ensure the development of the future infrastructure service
−    Consulting PAPs about their desires for the rehabilitation plan. This will be applied to severely
     affected and vulnerable people. The resettlement committee will notify PAPs of the plan and
     their entitlement to receive technical assistance before requesting them to make clear their
     desire for rehabilitation support.
12.3. Information disclosure
197. In accordance with the WB’s ESS10, the Vietnamese version of the RP will be disclosed
locally, at the office of PPMU, project CPCs, and PPMU’s portal, and its English version will be
disclosed on the WB's external websites before and after it is approved.
198. Besides, a project leaflet will be prepared and distributed to the PAPs during the project
preparation and implementation stages to ensure that they are well aware of the project and its
benefits. It will include details of the compensation and assistance policies mentioned in this RP.

13. IMPLEMENTATION ORGANIZATION

199. The implementation of resettlement activities requires the involvement of agencies and
organizations at the national, provincial, and commune levels. Ca Mau PPC will take overall
responsibility for the implementation of the RP. Site Clearance, Compensation Committee (SCCC)
will be established at provincial level in compliance with provisions of Decree No. 88/2024/ND-CP.
The provisions and policies of the RP will form the legal basis for the implementation of
compensation and resettlement activities of the project. Agencies in charge of land acquisition and

Independent Environmental and Social Monitoring Consultant (IESMC).
200. Following is an overview of the key implementation responsibilities of each unit participating
in the RP implementation of the project:
13.1. Ca Mau PPC
201. Ca Mau PPC is the highest authority for promulgating regulations, resolving issues pertaining
to guidelines and policies, and providing instructions for project implementation. They also serve as
the link between the functional organizations involved in project implementation. The PPC will be
responsible for the following:
−    Approve the RP;
−    Promulgate regulations to solidify the mechanisms for compensation, assistance, and
     resettlement in accordance with the laws and practices of the province.
−    Inform or authorize the project CPCs to announce land acquisition to affected
     households/organizations;
−    Approve replacement costs results that were undertaken by an independently certified land
     price appraisal company;
−    Direct the coordination among the concerned agencies and the provincial departments to
     implement the compensation, assistance, and resettlement in accordance with the approved RP;
−    Provide a sufficiently and timely budget for the resettlement activities of the project;
−    Ensure that the objectives of the RP must be achieved after completion of the resettlement
     implementation;
−    Resolve all grievances or complaints at the provincial level.

implementation. The PPMU shall be in charge of the project's RP implementation, and its main tasks
will be as follows:

−    Selecting potential resettlement sites for the project before organizing household relocation to
     those sites,
−    Developing the detailed resettlement and site clearance plan and signing contracts with related
     entities to perform the plan,
−    Coordinating with related agencies to implement IRP,
−    Joining the CSRCs of the project and providing all necessary information regarding the project
     as well as the detailed resettlement and site clearance plan to assist the CSRCs in their
     evaluation process,
−    Instructing the implementation of land acquisition and resettlement policies of the project in
     compliance with the RP,

−    Taking an active role in coordinating with PCs at all levels and the LFDC to solve
     grievances/complaints in a timely manner and setting up a database of PAPs and compensation

13.3. SCCC and LFDC
203. The SCCC shall take responsibility for compensation and site clearance for the project,
including:
−    Planning and implementing all daily resettlement activities for the project,
−    Making inventories of the acquired land, completing compensation lists and tables, and
     preparing sum-up tables to be submitted to competent authorities for approval and paying
     compensation directly to each affected person after receiving compensation funds,
−    Preparing allocated land and relevant procedures for the resettlement of relocated HHs,
−    Appointing staff for conciliation and handling of complaints by PAPs on compensation policy
     and entitlements,
−    If necessary, establishing commune SCCC and directing them in implementing resettlement
     activities,
−    Paying special attention to the needs and aspirations of vulnerable groups (the elderly,
     female/single household heads, etc.),
−    Coordinating closely with the IMC in independently monitoring the RP implementation.
204. The LFDC shall take responsibility for the implementation of compensation and site clearance
for the project, including:
−    Developing plans of land acquisition implementation according to the annual land use plan of
     the city for compensation, support, and resettlement,
−    Conducting the DMS and preparing the compensation plan,
−    Coordinating with an independent land appraiser to carry out a replacement cost survey for land
     and assets on land to estimate compensation costs,
−    Coordinating with PPMU, and project CPCs to implement the compensation, support, and
     resettlement and site clearance activities,
−    Participating in the GRM and providing all required information for the province and project
     communes in resolving land compensation and resettlement complaints,
−    Providing dossiers, maps, information, and data on land and assets on land in accordance with
     legal provisions.

13.4. CPCs
205. CPCs shall be responsible for:
−    Establishing commune-level working groups and managing their operations; assigning
     commune staff to assist the LFDC and PPMU in preparing the project DMS, preparing
     documents on land acquisition, and implementing resettlement activities;
−    Verifying PAHs’ legal titles or certifying their land use or transfer to meet the requirements of

−    Assisting other units and agencies, including the PPMU, in posting or disseminating project

−    Assisting other units and agencies, including the PPMU, in carrying out demographic surveys,

13.5. PAHs
206. PAHs shall be responsible for:
−    Coordinating with survey teams in carefully checking and certifying affected land and other
     assets as well as their entitlements;
−    Engaging in all phases of the RP preparation and implementation and giving feedback to
     improve the quality of the RP and advise solutions for implementing the RP smoothly; and
−    Handing over acquired land on time after receiving full compensation and entitlements.
13.6. IMC
207. The PPMU will hire an IMC to conduct socio-economic surveys and monitor and evaluate the

problems identified during the monitoring process on a regular basis. The proposed term of reference

       APPENDIX 1: GUIDELINES FOR PREPARATION OF AN IRP DURING PROJECT
                               IMPLEMENTATION
1.    GENERAL GUIDELINES
The following are some general guidelines for preparation of the IRP for the project:
 −   The IRP is developed specifically for severely affected by the loss of productive assets, losing
     30% or more of total productive land and/or assets, or 30% or more of their total income sources.
 −   In order to effectively address the needs of beneficiaries and maximize available resources, the
     IRP should be prepared with the full participation of eligible PAHs, concerned people's
     committees at all levels, relevant agencies and community organizations (agricultural and rural
     development; invalid, labor and social affairs; trade and industry; planning and investment, farmer
     union, and others).
 −   The outcomes of the IRP are more sustainable if it is designed in a way that allows participating
     PAHs to contribute or invest their own resources (including labor) in the program.
 −   Particular attention should be given to ensuring the participation of affected women, vulnerable
     groups and ethnic minorities in planning and design of the IRP. For example, by carrying out
     separate interviews with women, vulnerable households during detailed needs assessment,
     implementing IRP activities that facilitate the participation of and are responsive to the needs of
     these groups.
 −   IRP should be designed with clear institutional set‐up, clear roles and responsibilities, a realistic
     time frame for implementation, and clear monitoring and reporting arrangements.
 −   During both preparation and implementation, a proper assessment of impacts and risks is essential
     to be able to come up with accurate cost estimates for the IRP.
 −   Regular monitoring and evaluation of the progress of IRP implementation is required to ensure
     that the objectives are being met and that issues/problems as well solutions to these can be
     identified and implemented as soon as possible.
2.    PROCESS OF PREPARING THE IRP
a) Information required
The IRP is developed on the basis of a “Detailed needs assessment” which involves several data‐
gathering and participative activities used to determine the following:
 −   The list of PAHs eligible for participation in the IRP based on project impacts and their pre‐project
     socioeconomic conditions.
 −   Compensation and assistance package that the PAHs would receive for the project impacts.
 −   PAHs’ income restoration needs and preferences in terms of types of activities, contributions, etc.
 −   Existing livelihood resources (on‐going programs as well as the capacity of involved personnel)
     in the local area that could potentially be tapped for the IRP.
 −   Potential risks to the success of IRP activities, and measures to avoid /mitigate these risks.
b) Carrying out the detailed needs assessment
Prior to conducting the detailed needs assessment, all stakeholders, including PAHs and concerned
government agencies, should be aware of the criteria used to identify who is eligible for participation
Ca Mau Sustainable Fisheries Development Project                                           113 | P a g e
RESETTLEMENT PLAN
in the IRP. For this project, these are severely affected by the loss of productive assets, losing 30%
or more of total productive land and/or assets, or 30% or more of their total income sources.
The detailed needs assessment is a participative process: the needs, preferences and capacities of
PAHs are determined by the Ca Mau PPMU and commune and hamlet officers together with the
eligible PAHs.
c)    Measures to gather information
 −   Socio‐economic survey (SES): The SES will be used to produce baseline data and assess socio‐
     economic conditions and potential risks and impacts on PAPs due to land acquisition and
     involuntary resettlement. The SES can be carried out in combination with the DMS. However, if
     it cannot be combined with these activities, the SES is carried out on a sample basis. The following
     information will be required:
     ▪ Composition of members of the PAH (age, gender, physical conditions, educational level,
       skills, etc.). This information will serve as indicators for the capacity of PAH members to
       undertake potential livelihood activities;
     ▪ Interest in participating in the IRP;
     ▪ Preferred income restoration activities of PAHs;
     ▪ Available material resources of the PAPs after land acquisition and resettlement (including the
       space available to conduct potential activities such as backyard livestock production).
 −   Qualitative data gathering activities such as the following can also be used to supplement

     ▪ Key informant interviews,

the RP.
