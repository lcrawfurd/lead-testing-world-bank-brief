                                   1. Introduction and Project Description

African states have recorded some positive achievements in addressing Water Sanitation and Hygiene
(WASH) issues and making progress towards the water sanitation and hygiene related Sustainable
Development Goals and Agenda 20631 targets. Despite these achievements, universal access to clean
water and adequate sanitation remains a challenge for many African citizens. According to the World
Health Organization (WHO) and United Nations International Children’s (UNICEF), approximately 400
million people in Sub-Saharan Africa lack access to basic drinking water services, and 779 million lack
basic sanitation facilities including about 208 million that still practice open defecation2. Efforts to
realize universal access for all face numerous challenges. Key among these are the large financial gaps to
meet the investment requirements, the capacity of service providers and the efficiency, quality and
reliability of services and limited participation of some actors.

The level of investment required to achieve the Africa Water Vision 2025 was estimated at USD 64
billion per year, or around USD 50 per capita per year3. Generally, it is difficult to attract investments in
water and sanitation infrastructure in Africa because of the high perceived risks in the sector.
Strengthening governance and institutional capacities is a critical step in addressing part of the challenge
to attract investments in the sector and cost effectively and efficiently deliver services. The Regional
Phase of the Accelerating Access to WASH in the Eastern and Southern Africa Region (P506439) aims to
improve governance, institutional capacities and resource mobilization for water and sanitation service
delivery in the Eastern and Southern Africa (AFE) region.

The Accelerating Access to WASH in the Eastern and Southern Africa Region Project comprises the
following components:
     1. Strengthening Platforms to Accelerate Access to WASH
     2. Advancing Reforms, Governance and Regulation
     3. Improving Service Delivery Performance
     4. Leveraging financing for climate-resilient WASH infrastructure

The project development objective is to improve governance, institutional capacities and resource
mobilization for climate-resilient water and sanitation service delivery in the AFE region. Specifically, the
project will be implemented in the following countries that participate in the MPA, namely Angola,
Burundi, Ethiopia, Madagascar, Malawi, Mozambique, Somalia, South Sudan, the Democratic Republic of
Congo, the Union of the Comoros Islands, Tanzania, and Zambia, and for selected activities other Eastern
and Southern African countries that may express interest to participate in the project.

                                     2. Objective/Description of SEP

The overall objective of this SEP is to define a program for stakeholder engagement, including public
information disclosure and consultation throughout the entire project cycle. The SEP outlines the ways in
which the project team will communicate with stakeholders and includes a mechanism by which people
can raise concerns, provide feedback, or make complaints about project activities or any activities related
to the project.

                                3. Stakeholder identification and analysis
3.1 Methodology
In order to meet best practice approaches, the project will apply the following principles for stakeholder
engagement:
• Openness and life-cycle approach: Public consultations for the project will be arranged during the whole
  life cycle, carried out in an open manner, free of external manipulation, interference, coercion, or
  intimidation.
• Informed participation and feedback: Information will be provided to and widely distributed among all
  stakeholders in an appropriate format; opportunities are provided for communicating stakeholder
  feedback, and for analyzing and addressing comments and concerns.
• Inclusiveness and sensitivity: Stakeholder identification is undertaken to support better communications
  and build effective relationships. The participation process for the projects is inclusive. All stakeholders
  at all times are encouraged to be involved in the consultation process. Equal access to information is
  provided to all stakeholders. Sensitivity to stakeholders’ needs is the key principle underlying the
  selection of engagement methods. Special attention is given to vulnerable groups that may be at risk of
  being left out of project benefits, particularly women, the elderly, persons with disabilities, displaced
  persons, and migrant workers and communities, and the cultural sensitivities of diverse ethnic groups.
• Flexibility: Due to cost and time constraints, face-to-face consultations will be complemented by various
  forms of internet or phone-based consultations.

4.2. Summary of project stakeholder needs and methods, tools, and techniques for stakeholder
engagement
Different engagement methods will be employed during the stakeholder engagement process and will
include formal meetings, virtual discussions, stakeholder consultations tailored to stakeholder needs,
administered and online surveys, interviews, site visits and formal responses to specific, stakeholder
requests, clarifications and complaints. The stakeholder engagement process must be inclusive and in
view of the detailed aforementioned stakeholder characterisation and description, the stakeholder
engagement for this project will be conducted throughout the lifecycle of the project in a flexible manner
that ensures inclusivity of all interested and affected parties. The following presents a strategy and
anticipated periods when project stakeholders will be engaged. Initial engagements were conducted as
part of the preparation missions that involved the participation of the Pan African Sanitation Association
(PASA), the African Ministers' Council on Water (AMCOW), Global Water Partnership – Southern
Africa/AIP Secretariat, African Water and Sanitation Association (AfWASA), the Eastern and Southern
African Water and Sanitation Regulators Association (ESAWAS), the Southern African Development
Community Ground Water Management Institute (SADC-GMI) and the Intergovernmental Agency for
Development (IGAD) that globally represent a broader share of stakeholder interests. Beyond the scope
of stakeholders already engaged, in addition to the governments and relevant agencies from the MPA
participating countries, AUDA-NEPAD during the project preparation phase will identify and consult
stakeholders representing the elderly, children, women, girls, people living with disabilities, youth, micro,
small and medium enterprises and the private sector among others. The stakeholders will be kept
informed on the progress of the project and any emerging developments of interest or concern through
various channels that will remain publicly accessible. Information that will be disclosed publicly will
include:
    i.   The objectives, scope and expected outcomes
   ii.   The timeframes of the overall project and specific main project activities and milestones
  iii.   Potential risks and both positive and negative anticipated impacts of the project on local
         economy, communities and WASH operators and utilities while identifying specific requisite
         mitigation measures
  iv.    The proposed stakeholder engagement process highlights how stakeholders will participate in the
         engagement process and activities
   v.    The stage of the project when the stakeholder engagement activities, targeted period of the
         engagement, theme of the consultation, method of consultation and engagement and the entity
         responsible for the engagement.

All participants representing the interests and concerns of all identified and emerging stakeholders will
be timely notified about the meeting. All meeting proceedings will be recorded, reported and summaries
shared according to the needs of the interested and affected parties.

The entities responsible for carrying out stakeholder engagement activities are the PIU on behalf of
AUDA-NEPAD with strong involvement of the African Union Commission, Global Water Partnership
Southern Africa (GWP SA), Pan-African Sanitation Association (PASA), African Water and Sanitation
Association (AfWASA), Eastern and Southern African Water Association (ESAWAS) and the African Council

engagement activities (see Table 5 in Annex I), monthly and quarterly reports and summaries, mission
reports and grievance case reports and tracking logs, stakeholder feedback and written communication.

                                        6. Grievance Mechanism
Grievances will be handled specifically according to the grievance redress procedures of the Agency. All
grievances pertaining to the general activities of AUDA-NEPAD are received by the office of the Chief
Executive Officer (CEO) of the Agency. Specific grievances pertaining to staff matters are received and
addressed to the Human Resources Division while procurement related complaints are addressed to the
office of the CEO and the Internal Audit Division.

The main objective of a GM is to assist to resolve complaints and grievances in a timely, effective, and
efficient manner that satisfies all parties involved. This GM as a cost-effective tool for receiving, recording
and addressing any complaints, concerns or reports of misconduct and prohibited practices during
implementation of the project. It will provide a mechanism for accurately registering grievances and their

                                                                                                               15
timely resolution and feedback to the concerned and/or affected party.in an efficient, transparent and
inclusive manner. The grievance redress process will be easily accessible through the aforesaid
stakeholder engagement channels to facilitate timely decisions on grievances. The PIU will implement this
grievance mechanism in an adaptive manner to practicality respond and address grievances according to
the needs of the interested and affected parties and the realisation during implementation of the project.
This grievance redress mechanism will be administered in line with the African Union Development
Agencies staff rules and regulations and the World Bank’s grievance redress policy and procedures where
not provided for in the African Union policies and procedures. The PIU shall make all interested and
affected parties aware of the grievance redress process through all available AUDA-NEPAD.

Endnotes

1 For the purposes of effective and tailored engagement, stakeholders of the proposed project(s) can be divided into the following

core categories:
• Affected Parties: Persons, groups, and other entities within the Project Area of Influence (PAI) that are directly influenced
    (actually or potentially) by the project and/or have been identified as most susceptible to change associated with the project,
    and who need to be closely engaged in identifying impacts and their significance, as well as in decision-making on mitigation
    and management measures.
• Other Interested Parties: Individuals/groups/entities that may not experience direct impacts from the Project but who
    consider or perceive their interests as being affected by the project and/or who could affect the project and the process of its
    implementation in some way.
• Vulnerable Groups: Persons who may be disproportionately impacted or further disadvantaged by the project(s) compared
    with any other groups due to their vulnerable status, and that may require special engagement efforts to ensure their equal
    representation in the consultation and decision-making process associated with the project.
• It is important to note that sometimes projects have different components with very different sets of stakeholders for each
    component. Those different stakeholders should be considered in preparing the SEP.
2 It is particularly important to understand whether project impacts may disproportionately fall on disadvantaged or vulnerable

individuals or groups, who often do not have a voice to express their concerns or understand the impacts of a project, and to
ensure that awareness raising and stakeholder engagement be adapted to take into account such groups’ or individuals’ particular
sensitivities, concerns, and cultural sensitivities and to ensure a full understanding of project activities and benefits. Engagement
with vulnerable groups and individuals often requires the application of specific measures and assistance aimed at the facilitation
of their participation in the project-related decision making so that their awareness of and input into the overall process are
commensurate with those of other stakeholders.
