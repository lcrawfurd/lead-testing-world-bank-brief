B. Introduction and Context

Country Context

 1.     Brazil's real GDP grew by 3.4 percent in 2024, driven by robust private consumption, bolstered by a tight labor
 market and fiscal stimulus through expanded social transfers, and by recovering investment. The poverty rate declined
 from 23.4 to 20.6 percent (US$8.30 per day, 2021 PPP) in 2024, supported by a strong labor market. GDP growth is
 projected to moderate to 2.4 percent in 2025, as private consumption growth slows amid reduced government
 transfers, and rising household indebtedness. Elevated interest rates and global uncertainty are expected to weigh on
 investment and exports. Inflation is projected to gradually decline to 4.2 percent by 2027. Ensuring fiscal sustainability
 remains a critical challenge. Despite efforts to raise revenues and contain spending, additional measures are needed to
 reinforce fiscal credibility, while budget rigidity and the large share of indexed expenditures continue to limit policy
 flexibility. Limited fiscal space will also constrain poverty reduction efforts, with the poverty rate expected to reach 19.9
 percent by 2027. Brazil’s medium-term growth outlook is positive but constrained, with recent structural reforms
 improving the business environment, the financial sector, and indirect taxation, and expected to raise potential output
 to about 2.5 percent. However, achieving faster, more inclusive, long-term growth will require further reforms to
 enhance competitiveness, reduce financial and market distortions, expand infrastructure investment, integrate into
 global value chains, and improve the quality of education. A more adverse external environment, prolonged monetary
 tightening, and slower-than-expected fiscal consolidation could undermine growth prospects and exacerbate debt
 vulnerabilities.

Sectoral and Institutional Context

 2.    Brazil’s persistent regional inequalities remain a core barrier to inclusive and sustainable development,
 particularly in the North, Northeast, and Center-West regions. The North, Northeast, and Center-West regions,
 together covering nearly 80 percent of the country’s territory and 43 percent of its population, remain structurally
 disadvantaged compared to the South and Southeast in income, productivity, and access to infrastructure and basic

  services. The North and Northeast are also at the lower end of the urbanization process, with around 77 percent of their
  populations living in urban areas compared to 87 percent nationally, limiting economies of scale in service provision.
  While Brazil has made significant progress in reducing poverty and expanding social services over the past two decades,
  structural disparities endure. Rural poverty affects 38.7 percent of the population (more than double1, the urban
  incidence) and in 2024 per capita household earnings in the Northeast were roughly half of those in the Southeast
  (R$1,319 versus R$2,499)2. These gaps, rooted in historically concentrated industrialization and uneven public
  investment, continue to hinder inclusive territorial development.
  3.     Environmental and climate-related vulnerabilities further exacerbate structural inequalities in Brazil’s lagging
  regions, but their persistence is linked to inadequate and inaccessible financing. Degradation, deforestation, and
  drought already affect millions of livelihoods: 101 million hectares of pastureland—nearly two-thirds of Brazil’s total—
  are degraded, with restoration costs estimated at R$116.1 billion3. The Northeast faces the highest drought exposure,
  where projections indicate that by 2035 up to 20 million people could be at risk of severe water shortages. In the
  Amazon, forest loss and fires are accelerating—fire outbreaks tripled in intensity over the past decade—while
  biodiversity-rich bioeconomy value chains remain stunted, generating only US$2.4 billion in GDP and 340,000 jobs, far
  below potential4.Although sustainable land use practices, climate-smart agriculture (CSA), and bioeconomy investments
  yield high social and environmental returns, they are systematically underfunded because they require high upfront
  costs, long payback periods, and involve risks unattractive to commercial lenders.
  4.     These dynamics reveal a persistent financing gap across what can be conceptualized as Brazil’s “green” 5 and
  “blue” investment agendas. On the green side, only 28 percent of farmers nationwide access credit through formal
  financial institutions, while 72 percent rely on informal sources, self-financing, or trade credit. Smallholders and family
  farmers—who dominate the North and Northeast—are particularly excluded: just 12 percent of farmers in these regions
  access formal credit, compared to 30 percent in the Southeast. Formal credit programs such as Plano Safra6
  disproportionately benefit larger, lower-risk farms in the South and Southeast, with 44 percent of credit volume
  concentrated in large farms and 77 percent in short-term loans, leaving little space for long-term, transformative
  investment.7 In the Legal Amazon, only 3 percent of Plano Safra credit supports low-carbon practices, meeting less than
  half of the investment needed for a full transition to CSA. Weak enforcement of the Forest Code dampens demand for
  restoration finance, although a carbon-driven restoration market is beginning to emerge. Mid-size bioeconomy firms
  and forest concessionaires also face credit barriers due to limited guarantees, insufficient risk-mitigation tools, and
  lenders’ weak understanding of their value chains and markets.
  5.     On the blue side, water and sanitation and irrigation services face equally critical financing constraints.
  Achieving the universal access targets set by the 2020 ‘Basic Water and Sanitation’ National Law8 requires scaling annual
  investment from US$3 billion to US$18 billion, yet current flows remain closer to US$5 billion9 even with growing private

  6.     These persistent financing gaps reflect classic market failures that discourage private investment in socially
  optimal sectors. High transaction costs, maturity mismatches, and risk–return asymmetries reduce the attractiveness
  of green and blue investments, especially in lagging regions. Smallholders and family farmers lack collateral, long-term
  uptake contracts, land titles, or credit histories to secure bank lending. Many bioeconomy and restoration activities have
  long gestation periods and uncertain revenue streams, which are poorly aligned with banks’ preference for short-term
  lending. Information asymmetries further deter lenders, who often lack technical expertise in bioeconomy, forestry or
  CSA value chains. In water and sanitation, weak capacity among local service providers, challenging technical and
  management solutions, and political risks associated with tariff adjustments discourage investment in underserved
  territories. These failures perpetuate chronic underinvestment in activities with high social, environmental, and climate
  resilience returns. Green and blue investments in lagging regions have typically higher costs due to lower availability of
  qualified labor and poor infrastructure.
  7.     Gender disparities exacerbate these financing and service gaps, with significant opportunity costs for inclusive
  growth. Women play a central role in Brazil’s rural economy—85 percent engage in household farming and contribute
  about 43 percent of family income11—yet remain structurally excluded from productive resources and leadership. Only
  19 percent of agricultural establishments are women-led (23.2 percent in the Northeast; 19.4 percent in the North), and
  just 10 percent of rural women access credit, compared to far higher rates for male farmers. Only 5 percent of rural
  women benefit from technical assistance, limiting their participation in CSA adoption and bioeconomy value chains.
  Service deficits also impose disproportionate time burdens: around 16 million rural women lack treated water, and 38
  percent live without sewage collection, with the worst conditions in the North (40.7 percent) and Northeast (19.8
  percent). These inequalities constrain women’s productivity, limit time for education or paid work, and restrict their
  leadership roles in producer groups and cooperatives.

  9.     While the Regional Development Funds are central to Brazil’s territorial development strategy, their
  transformative potential has not yet been fully realized. To date, impact has been tempered by limited capitalization,
  fragmented project pipelines, and insufficient alignment with climate and biodiversity goals. While recent years have
  seen annual allocations ranging from R$500 million to R$1.5 billion per fund, the 2025 combined budget is projected at
  R$2.1 billion—well below demand, with proposed projects exceeding available resources sixfold. Operational
  bottlenecks, including bureaucratic procedures, lengthy approval processes, and limited technical capacity at the local
  level, have at times slowed disbursements and reduced incentives for private participation.15 In parallel, the absence of
  systematic monitoring and evaluation mechanisms has constrained the ability to measure outcomes, ensure
  accountability, and adapt strategies over time. In response, the MIDR is advancing governance reforms, notably through
  Visão 360°, a centralized data platform that integrates project information with external indicators from the Brazilian
  Institute of Geography and Statistics (Instituto Brasileiro de Geografia e Estatistica – IBGE), ANA, and other federal
  systems. This initiative is expected to improve transparency, strengthen diagnostics, and enhance decision-making,
  enabling the funds to mobilize private investment more effectively. By modernizing their operations, increasing its
  management capacity, the funds will be better positioned to support transformative green and blue investments and
  advance Brazil’s climate and sustainable development goals.
  10. The proposed Project, to be implemented by MIDR, seeks to enhance private sector investments through
  Brazil’s Regional Development Funds (FDA, FDNE, and FDCO) as catalysts for sustainable and inclusive territorial
  development. The proposed operation will leverage the Regional Development Funds to expand access to long-term
  credit for green (forestry, bioeconomy, CSA) and blue (water, sanitation, irrigation) investments, while streamlining
  approval procedures and strengthening governance. By lowering private investment costs and harmonizing approval
  and monitoring procedures16, the project will catalyze high-impact investments and enhance private-capital mobilization
  in underserved territories.

 D. Concept Description

  15. The proposed Project is a Financial Intermediary (FI) loan designed to enhance the capacity of the Regional
  Development Funds to finance inclusive and climate-smart investments in the North, Northeast, and Center-West. By
  doing so, the Project also seeks to enhance the economic and climate resilience of targeted beneficiaries. The proposed
  operation will be a US$500 million IBRD loan, potentially leveraging at least US$100 million of private capital financing.
  It will be implemented over a 6-year period. The Project will leverage public and private financial resources to scale up
  investment in support of Brazil’s sustainable development targets, particularly across land-based sectors including
  water, agriculture, and forestry. It will enhance access to credit to promote income generation, job creation, climate
  resilience, and social inclusion.
  16. The structured financing mechanism will involve the US$500 million World Bank loan being on-lent by the
  Federative Republic of Brazil, through MIDR lead by the National Secretary for Funds and Financial Instruments
  (SNFI)23 as the Project Coordination Unit (PCU), to the three regional development funds—FDNE, FDA, and FDCO—
  which are managed by their respective regional superintendencies (SUDENE, SUDAM, and SUDECO) acting as Project
  Implementation Units (PIUs) responsible for sub-loan allocation. Financial products will then be channeled through
  PPDBs meeting defined selection criteria24, that will act as financial intermediaries in delivering sub-loans. The operation

     21. Under the proposed project, SUDENE, SUDECO, and SUDAM will assume the credit risk of eligible PPDBs which
     will, in turn, assume the credit risk of the private sector borrowers. The financing will comprise a mix of investment
     and working capital sub-loans, prioritizing investment, tailored to each target sector’s needs. Sub-loans will be
     denominated in local currency, with the Federative Republic of Brazil bearing foreign exchange risk. Interest rates will
     follow National Monetary Council Resolution 4,960 (2021), aligning with monetary conditions and regional development
     needs.26 All participating PPDBs, private companies, and subprojects must meet eligibility criteria defined during
     preparation and set out in the Project Operational Manual (POM), ensuring compliance with fiduciary, environmental,
     social, and anti-corruption safeguards and the loan’s objectives. During preparation, roadshow and market assessments
     will be carried out using Grant for Project Preparation (GFPP) funds to better inform project expected investments and
     validate the existing bankable pipeline identified by MIDR and PPDBs.

     develop management tools included in the POM, annual work plan, procurement plan, and disbursement projections,
     project monitoring and reporting.

2. Overall Risk and Explanation
23. The overall Project risk is identified as Substantial. The key risks to successfully achieving the PDO of the proposed
operation would be:
24. Macroeconomic risks are rated as Substantial, arising mainly from the growth and monetary policy outlook.
Inflationary pressures have motivated monetary policy tightening, which could dampen growth prospects, further raise
the cost of finance, and reduce private sector and farmers’ appetite to invest in financed activities. These risks will be
mitigated through the Federal Government assuming the exchange rate risk. Furthermore, during preparation meetings
and workshops with potential investors will be organized to help build a bankable pipeline. In addition, during
preparation options will be explored to try to: reduce sub loan processing time; customize sub loan financial terms by
themes; study additional guarantee options; and improve PPBD’s administration fees.
25. Implementation Arrangements are rated as Substantial. The assessment, management and monitoring of the
sub-loans will be done through the three Superintendencies increasing the difficulty for MIDR to coordinate and compile
all generated information. Furthermore, MIDR is developing other similar projects (though not on the same credit lines)
with 3 other donors (IADB, New Development Bank - NDB and French Agency for Development – AFD), which will lead
to a total increase of around 1.7 USD billion in the three Development Funds, increasing MIDR workload. To mitigate
this risk, MIDR is fostering joint approaches between donors to align requirements (like ESF, and Financial Management).
The GFPP attributed to MIDR will also help enhance its capacities, through training and will support the improvement
of the Ministry’s tool to monitor the implementation of subloans – Visão 360, to facilitate and centralize the information
and data the project will generate. IADB is committed to providing Technical Assistance to MIDR and its
Superintendencies during implementation to strengthen their capacities to analyze and monitor sub-projects.
26. Fiduciary risks are rated as Substantial. At this stage, the Financial Management Risk is assessed as Substantial
mainly because (a) complex coordination among multiple financiers, with different eligibility criteria and timelines, and
implementation units may cause compliance issues, risk oversight and lack of transparency; (b) fragmented
responsibilities could hinder alignment of fiduciary and monitoring standards; (c) dual national and international
compliance adds complexity and may cause delays; (d) varying institutional capacities may lead to inconsistent controls
and a slowdown in sub-loan processing; and (e) sub-borrowers’ uneven capacity could increase costs, reduce sub-loan
uptake, hampering PDO achievement.
27. The proposed mitigation measures would be: (a) establish clear governance, roles, and procedures in a joint
Operations Manual; (b) harmonize requirements and eligibility criteria in a compliance matrix and ensure transparent
beneficiary selection; (c) appoint focal points and hold regular coordination meetings with standardized reporting; (e)
the PIUs need to provide training and robust monitoring for sub-loan borrowers, with regular financial reporting; (f) use
tiered support for sub-borrowers based on risk, leveraging technical assistance and grants; (vii) implement integration
protocols and digital systems; (g) conduct regular audits and compliance reviews; and (h) adapt financial systems for
dedicated accounts by funding source and expenditure.
28. Based on the information available at this stage, the Environmental and Social risks are considered Substantial,
from water and sanitation works, irrigation systems, and forest concessions. Key environmental concerns include waste,
water pollution, air emissions, noise, soil erosion, and habitat disturbance; irrigation can lead to water depletion,
salinization, and runoff, especially near dams; and bioeconomy and climate-smart agriculture may drive deforestation
and soil degradation. Social risks include land-use change, potential physical and economic displacement, impacts on
local, traditional, or Indigenous peoples, as well as social tension, labor influx, and SEA/SH risks. Most adverse effects
are expected to be localized, temporary, and reversible, and can be mitigated by applying Good International Industry
Practice, complying with Brazilian regulations, and leveraging the PPDBs’ robust ESMSs as tier-2 FIs.
