 B. Introduction and Context

 Country Context
1. Manaus is a strategic municipality for Brazil’s economic, environmental, and climate agendas. As the capital of
   Amazonas and Brazil’s second-largest urban area, it hosts 52.4 percent of the state’s population (2.28 million in 2024)
   and 78.5 percent of the state’s GDP (US$19.2 billion in 2021; US$43.4 billion in PPP). Its industrial base, logistical
   capacity, significant environmental research, and geographic location position the municipality at the heart of state,
   regional Amazon, and national efforts for low-emissions development, climate adaptation and mitigation, and forest
   conservation, in a state with over 96 percent native forest cover. Coordination among federal, state, and local policies
   is crucial for Manaus to reach its full potential. Meanwhile, the local economy remains heavily reliant on the Manaus
   Free Trade Zone (ZFM), established in 1967 and responsible for more than 500,000 direct and indirect jobs, with
   federal tax expenditures of R$29.9 billion in 2024 (roughly US$6.0 billion), focused mainly on electronics, motorcycles,
   and chemicals (more than 90 percent of manufacturing value added). Although the ZFM has helped reduce pressure
   on deforestation by focusing economic activity within the urban perimeter, its competitiveness faces increasing
   challenges from fiscal constraints, technological changes, and global market shifts. This highlights the need to shift
   toward higher-value, lower-carbon industries and the bioeconomy, better aligned with the region’s comparative
   advantages and aimed at diversifying the local economy.
2. Significant potential exists to grow green and bio-based activities. Studies suggest that the bioeconomy’s output
   could triple by 2050, creating over 800,000 jobs. Combining Amazon’s biodiversity with Manaus’s established
   industrial and commercial presence, as well as its scientific capacity and research institutions, can support economic
   diversification, help preserve forest resources, and strengthen the participation of Indigenous and riverine
   communities in sustainable value chains, reducing reliance on ZFM-related employment incentives. However,
   significant urban infrastructure gaps hinder inclusive growth and private-sector development: despite electricity
   access above 99 percent, shortages remain in water supply (76 percent in 2022), sewerage collection (62 percent),
   drainage, solid waste management (with annual waste exceeding 1 million tons and limited landfill and recycling
   capacity), mobility, and digital connectivity. Rapid urbanization has increased congestion, expanded informal
   settlements, and limited the development of modern service clusters. These issues are worsened by high climate
   vulnerability: average temperatures increased by 1.3°C from 1961–2020, with projections exceeding 4°C by 2100;
   extreme droughts and floods have become more frequent—the Rio Negro reached a record low in 2021, and floods
   affected over 50,000 residents in 2024—causing economic losses (around US$350 million for the ZFM in 2024), while
   wildfires have peaked since 2010, severely impacting air quality. Low-income households in informal settlements—
   often located in flood-prone or geophysically exposed areas—are especially affected due to limited access to climate-
   resilient infrastructure and basic services.
3. Brazil’s macroeconomic policy framework is considered adequate for the proposed operation, with broadly robust
   economic activity supported by resilient investment, strong private consumption, and a healthy labor market. Real
   GDP growth is projected to remain steady, converging toward its potential, while the government continues to
   advance growth-oriented structural reforms and maintain fiscal consolidation under the 2023 fiscal framework. The
   external position is strong, with low foreign currency debt and ample reserves, and the financial system is resilient
   under the oversight of an independent central bank. However, key risks remain, particularly from slower fiscal
   consolidation and adverse external conditions, which could undermine fiscal credibility, debt sustainability, and
   growth prospects. Achieving fiscal targets will require further revenue mobilization and expenditure controls, while
   external shocks could dampen investment and exports. Upside risks include continued labor market strength and
   effective fiscal stimulus, which could support household consumption and economic resilience, especially if structural
   reforms are implemented swiftly.

 Relationship to CPF
4. The prior actions supported by this DPF are closely aligned with both the Municipality of Manaus’s Government
   Program (PPA 2022-2025 and proposals for 2025-2028) and the World Bank Group Country Partnership Framework
   (CPF) for Brazil (FY24-FY28). The CPF prioritizes support to subnational entities and the Northern Region, where
   Manaus is located, with a focus on poverty reduction and climate action. The DPF’s design reflects the municipality’s
   strategic objectives of fiscal sustainability, economic growth, and environmental leadership, as outlined in the PPA’s
   pillars of Efficient Management, Economic Growth and Environment. Key reforms, such as strengthening fiscal
   management, modernizing public administration, promoting private sector investment through PPPs, and advancing
   green competitiveness, mirror the CPF’s high-level outcomes of increased productivity, job creation, and a greener,
   more resilient economy. The DPF also supports Manaus’s efforts to pioneer sustainable urban development, including
   green building incentives, climate adaptation policies, and circular economy initiatives, which are directly linked to
   the CPF’s objective 3.3 (Promote green and resilient cities and communities) as well as the CPF’s emphasis on climate
   resilience and improved natural resource management in the Amazon. This strong alignment ensures that the DPF not
   only promotes Manaus development priorities but also aides the World Bank Group’s strategic objectives in Brazil.
 C. Proposed Development Objective(s)
5. The Development Objective of this DPF is to promote Manaus' fiscal, economic and environmental sustainability.
   The proposed DPF supports reforms to strengthen fiscal sustainability through the adoption of (i) fiscal standards that
   limit current expenditure growth, (ii) comprehensive pension reform, and (iii) modernization of property taxation. It
   also aims to create an environment conducive to private investment by (iv) establishing a municipal investment
   partnership framework, (v) a permanent governance body dedicated to modernizing and optimizing municipal
   business licensing processes. Moreover, the DPF advances environmental sustainability and climate adaptation by (vi)
   enacting policies to promote adaptation to extreme climatic events, (vii) implementing reverse logistics programs for
   solid waste reduction and enhanced management, (viii) promoting urban and peri-urban agriculture, and (ix)
   incentivizing green building practices through urban planning measures. The set of policies complement and align with
   State policies promoted under the two previous DPFs implemented by the State of Amazonas.
  Key Results
6. The operation is expected to deliver measurable gains across its two pillars. Under Pillar 1 (fiscal sustainability and
    private sector development), results include a reduction in the current expense/revenue ratio from 89.9 percent
    (December 2024 baseline) to 87.7 percent, a decrease in annual municipal budget transfers to cover pension deficits
    from 1.5 percent of Net Current Revenue to below 0.8 percent, and improvements in the business environment
    reflected in a higher number of newly registered limited liability corporations and increased IPTU revenue (with
    baselines and targets to be finalized). Under Pillar 2 (environmental sustainability and climate resilience), outcomes
    encompass climate adaptation benefits reaching at least six high‑risk floodplain or hillside communities by December
    2027, expanded circular economy performance via a higher share of recyclable waste collected through reverse
    logistics programs, strengthened urban food systems evidenced by increased local food production under the
    municipal urban agriculture program, and greater uptake of sustainable construction practices through additional
    certifications under the Municipal Green Buildings Incentive Program.
 D. Concept Description
7. The proposed operation has two pillars that together support the Municipality of Manaus’s reform program to
    promote fiscal, economic, and environmental sustainability. Pillar 1 focuses on strengthening fiscal management and
    economic competitiveness by supporting policy actions to improve public financial management (PFM), enhance
    spending efficiency, and foster a more robust and diversified local economy. Pillar 2 focuses on strengthening
    environmental management and sustainability by supporting reforms to improve urban environmental quality,
    reinforce natural resource management, and promote a green and resilient development path for Manaus. Under
    Pillar 1, the operation anticipates prior actions that improve PFM practices and fiscal transparency (such as measures

   activity, and advances climate and environmental outcomes for Manaus.
 E. Poverty and Social Impacts, and Environmental, Forests, and Other Natural Resource Aspects

 Poverty and Social Impacts
9. Overall this operation is expected to have positive poverty and social impacts in the city of Manaus, where 55.8%
   of the population lives in slums. Under Pillar 1 - Fiscal Sustainability and Private Sector Development, Prior Actions 3
   and 4 are expected to have a positive impact on poverty and society through the creation of jobs by private enterprises
   – particularly in the infrastructure sectors. Prior Action 5 may also have positive distributive impacts insofar as the
   reformed Property Tax will keep exemptions that favor low-income families and people with disabilities, while
   charging more properly higher-income groups. The expected decrease of the ratio of current expenditures to current
   revenues resulting from Prior Action 1 may have an indirect positive impact on poverty if the municipality’s increased
   access to financing is invested in an agenda for inclusive development. Prior Action 2 is more controversial and faces
   opposition from public servants (and representative unions) who may lose some rights and benefits in the transition
   to the new pension system – particularly affecting the widows of deceased servants. The proposed changes are
   considered needed to ensure the financial and actuarial balance of the pension system in face of the increased number
   of retirees and pensioners compared to active civil servants.
10. The Prior Actions included under Pillar 2 - Environmental Sustainability and Green Growth are expected to bring a
    variety of positive poverty and social impacts through distinct transmission channels. Prior Action 6 is expected to
    benefit the most vulnerable social minorities – including: a) riverside communities, indigenous people, and family
    farmers, who will receive special treatment, respecting their practices and culture, with legal security; b) economically
    vulnerable environmental offenders may have the opportunity to convert fines into environmental services, such as
    reforestation; and, particularly, c) more than 112,000 city-dwellers who live in 438 areas classified as high and very
    high risk for disasters such as floods, erosion and landslides and are expected to benefit for better conditions to cope
    with natural and climate-related hazards. Meanwhile, Prior Action 7 is expected to positively affect the income and
    working conditions of waste pickers and their associations and organizations. Contributing to restore degraded urban
    ecosystems through the agroecological use of idle urban areas, Prior Action 8 is expected to create jobs, increase
    income and improve health conditions for poor families. Finally, Prior Action 9 may benefit poor people insofar as a)
    governmental tax incentives render green construction as affordable as traditional construction and expand the civil
    construction market generating jobs and b) the use of green buildings for social housing fulfills the expectation of
    reducing routine family expenditures with water and energy. These potential positive impacts will be checked
    throughout project preparation.

11. The Prior Actions supported by this operation are anticipated to generate significant positive impacts on the
    environment, forests, and other natural resources. The adoption of a Municipal Climate Adaptation Plan (PA6) is
    expected to have significant positive environmental effects by mandating climate resilient measures in vulnerable
    urban areas, protecting ecosystems, and reducing disaster risks. Likewise, the introduction of reverse logistics and
    circular economy mechanisms (PA7) is expected to yield significant positive effects by reducing solid waste, lowering
    greenhouse gas emissions, and promoting sustainable resource use, which are key factors for improving urban
    environmental quality and conserving natural resources. Lastly, the establishment of a municipal Public Private
    Partnership (PPP) framework (PA3) is also expected to contribute positively to the environment by promoting waste
    management operations within infrastructure investment policies. While these Prior Actions are designed to promote
    environmental sustainability, potential adverse effects may arise, particularly from the expansion of PPPs in sectors
    such as roads if implementation is not adequately managed. However, the State of Amazonas has a robust system in
    place to mitigate such risks, including Environmental Licensing Law Nº 3.785. The state's environmental framework,
    with IPAAM overseeing the licensing and permitting process for projects and investments, and the Secretaria de
    Estado do Meio Ambiente (SEMA) ensuring enforcement of these regulations, provides strong safeguards. The
    environmental impacts of these Prior Actions will be further assessed during project preparation.
