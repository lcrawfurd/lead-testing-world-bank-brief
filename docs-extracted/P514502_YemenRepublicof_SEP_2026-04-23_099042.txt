1. Introduction/Project Description

Yemen ranks as the lowest in the Middle East and North Africa (MNA) region, positioned 184 out
of 193 on the Human Development Index (HDI) in 2025, and sixth out of 179 countries on the
Fragile States Index. Food insecurity is widespread, with 63 percent of households reporting
inadequate food consumption in early 2025.1 Nineteen million people are facing food insecurity
and need humanitarian assistance, and 17.4 million people need water sanitation and hygiene
(WASH) support.2 The combination of these factors disrupts the delivery of essential health,
nutrition, and WASH services. The deteriorating livelihoods, and shrinking humanitarian funding,
which has forced significant reductions in essential nutrition and health services nationwide.
Overall, Yemen’s country and sector context illustrates a deeply fragile environment in urgent
need of sustained humanitarian and development assistance to prevent further deterioration of
health, nutrition, and social well-being outcomes.
The Yemen Health Nutrition and Wash Project (YHNWP) aims to deliver integrated health,
nutrition and WASH services to targeted areas of Yemen while building local institutional
capacity.

The Yemen Health Nutrition and Wash Project (YHNWP)comprises the following components to
be implemented in 465 facilities across 15 governorates accounting for 89% of stunted children
in Yemen, namely Abyan, Ad Dali’, Aden, Al Hodeidah, Amran, Dhamar, Hadramawt, Hajjah, Ibb,
Lahj, Marib, Sana’a, Sana’a City, Shabwah, Ta’iz:

COMPONENT 1: Delivery of integrated health, nutrition, and WASH services
        − SUB-COMPONENT 1.1: Prioritizing and optimizing the delivery of an integrated
           minimum service package (MSP) across the continuum of care
        − SUB-COMPONENT 1.2: Rehabilitating Infrastructure and WASH systems to
           improve PHC functionality
        − SUB-COMPONENT 1.3: Multi Sectoral Convergence through Geographic
           Accelerator Program
 • COMPONENT 2: Support to prevent and respond to communicable disease outbreaks
   (implemented by WHO)

    •    COMPONENT 3: Systems building for sustainable delivery of health and nutrition services

                 −    SUB-COMPONENT 3.2: Strengthen national health workforce to deliver high

           2. Objective/Description of SEP
            The overall objective of this SEP is to define a program for stakeholder engagement, including

            the project and any activities related to the project. The SEP specifically emphasizes methods to
            engage groups considered most vulnerable and that are at risk of being left out of project
            benefits.

       7.1. Summary of how SEP will be monitored and reported upon
The SEP will be monitored based on both qualitative reporting (based on progress reports) and
quantitative reporting linked to results indicators on stakeholder engagement and grievance
performance.

SEP reporting will include the following:
(i)    Progress reporting on the ESS10-Stakeholder Engagement commitments under the
       Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP)
(ii)   Cumulative qualitative reporting on the feedback received during SEP activities, in
       particular (a) issues that have been raised that can be addressed through changes in
       project scope and design, and reflected in the basic documentation such as the Project
       Appraisal Document, relevant Environmental and Social instruments including SEA/SH
       action plan, if needed;; (b) issues that have been raised and can be addressed during
       project implementation; (c); issues that have been raised that are beyond the scope of
       the project and are better addressed through alternative projects, programs or
       initiatives; and (d) issues that cannot be addressed by the project due to technical,
       jurisdictional or excessive cost-associated reasons. Minutes of meetings summarizing
       the views of the attendees can also be annexed to the monitoring reports.
(iii)
(iv)   Quantitative reporting based on the indicators included in the SEP. An illustrative set of

1. Introduction/Project Description

Yemen continues to face one of the world’s most acute and protracted fragility, conflict, and
humanitarian crises, driven by years of conflict, economic collapse, and institutional
fragmentation. Poverty has risen sharply, while widespread food insecurity and limited access to
essential services continue to undermine human capital development. The health, nutrition, and
WASH sectors are particularly affected by disrupted service delivery, weakened institutional
capacity, and significant gaps in access to quality care. In this context, the Yemen Health, Nutrition
and WASH Project is designed to address critical service delivery gaps through an integrated and
systems-strengthening approach.

The Yemen Health, Nutrition and WASH Project (HNWP – P514502) aims to deliver integrated
health, nutrition and WASH services in targeted areas of Yemen; strengthen disease surveillance
and outbreak response; and build institutional capacity.

The Yemen Health, Nutrition and WASH Project (HNWP – P514502) comprises the following
components:

Component 1: Delivery of integrated health, nutrition, and WASH services
Supporting the provision of essential health, nutrition, and WASH services through selected health
facilities, outreach services, and community-level interventions.

Component 2: Support to prevent and respond to communicable disease outbreaks

Strengthening disease surveillance systems, enhancing laboratory capacity, and supporting rapid
detection and response to public health threats.

Component 3: Systems building for sustainable delivery of health and nutrition services
Strengthening institutional capacity, health systems, and service delivery mechanisms at national
and subnational levels.

Component 4: Project management
Supporting overall coordination, implementation, monitoring, evaluation, and reporting of
project activities.

The Project will be implemented in fifteen priority governorates, selected based on disease
burden, malnutrition prevalence, and service delivery gaps, while maintaining national-level
support for disease surveillance and outbreak response systems.

The Yemen Health Nutrition and Wash Project (P514502) is being prepared under the World
Bank’s Environment and Social Framework (ESF).

       4.3. Proposed strategy to incorporate the views of vulnerable groups
       The project will seek the views of vulnerable or disadvantaged groups identified through
       the following methods:

   •   Conducting quarterly meetings with Governorate Health Offices (GHOs), District Health
       Offices (DHOs), and health facility managers during site visits allows for direct
       communication and immediate feedback from the ground level. This facilitates
       understanding of context‑specific needs and concerns, including those of vulnerable and
       disadvantaged groups in different regions.
   •   Conducting beneficiary surveys collected by the Third‑Party Monitor (TPM) enables the
       collection of structured feedback, including perspectives from vulnerable groups, on the
       quality and accessibility of hospital services. This information supports assessment of the
       effectiveness of WHO‑supported interventions.
   •   Maintaining a Grievance Mechanism (GM) allows beneficiaries to raise feedback,
       concerns, or complaints related to the Project. The GM provides accessible and
       confidential channels, including for vulnerable groups, and ensures that concerns are
       addressed in a timely manner.
   •   Distribution of posters in supported health facilities and dissemination of project updates
       through social media platforms enable outreach to a wider audience, including women
       and marginalized communities, and provide opportunities for feedback.
   •   Training and capacity‑building activities are delivered in languages understood by
       participants and include feedback questionnaires and awareness activities. The
       appropriate language and format are determined during preparation of project‑related
       instruments as part of the social baseline.

The following measures will be taken in order to remove obstacles to full and enabling
participation / access to information:


   •   Engagement activities will be tailored to local contexts, including appropriate timing,
       venues, and communication formats to facilitate participation of vulnerable and
       disadvantaged groups.
   •   Information disclosure materials will be prepared in simple, culturally appropriate
       language, primarily in Arabic, and disseminated through channels accessible to affected
       communities.
   •   Targeted or separate consultations, where necessary, will be conducted to ensure safe
       and meaningful participation of women, persons with disabilities, elderly people, and
       other vulnerable groups.
   •   Reasonable accommodation will be made to support participation of persons with
       disabilities, including accessible venues and formats.
   •   The Grievance Mechanism, including confidential and sensitive complaint pathways, will
       be widely communicated to ensure that vulnerable groups are aware of and able to access
       it safely.
   •   Feedback received through consultations, TPM surveys, and the GM will be documented,
       analyzed, and used to inform adaptive project management and improve service delivery.

Process:

Any person who has experienced or witnessed SEA/SH may anonymously contact the WHO

control over decision-making regarding next steps, including specifying what information is
shared, with whom, and for what purpose.
The GM operator notifies the Project and GBV Officer through an automated system-generated
email containing relevant information.
The GBV Officer provides psychosocial support and information on available services, and—upon
the survivor’s consent—facilitates referral to appropriate service providers, including through the
Yemen Women’s Union.
