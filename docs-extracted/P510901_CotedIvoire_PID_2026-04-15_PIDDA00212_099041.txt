B. Introduction and Context

Country Context

To accelerate poverty reduction and the transition to upper-middle income status, Cote d’Ivoire (CIV) will have to
rely more than in the past on the private sector to invest and create productive jobs. Over the last decade, CIV made
significant progress with development outcomes, with annual growth averaging about 6 percent, and GDP per capita
levels increasing by a cumulative 37.5 percent. However, the growth potential and capacity to create more and better
jobs remained constrained by persistent barriers to private investment and formalization, low productivity in the
agriculture sector, large disparities in access to basic infrastructure and services, and a lack of adequate skills and human
capital to stimulate structural transformation. As a result, growth translated into modest household income gains and
slow poverty reduction, with the national poverty rate declining only from 40.1 percent to 37.5 percent, driven largely
by progress in urban areas, while poverty in rural Côte d’Ivoire has largely stalled. Wage-earning jobs account for only
25 percent of total employment, while current human capital and labor utilization levels imply that labor productivity
only reaches 21 percent of its potential. Insufficient job opportunities and persistent skills mismatches are failing a
rapidly growing youth population: annual wage-earning job creation must accelerate from 430,000 to over 600,000 to
absorb the growing active-age population in the next five years. Addressing these development constraints and scaling
up opportunities for more and better paying jobs will require comprehensive reforms to leverage the private sector and
improve human capital to unlock the potential of critical sectors for structural transformation.

1.       Côte d'Ivoire's macroeconomic policy framework is adequate for this operation. Robust growth momentum,
led by the hydrocarbon and mining sectors and reinforced by the National Development Plan (PND 2026–2030), is
expected to persist despite renewed global headwinds and commodity price volatility. Revenue-based fiscal
consolidation, anchored in the Medium-Term Revenue Strategy (MTRS) and supported by IMF programs and World
Bank operations, reduced the fiscal deficit to the 3 percent of GDP target of the West African Economic and Monetary
Union (WAEMU). Ongoing public financial management reforms are improving the efficiency of public spending and
preserving fiscal space for priority infrastructure and social programs. Innovative financing instruments, including Debt-
for-Development operations, Sub-Saharan Africa's first local currency-denominated Eurobond, and an inaugural
issuance on the Japanese market, have broadened the financing base while maintaining moderate debt distress risks.
The financial sector remains sound, with well-capitalized banks, declining non-performing loans, and sustained credit
growth under the regional supervisory framework of the Central Bank of West African States (BCEAO). That said,
structural constraints, particularly low productivity and the limited transmission of growth into quality job creation,
continue to slow poverty reduction. While the unfolding Middle East crisis clouds the near-term outlook, a sustainable
macroeconomic policy framework and ongoing reforms, including those supported by this program, will help support
resilience.

 Relationship to CPF

The reform program of the operation targets an ambitious results framework that could enable substantial job creation
and private capital mobilization. The reform program advances core objectives of CIV CPF 2023-27 on more and better
jobs, human capital, and equity and has been deliberately designed to operationalize the new 3-by-5-by-7 WGB Jobs
Framework by targeting sectors critical to accelerating structural transformation for more and better jobs — such as
energy, digital, water, agribusiness, and healthcare— combining efforts to support economy-wide enablers through land
tenure security, investment facilitation, tax competitiveness, and skills formation for productive jobs. It is also aligned with
the Government’s The reform program is strongly aligned with national priorities outlined in the new National
Development Plan (PND) 2026-2030 notably on agriculture productivity, infrastructure quality, competitiveness, jobs and
human capital, and supports its effective implementation. The proposed DPF operation is fully aligned with the mitigation
and adaptation objectives of the Paris Agreement and seeks to maximize climate co-benefits in key sectors such as energy,
water, agriculture, and land management. It leverages the full WBG suite — IBRD, IFC, and MIGA — to maximize private
sector participation and crowd in financing at scale. For instance, energy reforms are backed by an IBRD guarantee,
potential MIGA second-loss, NEDA PforR, and an IFC IPP pipeline to advance M300 targets. Agriculture reforms
complement IFC and MIGA activities to drive Agriconnect's productivity and traceability agenda. Health sector reforms
benefit from joint WB-IFC advisory support to expand service capacity and workforce preparedness under Health at Work.
Throughout, the operation embodies the One WBG approach through upstream coordination with IFC and MIGA on
program design and complementary instruments.

 C. Proposed Development Objective(s)

 Scale-up private investment for more productive jobs by: (i) unlocking critical sectors for structural transformation,
 and (ii) strengthening the business climate and workforce readiness

 Key Results

The operation targets an ambitious results framework that could translate in more and better jobs and enable private
capital across key sectors. In the energy sector, generation capacity is projected to increase by around 500 MW by end-
2028 through privately financed solar and gas projects. Digital reforms will lower broadband costs and add over six million
broadband subscribers, accelerating connectivity. In agriculture and agroforestry, more than 570,000 additional farmers—
many of them women—will gain access to advanced training and technologies, while 90,000 jobs in agroforestry are
expected to be formalized. Healthcare reforms will expand the training pipeline for medical personnel and significantly
widen access to diagnostic services. Land reforms are expected to assign a unique identifier to approximately one million
parcels, while investment facilitation and tax reforms are projected to increase greenfield FDI projects, new business
registrations, and the effectiveness of tax incentives. TVET reforms will expand accreditation coverage and raise graduate
employment rates, better aligning skills with labor market demand. Using innovative simulations from a dynamic
Computable General Equilibrium model, it is estimated that the reform program could contribute to lifting real income
per capita by approximately by about 3.1 percentage points by 2031, enabling enabling approximately 179,400 new jobs
as well as better paying jobs for an additional 736,500 people, with the largest gains expected from agriculture innovation
and formalization efforts, reforms supporting investment promotion and facilitation, and broadband access and
affordability and close to US$2.2 billion in additional private investment. More and better jobs are primarily associated

 D. Project Description

The Program, which consists of a series of three single-tranche operations, seeks to stimulate private investment and
create more productive jobs in Côte d'Ivoire. The program is structured around two main pillars:

he Program, which consists of a series of three single-tranche operations, seeks to stimulate private investment and create
more productive jobs in Côte d'Ivoire. The program is structured around two main pillars:
Pillar 1 – Unlocking critical sectors for structural transformation. This pillar focuses on: (i) energy sector reforms aimed
at mobilizing private investment in renewable energy through competitive tenders for IPP projects, enabling self-
producers to access the grid, and opening a concession model for mini-grid development, while strengthening the financial
viability of the public utility CI-ENERGIES through payment discipline and tariff governance; (ii) digital connectivity reforms
expanding access to affordable broadband through competitive licensing, satellite frequency assignment, and
strengthened regulatory governance of ARTCI; (iii) water sector reforms introducing performance-based leasing contracts
and transforming ONEP into an asset management company to improve service quality and financial sustainability; (iv)
agribusiness reforms strengthening seed systems, authorizing agricultural extension in agroforests, and improving
governance of research, extension, and carbon-credit revenue sharing to advance productivity, climate resilience, and
traceability; and (v) health sector reforms facilitating private investment in laboratory and medical imaging services
through PPPs and establishing clear rules for private health training institutions, while safeguarding quality through
mandatory continuing education. Reforms implemented under the first operation include the adoption of: (i) an amending
Decree strengthening the governance framework for IPP concession agreements and enabling self-producers to sell
surplus electricity; (ii) inter-ministerial Arrêtés establishing the contractual architecture for self-producer grid access and
power purchase agreements; (iii) a Decree assigning satellite frequencies and implementing the 2024 Electronic
Communications Law to strengthen competition and ARTCI's mandate; (iv) a Decree approving a new performance-based
leasing contract for water services with published key performance indicators; (v) two Decrees establishing seed reserves
and authorizing agricultural extension in agroforests; and (vi) a Decree authorizing private investors to manage laboratory
and medical imaging services through PPPs.

Pillar 2 – Stimulating private investment and skills supply. This pillar focuses on: (i) land tenure security reforms
establishing the institutional framework to accelerate land registration, implement a unique land identifier (IDUFCI), and
reduce investment risk for households and firms; (ii) investment facilitation reforms consolidating fragmented investment
promotion functions, reforming the one-stop shop for business registration, and reforming CEPICI, the investment
promotion agency; (iii) tax policy reforms strengthening the transfer pricing framework, and improving the governance
and transparency of tax incentives; and (iv) TVET reforms establishing a national accreditation system for technical and
vocational education and training institutions to improve the quality and responsiveness of skills supply to productive
sectors. Reforms implemented under the first operation include the adoption of: (i) a Decree establishing the
Interministerial Commission for Land Tenure Security and its Technical Secretariat; (ii) a Decree revising CEPICI's statutes
and an Arrêté reforming the one-stop shop for business registration; (iii) a Council of Ministers communication formalizing
the Government's commitment to establish an Investment Development Council (CDI); (iv) an amendment to Article 38

 E. Implementation

 Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

The monitoring and evaluation (M&E) system remains the same as in previous DPF operations. M&E activities will be
anchored in the policy matrix and led by the Ministry of Economy, Finance, and Budget. Regular discussions will take
place with the government and the donor community on progress, results, and possible next steps. The monitoring and
evaluation process by the government and the World Bank will be based on a systematic review of implementation and
impact of prior actions and will compare results achieved with agreed results indicators in the Policy and Results Matrix.
The Matrix defines relevant indicators and empirical benchmarks to monitor progress during implementation, based on
publicly available or administrative data easily available for the government, overcoming the constraints typical of a data-
poor environment. It also enables a thorough ex-post evaluation following the end of the program in 2028. The World
Bank team will monitor the implementation of the reforms through missions, periodic reports from the Ministry of
Economy, Finance, and Budget, support from other operations to monitor reform implementation and progress towards
results indicators, and request corrective measures as needed until the closure of the operation. Continued support from
development partners through financing and/or TA is also necessary for the successful implementation of the reforms

 F. Poverty and Social Impacts, and Environmental, Forests, and Other Natural Resource Aspects

 Poverty and Social Impacts

Policy and institutional reforms supported under the proposed DPF are expected to have significant positive social
impacts over the medium and long term. Reforms under Pillar 1 aimed at improving agricultural productivity, energy
access, digital connectivity, water services, and health sector participation are expected to enhance welfare for the poor
and reduce inequalities. Strengthening seed systems and authorizing extension services in agroforests (PA#5) will raise
smallholder productivity, improve food security, and support rural income growth — accounting for an estimated 51% of
the total projected poverty impact. Expanding renewable energy access through competitive tenders, self-generation, and
mini-grid concessions (PA#1) will reduce energy costs for households and small businesses, particularly in underserved
rural areas. Improving CI-ENERGIES' financial viability through prepaid metering and payment discipline (PA#2) will reduce
service disruptions that disproportionately affect small enterprises and low-income consumers. Expanding affordable
broadband access through competitive licensing and improved governance (PA#3) will support digital inclusion and access
to services in underserved areas. Introducing a performance-based leasing contract and tariff optimization for water
services (PA#4) will expand access to safely managed water and sanitation for vulnerable populations. Facilitating private
investment in laboratory services and health training institutions (PA#6) will expand health service capacity and workforce
availability, particularly in underserved areas. Under Pillar 2, strengthening land tenure security (PA#7) will reduce
investment risk and improve credit access for smallholders, including women. Streamlining business registration and
establishing a clear investment facilitation framework (PA#8) will lower barriers to formalization, with specific measures
targeting female entrepreneurs. Improving the transfer pricing framework and rationalizing tax incentives (PA#9) will

broaden the tax base and expand fiscal space for pro-poor public spending. Strengthening TVET quality and responsiveness
(PA#10) will enhance youth employability, particularly for students from low-income and rural backgrounds.

Overall, the package of reforms is expected to have positive and mutually reinforcing poverty and social impacts by
improving access to essential services and expanding economic opportunities. Using model-based simulations, the
reform program is estimated to lift approximately 600,000 people out of poverty over the program period, with rural areas
capturing the largest share of gains driven primarily by agriculture, agroforestry, and energy reforms. The pace of impact
strengthens gradually—reflecting improvements in productivity, service delivery and reliability, and the investment
climate—and is expected to fully materialize in the later years of the DPF series as complementary reforms reinforce one
another. Distributional analysis confirms that the overall direction of impact is pro‑poor, though with only modest effects
on measured inequality. Gender gaps are also expected to narrow, with specific measures on land joint registration,
female entrepreneur support, and female farmer training contributing to faster poverty reduction among female-headed
households.

 Environmental, Forests, and Other Natural Resource Aspects

Côte d'Ivoire has a well-defined legal and regulatory environmental and social framework. The Ministry of Environment
and Sustainable Development is responsible for setting policy guidelines on environmental issues and ensuring compliance
with national environmental standards, supported by the Agence Nationale de l'Environnement (ANDE), which oversees
environmental and social impact assessments for all projects. The 2023 Environmental Code strengthens the country's
legal framework and mandates environmental and social assessments for relevant investments. While institutional
capacity for field supervision and monitoring remains uneven, ongoing World Bank support — including through the NEDA
PforR and the AgriConnect operation — is strengthening environmental management capacity across key sectors.

Reforms under the proposed operation have limited but predominantly positive environmental impacts. Energy sector
reforms (PA#1 and PA#2) reduce fuels reliance by promoting renewable energy generation and improved operational
efficiency, directly supporting Côte d'Ivoire's NDC goal to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 28% by 2030. Agriculture
and agroforestry reforms (PA#5) are aligned with the National Strategy for Forest Preservation and Zero-Deforestation
Supply Chains (SPREF), supporting climate-smart intensification, carbon sequestration, and sustainable land use. Digital
sector reforms (PA#3) enhance operational efficiency and reduce emissions through improved connectivity and
digitalization. Water sector reforms (PA#4) improve resource management and service sustainability. Land tenure reforms
(PA#7) incentivize sustainable land management practices by securing rights and reducing land-related conflicts in forest-
adjacent areas.

 G. Risks and Mitigation


Risks for the operation are assessed as substantial, reflecting governance, macroeconomic, and stakeholder challenges,
despite mitigation measures embedded in the program design. Governance risks notably stem from ministerial
reorganizations following the late-2025 elections, ambitious reforms stretching administrative capacity, and political-
economy constraints; these are being mitigated by anchoring reforms in the NDP and sector strategies, the use of robust
legal instruments, strengthened coordination mechanisms, high-level policy dialogue, and complementary World Bank
technical and program support. Macroeconomic risks have been upgraded to substantial, driven by the possibility of an
escalating crisis in the Middle East having larger than predicted effects on growth, jobs, and inflation, and compressing
the fiscal and political space needed to deliver on the ambitious reform program and results framework of this operation;

mitigation measures include efforts to improve domestic resource mobilization and reduce contingent liability risks,
complementary operations on public financial management efficiency, and continued alignment with IMF-supported
reforms and commitments. Stakeholder risks remain substantial, as reform implementation in key sectors may face
resistance from established interests despite efforts to mitigate them through structured consultations, coordination with
development partners, and continuous monitoring of stakeholder dynamics. Finally, fragility risks are expected to be
reduced through reforms that address contested access to land and natural resources, regional disparities, and youth
underemployment — all of which, if left unaddressed, could undermine both social cohesion and the broader
development agenda.
