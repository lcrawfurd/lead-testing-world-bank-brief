EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) has been prepared for the proposed
Program-for-Results (PforR) operation—Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Program
Phase 2 (SRWSSP2). The Government of Tanzania (GoT), through the Ministry of Water (MoW),
the Ministry of Education, Science, and Technology (MoEST) and the Ministry of Health (MoH)
are jointly implementing the Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Program (the parent
Program including its AF), or SRWSSP1, in collaboration with the World Bank. SRWSSP1 was
approved in July 2018, and the Additional Finance was approved in May 2022. The closing date
of SRWSSP1 is July 2025.

Tanzania SRWSSP2 is part of a regional Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) in Africa East
region. The MPA adopts a Systems Change approach to improve and accelerate service delivery,
in line with the strategic vision outlined by AFE countries. The MPA includes 4 pillars: 1)
developing WASH accelerating platform; 2) introducing sector governance reforms; 3) improving
service provision performance; and 4) Financing Climate-Resilient WSS Infrastructure and
Promoting Private Sector Participation. The MPA comprises a Regional Phase (Phase I), which
aims at improving governance, building institutional capacities for climate-resilient service
delivery, and strengthening resource mobilization for water supply and sanitation services in the
Eastern and Southern Africa region, and individual country operations (PforRs and IPFs) as other
phases, where Tanzania SRWSSP2 comprises Phase II.

SRWSSP1 was staged to support the second phase of the Water Sector Development Program
(WSDPII) with its first and second stages), the government program, which is expected to end in
2025 and will be succeeded by WSDP III. One of the achievements of SRWSSP1 was the
establishment of Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Agency (RUWASA), under Section 48 of the
Water Supply and Sanitation Act No. 5 of 2019, which took over the responsibilities of managing
rural water supply and sanitation services.

To continue improving Tanzanian water supply and sanitation services, the GoT, through the
Ministry of Water (MoW) in collaboration with the World Bank, are preparing the SRWSSP2, a
five-year program (2025-2030). The SRWSSP2 is set to expand access to rural water supply and
sanitation services in 137 district councils in 25 regions by improving Water, Sanitation, and
Hygiene (WASH) services; strengthening governance; and engaging the private sector for
sustainable solutions. The PforR financing is US$ 307 million, which includes a government
contribution of US$ 100 million, an IDA financing of US$ 182 million, US$2 million through a grant
from Energy Sector Management Assistance Program (ESMAP) and US$ 25 million is to be
leveraged from commercial financing through PPPs and through carbon finance.

The PforR includes an Investment Project Finance (IPF) component that consists of US$ 25 million
in IDA credit. The aim of the IPF component is to provide Technical Assistance (TA) including
capacity development, and program delivery support to key implementing agencies. The IPF
component will also support, through Technical Assistance (TA), the Water Development
Department of the Ministry of Water, Energy and Mines (MoWEM) towards strengthening sector

proposed program Implementing Agencies (IA) are the MoW, Ministry of Education, Science, and
Technology (MoEST), Ministry of Health (MoH) and President’s Office – Regional Administration
and Local Government (PO-RALG), while Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Agency (RUWASA)
and Local Local Government Authorities will be the primary implementing entities responsible
for the Program day to day activities at the districts level.

This ESSA is prepared for SRWSSP2 and is built on the analysis included in the two ESSAs of
SRWSSP1 (both the original ESSA prepared in 2018 and the updated one prepared in 2022) while
adding aspects related to further expansion of the Program geographical coverage, assessing
updates on the environmental and social systems after 2022, including a thorough evaluation of
SRWSSP1 Environmental and Social (E&S) performance, and identifying any related gaps.

ESSA Objectives
The SRWSSP2 ESSA seeks to achieve the following: (i) identifying any potential E&S effects of the
activities under the proposed Program; (ii) analysing the existing national environmental and social
system in Tanzania against the core principles of the PforR Policy; (iii) examining the current E&S
capacity of implementing institutions in managing environmental and social effects and determine
if there is a need to strengthen the capacity; (iv) identifying lessons from the implementation of the


                                                               vii
environmental and social management of the parent SRWSSP; and (v) recommending actions to fill
any gaps as an input for PAP to strengthen the Program’s performance.

This SRWSSP2 ESSA was prepared according to the requirements of the World Bank’s PforR
Policy, the Program-for-Results Directive, and the World Bank’s Guidance for preparing ESSAs.


SRWSSP2 Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) Findings
A. Potential Environmental and Social Benefits of the Program

Program Social Economic Benefits: The program aims to enhance Tanzania's water sector by
improving WASH services in 137 districts across 25 Regions. It will provide well-designed rural
water and sanitation facilities, promote sanitation through Behavior Change Communication
(BCC), and reduce open defecation. The initiative will support safe fecal sludge management and
incentivize proper waste collection, treatment, and disposal. Additionally, it will optimize water
resource use, reduce losses, and improve drinking water quality. Climate-resilient water
management will be strengthened, incorporating integrated water resource management for
various needs, such as livestock and irrigation. The program will ensure long-term water
availability by preventing over-extraction and coordinating with water user associations and
pastoralists. Overall, SRWSSP2 aims to improve water and sanitation service delivery, addressing
key challenges in Tanzania’s water sector.

Social Benefits: By increasing access to safely managed water and improved sanitation services,
the program is expected to deliver substantial social benefits. These include improved public
health outcomes through the reduction of waterborne diseases, time savings for women and
children who typically bear the responsibility for water collection, and enhanced dignity and
privacy, particularly for girls and women, through improved sanitation facilities.

The program also promotes inclusive development by targeting underserved and vulnerable communities,
thereby contributing to social equity. Capacity building of local water user associations and community-
based organizations will strengthen local governance and accountability mechanisms. This will increase
ownership and long-term sustainability for water facilities.

Further to the above, the program aligns directly with the objectives of SDG 6 (Clean Water and
Sanitation), particularly Targets 6.1 (universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water)
and 6.2 (access to adequate and equitable sanitation and hygiene for all). It also supports broader
development goals, including SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being), SDG 5 (Gender Equality), and SDG 10
(Reduced Inequalities), by addressing the structural barriers that limit access to essential services for
marginalized populations.

Social Risks: The possible social risks are:
a) Affordability Constraints for Low-Income Communities: low ability of low-income rural communities
   to afford regular water service payments

                                                    viii
b) Physical Accessibility of Water Delivery Points for Vulnerable Groups: possibility of the physical
   accessibility of water delivery infrastructure especially for elderly persons and individuals with
   disabilities in rural areas.
c) Physical and Economic Displacement – If land acquisition is required, it may displace
   communities or create conflicts over land ownership due to physical and economic
   displacement.
d) Health and Safety Hazards – Workers and communities may face risks such as accidents,
   exposure to chemicals, and waterborne diseases.
e) Gender and Social Inclusion Issues – If not properly planned, the facilities may fail to meet
   the needs of women, children, and persons with disabilities.
f) Community Resistance and Conflicts – Lack of community engagement in project planning
   may lead to opposition or disputes. This is likely to happen especially in areas which are
   inhabited by IPs.
g) Long-Term Maintenance Challenges – If local communities are not trained or involved in the
   management of the facilities, constructed facilities may face premature deterioration.
h) Cultural Sensitivities – Certain locations or designs may conflict with local beliefs and
   customs, causing resistance to facility use.
i) Further spread of GBV, SEA, and STDs: The presence of non-local and local workers for the
   civil works could exacerbate the risk of GBV, sexual harassment and/ or other sexual offenses
   including rape.
j) Workers’ Rights and Social Inclusion Risks: The program will require a substantial workforce
   for constructing water supply and sanitation facilities. Unskilled workers are especially
   vulnerable to exploitation, including long working hours, irregular payments, lack of rest
   periods, and the absence of formal contracts.
k) Risk of exclusion and discrimination against vulnerable groups, including Indigenous
   Peoples (IPs), from project benefits such as water supply and WASH services. Given the high
   poverty levels and limited education access in some rural areas, disadvantaged communities
   may be left out of consultations and safeguards.
l) Violation of Workers’ Rights: The program will require a considerable amount of labour to
   be used in construction of water supply and sanitation facilities. Construction staff especially
   unskilled one tend to be victims of exploitation and unfair treatment by employers including
   long working hours, irregular payments, lack of rest periods, payments, and lack of contracts.
   Their exploitation is mainly perpetrated because unskilled and semi-skilled workers, unlike
   the skilled ones, lack knowledge of their rights or be willing to waive these rights just for the
   mere sake of securing employment.

m) Likelihood of Exclusion and Discrimination of Vulnerable Groups Including IPs from Project
   Benefits such as water supply, WASH services: The project will primarily be implemented in
   rural areas, where some communities face high poverty levels and limited access to
   education. In certain regions, disadvantaged communities and vulnerable groups, including
   Indigenous Peoples (IPs), may be present. There is a risk that these groups could be excluded
   from consultations and safeguards against risks associated with SRWSSP2 benefits. To ensure
   inclusive and meaningful engagement, the program will adopt the Vulnerable Groups Plan
   under the Productive Social Safety Net (PSSN) P124045.

Environmental Benefits: The environmental benefits of the Program include: (i) improving
community health by providing safe drinking water and sanitation; (ii) reducing impacts on
surface water, soil and ground water discharge and disposal of un-treated sewage and septage;
(iii) more climate resilient water resource management; and (iv) less GHG emissions from
disposal of untreated sewage and septage. Through the proposed Program Action Plan (PAP),
the Program will assist implementing institutions in capacity-building initiatives, including: (v)
supporting local communities in enhancing sanitation and ensuring safe fecal sludge
management along with promotion of WASH services; and (vi) promoting the sustainable
management of water resources in rural areas.

Environmental Risks: None of the Program activities are likely to cause significant
adverse impacts that are sensitive, diverse, or unprecedented on the environment. The Program will
adopt screening criteria to screen out ineligible activities that may cause significant adverse impacts. The
possible environmental risks of eligible activities are:

    a) Affecting water availability – Over-extraction of groundwater or surface water can affect
       water availability, and impacting associated ecosystems.
    b) Water Contamination – Poorly planned sanitation facilities (e.g., latrines near water
       sources) and improper collection, treatment, and disposal infrastructure for fecal sludge
       can lead to contamination of water sources thus increasing health risks.
    c) Soil Erosion & Sedimentation – Construction activities may cause soil disturbance, leading
       to erosion and sedimentation in nearby water bodies.
    d) Loss of Vegetation & Biodiversity – Clearing land for infrastructure may affect habitats
       supporting local flora and fauna.
    e) Waste Generation – Construction and operation of water supply and sanitation systems
       generate solid and liquid waste, which, if not managed properly, can lead to
       environmental pollution.
    f) Impacts of air emissions. This includes air emissions from incinerators used in HCF, which,
       if not properly managed, include harmful gases. This also includes air emissions from
       construction activities such as earthworks, exhaust of machinery, vehicles and generators
    g) Flooding & Drainage Issues – Poor drainage planning can result in water stagnation,
       increasing the risk of flooding and waterborne diseases.
    h) Handling of hazardous substances – the operation of water supply and sanitation would
       involve storge, utilization and disposal of hazardous substances (such as disinfectants,
       oils, fuels … etc.)
    i) Health and safety hazards to workers and the community – Construction and operation
       works of water supply and sanitation systems are associated with health and safety risks
       to workers (eg. Working at heights, confined spaces, deep excavations, hot works,
       machine movements … etc.) and community (unauthorized access to construction sites,
       increased traffic, storage of hazardous substances, substandard water quality … etc.). Use
       of incinerators can raise fire risks and risks related to handling hazardous waste and ash.

B. Client Capacity to Manage Environmental and Social Issues
(i) Country’s Legal and Institutional Position

Tanzania’s regulatory framework for Environmental and Social (E&S) management aligns well
with the World Bank’s PforR Policy and Directive, providing a strong foundation for addressing
environmental, health, safety, and social concerns under SRWSSP2. The country has established
legal provisions and regulatory authorities to oversee environmental assessments, occupational
health and safety (OHS), and protections against gender-based violence (GBV) and sexual
exploitation and abuse (SEA).

For WASH services in schools and health facilities, Tanzania has national guidelines, including
toolkits and a School WASH (SWASH) strategic plan, all of which apply to the Program. The water
sector is governed by five key legislations and 21 regulations covering water resource
management, supply, sanitation, dam safety, storm water control, and wastewater management.

Under the SRWSSP Parent Program, several safeguard instruments have been implemented, such
as the Gender Action Plan, Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF), Grievance Redress Mechanism,
and Labor Management Procedures. These instruments were specifically developed to address
the distinct requirements of the IPF component, thereby ensuring comprehensive E&S coverage
and facilitating the effective implementation and monitoring of safeguards across both financing
modalities (More details about those instruments are provided in Section 5.7.1). These
instruments will be further strengthened in SRWSSP2


(ii) Capacity of Implementing Institutions to Manage Environmental and Social Issues
MoW, MoH, MoEST
The Implementing Agencies have been implementing SRWSSP1 and the implementation ratings
have been either satisfactory or moderately satisfactory. They have developed capacity for
managing E&S issues. MoW for instance, has established Environmental and Social Team at the
national level and provided them with capacity to manage and monitor environmental and social

                                               xi
risks. Both the MoH and the MoEST have strengthened their capacity to integrate environmental
and social risk management into their operations through multiple World Bank-funded projects
(both IPF and PforRs). The MoH has successfully implemented the Tanzania Maternal and Child
Health Investment Program (TMCHIP) TMCHIP and coordinated the National Sanitation
Campaign (NSC) under WSDP-2. It has also established a dedicated ESS unit and recruited two
specialists—an environmentalist and a social safeguard expert. Similarly, MoEST has enhanced
its ESS capacity through projects like the Education and Skills for Productive Jobs (ESPJ) PforR and
Higher Education for Economic Transformation (HEET) Project and has appointed an ESS
Technical Assistant for environmental and social management. With their expertise, institutional
capacity and representation in LGAs, both ministries are well-equipped to manage the social and
environmental risks of the WASH activities under SRWSSP2.
Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Agency (RUWASA): Since its inception, RUWASA has made
remarkable steps in recruiting skilled personnel, enhancing institutional and staff capacity,
developing operational tools, and improving reporting systems. These initiatives have
strengthened its ability to design and manage sustainable rural water supply and sanitation
projects, including management of environmental and social risks. Participation in SRWSSP1 has
further reinforced RUWASA’s environmental and social capacity, equipping it to manage the E&S
risks associated with SRWSSP2.


(iii) Identified Gaps

Challenges and Gaps in E&S Performance: While SRWSSP1 has successfully conducted
Environmental and Social Impact Assessments (ESIAs), ensured land acquisition through
voluntary donation, promoted grievance management and GBV prevention, prevention
measures, and implemented E&S capacity-building for its staff, key gaps remain:

    •   Some investments may not have appropriate environmental and social assessment and
        management instruments because an E&S instrument for some types of works are not
        mandatory according to the national regulations, and works may start before having
        instruments ready due to time constraints
    •   Clear screening criteria for investments eligibility under the PforR Policy need to be in
        place
    •   Lack of Dedicated E&S financial and human resources for supervision and monitoring of
        construction and operation works.
    •   Limited Reporting on environmental, social, health, and safety issues in progress reports.
    •   E&S teams dedicated to supporting the program are consistently engaged in other
        government activities which limit their time to support the program.
    •   Inadequate Stakeholder Engagement Records, with poor documentation of consultations.
    •   Non-Compliance with design standards of some facilities, leading to poor-quality
        infrastructure and inadequate support for disabled users.
    •   Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) violations due to weak contract management and
        safety culture among workers and employers leading to absence of essential safety
        control measures.

       President Office (VPO) and Occupational Safety and Health Authority (OSHA), limiting
       environmental oversight


C. Recommendations for Improvement

Given the above-mentioned challenges and gaps experienced while implementing SRWSSP1, the
following actions have been recommended for SRWSSP2. Some of the recommendations below
were part of SRWSSP1 but have been presented in a more explicit manner to address certain
gaps. For example, SRWSSP1 required building the capacity of different implementing agencies,
while the recommendations for SRWSSP2 have been more specific to the identified challenges
such as appointing certain E&S focal points and addressing weaknesses in contact management
and reporting. Other recommendations were added to improve the environmental/health
performance of the Program, such as ensuring that water meets the Tanzanian standards. The
recommendations will be included as part of DLI verification protocol, PAP, and POM.

DLI verification
    - Verification of DLIs 1, 3 and 4 to include evidence that investments have been screened
        against eligibility criteria according to PforR policy
    - Verification of DLI 1 ensures water quality meets Tanzanian Standards
    - Verification of DLI 5 includes evidence that CBWSOs have sufficient capacity to manage
        E&S risks during O&M. This will be through preparing EHS manual for operation and
        maintenance
    - Verification of DLI 7 includes evidence that RUWASA has developed EHS Manual as part
        of their O&M Manual

Program Action Plan (PAP)

   -   Enhance the institutional capacity of Implementing Agencies (IAs) including appointing
       E&S focal points at the Program level by RUWASA, appointing at least one qualified
       environmental and one qualified social specialist are in each Region by RUWASA, MoH
       and MoEST.
   -   Provided sufficient training on E&S contract management and health and safety aspects
       to RUWASA, MoH and MoEST.
   -   Procurement documents of works should include an ESIA or ESMP approved by NEMC
   -   Procurement of incinerators in HCF is according to safe standards for preventing
       emissions of harmful gases
   -   Implement and operationalize the voluntary land donation protocols developed under
       SRWSSP1.
   -   Secure land titles for all sites designated for the construction of WASH facilities
   -   Integrating SEA/SH and STD awareness programs into Program activities
   -   Building the capacity of contractors and other project implementers to uphold workers’
       rights and local population by training them on: The Employment and Labour Relations

                                             xiii
       Act, Act No. 6 of 2004, the Program’s Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM), land
       Donation Protocols, Gender Action Plan (GAP), Contractors' E&S Reporting
       Requirements and the Vulnerable Plan Framework.

Program Operation Manual (POM) measures

   -   Ensure designs of WASH infrastructure consider climate change impacts and improve
       resilience to climate change.
   -   Ensure WASH facilities consider the needs to disabled people in schools and hospitals.
   -   Works contracts do not allow contractors to mobilize except after meeting all required
       safety standards including having competent health and safety staff, and Contractor
       Environment, Social Management Plans (CESMPs) include H&S safety risk assessment and
       control and approved.
   -   Coordinate with OSHA to carry out audits to active construction sites on yearly basis and
       as needed (after incidents or in sites with persistent noncompliance).
   -   Fostering collaboration with regulatory bodies such as the NEMC, Division of Labour in
       the Prime Minister’s Office Labour, Youth, Employment, and Persons with Disabilities.

1. INTRODUCTION

This report presents the Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) prepared by the
World Bank for the Program-for-Results (PforR) – Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation
Program Phase 2 (SRWSSP2). The ESSA covers the following aspects: an overview of the
Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Program; the anticipated environmental and
social effects associated with activities under the Program; an evaluation of the borrower’s
environmental and social management systems relevant to these activities; an assessment of the
borrower’s performance and track record in implementing these systems for the parent Program
(including its Additional Finance); or SRWSSP1, and recommendations along with agreed-upon
actions for enhancing the implementation of applicable systems.

Tanzania SRWSSP2 is part of a regional Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) in Africa East
region. The MPA adopts a Systems Change approach to improve and accelerate service delivery,
in line with the strategic vision outlined by AFE countries. The MPA includes 4 pillars: 1)
developing WASH accelerating platform; 2) introducing sector governance reforms; 3) improving
service provision performance; and 4) Financing Climate-Resilient WSS Infrastructure and
Promoting Private Sector Participation. The MPA comprises a Regional Phase (Phase I), which
aims at improving governance, building institutional capacities for climate-resilient service
delivery, and strengthening resource mobilization for water supply and sanitation services in the
Eastern and Southern Africa region, and individual country operations (PforRs and IPFs) as other
phases, where Tanzania SRWSSP2 comprises Phase II.

1.1. Tanzania Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Program (SRWSSP1)
The Government of Tanzania (GoT), through the Ministry of Water (MoW), the Ministry of
Education, Science, and Technology (MoEST) and the Ministry of Health (MoH) are jointly
implementing SRWSSP1 in collaboration with the World Bank. SRWSSP1 was approved in July
2018, and the Additional Finance was approved in May 2022. The closing date of SRWSSP1 is July
2025. SRWSSP1 was staged to support the second phase of the Water Sector Development
Program (WSDP with its first and second stages), the government program, which is expected to
end in 2026 and will be succeeded by WSDP III. The WSDP comprises five key components
covering the entire water sector: 1. Water Resources Management and Development which
ensures the nation’s water resources are sustainably managed and developed; 2. Water Quality
Management to improve monitoring and assessment of ambient water quality; 3. Water Supply
to achieve universal access to adequate, clean and safe water, including for households, schools
and HealthCare Facilities (HCFs) in rural areas; 4. Sanitation and Hygiene to achieve universal
access to adequate sanitation and hygiene services including for households, schools and
healthcare facilities in rural areas; and 5. Programme Coordination and Delivery Support to
enhance planning, coordination, monitoring and evaluation; and institutional and policy
strengthening. SRWSSP1 included 3 Result Areas: 1) Increased access to improved water services
in rural areas; 2) Increased access to safely managed sanitation services in rural areas; and 3)

2. PROGRAM DESCRIPTION AND OBJECTIVES

2.1. Country Context and Rationale for the Proposed Program (SRWSSP2)
SRWSSP1 has attained some achievement in water and sanitation sector in Tanzania. For
instance, SRWSSP1 supported the newly established Rural Water and Sanitation Agency
(RUWASA). RUWASA has monitored and invested in the rural water sector, leading to a significant
increase in functional rural water points and improved access to water and sanitation for millions
of people. The SRWSSP also improved WASH access in primary schools and health care facilities
and encouraged private sector involvement.

2.1.1. Challenge that Constrains Tanzania Water Sector
The Tanzania water sector is still facing significant challenges in meeting the universal WASH
services to all its citizens. The access gap to WASH services in Tanzania remains high, in part due
to rapid population growth rate which has limited the proportion of the population gaining
access. According to the Joint Monitoring Program2, rural access to basic drinking water supply
has improved from nearly 34 percent in 2012, to 49 percent in 2022, however it remains below
the Sub-Saharan African average of 50 percent with access to at least basic and falls significantly
beneath Tanzania’s Vision 2025 goal of 85 percent access to safely managed water supply.
Access to at least basic rural sanitation is also very low, with only a modest increase from
approximately 12 percent in 2012 to a little over 21 percent in 2022, below the Sub-Saharan
African average of just over 24 percent. Nearly 10 percent of rural Tanzanian’s still practice open
defecation. The sector is far from achieving the Government of Tanzania’s (GoT) target of 95
percent rural access to improved sanitation by 20253.
Although higher than the average of 16.5 percent in Sub-Saharan Africa, 2022 rural access to
basic hygiene was unchanged since 2012 at 22 percent. In 2023, nearly 34 percent of rural
schools in Tanzania lacked access to any drinking water facilities, 60 percent lacked access to
even basic sanitation, and more than 83 percent lacked access to basic hygiene. In 2021,
approximately 26 percent of rural healthcare facilities lacked any water supply, nearly 52 percent
were without any sanitation service and only 32 percent had basic healthcare waste services4.

                 2.3. Investment Project Finance (IPF) Component

             The IPF component will provide targeted technical assistance and capacity-building support to
             implementing agencies, with a focus on institutional strengthening enhancing sector coordination,
             monitoring, and reporting. On the mainland, the IPF will deliver targeted technical assistance and
             capacity-building on digital data management, intersectoral coordination, private sector participation,
             program supervision and implementation (including independent results verification), sanitation and
             hygiene behavior change communications and support for the evaluation of WSDP III and preparation of
             its successor government program. The IPF component will also support government initiatives on
             integrated water resources management including conducting aquifer mapping, development of
             groundwater data systems, integrated water resources planning and allocation, dams inventory and
             strengthening of the Basin Water Boards. In Zanzibar, the IPF shall provide targeted Technical Assistance
             (TA) to the Water Development Department of the Ministry of Water, Energy and Mines (MoWEM)
             towards strengthening sector governance, institutional restructuring, service delivery, and capacity
             building capacity. It will include finalization of a Fecal Sludge Management (FSM) and wastewater
             management strategy and support to the detailed designs and preparation of tender documents for
             priority sanitation management infrastructures.

3. ESSA SCOPE AND METHODOLOGY

3.1. ESSA Objectives

SRWSSP2 ESSA was designed to achieve the following: (i) identifying any potential E&S effects of
the activities under the proposed Program; (ii) analysing the existing national environmental and
social system in Tanzania against the core principles of the PforR Policy; (iii) examining the current
E&S capacity of implementing institutions in managing environmental and social effects and
determine if there is a need to strengthen the capacity; (iv) identifying any lessons from the
implementation of the environmental and social management of the parent SRWSSP; and (v)
recommending actions to fill any gaps to strengthen the Program’s performance.

3.2. Methodology Employed
    a) A Desktop Review of (i) literature related to the E&S considerations in the governance
       sector including policies, legal framework, regulations and (ii) Program documents
       (Concept Note, Program Appraisal Document etc.);
    b) Analyse potential Environment, Social, Health and Safety (ESHS) risks of the Program
    c) Institutional analysis through a Capacity Assessment carried out through consultation
       with relevant institutions responsible for implementing the Program to identify their
       roles, responsibilities and structures. The capacity assessment included both
       implementing agencies (MoW, MoH, MoEST, and RUWASA and regulatory authorities
       including Occupational Health and Safety Authority (OSHA) and National Environmental
       Management Council (NEMC);
    d) Stakeholders’ engagement.
    e) Data analysis.
    f) Analyse lessons learned from the implementation of other PforRs (and IPFs) regarding
       management of E&S risks and impacts as well as adopt the gains made.
    g) Conduct Strengths-Weaknesses-Opportunities-and-Threats (SWOT) analysis reviewing
       oversight mechanisms in place and identifying measures that could be introduced to
       strengthen them.

4. ENVIRONMENT AND SOCIAL POLICY, LEGAL AND INSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK RELEVANT
    TO SUSTAINABLE RURAL WATER SUPPLY AND SANITATION PROGRAM PHASE 2
Tanzania has established a comprehensive framework of policies, legislations, and regulations
governing environmental and social management. Additionally, there is a requirement for sectors
to integrate environmental and social considerations into their decision-making processes, as
outlined below.

4.1. National Policy and Legislative Framework
4.1.1. Environmental Management

4.1.1.1. The National Environmental Policy (2021)
The National Environmental Policy (NEP) serves as a guiding framework for incorporating
environmental and social (E&S) considerations into decision-making in Tanzania. Originally
introduced in 1997, the policy was updated in 2021 to address emerging challenges such as
climate change, invasive species, electronic waste (e-waste), genetically modified organisms
(GMOs), oil and gas management, and the safe handling of chemicals. This policy has significantly
advanced environmental management in Tanzania, leading to the enactment of the
Environmental Management Act (EMA) in 2004 (Amended 2024), which serves as the principal
framework for environmental governance.
The objectives of the 2021 National Environmental Policy are:
    • Strengthen environmental management coordination across sectors and governance
        levels.
    • Promote sustainable land use for socioeconomic growth.
    • Enhance water source protection and management.
    • Conserve wildlife habitats and biodiversity.
    • Ensure sustainable forest ecosystem management.
    • Reduce pollution for a safer, healthier environment.
    • Build capacity to address climate change impacts.
    • Protect aquatic ecosystems.
    • Ensure the safe use of modern biotechnology.
    • Integrate gender considerations into environmental policies.
    • Promote good governance in environmental management.

4.1.1.5. Water Resource Management Act (2009)
The Act outlines the principles and objectives of water resource management in Tanzania,
specifying the ownership and control of water, the management of water resources, the
development of water resource management plans, the protection of water resources, water
usage and obstruction, water user associations, transboundary water issues, financial provisions,
as well as offenses, penalties, and judicial procedures, among others. It ensures that the nation's
water resources are protected, used, developed, conserved, managed, and controlled in a
sustainable manner. As such, the implementation of the SRWSSP2 shall comply with this Act.

Specifically, the program will ensure adherence to the principles that govern the development of
the water sector in Tanzania that include:

   a) Meeting the basic human needs of the present and future generations;
   b) Promoting equitable access to water and principle that water is essential for life and that
      safe drinking water is basic human right;
   c) Promoting the efficient, sustainable and beneficial use of water in the public interest,
   d) Facilitating social economic development;
   e) Promoting stakeholders involvement in water resources management at all levels
      especially by ensuring decentralization to the lowest possible level of government,
      consistent with available capacity at such level;
   f) Protecting biological diversity especially the aquatic ecosystems;
   g) Promoting for systems for managing the growing demand for water use through
      integrated planning and management of surface and ground water resources, in ways

      process;
   h) Preventing and controlling pollution and degradation of water resources;
   i) Providing implementation of international obligations stipulated under international legal
      instruments to the United Republic is a party; and
   j) Promoting dam safety security and the management of water related disasters.

Framework for River Management in Tanzania: Both the National Water Policy (2002) and the
Water Resource Management Act (2009) establish a hierarchical framework for river
management. At the national level, the Ministry of Water (MoW) oversees water governance,
while nine Basin Water Boards manage river and lake basins. At the sub-basin level, Catchment
Committees operate on the ground. Below these levels, Water Users Associations (WUAs) serve
as a formal mechanism for public participation in water management at a more localized scale.
WUAs represent the lowest tier in Tanzania’s hierarchical water management system, covering
entire watersheds or specific sections and entrusting residents with responsibilities related to
water conservation and certain aspects of water allocation permitting. The policy and legislation
mandate that the needs of different user groups be considered and met in water resource
development. Domestic water needs—such as drinking, bathing, cooking, and subsistence—are
given top priority. Ensuring the sustainability of ecosystems, both now and in the future, is the
second priority, followed by the water demands of agriculture, hydropower, industry, and other
off-channel users.

4.1.2.1.      Constitution of Tanzania 1977 (amended in 1998, 2005)
The constitution contains provisions that uphold human rights and well-being. For instance,

4.1.2.2. Employment and Labour Relations Act, Act No. 6 of 2004 and the Labour Institutions

The Employment Act sets labor standards, rights, and responsibilities, while the Labour Institutions

4.1.2.4. The Occupational Health and Safety Act (No. 5, 2003)
This is the primary legislation governing Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) issues in Tanzania.

4.1.2.7. Village Lands Act, No. 5 of 1999:
The act requires each village to identify and register all communal land, obtaining the approval

The Ministry of Community Development, Gender, Elderly, and Children (MCDGEC) oversees and

5.1. Potential Health, Environmental and Social Effects of the Program

To a large extent, the activities proposed under SRWSSP2 are similar to those which were

5.2. Social Benefits:
By increasing access to safely managed water and improved sanitation services by enhancing
WASH services in 137 districts across 25 regions. The program is expected to deliver substantial
social benefits. These include improved public health outcomes through the reduction of
waterborne diseases, time savings for women and children who typically bear the responsibility
for water collection, and enhanced dignity and privacy, particularly for girls and women, through
improved sanitation facilities.

The program also promotes inclusive development by targeting underserved and vulnerable
communities, thereby contributing to social equity. The capacity building of local water user
associations and community-based organisations will strengthen local governance and
accountability mechanisms, increasing ownership and long-term sustainability of water facilities.

Further to the above, the program aligns directly with the objectives of SDG 6 (Clean Water and
Sanitation), particularly Targets 6.1 (universal and equitable access to safe and affordable
drinking water) and 6.2 (access to adequate and equitable sanitation and hygiene for all). It also
supports broader development goals, including SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being), SDG 5
(Gender Equality), and SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities), by addressing the structural barriers that
limit access to essential services for marginalized populations.

   5.6. Climate Change effects

Climate change is now becoming increasingly significant because of its impact on the available
sources of water, as well as on the competing demands on its use. A study on climate change and
variability has reported that rainfall in all regions of Tanzania, are decreasing (Luhunga and
Songoro, 2020). Small water utilities must be alert to these impacts as they pose threats to their
long-term viability and sustainability. Climate change effects range from rising sea levels,
increased saline intrusion into groundwater aquifers (raising the need for desalination), increased
bromide (adding challenges to water treatment), to increased risk of direct storm and flood
damage to water utility facilities. Warmer climates can cause changes in discharge characteristics
of major rivers due to upstream changes; changes in recharge characteristics of major
groundwater aquifers due to upstream changes; increased water temperature leading to
increased evaporation and eutrophication in surface sources; water treatment and distribution
challenges; increased competing demands for domestic and irrigation; increased urban demand
with more heat waves and dry spells; and increased drawdown of local groundwater resources
to meet the increasing water demands.

Despite strong legal instruments for freshwater conservation, future changes in freshwater
supply and demand in Tanzania will present challenges for balancing future river water
allocations between ecosystems and human users. The World Bank’s Climate and Disaster Risk
Screening assessment indicates that Tanzania is exposed to potential future increases in extreme
temperatures, extreme precipitation and flooding, recurrence of droughts, and recurrence of
strong winds. River flows in some Basins are declining in part because of high rates of water
withdrawals, which threatens numerous species of amphibians and reptiles. Current projections
suggest that human demand for freshwater will substantially increase in the next decades.
Human population growth, agricultural expansion, planned construction of new dams,
industrialization, introduced species, and climate change are all issues relevant on a national
scale; however, these challenges are likely to be most acute for river systems that support
agricultural and urban areas in eastern Tanzania.

To address climate change and related challenges in the water sector, the Ministry of Water
(MoW) developed the Water Resources Management Strategic Interventions and Action Plan for
Climate Change Adaptation in 2013. This strategy identifies four major environmental issues
requiring urgent attention: water catchment degradation, water management technologies,

                                                30
groundwater exploration and extraction, and access to high-quality water for both urban and
rural populations.

The plan serves as a key tool for implementing the National Climate Change Strategy while
integrating climate change challenges into the development planning of the water sector. In the
same efforts, some water basin authorities’ such as WAMI/RUVU Basin, developed an Integrated
Water Resources Management (IWRM) Baseline and Action Rationale of 2022 for fostering climate
resilience. SRWSSP2 will further strengthen the efforts to mitigate and adapt to climate change
through building more resilience for water schemes. Specifically, Result Area 1 ensures climate-
resilient water resources management. Through quality planning and promotion of an integrated
water resource management approach, multiple uses of water will be enhanced. It will also assist
in preventing the over-extraction of water in order to improve the long-term water availability in
rural areas.

5.7.1.2.   Procedure for Land Acquisition/Allocation Procedure under Parent Program

Both voluntary and involuntary land acquisition approaches are used, with over 90% of projects
implemented on land voluntarily donated by individuals or village governments. To complement
Tanzania’s legal framework on involuntary land acquisition, the program under the E&S PAP has
adopted the Water Sector Support Project (P087154) – Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF),
which will also apply to SRWSSP2. RUWASA utilizes a customized voluntary land acquisition
agreement, signed between the Landowner and the Regional Manager (Annex IV). In cases of
involuntary acquisition, land valuation is conducted to determine compensation. Overall,
communities have largely supported the parent project by willingly donating land which have
saved government costs for land acquisition and improving community sense of projects
ownership.
Voluntary Land Donation Protocol: The process of accessing land for program purposes through
Voluntary Land Donation (VLD) involves the following steps:
    a. Stakeholder Engagement: A stakeholders’ meeting is conducted to share project
       information, clarify land requirements, and explain land acquisition protocols. All relevant
       authorities in the project’s implementation area are considered stakeholders.
    b. Village Meetings: Meetings are held in each village to raise awareness about the project,
       voluntary land donation, and compensation for any property damage, such as houses and
       crops.

Once there is a general understanding and valuation has been prepared, the local protocol
proceeds with the signing of a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) between the relevant
authority, recipients, and Project-Affected People (PAPs). This process includes the following key
activities:

Key Activities in the Voluntary Land Donation Process
   a) Verification of Informed Consent: The village chairperson, in coordination with sub-
       project staff, verifies that individuals donating land or assets do so voluntarily. The
                                                32
       following aspects must be confirmed and documented in the Voluntary Donation Report
       (Valuation Report and Voluntary Land Donation Agreement):

       •  The donated land will be used for public interest, specifically for the construction of
          water supply infrastructure under SRWSSP, and will be owned by the local
          government authority (Village, District, or Region).
      • Any competing claims of ownership or land use will be addressed through the
          Grievance Redress Mechanism.
      • Affected individuals agree to donate land or assets based on face-to-face meetings,
          valuation reports, and the Voluntary Land Donation Agreement.
      • The identities of land users and any occupants (whether through ownership,
          livelihood activities, or business operations) will be verified. Property valuation will
          comply with land acquisition laws.
      • If land is required for water infrastructure development, owners may be offered in-
          kind compensation or physical relocation.
      • The donated land will remain permanently designated for public use, regardless of
          future changes in its economic viability.
   b) The Right to Refuse: The right to refuse land donation must be absolute, unconditional,
      and freely exercised within the local community and political context. The decision to
      donate land must be made without coercion, manipulation, or pressure from public or
      traditional authorities. In cases of communal or collective land, donation must be based
      on the informed consent of all individuals using or occupying the land.

   c) Proper Documentation: During the voluntary land donation process for sub-project
      investments, it is crucial to distinguish between:
      • The agreement to donate the land, and
      • The legal transfer of land ownership.

While documenting the donor's intention is necessary, ensuring the legal transfer of land—where
applicable—is equally important. Though legal transfer processes may be complex and time-
consuming, they must be addressed to avoid future disputes. In cases where land is transferred
to the community, formal legal transfer may not be required. However, experience shows that
the absence of formal documentation can create significant uncertainty, negatively affecting the
program and community relations.

   d) Responsibilities of the Village Chairperson, Village Executive Officer, and Sub-Project
      Staff

These officials must:
   • Identify the appropriate documentation, including agreements for land transfer and any
       legal records required.
   • Ensure that agreements:
           o Reflect consultations conducted with stakeholders.
           o Clearly outline the terms of the land transfer.

           o  Confirm that the donation was voluntary, without coercion, manipulation, or
              pressure.
          o Include an accurate map showing the land’s boundaries and coordinates.
          o Specify who bears the costs of the transfer (e.g., notarial fees, taxes, and title
              registration).
          o Document any remaining land rights after the transfer.
   •   Ensure that all necessary parties sign the agreement, including obtaining consent from
       spouses and children who are of a specified age.
   •   Ensure that land transfer and title registration are completed, where applicable.
   •   Properly record and register any remaining land after excision of the donated portion.

Since the project is primarily implemented in rural areas where most land is under customary
ownership, proper land registration may not always exist.


   e) Record-Keeping: Maintaining comprehensive records of the voluntary land donation

       •   Public notifications specifying the location, size, and intended use of the land in

       •   Records of consultations held, including key discussions.
       •   Due diligence reports verifying land ownership and use.
       •   Formal statements of donation, signed by each owner or user, confirming informed

       •   Legal transfer documents, registrations, or records evidencing the land transfer.

5.7.1.3. Program Grievance Redress Mechanism for Sustainable Rural Water Supply and
       Sanitation Program Phase Two

In 2022, the MoW established a Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) to manage grievances that
may arise during the implementation of the Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation
Program (SRWSSP). The primary objectives of the GRM include receiving, evaluating, and
resolving project-related grievances from affected communities at all levels. It also provides
stakeholders with a platform to submit questions, comments, suggestions, or complaints. This
GRM will also be applied to the SRWSSP2, and the program will further strengthen it through
increased sensitization and training of implementing agencies on the GRM.
The GRM operates through a four-tier structure, beginning at the village level and progressing
through the ward, district, regional, and RUWASA headquarters levels. This structure allows
individuals to escalate their grievances to a higher level if they are not satisfied with the decision
made at a lower level. Additionally, the mechanism includes provisions for capacity building,
monitoring and reporting, and budget allocation to ensure its effectiveness.


                                                 34
The GRM is fully functional and has been operationalized at the project level, with a well-
established structure extending to the district and village levels, including Community-Based
Water Organizations (CBWOs). Grievance committees are in place at all levels, and their
members are well-versed in the mechanism. Furthermore, all reported grievances are
systematically documented in registers at each level to ensure transparency and accountability.
The GRM also has guidelines for managing GBV/SEA cases. Committees for grievance handling
have been established in some Regions, and as such, complaints are handled at various stages
depending on their nature and complexity. In March 2024, RUWASA and MoW representatives
attended a World Bank training on GRM to enhance their capacity in GRM/Gender Based
Violence/Sexual Harassment, as well as related project aspects.

Weaknesses
  • Other institutions implementing the SRWSSP parent program and subsequent additional
     financing, such as health centres and schools, lack Grievance registers.
  • There is no systematic feedback provided to the community on resolved grievances.
  • Lack of suggestion boxes.
  • The reporting system is conducted through letters and meetings.

environmental and social management procedures and tools. This was the second training of its
kind, following an initial session held in July 2020, which was specifically targeting Regional
Managers. Further capacity enhancement is planned under SRWSSP2 as further indicated in
Chapter 6. This will include targeted training in contract management, health and safety and E&S
aspects of operation and maintenance.

5.7.4. Exclusion Criteria
The SRWSSP2 will respect the exclusionary provision under the Policy and exclude activities that
are “judged to be likely to have significant adverse impacts that are sensitive, diverse, or
unprecedented on the environment and/or affected people are not eligible for financing and are
excluded from the Program.” More specifically, SRWSSP2 financing should not be used to support
programs, or activities with the following features:
    (a) Have significant impacts on Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically
        Underserved Traditional Local Communities’ cultural heritage that is material to the
        identity and/or cultural, ceremonial, or spiritual aspects of the affected communities.
    (b) Major water dams which cause significant conversion or degradation of critical natural
        habitats or cultural heritage sites.
    a) Workplace conditions that expose workers to significant health and personal safety risks.
    b) Land acquisition and/or resettlement of a scale or nature that will have significant adverse
        impacts on affected people or the use of forced evictions.
    c) Large-scale changes in land use or access to land and/or natural resources.
    d) Adverse E&S impacts covering large geographical areas, including transboundary impacts,
        or global impacts such as greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions.
    e) Activities in which forced or child labour is engaged.

6. OPERATIONAL PERFORMANCE AND INSTITUTIONAL CAPACITY IN MANAGING
   ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL IMPACT

This section provides a summary of Tanzania’s Environmental and Social (E&S) Systems and
institutional capacity assessment based on the World Bank’s Program-for-Results (PforR) core
principles

 6.1. Performance Overview

This section assesses the alignment of the relevant national E&S regulatory framework (systems)
with the core principles and key planning elements outlined in the Bank’s policy on PforR. It also
examines the capacity of the implementing agencies and identifies areas where gaps exist
between Bank policy requirements and the country's systems. The analysis is based on an
assessment of the environmental and social effects (benefits, impacts, and risks) of the Program,
as well as consultations and discussions with stakeholders.

The SRWSSP2 ESSA concludes that Tanzania’s regulatory framework for Environmental and
Social (E&S) management is largely aligned with the World Bank’s PforR Policy and Directive,
particularly in terms of key principles and critical elements. The existing legal framework provides
a strong foundation for addressing environmental, health, safety, and social concerns that may
arise under the proposed SRWSSP2. Furthermore, regulatory authorities and legal provisions are
in place to oversee environmental and social assessments, occupational health and safety (OHS)
risks, and to protect citizens from gender-based violence (GBV) and sexual exploitation and abuse
(SEA).

For managing WASH (Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene) services in schools and health facilities,
Tanzania has developed national guidelines, including toolkits for school water, sanitation, and
hygiene, as well as a national strategic plan for School WASH (SWASH), which all apply to this
project.

The country has made significant progress in establishing laws and regulations for the water
sector. Currently, there are about five key water legislations governing water resource
management, supply, and sanitation, supported by 21 water regulations that address various
issues such as water funds, tariff setting, clustering of water supply and sanitation authorities,
sewerage service management, and community-based water supply organizations. Additional
regulations cover dam safety, groundwater management, storm water control, water use
transfers, discharge permits, and water abstraction.

Given that SRWSSP parent program encompassed both PforR and IPF components, the
preparation of additional instruments became imperative, include FPF, voluntary land donation
protocols, GRM, LMP, and SEP. These instruments were specifically developed to address the
distinct requirements of the IPF component, thereby ensuring comprehensive E&S coverage and

                                                40
facilitating the effective implementation and monitoring of safeguards across both financing
modalities. These instruments have been operationalized and will be further strengthened and
promoted during the implementation of SRWSSP2.

In general, the performance of SRWSSP1 has been either satisfactory or moderately satisfactory.
Positive progress has been made in key areas, including the preparation of Environmental and
Social Impact Assessments (ESIAs) prior to the commencement of civil works in most cases, land
acquisition through land donation, the inclusion of vulnerable groups in program benefits,
grievance management, and the protection of local communities from GBV incidents.

Despite the specified achievements, several Environmental and Social Safeguard (ESS) challenges
remain. These include:

   •   Lack of Dedicated Funding for E&S supervision, monitoring, and support.
   •   Limited Reporting on E&S Issues, as most progress reports fail to include environmental,
       social, health, and safety matters.
   •   Inadequate Stakeholder Engagement Records, with poor documentation of events and
       outcomes.
   •   Land Acquisition Challenges, where the Ministry of Water (MoW), RUWASA, and CBWSO
       have struggled to secure land for constructed water assets, posing risks to sustainability.
   •   Design and Construction Deficiencies: Poor adherence to design standards for Health
       Care Facilities (HCFs), including substandard fixtures, improper installation of disability
       support rails, and inadequate sewage systems. Strengthened supervision and adherence
       to standard designs are recommended.
   •   Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) Compliance: During construction works, the OHS
       supervision needs to be maintained, and the safety culture improved. Many completed
       projects require proper OHS supervision during operation and maintenance, but often
       lack essential safety facilities, such as fire extinguishers.
   •   Solid Waste Management: Poor waste management practices and improper operation of
       incinerators, with inadequate sorting and separation of waste.
   •   Regulatory Compliance: Some District Managers (DMs) are hesitant to fully comply with
       environmental and social regulations. Staffing shortages at RUWASA further hinder
       effective project monitoring.
   •   Poor siting of facilities: has led to contamination from fecal sludge and destruction due
       to floods.
   •   Weak Institutional Collaboration: Limited coordination with NEMC and the Vice President’s
       Office (VPO).

To ensure the success of SRWSSP2, these challenges must be addressed through improved
funding, stakeholder engagement and consultation, compliance with standards, and
strengthened institutional coordination. In addition, strict adherence to design standards for
HCF, enhanced supervision, and improved training and accountability for District Engineers is key.

The current capacity of the Environmental and Social (E&S) systems relevant to the Program have
been evaluated, focusing on national regulatory bodies such as the National Environment
Management Council (NEMC), the Occupational Safety and Health Authority (OSHA), Local
Government Authorities (LGAs), as well as the Program's implementing agencies. The primary

below:
    • Strengths of the system, or where it functions effectively and efficiently and in accordance

    • Inconsistencies and gaps (“weaknesses”) between the principles of the PforR Policy and

    • Actions (“opportunities”) required to strengthen the existing system within the

    • Threats (Risks) that are likely to affect program implementation and performance.


  6.2. Systems Assessment by Core Principles

Core Principle 1: Program E&S Management Systems are designed to (a) promote E&S
sustainability in the Program design; (b) avoid, minimize, or mitigate adverse impacts; and (c)
promote informed decision-making related to the Program’s E&S effects.
Summary of Findings: Core Principal No. 1 is applicable because the SRWSSP2 will involve the
construction of different utilities including water supply networks in villages, WASH facilities for
households, schools, and health facilities and fecal sludge treatment systems. Tanzania possesses
a comprehensive national regulatory framework, along with technical guidelines, to conduct
Environmental and Social (E&S) due diligence, particularly concerning the construction and
refurbishment of health facilities. However, implementation and enforcement of the regulations
has not been up to standards and the assessed weaknesses are systemic, related to insufficient
resources – both financial and manpower - for overseeing and monitoring and reporting of
implementation of environmental and social measures, enforcement weak coordination and
inadequate attention to environmental and social concerns. Therefore, the MoW, MoEST, MoH
and RUWASA must strengthen their capacity to effectively integrate and manage E&S aspects
within their operations. A key focus should be on allocating dedicated funding for E&S
supervision; improving stakeholder engagement documentation; strengthening adherence to
HCF design standards and OSHA regulations; Enhance supervision, training, and accountability of
District Engineers; and Improve collaboration with regulatory bodies (NEMC/VPO).

System Strengths
   • The Ministry of Water (MoW), Ministry of Health (MoH), Ministry of Education, Science,
      and Technology (MoEST) and RUWASA all recognize the importance of environmental
      sustainability and the need for Program interventions to enhance access to water,
      sanitation, reduce pollution, improve quality of life, and strengthen institutions.


                                                42
   •   ESIA regulations are in place, and the National Environment Management Council (NEMC)
       is currently represented in 13 zones to support stakeholders with environmental and
       social assessments, as well as enforcement.
   •   Implementing Agencies have participated in the implementation of SRWSSP1, and the
       general performance was between satisfactory and moderately satisfactory.
   •   Supervision reports and field visits have confirmed that mitigation measures for potential
       impacts in water sector projects have been reasonably successful in their
       implementation.
   •   The Environmental Management Act of 2004 provides for the establishment of
       Environmental Committees at the community level. Collaboration between CBWSOs and
       existing Community Environmental Committees will ensure the integration of
       environmental and social management in the SRWS implementation.

Weaknesses/Gap
  • Most of SRWSSP2 investments fall under the B2 category of the Environmental
     Management Regulations of 2018, which makes preparing and environmental and social
     assessment and management instrument non mandatory.
  • Inadequate dedicated funding, human resources and equipment for E&S supervision
     impacts the effectiveness of E&S supervision and monitoring.
  • Oversight on environmental and social management is still weak, primarily due to:
         o Weak and insufficient institutional and technical capacity;
         o Inadequate resources, including human resources, technology and equipment;
         o Inadequate training, monitoring and enforcement; and
         o Weak interinstitutional and coordination between the various related agencies.
  • Desings and specs of some WASH facilities and incinerators do not always follow the
     proper specifications, especially for ensuring adherence to emission standards (for
     incinerators), resilience to climate change and considering the needs of disabled persons.

Opportunities and Actions
   • SRWSSP2 will benefit from guidelines developed by SRWSSP1, such as the GAP, GRM,
      Land Donation Protocols, LMP, and SEP, which will be utilized in SRWSSP2.
   • Implementing Agencies (MoW, MoH, RUWASA, and MoEST) have established
      Environmental and Social Management teams, received training in Environmental and
      Social Standards (ESS), and undergone capacity building. They have also operationalized
      the ESS instruments developed by SRWSSP1.
   • Several institutions have participated in the implementation of SRWSSP1, conducting and
      preparing Environmental and Social Impact Assessments (ESIA).
   • The SRWSSP2 will adopt the specified instruments and continue to capacitate safeguard
      personnel to operationalize them.
   • The Program, through DLIs 5 and 6 provides opportunity to build the capacity of CBWSOs
      and District Councils in O&M of water supply assets, and M&E respectively.

  • Weak health and safety management of constructions contracts and operational manuals

6. STAKEHOLDERS CONSULTATION


  6.1. Consultations during ESSA preparation

To align with the Program-for-Results policy, guidelines, and the World Bank’s Access to
Information policy, the World Bank Environmental and Social (E&S) Team organized a multi-
stakeholder consultation meeting in Dodoma on July 14, 2025, Mwanza on July 16, 2025 Mbeya
on July 18, 2025. and attended by approximately 81 participants in total. During this engagement,
extensive consultations were conducted, and the Environmental and Social Systems Assessment
(ESSA) Report was disclosed to facilitate the finalization and refinement of the Program Action
Plan. Participants were also invited to validate the ESSA findings and provide insights on ensuring
the program’s implementation does not negatively impact on the environment or communities.

The multi-stakeholder consultation brought together representatives from central and local
government agencies, water user associations from the targeted regions and districts, and
regulatory bodies. Participants included RUWASA Regions, Implementing Partner Units (IPUs)
from the MoW, PORALG, the MoH, MoEST and the Water Basin Water Boards. Other key
stakeholders included development water partners, international and local NGOs involved in
water and WASH programs, as well as academicians from water-related institutions.

Overall, participants expressed strong support for the proposed program, emphasizing its
potential to enhance water and sanitation services in rural areas while strengthening
environmental and social management in the sector. Key issues raised during the consultations
will be summarized in Table 4, with a list of participants provided in Annex I..

ANNEX III: Project Screening Criteria

Project Screening Criteria made under SECOND SCHEDULE of the Environmental Management
(Environmental Impact Assessment and Audit) (Amendment) Regulations GN. No. 474 (contd.)

1. The project will not substantially use natural resources in a way that pre-empts the use
or potential use of that resource for any other purpose.
2. Potential residual impacts on the environment are likely to be minor, of little
significance and easily mitigated.
3. The type of project, its environmental impacts and measures for managing them are
well understood in Tanzania.
4. Reliable means exist for ensuring that impact management measures can and will be
adequately planned and implemented.
5. The project will not displace significant numbers of people, families or communities.
6. The project is not located in, and will not affect, any environmentally sensitive areas
Such as:
        (a) national parks;
        (b) wetlands;
        (c) productive agricultural land;
        (d) important archaeological, historical and cultural sites;
        (e) areas protected under any law;
        (f) areas containing rare or endangered flora or fauna;
        (g) areas containing unique or outstanding scenery.
        (h) mountains or developments on or near steep hill-slopes.
        (i) dry tropical forests, for instance brachystegia woodlands;
        (j) development near lakes or its beaches.
        (k) development providing important resources for vulnerable groups such as
        fishing communities along the lake-shore.
        (l) development near high population concentrations or industrial activities where
        further development could create significant environmental problems; and
        (m) prime ground-water re-charge areas or areas of importance for surface run off of
        water.
7. The project type shall not result in:
        (a) policy initiatives which may affect the environment such as changes in
        agricultural pricing subsidies or the tobacco liberation;
        (b) major changes in land tenure; or
        (c) changes in water use though irrigation, drainage promotion or dams, changes in
        fishing practices.
        8. The project shall not cause:
        (a) adverse socio economic impact;
        (b) land degradation;
        (c) water pollution;
        (d) air pollution;
        (e) damage to wildlife and habitat;

                                              65
        (f) adverse impact on climate and hydrological cycle; and
        (g) creation of by-products, residual or waste materials which require handling and
        disposal in a manner that is not regulated by existing authorities.
9. The project shall not cause significant public concern because of potential
environmental changes. The following are guiding principles:
        (a) is the impact positive, mainly begin or harmful;
        (b) what is the scale of the impact in terms of area affected numbers of people or
        wildlife;
        (c) what is the intensity of the impact;
        (d) what will be the duration of the impact;
        (e) will there be cumulative effects from the impact;
        (f) are the effects politically controversial;
        (g) have the main economic, ecological and social costs been quantified;
        (h) will the impact vary by social group or gender; and
        (i) is there any international impact due to the proposal projects.
10. The project shall not necessitate further development which is likely to have a
significant impact on the environment.

HIVYO BASI pande zote mbili zinashuhudia na kukubaliana kama
ifuatavyo:- KWAMBA Mtoa Ardhi kwa hiari yake mwenyewe,anaridhia
kutoa eneo/shamba/kiwanja tajwa katika Mkataba huu kwa ajili ya
kuwezesha utekelezaji wa Mradi huo.

 1. KWAMBA Mpokea Ardhi atatumia eneo analopewa kwa ajili ya
    Ujenzi wa Miundombunu ya Maji na si kwa matumizi mengineyo.

 2. KWAMBA Mtoaji Ardhi hatoruhusiwa kufanya shughuli yoyote ya
    kudumu ambayo itahatarisha uwepo wa miundombiu ya mradi.
 3. KWAMBA Mtoa Ardhi ambaye eneo lake lina miundombinu ya
    kudumu ambayo itaharibiwa kwa namna moja ama nyingine na
    utekelezaji wa mradi atalipwa fidia kwa utaratibu utakaoelekezwa
    kutokana na uthamini uliofanyika kwa mujibu wa sheria.

 4. KWAMBA Mtoaji Ardhi anahakikisha kuwa ardhi aliyotoa kwa ajili
    ya Mradi ni mali yake mwenyewe na hakuna mtu mwingine
    mwenye umiliki na eneo hilo na endapo atatokea mwingine kudai
    umiliki wa eneo hilo na ikathibitika kuwa ndiye mmiliki halali wa
    eneo hilo, basi Mtoa Ardhi atakuwa na jukumu la kulipa fidia kwa
