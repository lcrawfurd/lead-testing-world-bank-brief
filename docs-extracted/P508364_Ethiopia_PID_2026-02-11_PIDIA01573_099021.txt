                              B. Introduction and Context

                              Country Context
                          1.      Ethiopia is one of the fastest-growing economies in the world, yet it remains one of the poorest. As the second
                          most populous nation in Africa, Ethiopia is home to about 132 million people.1 From 2004 to 2019, the country achieved
                          remarkable economic growth with an average growth rate of nearly 10 percent, one of the highest globally. However, this
                          rapid expansion has masked structural weaknesses, including high inflation, significant external debt, limited private
                          sector participation, while conflicts, insecurity, and recurrent droughts have further constrained growth and resilience. As

                          Poverty rose from 27 percent in 2015 to 32 percent in 2021, returning to 2010 levels. 3
                          2.       Food insecurity is a persistent challenge in Ethiopia, largely driven by climate change-exacerbated extreme
                          events and heavy reliance on rain-fed agriculture. Ethiopia is among the most food-insecure countries. Over 18 percent
                          of the population faces severe food insecurity,4 and nearly 40 percent of children under five are stunted.5 Ethiopia is one
                          of the most vulnerable countries to climate variability6 in part due to its high dependence on rain-fed agriculture.7 More
                          than 90 percent of cultivated lands are rain-fed, making food production highly vulnerable to recurrent droughts and
                          floods. 8 Ethiopia is one of the most drought-prone countries in the world. 9 Over the past century, Ethiopia has
                          experienced 19 periods of severe food shortages due to droughts.10
                          3.       Agriculture remains the backbone of Ethiopia’s economy and jobs, but the productivity is low due to high
                          reliance on rain-fed agriculture. The agricultural sector accounts for 33 percent of GDP, 73 percent of employment, and
                          77 percent of exports.11 Despite its significant role, productivity remains very low, particularly among female farmers who
                          make up over 40 percent of the agricultural labor force. Erratic and increasingly unpredictable rainfall due to climate
                          change led to inconsistent and often insufficient crop yields. Recognizing this, the Government of Ethiopia (GoE) has set
                          ambitious goals to reduce reliance on rain-fed agriculture by developing irrigation capacity. The Ten-Year Development
                          Plan of Ethiopia (2020-2030)12 aims to expand irrigation, promote private sector participation, enhance climate resilience,
                          and create over 1 million job opportunities in the irrigation sector.

                              Sectoral and Institutional Context
                          4.       Irrigation is essential for food security and economic development in Ethiopia. It provides reliable and drought
                          resilient water supply to farms, reducing dependence on erratic rainfall and enabling more stable crop production. 13
                          Irrigation also allows farmers to cultivate multiple cropping seasons and a wider variety of crops, including high-value and
                          drought-resistant species, thereby diversifying diets and income sources and strengthening resilience to market and
                          climate shocks.14 Irrigation is also a key enabler of private sector engagement, enabling agribusinesses to participate
                          through offtake markets and value-chain linkages.15 Under the Ten-Year Development Plan (2020–2030), Ethiopia aims to
                          increase crop production through irrigation from 0.8 million to 3.8 million tons.
                          5.       Irrigation is critical for more and better jobs in Ethiopia. Over 2 million youth enter the labor market every year,
                          presenting both challenges and opportunities for job creation. Irrigation can raise productivity, promote agribusiness
                          growth, and support a shift toward market-oriented production. It is estimated that irrigated agriculture generates 1.5–2
                          direct jobs per hectare annually, with each direct job supporting 1.1–1.8 additional jobs in input supply, equipment
                          services, logistics, and food processing. Much of this growth is driven by the private sector, from small and medium-sized
                          enterprises (SMEs) to large agribusinesses. Under the Ten-Year Development Plan (2020–2030), the GoE aims to create

                          6.       Rehabilitation and modernization of existing irrigation schemes offer opportunities to boost irrigation capacity
                          in Ethiopia. While irrigation coverage remains low, most existing schemes perform poorly due to weak design and
                          construction and are more vulnerable to floods and droughts, the absence of quality management systems, limited
                          institutional and community capacity, and insufficient operation and maintenance (O&M). Underperformance is observed
                          across all types of schemes (small, medium, and large schemes), and across different governance levels (community-
                          managed or co-managed public schemes). Under the Climate Resilient Irrigation for Sustainable Production (CRISP)
                          Project, the GoE and development partners have prioritized the rehabilitation and modernization of existing schemes—
                          addressing the root causes of their decline and strengthening sector capacity—before expanding to new investments.
                          7.       Dam safety is key to irrigation reliability and climate resilience in Ethiopia. The country has over 5,000 existing
                          dams, including more than 100 large dams. Ethiopia also plans to construct around 200 new dams, mostly for irrigation,
                          with about 100 under development. Many dams lack formal O&M procedures, emergency preparedness plans (EPPs), and
                          functioning monitoring systems. The Bank has recently supported the GoE in updating the Ethiopia Dam Safety Guidelines
                          and establishing a Dam Safety Unit under The Ministry of Water and Energy (MoWE), 16 laying the technical and
                          institutional foundation for improving dam safety management.

                             D. Project Description
                          12.      CRISP is the first country program under the Africa Resilient Irrigation for Sustainable Economies Multi-Phase




_For Official Use Only_
                          Programmatic Approach (Africa RISE MPA) (P514153). Africa RISE is an MPA program under which multiple operations
                          will be carried out under a common operational framework designed for the eastern and southern Africa region. Africa
                          RISE MPA has five pillars: i) Pillar 1: Accelerating Farmer-Led Irrigation Development (FLID); ii) Pillar 2: Driving the
                          Performance and Expansion of Public and Community-Based Irrigation; iii) Pillar 3: Advancing Water Security for Resilient
                          Irrigation; iv) Pillar 4: Developing Agrifood Value Chains; and v) Pillar 5: Strengthening Sector Governance, Coordination,
                          and Capacity. CRISP will contribute to the overall targets of Africa RISE MPA and is aligned with Pillar 2, 3, 4 and 5.
                          13.      CRISP is a nationwide project covering all 13 regions of Ethiopia and is aligned with the country’s National
                          Irrigation Program (NIP). Irrigation development in Ethiopia has so far followed a fragmented, infrastructure-focused, and
                          project-based approach. Since 2022, the Bank has worked closely with the MILLs and supported a Rapid Irrigation
                          Diagnostic. The key recommendation from the Diagnostic is to develop a National Irrigation Program (NIP) to guide
                          integrated irrigation development over the medium to long term, along with an associated 5-year Investment Plan to
                          prioritize quick-win investments for the short-term.

                                (1) Component 1: Irrigation Rehabilitation and Modernization. Component 1 supports the rehabilitation and

                                    Agrifood Value Chains” under the Africa Resilient Irrigation for a Sustainable Economy (RISE) Multi-Phase
                                    Approach (MPA).
                                    1.1 Large- and Medium-Scale Irrigation Rehabilitation and Modernization. CRISP will focus on improving the
                                        performance of existing large- and medium-scale irrigation schemes. Three schemes—Gode, Gidabo, and
                                        Ada’a Becho have been prioritized for this work. Key activities include: (a) rehabilitation of headworks and
                                        main, secondary, and tertiary canals, pumping stations, grids, regulating/control and safety structures,
                                        strengthening of flood protection structures, and other critical equipment; (b) modernization of existing
                                        irrigation schemes with appropriate advanced technologies (drip, sprinkler, and center pivot), irrigation
                                        energy improvements, and water measurement facilities; (c) climate risk assessment to guide infrastructure
                                        improvements, improve water use efficiency, and integrate climate-resilient practices; and (d) support the
                                        preparation of pipeline projects to roll out investments under the Investment Plan of NIP.

                                        infrastructure interventions with O&M, performance improvement activities, and value chain development.

                                        formation and operation, introduce irrigation service fee systems, and develop Asset Management Plans; (b)
                                        pilot irrigation service fee across the three prioritized schemes; (c) pilot innovative O&M models to enhance
                                        efficiency and sustainability; (d) salinity management pilots to improve soil and water management, and
                                        promote salinity-resilient crop types; (e) strengthen agricultural production, market, and value chains linked
                                        to project irrigation investments; and (f) enhance the capacity of female farmers and promote inclusive
                                        participation in irrigation and agricultural production.
                                    1.3 Dam Safety and Water Security. Strengthen coordination between irrigation and water sectors to ensure
                                        water security and flood and drought resilience. Key activities include: (a) support dam safety assessments,

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts


                          The Environmental Risk rating is rated as High. This rating is based on the project’s focus on rehabilitation and
                          modernization of existing large- and medium-scale irrigation schemes, development of small-scale irrigation (SSI), dam
                          safety and irrigation storage improvement, and the potential environmental impacts associated with these activities in
                          the Ethiopian context. Key potential environmental risks include those related to water resource management, ecosystem




_For Official Use Only_
                          impacts, pollution, and dam safety. The rehabilitation and modernization of irrigation schemes (Component 1 & 2) could
                          lead to altered hydrological regimes, increase water stress, affecting downstream water availability and potentially
                          impacting aquatic ecosystems. Increased water extraction for irrigation purposes may exacerbate water scarcity issues,
                          particularly in already water-stressed regions. Further construction of new SSIs carries Labor and OHS related risks, risks
                          of habitat loss, soil erosion, and sedimentation of water bodies if not properly managed. Furthermore, the use of
                          agrochemicals (fertilizers, pesticides) associated with intensified irrigated agriculture could result in water and soil
                          pollution, affecting both human and ecological health. In addition, the Project might contribute to impacts associated with
                          salinity, dam safety and pose risks to local ecosystems and water resources if activities involving large dam involved
                          rehabilitation, and small dams’ construction if not carefully planned and implemented. The planned TAs, such as
                          preparation of pipeline investment projects and development of a national irrigation program, may result in downstream
                          EHS risks and impacts. The project area may include areas of ecological value or sensitivity, requiring careful consideration
                          of biodiversity impacts. Mitigation measures are generally well-understood, but their effective implementation will be
                          critical to minimizing environmental risks. The Project Social risk rating is Substantial. The social risk is driven by: (i)
                          potential land acquisition and restrictions on land use for canal rehabilitation, storages, and ancillary works that could
                          lead to temporary or permanent economic displacement (loss of crops/assets, access disruptions) and grievances if
                          procedures are inconsistently applied across regions (ESS5); (ii) risks of exclusion and elite capture, with women, youth,
                          persons with disabilities, IDPs, and Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities at
                          heightened risk of being left out of benefits or decision-making, including possible inequities in access to water permits or
                          irrigation service fees (iii) (iii) increased abstraction can reduce water availability for downstream users, pastoralists, and
                          ecosystems, potentially leading to disputes. iv)labor and working conditions risks for direct and contracted workers,
                          including OHS, fair terms, and access to a worker GRM, with uneven contractor compliance in dispersed/rural settings (v)
                          SEA/SH risks linked to labor presence and remote sites, requiring survivor-centered GM pathways and contractor Codes
                          of Conduct; (vi) community health and safety risks from construction interfaces and traffic near settlements, as well as
                          social tension around works scheduling that may interrupt irrigation during critical periods and (vii) security and access
                          constraints in FCV-affected areas that can impede consultations, supervision, timely incident reporting, and service
                          continuity, thereby elevating residual risk. Further, the SEA/SH risk is assessed as Substantial based on a combination of

                           E. Implementation

                           Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
                          15.      At the federal level, the MILLs will serve as the lead implementing agency for CRISP. MILLs will host the Federal




_For Official Use Only_
                          Project Implementation Unit (FPIU). The FPIU will be responsible for day-to-day coordination, oversight of implementation,
                          and consolidation of project reporting and monitoring and evaluation (M&E). The Ministry of Agriculture (MoA) and MoWE
                          will designate dedicated focal persons, rather than establish separate PIUs. The FPIU will work closely with focal persons
                          from the MoA and MoWE to implement activities within their respective mandates. A Federal Steering Committee (FSC),
                          to be established during project preparation and maintained throughout implementation, will provide strategic guidance
                          and ensure effective multi-ministry and federal–regional coordination.
                          16.     At the regional level, CRISP will adopt a hybrid implementation approach, leveraging the existing capacity of
                          Bank-financed projects while establishing dedicated structures where needed. Regional Project Implementation Units
                          (RPIUs) will be established under the Regional Bureaus of Irrigation to manage project activities, particularly under
                          Component 2. Mirroring the federal structure, Regional Steering Committees will be formed in each region to coordinate
                          across the irrigation, agriculture, and water sectors.
