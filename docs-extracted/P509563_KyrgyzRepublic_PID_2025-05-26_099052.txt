                         The Kyrgyz Republic, a lower-middle-income country, has experienced strong but volatile growth, averaging 4.5
                         percent Gross Domestic Product (GDP) over the past two decades. This has led to a significant poverty reduction,
                         declining from 37 percent in 2013 to 20 percent in 2019. The trend was reversed by the COVID-19 pandemic, political
                         instability, and global food and fuel price shocks, but growth rebounded in 2022 to 9 percent, driven by construction
                         boom, gold production, agriculture, and services sector. The country’s growth forecast is less optimistic, with 6.5
                         percent in 2025. The Kyrgyz Republic has pursued market-oriented reforms but remains vulnerable to external shocks
                         due to its reliance on a narrow range of exports, fluctuating commodity prices, and remittances from labor migrants.
                         While the gap between urban and rural has narrowed, poverty remains largely concentrated in the rural areas of the
                         country where 65 percent of 7.3 million Kyrgyz reside. 33 percent of the population still lives below the poverty line
                         (National Statistical Committee of the Kyrgyz Republic, 2023). Rural areas are disproportionately affected, with typically
                         less access to basic services, employment opportunities, and infrastructure development. Social protection
                         mechanisms, while in place, require further strengthening to provide adequate support to vulnerable populations.
                         Gender disparities exist, in particular in labor market participation, where the female labor force rate stands at 53
                         percent, compared to 79 percent for men (World Bank, 2023).
                         Climate change is one of the most critical risks to the country’s key economic sectors—agriculture, energy, and
                         tourism—all of which depend on the availability and reliability of water. Irrigated agriculture contributes 13–15




Public Disclosure copy
                         percent of GDP and employs a quarter of the population, while hydropower provides 90 percent of electricity. Tourism
                         relies on healthy aquatic ecosystems, including Lake Issyk-Kul, which drew over 8 million visitors in 2019. Yet, the
                         economy faces a variety of natural and climate-related hazards, such as droughts, floods, and mudslides. Between 1991
                         and 2011, water-related losses averaged US$14 million in agriculture and US$73 million from floods annually.
                         Temperatures could rise by 5.6°C by the 2090s, with drought risk more than doubling (World Bank, 2021). Climate
                         change disproportionately impacts the poor and raises public health risks from unsafe water and poor sanitation.
                         The National Development Strategy of the Kyrgyz Republic 2018-2040 (NDS 2040) aspires to a strong, self-sufficient,
                         and developed upper-middle-income country by 2040. The medium-term National Development Program until 2026
                         lays the foundation for successfully achieving the NDS 2040 by supporting the labor-intensive sectors of the economy—
                         agriculture, light industry, and tourism. The government’s priority for improving rural quality of life is to provide every
                         settlement with access to clean, safe drinking water through sustainable, affordable, and economically viable services.

                         Sectoral and Institutional Context
                         Despite progress on expanding access to water supply, sanitation services lag significantly behind. In 2022, 94 percent
                         of households in the country were connected to piped water supply but only 16 percent to centralized sewerage
                         systems. The majority relied on on-site facilities—primarily pit latrines (84 percent) and a small share on septic tanks
                         (less than 1 percent) (WHO/UNICEF, 2022). Centralized sewerage systems and wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs)
                         are concentrated in urbanized areas such as Bishkek, Osh, and Chuy region. Countrywide, only 30 percent of the 350
                         WWTPs meet sanitary standards, and 40 percent are non-functional (Karimov, 2024). Most existing sewerage systems
                         are outdated and require rehabilitation due to prolonged underinvestment and neglected maintenance.
                         In rural regions, WWTPs and sewerage systems are largely absent, and households predominantly rely on on-site
                         sanitation solutions. The majority of rural households (97 percent) have pit latrines–simple excavated pits used for
                         excreta disposal, often without proper lining or ventilation and inadequately maintained. Less than 1 percent have
                         septic tanks–underground tanks that collect and partially treat sewage through sedimentation and anaerobic digestion.
                         These systems are typically managed by individual households, many of whom lack the financial resources and technical
                         knowledge to construct and safely operate and maintain their facility. Few residential buildings and social institutions,
                         such as schools and health-care facilities, are connected to sewage systems. Open defecation is practiced by around 2
                         percent of the rural population (WHO/UNICEF, 2022).

                         Fecal sludge management (FSM)–including collection, transport, treatment and disposal–is not developed. Only 4
                         percent of households remove excreta from on-site sanitation facilities for off-site treatment, without data whether it
                         is safely treated (WHO/UNICEF, 2022). Municipalities often lack the technical and financial capacity for FSM, and in the
                         absence of treatment facilities, sludge is frequently dumped illegally. Where services exist, private or municipal tankers
                         often dispose sludge at non-functional WWTPs or open sites, leaving most untreated. High service costs, especially for
                         low-income households, lead to unsafe practices such as dumping into drains or river, or abandoning full pits, causing
                         widespread contamination of soil, surface and groundwater. This poses a major risk, particularly in rural areas where
                         80 percent of households rely on groundwater for drinking.
                         Inadequate sanitation undermines public health and costs the country an estimated US$120 million annually in
                         healthcare, lost productivity, and premature deaths (World Bank, 2019). Poor water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH)
                         is linked to waterborne diseases and high child mortality, with 4,200 cases per 100,000 children under five—well above
                         regional averages (WHO, 2019). Rural schools are particularly affected: in 2021, 25 percent of schools lacked access to
                         safely managed water and 75 percent relied on unsanitary outdoor latrines—often without doors, ventilation, or
                         handwashing facilities—and inaccessible in winter. The lack of gender-segregated toilets and menstrual hygiene
                         support leads to absenteeism, with 15 percent of adolescent girls missing school during menstruation (UNICEF, 2024).
                         Local governments are responsible for maintenance but often lack funds. Poor households—especially female-headed




Public Disclosure copy
                         and those in underserved areas—are hardest hit. Women, typically tasked with WASH, face time burdens and care
                         responsibilities due to waterborne illnesses, reinforcing cycles of poverty, poor health, and human capital decline.
                         The lack of adequate wastewater treatment is also an increasing environmental threat, particularly for the country’s
                         natural jewel, Lake Issyk-Kul. As the world’s second largest salt lake and a designated Ramsar site, Issyk-Kul is globally
                         recognized for its biodiversity and holds significant ecological and economic value for the region. However, rapid
                         tourism and resort development along its shores are straining its fragile ecosystem. As an oligotrophic (nutrient-poor)
                         and endorheic (closed-basin) lake, it is particularly sensitive to nutrient and contaminant inflows, including untreated
                         wastewater from expanding human activities, such as tourism. Climate change worsens the impact–floods can cause
                         latrines to overflow and spread fecal matter, while droughts limit water availability for flushing and maintenance,
                         compounding sanitation challenges.
                         Safely managed sanitation in lakeshore areas–like Aku District–is critical to preserving the lake's ecosystem,
                         safeguarding public health, and supporting rural livelihoods. Located on the lake’s eastern shore, Aksu is a
                         predominantly rural district. It comprises the administrative center Aksu and 44 villages, with a population of 68,421
                         (49 percent women), 80 percent of whom reside in the rural villages. Despite rich natural resources, Aksu faces
                         significant development challenges including high poverty (962 of 12,834 households), limited job opportunities, and
                         heavy reliance on agriculture, which employs 75 percent of residents (National Statistics Committee, 2024). Agriculture
                         is seasonal, income-instable and yields are constrained by expensive fertilizers and poor market access, which
                         particularly affects poor subsistence households. Vulnerable groups face additional barriers to education, healthcare,
                         and social services. Climate risks further threaten water resources, agriculture and livelihoods.
                         The lack of investments, especially in wastewater and sanitation infrastructure, hinder the district’s
                         development. The district center’s water supply system, built in the 1960’s, is outdated and today serves only 20
                         percent of the population. In the rural villages, only 25 percent of villages have functioning water supply systems, 64
                         percent require rehabilitation, and 11 percent have no water supply at all. Sanitation conditions are even more
                         alarming. The district center’s sewer network connects only 20 percent of the population. The WWTP is non-functional,
                         leading to untreated wastewater being discharged into nearby agricultural land and the Aksu River, which flows into
                         Lake Issyk-Kul. In the rural villages, most residents rely on unsafe pit latrines. Only 5 percent of households who can
                         afford it have indoor toilets connected to septic tanks. Sanitation facilities are also largely inadequate in public
                         institutions. There are no formal or informal FSM service providers, leaving residents without safe waste collection or
                         disposal, and threatening the lake’s ecosystem—and the area’s tourism potential.

                         Over the last decade, the Government of the Kyrgyz Republic (GoKR) has taken steps to strengthen the sector’s
                         legislative framework for sanitation. In 2016, it adopted the Program for Development of Drinking Water Supply and
                         Sanitation Systems (2016–2026), aiming for 90 percent water supply and 70 percent sanitation coverage in rural areas.
                         Water supply investments were estimated at over US$600 million, while sanitation needs remain unquantified. The
                         GoKR revised the Law on Drinking Water and drafted a new law—the first to include sanitation–expected to be
                         approved in 2025. The design norms and standards for water and wastewater infrastructure were updated in 2023,
                         which among others, allow for fecal sludge reuse.
                         Yet, government and donor efforts remain primarily focused on urban wastewater and sewerage infrastructure,
                         neglecting the full FSM service delivery chain, especially in rural areas where non-sewered systems dominate. Rural
                         sanitation receives only about 0.5 percent of the national water supply and sanitation (WSS) budget. Local governments
                         and Municipal Enterprises (MEs) in charge of rural WSS services often struggle to mobilize resources, as funds from the
                         central level and donors are disproportionately allocated to urban wastewater infrastructure. Ongoing donor initiatives
                         in the field of wastewater and sanitation include the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development’s “Kyrgyz
                         Water Resilience Framework” (supporting urban WWTPs and sewerage upgrades), the Asian Development Bank’s
                         “Issyk-Kul Wastewater Management Project” (focused on WWTP upgrades and urban utilities), and “Naryn Rural WSS
                         Program”, with some support for rural on-site sanitation.




Public Disclosure copy
                         In response to these challenges, the State Institution for Drinking Water Supply and Wastewater Disposal (SIDWSWD)
                         under the Ministry of Water Resources, Agriculture and Processing Industry requested support from the World Bank
                         and the Japan Social Development Fund (JSDF) to pilot an innovative FSM model that addresses market,
                         technological, and infrastructure barriers. This Project fills that gap through an integrated approach, introducing a
                         system for the safe collection, transport, and treatment of sewage and fecal sludge. At the heart of the Project is the
                         construction of a combined treatment plant capable of processing both sewage and fecal sludge, ensuring inclusive
                         service across different settlement types. It also promotes resource recovery, by enabling the reuse of treated sludge,
                         thereby advancing environmental sustainability, climate resilience, and circular economy goals. Social inclusion is a core
                         focus, ensuring access to safe, equitable, and climate-resilient sanitation services, in particular for vulnerable groups.
                         The Project area (Aksu district) was selected based on the following criteria: (a) high poverty rate and limited economic
                         opportunities; (b) current state of WSS infrastructure and access gaps; (c) willingness of the local government to pilot
                         FSM and reuse; and (d) synergies with the Water Supply and Sanitation Universal Access Program (WASUAP, P500620)
                         and potential for scale-up under its Phase 2.
                         The JSDF Project builds on ongoing World Bank-financed WSS operations in the Kyrgyz Republic while piloting
                         municipal FSM service delivery and reuse for scale-up under the WASUAP - Phase 2”. The “Sustainable Rural WSS
                         Development Project” (SRWSSDP, P154778) addresses rural on-site sanitation for households and social institutions in
                         Osh, Chui, and Issyk-Kul, while the “Climate Resilient Water Services Project” (CRWSP, P173734) finances WASH
                         improvements in social institutions and conventional WWTPs in Batken and Issyk-Kul regions including TA for
                         developing a FSM plan. The WASUAP (approved in April 2025) is a 10-year multiphase program (2025–2034) aimed at
                         achieving universal access to WSS. Its Phase 1 (2025-2029) will run in parallel with the JSDF Project and will cover,
                         among others, Aksu District. WASUAP Phase 1 will finance the designs for the combined treatment plant and sewer
                         network. Plant construction will be financed by the JSDF grant, with the local government covering the works for sewer
                         extension. Moreover, WASUAP Phase will finance TA for establishing private FSM service providers, which the JSDF
                         grant will complement by focusing on municipal operators.

                         D. Preliminary Description
                         Activities/Components
                         The Project aims to improve livelihoods in Aksu district—particularly for vulnerable groups—by expanding sanitation
                         access, creating jobs, and strengthening climate resilience. With 80 percent of the population lacking adequate
                         sanitation, it will help reduce disease and pollution while generating employment through FSM and reuse-related
                         opportunities. Treated sludge will be promoted as fertilizer for non-food crops, cutting reliance on costly chemical
                         inputs and supporting climate adaptation. Community-led efforts will drive health, economic, and environmental
                         improvements, building a more resilient and empowered Aksu community. The Project includes three components:
                         Component 1: Developing Sanitation and Fecal Sludge Treatment Infrastructure (US$3.79 million). This component
                         will finance works, goods and services for the construction of sanitation infrastructure.
                         Sub-component 1.1: Combined Wastewater and Fecal Sludge Treatment Plant (US$2.48 million). This subcomponent
                         will finance the construction of a combined wastewater and fecal sludge treatment plant in Aksu center, capable of
                         safely processing sewage and fecal sludge from households and social institutions across the district. The treatment
                         plant will be designed to enable sludge reuse and generate secondary by-products for productive use by converting
                         sludge into an organic fertilizer for agriculture for non-consumptive crops and other purposes (e.g., landscaping,
                         reforestation, horticulture), and biogas for on-site use.
                         Sub-component 1.2: On-Site Sanitation Improvements (US$1.31 million). This subcomponent will finance the
                         construction and rehabilitation/retrofitting of on-site sanitation improvements, including latrine and septic tank
                         upgrades (with unit costs ranging from US$1,000-1,900), for around 900 households or 4,530 people) of which 50
                         percent are vulnerable. Project beneficiaries will be jointly selected with the local government during implementation
                         based on established vulnerability/ poverty criteria.
                         Component 2: Establishing FSM Services, Local Job Creation and Community Awareness (US$0.70 million). This
                         component will finance works, goods and services to establish sanitation and FSM services, promote circular economy
                         and raise community awareness of the benefits of FSM and re-use in the Project area.

                         Sub-component 2.1: Establishment of Sanitation and FSM operators and Vocational Training (US$0.60 million). This
                         subcomponent will provide TA in developing a FSM model and support the establishment of dedicated sanitation/FSM
                         units within the four existing MEs in Aksu district which will be responsible for management, operation and
                         maintenance (MOM) of the treatment facility, and of sanitation and FSM services across the district. The Project will
                         target residents, particularly vulnerable, to be employed and trained in managing the entire service delivery chain
                         (collection, transport, treatment and end use/reuse). It will develop standard operating procedures for sludge collection
                         and transportation, and an operations manual for equipment maintenance, hygiene, and worker safety. The Consultant
                         will also define a pricing structure for the FSM model including subsidies for vulnerable households to ensure long-term
                         financial sustainability, including for the MOM of the treatment facility.
                         This subcomponent will also finance a certified vocational training program. A Consultant will develop and deliver a
                         training program to build technical, safety, and entrepreneurial skills in sanitation and FSM for Aksu’s MEs as well as
                         partner institutions (e.g., Sanitary Epidemiological Services (SES) under the Ministry of Health, agricultural research
                         centers) in the Project area–and be open to other residents. The program aims to empower residents, especially
                         vulnerable, to take on technical or leadership roles in the field of sanitation and FSM.
                         Further, it will provide quality assurance and support marketing of the sludge-by products being developed under
                         the Project. Aksu’s ME’s and partner institutions will be provided with the necessary equipment for ongoing monitoring




Public Disclosure copy
                         and quality control of sludge, treatment processes, and sludge by-products to ensure treated sludge meets health,
                         safety, and environmental standards before disposal, conversion into or distribution of generated by-products. In
                         addition, a Consultant will support product certification and branding to validate quality and market credibility and to
                         highlight environmental benefits and boost acceptance among residents. The ME in Aksu center will serve as local hub
                         for storage and distribution. The fertilizers will be distributed at no cost to farmers, particularly vulnerable farmers,
                         under this pilot Project to test its demand and market value. The long-term aim is to promote it as a cost-effective, soil-
                         friendly alternative to chemical fertilizers for scale-up under WASUAP Phase 2.
                         Sub-component 2.2: Community Awareness and Behavior Change Campaign (US$0.10 million). This subcomponent
                         will support a community awareness and behavior change campaign focused on community-level FSM awareness and
                         on the benefits of resource recovery, highlighting the safe use of sludge by-products. Educational efforts will illustrate
                         the importance of proper sludge emptying and transport, and how treated sludge can become valuable compost.
                         Campaigns will also emphasize compost use in enhancing soil for non-consumptive crops, to address safety concerns
                         and boost acceptance. Community events and farmer fairs will showcase sludge by-products and demonstrate their
                         role in supporting sustainable agriculture. This approach ensures that residents are well-informed, engaged, and
                         motivated to support the FSM system and recognize its practical benefits.
                         Component 3: Project Management and Administration, Monitoring and Evaluation, and Knowledge
                         Dissemination (US$0.24 million). This component will finance project management and administration, including
                         incremental operating expenses, Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E), stakeholder engagement and knowledge
                         dissemination.
                         The Project will utilize WASUAP's M&E system and introduce a participatory approach involving community
                         members. Members will receive training for project monitoring. A feedback mechanism via social media accessible to
                         all residents will further ensure transparency and responsiveness. The Project will also conduct baseline, mid-term, and
                         final evaluation.
                         A strong focus will be set on knowledge sharing and dissemination. A launching workshop will be held with local
                         authorities, civil society, and other stakeholders to share the Project’s objectives. During implementation, the PIU
                         Communications Specialist will document and share lessons learned, good practices, and Project impacts. Knowledge
                         will be disseminated through established networks at local, national, and global levels. The Project will also engage
                         international partners, such as Japanese FSM experts, to integrate advanced technologies and global best practices.

                         Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                         The standards relevant to the Project are ESS1, ESS2, ESS3, ESS4, ESS5, and ESS10. The environmental and social risks
                         and impacts are mainly associated with the construction of a wastewater and fecal sludge treatment plant and the
                         processing of waste from septic tanks and pit latrines from social institutions and households. These include
                         occupational health and safety (OHS), pollution, traffic, water, odor, emission, and Community Health and Safety (CHS)
                         during both the construction and operation & maintenance (O&M) phases. This Project will support the construction
                         and operation of a wastewater and fecal sludge treatment plant and ancillary activities such as utilizing vacuum trucks
                         or tankers for safe sludge collection, extracting valuable resources like biogas, compost, or recycled water to treat and
                         safely dispose human waste collected from sewered and non-sewered sanitation systems. The project is expected to
                         provide essential sanitation services with overall beneficial environmental impacts. However, the construction and
                         operation of the plant can pose environmental risks and impacts. The potentially significant environmental risks
                         resulting from the proposed project include: (i) soil contamination, (ii) surface and groundwater pollution, odor,
