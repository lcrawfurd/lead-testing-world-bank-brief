                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The REPAIR Program aims to strengthen the financial and operational preparedness of participating countries to
                     respond quickly to climate and other shocks. It sets up a Regional Climate Risk Fund to provide countries with access to
                     different financial instruments to respond to shocks of various frequency and severity. And it provides technical
                     assistance to participating countries to ensure that the funds from the financial instruments flow quickly and
                     transparently to respond to disasters through country delivery channels. It is managed by one regional implementing
                     entity African Risk Capacity Limited (ARC Ltd). Comoros, Madagascar and Mozambique are in the Program in Phase 1.

                     Phase 2 countries include Angola, Burundi, Malawi, Seychelles and Zambia. The phase 3 operation will be for DRC. The
                     African Risk Capacity (ARC) Group is comprised of ARC Agency, a Specialised Agency of the African Union founded in
                     2012; and ARC Insurance Company Limited (ARC Ltd), a hybrid mutual insurer and the commercial affiliate of the Group
                     founded in 2014. ARC Agency was established to help African governments improve their capacities to better plan,
                     prepare, and respond to natural disasters triggered by extreme weather events, as well as outbreaks and epidemics.
                     ARC Ltd offers complementary risk pooling and risk transfer services. The RCRF will be managed by ARC Ltd, which will
                     ensure strong governance, and build the capacity of Ministries of Finance on managing their contingent liabilities
                     related to climate disasters. Under Component 1, the MPA will establish a Regional Climate Risk Fund (RCRF) to provide
                     affordable, pre-arranged financing for post-disaster responses to participating countries ahead of climate and other
                     shocks. Activities under this component will be oriented to setting up and capitalizing the RCRF in the different financial
                     layers, with ARC Limited as its regional manager . This component will be funded by IDA/IBRD and make up the largest
                     portion of World Bank funding for the project. Component 2 is funded by the regional IDA grant that was approved in
                     phase 2 of the program in July 2025. Technical assistance will support the operationalization of the RCRF and the
                     operational preparedness of country systems to facilitate swift flows of funds upon a shock. This includes start-up and
                     operating cost of the RCRF including staffing; preparation and testing of operational process and procedures to facilitate
                     the fund flows between MoFs, country systems and end beneficiaries; consultancies to help MoFs decide the allocation
                     between the instruments based on their risk appetite, and improve the management of disaster-related contingent
                     liabilities; and capacity building of country officials on DRF, of country systems on contingency planning and of women
                     and WSMEs on financial literacy.

Public Disclosure
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The Regional Emergency Preparedness and Access to Inclusive Recovery (REPAIR) Phase 3 will support the Democratic
                     Republic of Congo (DRC) under the ongoing MPA Program. The project is nationwide in scope, focusing on financial
                     preparedness and operational delivery of disaster risk financing. It does not finance large infrastructures but channels
                     pre-arranged funds through existing delivery institutions for emergency response, social protection, and small-scale
                     infrastructure repairs.

                     DRC faces high disaster and climate risks (floods, droughts, epidemics, conflict shocks) within a fragile and conflict-
                     affected context. Vulnerable groups include smallholder farmers, women, and small businesses with limited coping
                     capacity. Indigenous Peoples are present in several provinces, requiring inclusive approaches to avoid exclusion from
                     benefits.

                     Implementation relies on pre-identified channels: Fonds pour la Promotion de l’Inclusion Financière (FPM) for small
                     businesses, the National Agriculture Development Program for farmers, and potentially NGOs for social transfers or
                     cash-for-work. Risks stem mainly from ensuring transparent fund flows, the capacity of delivery channels to manage
                     social risks (including SEA/SH), and localized E&S risks from small repairs or community works. These risks are site-
                     specific and moderate, requiring clear screening, OHS measures, and stakeholder engagement.

                     Given its financial nature, limited footprint, and reliance on existing systems, the project’s overall E&S risk is moderate.
                     However, governance capacity and stakeholder risks are significant, and attention is needed to ensure that women,
                     Indigenous Peoples, and vulnerable households equitably benefit from post-disaster financing.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) has a limited but growing ability to manage environmental and social (E&S)
                     risks. While several ministries and project implementation units (PIUs) have some experience with World Bank
                     Safeguards and Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) requirements, their capacity remains uneven, particularly in
                     social risk management, grievance handling, and financial oversight. The Ministry of Finance (MoF) coordinates the
                     REPAIR initiative but lacks a dedicated E&S unit, relying instead on delivery channels such as FPM (for small businesses)
                     and PNDA (for farmers), which have varying familiarity with labor risks, SEA/SH, stakeholder engagement, and
                     Indigenous Peoples inclusion. NGOs may also participate in cash transfer or community programs, requiring careful
                     vetting.

                     ARC Ltd, the regional implementation unit, has developed E&S screening tools and capacity-building programs in earlier
                     phases and will extend these to DRC. Lessons learned point to the importance of performance contracts between MoF
                     and delivery channels, fund flow tracking, and integrating E&S requirements into operations. The Borrower has shown
                     commitment by allocating a budget for REPAIR and forming an inter-ministerial task force, but institutional gaps remain.
                     Targeted support is needed to improve delivery channels’ ability to manage enviornmental and social risks, strengthen
                     grievance redress and monitoring, and enhance coordination in a fragile context.

                     In summary, while the Borrower’s capacity is currently adequate, substantial support remains necessary. With technical
                     assistance from ARC Ltd and support from the World Bank, the identified E&S risks can be effectively managed—
                     provided that capacity-building measures are implemented early in the project preparation phase.

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                           Moderate


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                    Moderate
                     The environmental risk associated with REPAIR Phase 3 in the DRC is classified as Moderate. The project is primarily
                     focused on financial and technical assistance rather than large-scale civil works or infrastructure investments. Its main
                     objective is to strengthen disaster risk financing mechanisms and ensure the efficient transfer of pre-arranged funds
                     to delivery institutions for rapid response following shocks. Potential environmental risks may arise indirectly from
                     small-scale infrastructure repairs or community works undertaken by delivery channels such as FPM, PNDA, NGOs, or
                     public works entities. These activities could generate localized impacts including dust, noise, waste, and minor OHS
                     concerns. Such risks are expected to be site-specific, temporary, and reversible, and can be effectively mitigated
                     through standard good practices and appropriate risk management measures. The involvement of multiple delivery
                     channels introduces variability in environmental management capacity. While some entities have prior World Bank
                     project experience, others have limited familiarity with ESF requirements. This unevenness could hinder consistent
                     application of screening and monitoring measures. Consequently, institutional capacity gaps represent a moderate
                     risk, highlighting the need for targeted training, capacity support, and strengthened oversight to ensure compliance.
                     The project does not involve activities in sensitive ecosystems, protected areas, or high-biodiversity regions, thereby,
                     reducing the likelihood of significant adverse impacts. However, implementation in a fragile and conflict-affected
                     setting increases operational complexity and emphasizes the need for robust monitoring, supervision, and clear
                     contractual obligations across all entities. Overall, the Moderate rating reflects the limited scale of potential impacts,

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     Based on the currently available information, the social risk is classified as Moderate. The project is financial in nature
                     and will not finance any major civil works and any form of resettlement , and the downstream impacts of the TA to be
                     provided are not expected to be significant either and to have low-moderate risks. Its activities are centered on
                     strengthening disaster risk financing mechanisms and channeling pre-arranged funds to delivery institutions for rapid
                     response to shocks. Social risks may arise from limited equity, transparency, and accountability of fund distribution
                     through delivery channels such as FPM, PNDA, NGOs, or public entities. Key concerns include (i) the potential
                     exclusion of vulnerable groups (women, Indigenous Peoples, people with disabilities, smallholder farmers, and
                     displaced persons) in a fragile context, along with potential of elite capture of project benefits, paired with
                     weaknesses in targeting and delivery mechanisms, particularly for people in conflict and hard-to-reach areas; and (ii)
                     risks associated with the perception of misuse or diversion of funds, which may lead to increased social tensions and
                     increased stakeholder risk. Where funds support small-scale infrastructure repairs or cash-for-work programs, there
                     may also be (iii) risks related to labor management, OHS, community health and safety, and localized grievances.
                     Delivery channels have varying levels of capacity to manage such risks. This variability underscores the need for
                     strengthened institutional arrangements, clear contractual obligations to be passed on to participating entities, and
                     capacity support of ESF requirements. There are also downstream risks associated with the activities to be financed as
                     part of the TA. Resources will be channeled through ministries, implementing agencies, and partners already engaged
                     in Bank-supported operations, helping to reduce social risk exposure by leveraging existing systems, and institutional

                     B. Relevance of Standards and Policies at Concept Stage

                     B.1 Relevance of Environmental and Social Standards

                      ESS1 - Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts                                   Relevant

                      The project is primarily financial in nature but may generate indirect risks through delivery channels managing small-
                      scale infrastructure repairs or community works. These risks are expected to be site-specific, temporary, and low to
                      moderate in magnitude, including localized OHS, waste, community safety concerns, and SEA/SH. Institutional
                      capacity to manage risks is uneven, requiring robust screening, monitoring, and oversight. Performance contracts
                      with delivery channels will integrate ESF requirements, supported by ARC Ltd capacity-building and Bank supervision,
                      to ensure consistent risk management. The security arrangements will be specified in the POM. The A-ESRS will
                      provide more detailed information on how the project will operate in those areas.

                      ESS10 - Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure                                                        Relevant

                      MoF in DRC will prepare a Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) and Grievance Mechanism (GM), with ARC Ltd support,
                      to manage engagement with stakeholders affected by or interested in REPAIR. Stakeholder engagement activities will
                      need to be carried our during project preparation. Delivery channels such as FPM, PNDA, NGOs, or public agencies
                      may already have SEPs and GMs; these will be reviewed and updated to ensure they address REPAIR-specific
                      requirements. End-beneficiary concerns, information needs, and grievances related to fund distribution, small

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                               Relevant

                      The project will engage several worker categories under ESS2: (i) direct workers, including MoF and ARC Ltd staff for
                      coordination; (ii) contracted workers via delivery channels (FPM, PNDA, NGOs, public works entities) for small-scale
                      repairs or cash-for-work; and (iii) primary supply workers, as needed, for materials. Key labor risks include OHS, fair
                      treatment, forced and potential child labor among suppliers, and SEA/SH risks in the workplace. To mitigate these,
                      contracts will need to incorporate ESS2-compliant labor provisions and require accessible worker grievance
                      mechanisms for all categories, ensuring safe and confidential reporting and resolution.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                                Relevant

                      The project is not expected to significantly affect resource efficiency or cause major pollution given its financial focus.
                      However, small-scale infrastructure repairs or community works may generate localized impacts, including waste,
                      dust, noise, and minor air quality emissions. In line with ESS3, delivery channels will be required to apply good
                      international industry practices (GIIP) on waste management, pollution prevention, and efficient material use.
                      Performance contracts will include clear obligations on waste handling and OHS protocols. To keep risks low and
                      manageable, delivery channels and contractors will receive targeted training and be subject to continuous oversight

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                                Relevant

                      While the project is primarily financial, small-scale repairs or cash-for-work activities may pose localized community
                      health and safety risks such as traffic hazards, construction nuisances, and waste mismanagement. In fragile and post-
                      disaster contexts, risks of SEA/SH and social tensions related to the distribution of funds are also relevant. Delivery
                      channels will be required to adopt good practice measures to safeguard communities, integrate SEA/SH mitigation
                      into performance contracts, and ensure proper management of community interfaces during implementation.
                      Accessible grievance mechanisms will provide safe reporting channels. Oversight by ARC Ltd and the Bank, supported
                      by training and monitoring, will help ensure consistent compliance with ESS4 and protection of affected communities.

                      ESS5 - Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement                     Not Currently Relevant

                      The project will not finance activities that require land acquisition, restrictions on land use, or involuntary
                      resettlement. REPAIR Phase 3 focuses on disaster risk financing and channeling pre-arranged funds through delivery
                      institutions, with no large-scale civil works or infrastructure investments. Small-scale repairs or cash-for-work
                      activities, if supported, will take place on existing public or community land and are expected to avoid displacement
                      or resettlement impacts. Screening procedures and performance contracts will ensure that any activity involving land
                      acquisition or resettlement is excluded from project financing.

                      delivery channels integrate these measures throughout implementation.

                      ESS9 - Financial Intermediaries                                                                                    Relevant

                      ARC Ltd, which manages the RCRF, is not considered a Financial Intermediary (FI). However, national delivery
                      channels such as FPM for MSME finance may be considered FIs under ESS9. These entities will be required to operate

                      their financing activities. Where delivery channels already manage Bank-financed projects with ESS9 requirements,
                      existing ESMSs (including screening criteria and exclusion lists) will be reviewed for adequacy and strengthened or
                      updated as needed. Channels without ESMSs will be required to establish one, with support from the Bank and ARC
                      Ltd, before becoming eligible to receive project funds. This ensures consistent compliance with ESS9 and other
                      applicable ESF requirements throughout project implementation.
