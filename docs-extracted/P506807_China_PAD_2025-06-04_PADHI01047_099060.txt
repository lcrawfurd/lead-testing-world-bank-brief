 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT
 A. Project Strategic Context
 1. China's rapid urbanization is associated with significant economic growth but at high environmental costs. Over
    the past four decades, nearly 800 million people have been lifted out of poverty, 1 with the urban population
    increasing from 459 million in 2000 to about 932 million in 2023 (66 percent of the population).2 However, this
    growth has severely impacted natural assets like forests and wetlands, fragmenting ecological land and reducing
    connectivity between landscapes and cities. This weakens ecosystem services and affects urban livability. If China
    continues this model, approximately 65 percent of its Gross Domestic Product (GDP) (US$9 trillion in annual economic
    output) is at risk due to nature loss. 3 Conversely, a 'nature-positive' economy could generate US$1.9 trillion in
    additional annual revenue and create 88 million jobs by 2030.4

 2. China's urban areas contribute up to 85 percent of the country's Carbon Dioxide (CO2) emissions, a significant part
    of global carbon emissions.5 From 2000 to 2020, China's CO2 emissions surged by 227 percent (from 3.35 to 10.95
    billion metric tons) highlighting the critical role of urban development in achieving national and global climate goals.6
    With the urban population projected to reach one billion by 2035, cities will play an increasingly important role in
    reducing CO2 emissions. In particular, the way cities are organized and grow –their ‘urban forms’– have strong and
    lasting lock-in effects. Further, transport, building and other municipal services are major contributors to energy
    consumption and emissions. For instance, if unmitigated, transport emissions are expected to peak at 150 percent
    of current levels around 2040.7 The urban water sector also significantly contributes to CO2 and other greenhouse
    gas (GHG) emissions due to the degradation of organics in wastewater treatments8 and the operation of treatment
    facilities.9

 3. China is taking significant steps to peak CO2 emissions by 2030 and reach carbon neutrality by 2060, and has been
    reshaping its urbanization and ecological strategy, focusing on sustainable growth models that prioritize carbon
    reduction and environmental resilience. Since 2010, China has introduced a low-carbon cities (LCC) program,
    designating LCC pilots with the goal to reduce carbon emissions and enhance high-quality urban development. In
    2017, the country established the “ecological conservation red line” to protect key ecological functions and
    environmentally sensitive areas. China's 14th Five-Year Plan (2021-2025) emphasizes the role of cities in transitioning
    to a low-carbon future and for restoring urban ecosystems, advocating for compact, innovative, and resilient urban
    development. City action is crucial for translating national decarbonization and ecological targets into tangible
    outcomes, as cities are at the forefront of implementing national policies. However, more could be done, such as by
    mainstreaming and scaling-up nature-based solutions (NBS) as a key component of China’s climate policies. It is

 4. China plays a crucial role in global environmental resources and biodiversity, boasting significant wetland areas
    and a rich variety of plant and animal species, while facing substantial risks of ecological degradation. While China's
    total wetland area is accounting for four percent of the world's wetland area, ranking fourth in the world and first in
    Asia,11 China’s wetlands are under threat. China lost 2,883 km2 of wetlands from 1990 to 201012 and 20.6 percent of
    natural waterbody area from 1990 and 2018 (in 159 Chinese cities above one million in population) due to urban
    expansion.13 China is also rich in biodiversity and is home to ten percent of the world's known plant and 14 percent
    of animal species, including 3,000 globally endangered species.14 48 percent of the national bird diversity and 42
    percent of its threatened species have been recorded in the top-20 most avian-diverse cities of China15 and 2,640
    woody plant species have been recorded in urban forests in China,16 demonstrating the important role of Chinese
    cities for biodiversity.

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
 5. Urban development today remains largely carbon-intensive and harmful to nature, putting nearly half of GDP in
    cities globally at risk due to nature loss.17 Historically, climate change and nature loss in urban areas have been
    addressed separately, despite their interconnection. Neglecting either could cause irreversible ecological damage
    and jeopardize the well-being of current and future generations. Although nature’s value is recognized,
    environmental management often takes a backseat to development priorities like economic growth, infrastructure
    and services, migration and rising debts. Biodiversity provides essential benefits that support urban living beyond its
    intrinsic worth. Designing, building and managing cities to be nature-positive is crucial in combating climate change.

 6. Addressing the two intertwined global challenges of climate and nature crises requires hardwiring low-carbon and
    nature-positive urban development to build livable cities. Low-carbon and nature-positive urban development are
    essential, interconnected strategies. Low-carbon cities focus on planning, implementing, monitoring, and accounting
    for low-carbon investments and climate change mitigation. Nature-positive cities enhance resilience and livability by
    integrating nature and ensuring urban development benefits nature. This dual approach creates mutually reinforcing
    and synergistic benefits for sustainable, livable cities. Separating these approaches risks negative impacts, such as
    implementing transport infrastructure which harms the environment and biodiversity, or isolated protection of
    natural assets affecting low-carbon goals by increasing traffic and GHG emissions. Urgent action is needed to
    transform China's urban infrastructure towards carbon-neutral, nature-positive outcomes by reducing energy
    consumption in "grey infrastructure" and investing in "blue and green infrastructure." Planning, designing, and
    shaping cities to be carbon-neutral and nature-positive will reduce climate and nature impacts, revitalizing and
    regenerating services and spaces to ensure people can live, work, and thrive in livable cities.

City Context and Selection
 7. Guiyang, the provincial capital of Guizhou in Southwest China, exemplifies a rapidly urbanizing city undergoing
     economic transformation, but remains heavily indebted. From 2000 to 2022, Guiyang’s population grew by 84.3
     percent from 3,374,500 to 6,220,400,18 and its GDP grew at an annual average rate of 14 percent, 19 consistently
     ranking among the top in China. The city has successfully diversified from traditional industries like aluminum
     production and phosphate mining to the service sector, big data, and technological innovation. Guiyang is crucial for
     Guizhou’s growth and poverty reduction.20 Strategically, Guiyang is vital for the Yangtze River Economic Belt, and the
     new land-sea corridor in western China, highlighting its economic growth potential. However, Guiyang faces
     significant debt and municipal finance challenges.21 Annual interest payments on debt consume 56 percent of yearly
     revenues. China’s local government debt rose from 29.4 percent of GDP in 2012 to 70.5 percent in 202122, highlighting
     that Guiyang shares a similar challenge as many other Chinese cities. To further unlock potential for economic
     development and green job creation, it will be important for Guiyang to transition fully to an economy consistent
     with net-zero carbon emissions and net-positive impacts on nature. A holistic, dual approach is more efficient and
     cost-effective, ensuring continued economic competitiveness and livability.

 8. Guiyang set an ambitious goal for carbon peaking by 2030, aligning with China's national targets; per capita
    emissions and emission intensity are decreasing, with a shift in contributing sectors from energy to transport.
    Guiyang ‘s total GHG emissions in 2017, 2019, and 2020 were estimated as 33.71 million, 42.03 million, and 41.92
    million tCO2e, respectively. Since 2010, per capita carbon emissions have decreased, reaching approximately 6.04
    tons per person in 2020, slightly below the national average. From 2016 to 2020, Guiyang's carbon emission intensity
    decreased by 38 percent, by optimizing energy structure, adjusting industrial structure, and implementing energy-
    saving and emission reduction measures. The industrial sector's emissions dropped from 50 percent in 2010 to 38
    percent in 2020, while transport sector emissions increased from 20 percent to 33 percent, and building sector
    emissions remained stable at about 28 percent. 23 The city has been actively pursuing green and low-carbon
    development in the construction sector, promoting energy efficiency and new materials. By 2021, prefabricated
    buildings make up 20 percent of new buildings and in 2022, 77.57 percent of new constructions were green buildings.
    However, transportation remains the hard-to-abate sector requiring targeted solutions.

 9. Guiyang is largely green with over 70 percent of its land covered by forests or grasslands, but its urbanization faces
    significant land constraints and overconsumed ecosystem services, creating an imbalance between ecological
    conservation and economic development. The built-up area more than doubled from 111 km² in 1985 to 397 km² in
    2015, with rapid growth occurring after 2000.24 The hilly terrain and karst25 landscape confine the built-up area to 11
    percent, 26 leaving little room for expansion, resulting in high-density development clusters. Located at the watershed

 12. Aha Lake, a national wetland park located in the core of Guiyang, embodies many of Guiyang’s urbanization
     challenges, including encroachment and nature degradation. The Aha Lake has been designated as a primary
     drinking water source since 1982 but was declassified in 2021 and no longer serves such a purpose. Rapid
     urbanization and the completion of the 3-ring and 16-ray road network in 2016 resulted in the lake being situated at
     the center of Guiyang. The dense population and rapid industrial development in the lake basin have led to domestic
     sewage and wastewater discharge into the river system, causing poor water quality and ecological degradation.
     Vegetation cover has declined, biodiversity has decreased, and ecosystem functions are impaired. The complex
     terrain and severe soil erosion result in significant sediment flow into the lake during rains, affecting water storage
     capacity and quality. An inefficient transportation network around Aha Lake, and key urban clusters (such as
     Guanshanhu, and Guian districts) leads to congestion of major roads that connect the city’s northern and southern
     districts, increased GHG emissions, and limits access to Aha Lake and its ecotourism potentials. The current lack of
     Non-Motorized Transport (NMT) paths and the subpar street conditions make it unsafe for pedestrians and cyclists
     discouraging low-carbon transportation options. Due to limited access, deteriorating conditions, and lack of new
     amenities, visitor levels dropped from 4 million in 2014 to 1.7 million in 2021 (despite an annual ecological capacity
     of 3.77 million).32 Guiyang seeks to address these root causes of the visitor level drop as per its prioritization of urban
     ecotourism outlined in Guiyang's "14th Five-Year Plan" on culture and tourism.

Contribution to Global Public Goods
 17. The project will support GPGs in four key areas:
      a. GHG Emissions Reduction: The project will reduce GHG emissions from the transport and water sectors by
         investing in non-motorized and public transport, improving road network efficiency and improving water quality
         and pollution control. Water pollution increases GHG emissions while reducing nutrient pollution from
         agricultural and industrial sources, urban stormwater, and wastewater treatment plants can lower GHG
         emissions. Additionally, improved land use and spatial planning will promote efficient land use and reduce
         unnecessary conversions to urban land, with all infrastructure complying with low-carbon standards.

      b. Carbon Sequestration Enhancement: The project will increase carbon sequestration in Guiyang through water
         quality improvement, wetland construction and Aha Lake rehabilitation, and protecting these ecosystems from
         encroachments and land use changes. Improved water quality facilitates carbon sequestration, especially in
         wetlands that act as CO2 sinks. Further research on the methodologies to enhance carbon sequestration is
         supported under the project.

      c. Biodiversity Conservation: The project will enhance biodiversity within targeted ecosystems, contributing to
         the United Nations (UN) Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (2022) goals. It will support halting
         human-induced extinction of threatened species, mitigating impacts of invasive species, and enhancing
         ecosystem functions and services. It will also improve the area, quality, and connectivity of green and blue
         spaces in urban areas.

     enhancement projects in China.43 The project is also informed by technical and global experience by the World Bank’s
     urban practice on cities and climate change, drawing upon numerous flagship reports and internal and global experts.

 34. Tailored Solutions and Context-Specific Approach. Lessons from past LCC initiatives emphasize the importance of
     optimizing resource allocation, tailoring solutions to specific circumstances, and promoting public awareness. The
     project recognizes that while many urban issues and goals are similar, different cities also face contextual challenges
     and opportunities. For example, the parallel design and implementation of the Low Carbon City Program (P506602)
     under preparation in Shijiazhuang showcase how to adopt context specific solutions to attain similar goals in different
     contexts. Both cities face rapid urban growth and environmental challenges, having similar objective of reducing GHG
     emissions requiring responses for hard-to-abate sectors like transport. While Shijiazhuang’s approach is informed by
     rapid urban expansion, Guiyang’s approach considers its limited land availability due to environmental and
     geophysical constraints, while having rich natural assets, necessitating solutions like nature-positive activities.

 35. Importance of multi-sectoral, area-based approach. Area-based approaches to investment can help enhance value
     for money by maximizing synergies between smaller-scale, spatially clustered investments. It also allows for the
     integration of various sectors such as urban planning, transport and mobility, water quality, environment and
     ecosystem conservation leading to more comprehensive and impactful outcomes. Lessons learned demonstrate that
     projects that are multi-sectoral and area-based yield more benefits than standalone, isolated interventions and this
     is the approach adopted in this project. It is crucial for tackling complex urban challenges such as carbon-intensive
     development and environmental degradation.

 36. Need for strong government commitment and coordination. Urban projects are multi-sectoral and require robust
     implementation arrangements involving various actors. Drawing on this experience, the project’s implementation
     entity includes a co-chair from Guiyang’s Municipal Development and Reform Commission (MDRC), a key planning
     body with authority over sectoral departments, responsible for overseeing the city's carbon peak and neutrality goals,
     and urban development and climate change policies. World Bank Implementation Completion and Results Reports
     (ICRs) and Independent Evaluation Group ICR review reports emphasize the importance of a powerful steering
     committee, led by the Mayor’s Office and including heads of all relevant departments, which has been integrated
     into the project’s design. Additionally, the project supports the appointment of an entity to managethe Aha Lake
     area, and to coordinate future development across sectors and jurisdictions.

 43. The proposed project establishes a strong foundation for Guiyang's future development, focusing on low-carbon
     and nature-positive principles, with Aha Lake as a demonstration area. Short-term priorities include conserving the

     infrastructure systems and services (water and transport). The project aims for balanced, low-carbon urban

     Global Public Goods. Interventions in water, environment, and transport will have positive reinforcing effects and

               technologies. A quantitative analysis projects a 15-20 percent reduction in private vehicle trips through
               enhanced NMT and public transit access, and a 30-35 percent decreased idle time through improved traffic

62. Environment. The project aims for ecological net gains by reducing GHG emissions and enhancing biodiversity
    conservation around Aha Lake in Guiyang. Municipal-level technical assistance activities will support Guiyang’s GHG
    reduction and biodiversity conservation, and addressing downstream E&S implications. Physical investments,
    including construction and upgrades, will occur in the Aha Lake area (100 km2), with some within the delineated
    ecological redlines (0.33 km2) and the national wetland park (0.2 km2) but without disturbing and negatively affecting
    core reserves. Aha Lake, a reservoir and National Wetland Park, is a Tier 3 natural park in China’s protection system
    and a critical habitat for terrestrial wildlife. It has been an urban park open to the public since 2013. The project’s
    activities will not pose additional restrictions to accessing the park. The project will enhance the park’s functions
    including flood control, biodiversity conservation, public education and recreation.

63. A preliminary Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA), including an Environmental Management Plan (EMP) and a
    biodiversity impact management plan, has been prepared per ESF requirements. The Feasibility Study Report (FSR)
    and preliminary EIA confirmed that all project activities are planned in compliance with the eco-redline strategy and
    ecological functions, without disturbing and negatively affecting score reserves. Construction nuisance like noise,
    wastewater, dust, solid waste and soil erosion, and pesticide use during reforestation are expected to be temporary,
    site-specific and manageable with known mitigation techniques considering the nature and scale of these activities.
    During operation, negative impacts include domestic wastewater, waste, and occupational health and safety risks
    from operating wastewater and drainage facilities. Mitigation strategies are included in the generic EMP annexed to
    the preliminary EIA. A dam safety assessment confirmed that the dam, rehabilitated in 2023, is well-operated with
    no safety risks. The small section of proposed greenway (cycling and walking path) along the dam will not negatively
    impact it.

64. Guiyang, with previous World Bank experience but new to ESF, will build capacity through this project. A PMO has
    been established and fully staffed, with capacity-building activities planned in the EIA for the PMO and other
    stakeholders throughout the project cycle. The environmental risk is rated as substantial due to the ecological
    importance of Aha Lake and the implementation of some activities within protected areas (ecological redlines),
    mitigated by site-specific recommendations from the detailed EIA and biodiversity impact assessment.

65. Social. The project focuses on habitat restoration (river, wetland, and vegetation) and biodiversity monitoring within
    an existing wetland park. It also includes constructing connection roads and greenways around Aha Lake to improve
    access. While habitat restoration will not change forest land use or restrict access, the construction will require land
    acquisition and house demolitions in 11 villages, potentially disrupting residents' lives and livelihoods during
    construction. A Social Impact Assessment (SIA) was conducted based on the FSR design identifying social impacts and
    risks related to land acquisition and housing development on communities and ethnic minority groups, the use of
    temporary construction workers, and disruptions to daily life. The land acquisition will affect 1,697 households and
    6,387 people, 74 percent of whom are ethnic minorities, mainly Buyi and Miao. Despite the significant number of
    affected individuals, the project's linear nature minimizes the impact on each village and household. Most acquired
    land is forest or unused land not intended for farming, with less than 10 percent impact on livelihoods. The land
    acquisition will not harm the natural resources or environment essential for residents’ livelihoods and development.
    Temporary labor, including migrant and local workers will be employed for construction. Potential risks to workers'
    health and safety may arise from gaps in labor rights awareness and deficiencies in managing working conditions.
    Ethnic minority communities in the project area are under urban administration, not attached to ancestral lands, and

                      Official Use Only                                                                             Page 23

      to those of the Han group. The impact of land acquisition on the livelihoods of ethnic minorities is also minimal.

4. The Component 1 climate mitigation measures include (i) reducing disturbance to habitats at river estuaries during

5. This component consists of three subcomponents:

      •    Subcomponent 1.1: Accelerate Non-Motorized Transport (NMT) Network Development (US$84.5 million
           equivalent). This subcomponent will finance the Aha Lake Greenway, an 18.2km cycling and walking path
           around the lake, and 33.2km of connecting lines linking to city-wide green networks like the Xiaowan River
           Greenway in Yunyan District. Improved NMT connections to nearby metro stations (Line 3 and S1, 1-2km away)
           will complement efforts under subcomponent 1.2. Additionally, energy efficient amenities and basic utility
           networks in the Aha Lake area will be supported, including (i) 17 bicycle stations, (ii) LED street lighting, (iii)
           water supply, drainage and public toilets, (iv) rest areas, (v) outdoor museum, (vi) other ancillary utilities e.g.
           power, telecommunications, information service and digital management platform, and (vii) other small-scale
           recreational facilities. Investments will use energy-saving technologies per national and international standards.
           The green network design focuses on eco-friendliness, user-friendliness, connectivity, and sustainability,
           integrating with the natural environment, respecting topography, minimizing habitat impact, and protecting
           ecosystems. Environmentally friendly materials like wood, bamboo, and gravel, energy-saving technologies, and
           barrier-free designs will enhance flood resilience and usability.

      •    Subcomponent 1.2: Enhance Public Transit Solutions and Modal Share (US$24.8 million equivalent). This
           subcomponent will enhance the public transit system and increase its modal share by: (i) expanding coverage
           with new bus lines through upgrading and rehabilitating of existing roads to allow bus lines to traverse,
           especially connecting to ultra-dense residential areas like Huaguoyuan, east of Aha Lake, as well as an integrated
           bus station of 6,000 m2; (ii) improving bus service quality and operations through infrastructure upgrades, road
           traffic improvements, and service enhancements in frequency, speed, and route optimization; and (iii)
           improving last-mile connections from metro stations by the provision of bus services to communities around
           the Aha Lake. These measures aim to reduce private vehicle use, decrease congestion and emissions, and
           improve access around Aha Lake. The foundation for TOD efforts around metro stations in the Aha Lake area
           will be integrated with the NMT and public transit network in the Aha Lake master plan.

      •    Subcomponent 1.3: Enhance Travel Efficiency and User Experience (US$88.1 million equivalent). This
           subcomponent finances (i) the construction and upgrading of two critical connections south of Aha Lake, linking
           the West Second Ring Road (east of Aha Lake) and the Ring Expressway (west of Aha Lake), providing direct
           access to the Aha Lake area; (ii) installation of EV charging stations; and (iii) modernizing intelligent transport
