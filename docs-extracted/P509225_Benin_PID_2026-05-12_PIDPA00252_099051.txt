 B. Introduction and Context


  Country Context

1. Benin, a coastal West African country of about 13 million, has recorded strong and sustained economic growth in
   recent years, positioning it among the fastest growing economies in the subregion. Gross domestic product (GDP)
   growth reached 7.5 percent in 2024, the highest rate since 1990, driven by robust performance across services,
   industry, and agriculture.1 Benin’s growth performance has been supported by rising private investment; expanding
   exports; and continued public investment in transport, energy, and logistics infrastructure, which have helped
   strengthen Benin’s role as a regional trade and transit hub.2 At the same time, fiscal consolidation efforts have
   strengthened macroeconomic stability, with the fiscal deficit meeting the West African Economic and Monetary Union
   convergence target of 3 percent of GDP and public debt declining to just over half of GDP. These trends have
   contributed to improved access to international capital markets.

2. Despite this strong macroeconomic performance, economic growth has not translated into rapid poverty reduction
   or corresponding improvements in job creation, labor productivity, and human capital accumulation, particularly
   in rural and northern regions. The relationship between growth and poverty reduction remains weaker than the Sub-
   Saharan Africa average, reflecting persistent structural constraints related to low labor productivity, limited job
   creation in higher-value sectors, and uneven access to basic services.3 More than three-quarters of total employment
   remains vulnerable and concentrated in low-productivity informal activities, with only marginal improvement over
   the past decade.4 Rural areas bear a disproportionate share of this burden, with vulnerable employment particularly
   affecting women and youth: Women account for especially high shares of informal and precarious work, while the
   majority of young people remain engaged in jobs with limited productivity and earnings potential, especially in

    agriculture and related activities where access to inputs, markets, and skills remain insufficient.5 These structural
    dynamics are inseparable from Benin’s human capital challenges, particularly underinvestment in health, nutrition,
    and education, which continue to constrain productivity growth and poverty reduction.

3. The First 2,000 Days of life — from conception to a child's second birthday — represent the most critical and
   irreversible window for human capital formation, and one where accelerating and deepening investment holds the
   greatest transformative potential for Benin's development trajectory. A child born in Benin today is estimated to
   achieve only about 42 percent6 of their potential productivity under current conditions, a deficit largely sealed before
   a child ever sets foot in a classroom. This is driven in significant part by chronic undernutrition: 36.5 percent of
   children under five were stunted in 2022,7 suffering irreversible cognitive and physical damage from prolonged
   nutritional deprivation concentrated in these earliest years, with economic costs of undernutrition estimated at up to
   16.5 percent of GDP annually.8 These deficits do not resolve over time — they compound. Benin’s 2025 Human Capital
   Index+ (HCI+) score of 125 out of 325, below both the Sub-Saharan Africa regional average of 126 and the lower-
   middle-income country average of 153, confirms that gaps rooted in the first 2,000 days persist through primary
   school, where completion rates stood at only 77 percent in 2013, through tertiary education and working life, where
   a pronounced gender gap leaves women’s earning potential roughly 13 percent lower than that of men. Spanning
   nutrition, education, and labor market outcomes, micronutrient deficiencies not only undermine learning outcomes
   and drive retention and progression challenges across all levels of schooling, but also diminish adult labor productivity
   — compressing the earnings and economic contribution of a workforce that never fully recovered from deprivation in
   its earliest years. Addressing this intergenerational cycle therefore requires simultaneous, sustained action across
   multiple sectors — not sequentially, and not through isolated projects — underscoring the central role of nutrition
   investments in supporting inclusive employment and long-term growth.

4. Compounding these structural human capital deficits is the growing insecurity in the wider Sahel region, which has
   begun to spill into northern Benin, further weakening communities’ resilience, disrupting access to services, and
   worsening nutritional outcomes. This instability has disrupted livelihoods, strained basic services, and threatened
   the recent development gains in Benin and neighboring countries. Insecurity and displacement are also well-
   documented drivers of gender-based violence (GBV): as community protection mechanisms break down and
   household economic stress intensifies, women and girls face heightened risks of intimate partner violence, sexual
   violence, and early marriage — risks that compound their nutritional vulnerability and further constrain their access
   to health and nutrition services. Moreover, the share of the population facing severe food insecurity has risen from
   10.4 percent in 2014–2016 to 15.8 percent in 2021–2023,9 disproportionately affecting northern regions and forcibly
   displaced populations. This deterioration is driven by the convergence of household’s inability to afford nutritious
   foods due to insecurity, unemployment, low and unstable informal sector wages, high food prices, climate shocks, and
   structurally weak food systems. Together, these compounding stressors push households to reduce meal frequency
   and dietary diversity, especially during lean seasons, heat waves, and crises, directly worsening nutritional outcomes
   and labor productivity.10

5. Recognizing the central role of nutrition and early childhood development (ECD) in building human capital,
   productivity, and resilience, the Government of Benin (GoB) has elevated nutrition as a national development
   priority. This commitment is reflected in the National Food and Nutrition Policy (La Politique nationale de
   l’alimentation et de la nutrition, PNAN, 2024–2033), which articulates a multi-sectoral, life cycle approach spanning
   pregnancy, early childhood, school age, and adolescence. Nutrition objectives are embedded within the GoB’s broader
   development vision, including Benin Vision 2060 and the Government Action Program, which emphasize human
   capital development as a cornerstone of economic transformation, social inclusion, and resilience.11 However, the
   scale, complexity, and multi-sectoral nature of Benin’s nutrition challenge require sustained investments and
   institutional strengthening beyond the scope of traditional stand-alone operations to deliver in advancing the GoB’s
   1,000 Days+ Agenda. Past interventions have been constrained by fragmented financing arrangements, uneven
   service quality, limited coordination across sectors, and persistent capacity constraints at decentralized levels, limiting
   effectiveness, coverage, and sustainability.12 Addressing these structural challenges calls for a phased, programmatic
   approach that can build foundational systems, strengthen coordination, incorporate learning over time, and
   progressively scale effective interventions nationwide.

6. In this context, the ALAFIA:13 Nourishing Benin’s Future Multi‑Phase Programmatic Approach (MPA) (P512948)
   through a One World Bank Group (WBG) approach, provides an appropriate framework to support Benin’s
   long‑term nutrition, human capital, and jobs agenda. ALAFIA is deliberately designed to break new ground: while the
   GoB has made important strides in addressing human capital deficits in the school-age population, most notably
   through its Integrated National School Feeding Program (Programme nationale d’alimentation scolaire intégré,
   PNASI), which has expanded access to food for children above five, the critical First 2,000 Days has remained
   comparatively underserved. ALAFIA and its first phase anchor a long-term GoB strategy that addresses this gap head-
   on, bringing together multi-sectoral nutrition interventions with the support of other WBG practices and development
   partners, community resilience, and private sector engagement into a single, coherent program — a regional first.
   Because intervening effectively in the First 2,000 Days requires testing and refining approaches that are not yet fully
   established at scale in the Beninese context, a traditional stand-alone project would be insufficient. The MPA structure
   is uniquely suited to this challenge: it allows the Government and the WBG to learn as they go — systematically
   capturing implementation evidence, adjusting course across phases, and building a knowledge base that informs scale-
   up and replication. Consistent with the subnational stunting profile, Phase I prioritizes communes in regions with the
   highest stunting burden and service delivery gaps, while Phase II will progressively expand coverage nationwide,
   informed by that accumulated learning — ensuring that each phase delivers not only results on the ground but a
   scalable model for sustained impact.

7. Despite sustained policy attention and investments, Benin continues to face persistently poor nutrition outcomes,
   particularly among women and children. Stunting remains a critical concern, reflecting chronic deficits in diet quality,
   health, and care practices during the first 1,000 days of life. Stunting prevalence among children under five increased
   from 34 percent in 2014 to 36.5 percent in 2022, compared to 23.6 percent among structural peers and 32 percent
   across Sub-Saharan Africa. There are marked geographic disparities in stunting outcomes with rates significantly
   higher in the northern regions with pockets in the southern regions, underscoring the need for a phased approach
   that prioritizes communes with high rates of stunting in Phase I before scaling up geographically in Phase II. Studies
   have shown that stunting prevalence exceeds 40 percent in several northern and central regions. Stunting also
   contributes to approximately 45 percent of under-five mortality, estimated at 77.9 per 1,000 live births in 2023,
   compared to 45.6 among structural peers. Moreover, anemia remains pervasive, affecting 72 percent of children ages
   6–59 months and 58 percent of women of reproductive age, with increasing rates among adolescent girls

8. Climate change is a key risk multiplier, deepening and accelerating malnutrition burden in Benin. The country is
   highly exposed to rising temperatures, increasing rainfall variability (including floods and droughts), sea level rise, and
   heat stress, with impacts most pronounced in northern regions where poverty, food insecurity, and malnutrition are
   already concentrated. Temperatures have increased by 0.78°C between 1971 and 2020 and are expected to increase
   by 0.28°C per decade by mid-century, with higher impacts in the northern regions.14 Seasonal shifts and climate shocks
   directly affect agricultural productivity, food availability, and household incomes, while also placing additional
   pressure on health, nutrition, and social protection systems.15 For instance, climate-induced harvest failures and
   prolonged lean seasons are closely associated with acute spikes in wasting among children under five, while repeated
   shocks over time, which deplete household coping strategies, reduce dietary diversity, and increase food insecurity,
   also entrench stunting, reinforcing intergenerational cycles of malnutrition. Benin ranks 150 out of 187 on climate
   vulnerability and readiness, in part due to low scores in projected change of cereal yields and agricultural capacity,16
   reflecting how deeply climate change is undermining the food and nutrition security of its population.

9. Malnutrition, and in particular high stunting rates in Benin, are driven by four interrelated and binding constraints.
   First, service delivery remains uneven and insufficient, particularly in rural and northern areas. Geographic and
   financial barriers, shortages of skilled frontline health personnel, and variable service readiness—including persistent
   medication and supplementation stockouts—limit access to quality Reproductive, Maternal, Neonatal, Child,
   Adolescent Health and Nutrition (RMNCAH-N) services. Frontline health professionals, including skilled community
   relays (CRs) and skilled community health workers (CHWs), lack the competencies and incentive structures needed to
   deliver high-quality, integrated care. The ALAFIA I - Nourishing Benin’s Future Program (the Program) addresses these
   gaps by strengthening community health delivery systems using the GoB’s 2020 National Community Health Policy
   (Politique nationale de la santé communautaire, PNSC) and supporting frontline worker capacity under Results Area
   (RA) 1 (RA1). In addition to their roles as service delivery agents, there is an opportunity to leverage CRs and CHWs as
   active “engagement agents” within communities. As trusted local actors, they can serve as two-way communication
   channels, gathering community concerns, identifying barriers to access, and relaying feedback to inform adaptive
   management at the commune and program levels. This engagement function will be strengthened as part of the
   capacity-building efforts supported under the Program, with further operational detail to be provided through a
   dedicated Citizen Engagement and Inclusion Plan to be developed after effectiveness. By integrating this role, the
   Program aims to strengthen community accountability and ensure more responsive, needs-driven service delivery.

10. Second, social and gender norms constrain the adoption of effective nutrition and care practices.17 Women’s
    decision‑making power over food, health, and household resources remains limited, which when combined with time
    and mobility constraints undermines uptake of services and adoption of recommended practices. Only 42 percent of
    infants are exclusively breastfed until six months and only 28 percent of children ages 6–23 months achieve minimum
    dietary diversity. Economic barriers further compound this: A healthy diet costs approximately US$4 per household
    per day, beyond the reach of at least 60 percent of households.18 Poor water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH)
    conditions further drive child morbidity and higher malnutrition rates, with 47 percent of households practicing open
    defecation and only 10 percent having access to a handwashing facility with soap and water.19 The nutritional
    consequences of these dynamics are particularly acute for adolescent girls. Anemia prevalence among this group has
    worsened significantly, rising from 41.6 percent in 2012 to 57.4 percent in 2017, a trajectory that signals a deepening
    micronutrient crisis among a population whose nutritional status has direct intergenerational consequences. This
    deterioration reflects not only dietary gaps but also the intersection of gender norms, limited agency, and inadequate
    targeting of adolescent-specific nutrition interventions — dynamics that are further compounded by harmful practices
    such as female genital mutilation (FGM) that directly affect women's nutritional status and their capacity to support
    healthy pregnancies and child development. The Program responds through an integrated package of nutrition,
    health, and childhood development services that include social and behavioral change communication (SBCC)
    targeting women and caregivers, alongside WASH-nutrition linkages under RA1.

11. Third, food systems and community delivery mechanisms are insufficiently equipped to deliver affordable, diverse,
    and nutritious foods and nutrition services at scale. Dietary diversity scores among women of reproductive age and
    children under five remain well below recommended thresholds, with pronounced rural-urban gaps.20 Last-mile
    distribution of nutritious and fortified foods is fragmented, and nutrition-focused micro, small, and medium
    enterprises (MSMEs) face persistent constraints in access to finance, quality assurance, and supply chains with only
    40.9 percent of businesses having access to bank credit and 42 percent citing finance as their primary constraint. 21
    Women-owned enterprises are particularly excluded, with only 12.4 percent formalized. Evidence from comparable
    regional programs points to the potential of MSME-centered food systems approaches, for example, West Africa Food
    System Resilience Program (FSRP), an MPA covering Burkina Faso, Chad, Ghana, Mali, Niger, Sierra Leone, Senegal,
    and Togo (Phase 1: P172769; Phase 2: P178132), which demonstrates that targeted technical assistance (TA) and
    catalytic finance can improve MSME performance and expand household access to nutritious foods. Moreover, the
    school feeding program illustrates the broader quality gap in food systems delivery. While the GoB has prioritized
    retention by scaling the PNASI with WFP support to reduce short-term hunger and stabilize attendance, meal quality
    remains a critical gap. Recent studies show that even with expanded PNASI coverage, micronutrient deficiencies
    among school-age children remain high, with no significant difference in dietary diversity between schools with and
    without canteens. This reflects the same last-mile nutritional quality failure that characterizes broader food systems
    gaps in Benin. The Program (Results Area 2 [RA2]) addresses these market-side gaps through business development
    services and phased financing for MSMEs in Phase I, building toward blended finance instruments in Phase II,

12. Fourth, weak governance and fragmented coordination undermine multi-sectoral nutrition delivery. The
    establishment of ANAN, under presidential decree in 2023, reflects strong political commitment. This reform builds
    on Benin’s long‑standing engagement in multi-sectoral nutrition governance, including the Food and Nutrition Council
    (Conseil de l’Alimentation et de la Nutrition) and participation in the Scaling Up Nutrition (SUN) Movement since 2011.
    However, operational capacity and accountability mechanisms, particularly at the municipal level, remain uneven. The
    Program supports the strengthening of ANAN’s coordination role and invests in municipal-level accountability systems
    under RA1.

13. The proposed ALAFIA: Nourishing Benin’s Future MPA (P512948) and its first phase (P509225) aim to improve
    nutrition and human capital outcomes in Benin, driving a long-term transformation for the well-being of its
    population. Drawing on the WBG’s Investment Framework for Nutrition 2024, the Program is anchored in high-
    priority, evidence-based ‘best-buy’ interventions across four target groups: (a) antenatal interventions through
    micronutrient supplementation; (b) perinatal interventions such as skilled birth attendance and early initiation and
    support for exclusive breastfeeding; (c) infancy and childhood interventions including micronutrient supplementation,
    growth monitoring, and infant and young child feeding (IYCF) counseling; and (d) cross-cutting interventions for the
    general population such as cash transfers, staple food fortification, and interventions to reduce obesity and unhealthy
    diets. Through two interconnected pillars, the Program will scale up these best-buys focusing on a multi-sectoral
    convergence approach, strengthening community resilience, and catalyzing private sector engagement, while building
    the institutional foundations for sustained impact across the country. The Program is transformational not only in its
    holistic, life-course design—spanning preconception through early childhood and beyond—but also in its embodiment
    of the One WBG approach, bringing together the World Bank, the International Finance Corporation (IFC), and a range
    of complementary sectoral programs under a single, coherent framework. By simultaneously addressing multiple and
    interrelated constraints, from access to quality nutrition and health services to community food systems, governance,
    and financing, the Program seeks to unlock Benin’s human capital potential while leveraging the full strengths of the
    WBG. It builds on over a decade of World Bank engagement with the GoB through multi-sectoral nutrition and ECD
    operations—including the Benin Multisectoral Food Health Nutrition Project (P143652, 2014–2019) and the Early
    Years Nutrition and Child Development Project (P166211, 2019–2024)—which demonstrated that the effectiveness
    and sustainability of a life-course continuum depend on several interrelated factors: scaling up evidence-based
    interventions, fostering community ownership, building decentralized capacity, and strengthening multi-sectoral
    coordination at the institutional level.



  PforR Program Scope

14. The Program-for-Results (PforR) focuses on high-impact nutrition, health, and local food system interventions with
    the greatest potential to advance Benin’s human capital and nutrition objectives under the National Multisectoral
    Nutrition and Food Strategy (SNAN 2026–2030). As Phase I of the proposed Multiphase Programmatic Approach, the
    PforR concentrates on two core results areas—improving utilization of integrated nutrition, health, and child
    development services, and strengthening community resilience and access to nutritious foods—in up to 46 priority
    communes. These areas and geographic focus were selected based on the burden of stunting and anemia, poverty
    and food insecurity, vulnerability to climate and security shocks, and gaps in service delivery and local food systems,
    particularly in northern regions. The design builds on recent analytical work and operational experience of the World

15. The Program-for-Results (PforR) supports Results Areas that are fully aligned with Benin’s National Multisectoral
    Nutrition and Food Strategy (SNAN) 2026–2030 and is nested within the Government’s broader national nutrition
    program. All Results Areas supported under the PforR correspond to Government budget lines and program codes,
    ensuring alignment with national planning and expenditure frameworks. The design reflects lessons from previous
    World Bank operations and analytical work in Benin and leverages close collaboration with the International Finance
    Corporation (IFC). The Program is organized around two mutually reinforcing Results Areas, summarized below.

Results Area 1: Increase utilization of quality integrated nutrition, health and child development services

16. Results Area 1 focuses on strengthening delivery systems and service utilization across the life course, with particular
    emphasis on the first 1,000 days of life. The Program supports integrated delivery of nutrition, maternal and child
    health, and early childhood development services through community platforms, primary health care facilities, and
    schools. Interventions include maternal and adolescent nutrition, infant and young child feeding, micronutrient
    supplementation, prevention and management of acute malnutrition, post-natal care, and early childhood
    stimulation, complemented by behavior change communication and strengthened frontline capacity.

17. Under this Results Area, the PforR links disbursements to tangible improvements in service readiness, coordination,
    and utilization. Key Disbursement-Linked Indicators incentivize: (i) functional multisectoral coordination platforms at
    the commune level, (ii) expanded access to preventive nutrition supplementation for pregnant and breastfeeding
    women and young children, (iii) increased use of post-natal care services, and (iv) strengthened frontline delivery of
    integrated nutrition services. These results are supported by upstream technical assistance under the Investment
    Project Financing (IPF) component, including training, monitoring and evaluation systems, communal governance
    support, and supply chain strengthening.

Results Area 2: Enhance community resilience and access to quality nutritious food supply through local engagement
and private sector partnerships

18. Results Area 2 addresses the food system and livelihood drivers of malnutrition by strengthening community resilience
    and engaging local private sector actors. The Program supports climate-resilient nutrition gardens, small-scale
    livestock, and women- and youth-led agri-food microenterprises to improve the availability, affordability, and quality
    of nutritious foods in targeted communes. Activities include biofortification, food safety and quality improvements,
    clean cooking solutions, and community early warning and response systems for climate and food security shocks.

19. Disbursement-Linked Indicators under this Results Area incentivize the adoption of improved practices by women- and

    schools to climate-resilient food production systems. Private sector engagement is deliberately sequenced, with
    upstream advisory support in Phase I and potential scaling and investment under Phase II.

Program Beneficiaries

21. Phase I of the ALAFIA Multiphase Programmatic Approach is expected to directly benefit women, children,
    adolescents, and youth in up to 46 targeted communes, with a strong focus on populations facing high levels of
    malnutrition, food insecurity, climate vulnerability, and insecurity-related displacement. Primary beneficiaries include
    pregnant and breastfeeding women; children under five years of age, particularly during the first 1,000 days; and
    adolescent girls, who will benefit from expanded access to integrated nutrition, health, and early child development
    services. Phase I will also directly support women- and youth-led agri-food micro, small, and medium enterprises, as
    well as households and schools, through climate-resilient gardens, small livestock, and improved local food
    production, processing, and distribution practices. By strengthening community-level delivery systems, local food
    supply chains, and multisectoral coordination, the Program is expected to generate broader community-wide benefits
    and lay the foundation for national scale-up under Phase II.

    D. Environmental and Social Effects

23. Given the nature and scope of activities supported under Phase I of the ALAFIA Multiphase Programmatic Approach,
    adverse environmental and social impacts are expected to be limited, site-specific, and manageable. An
    Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA) has been conducted and confirms that the six core principles
    applicable to Program-for-Results financing are relevant and that Benin’s environmental and social management
    systems are generally adequate to manage the associated risks, while identifying areas where system strengthening
    is required.

24. The Program provides an opportunity to strengthen national and local environmental and social systems in three key
    areas: (i) building institutional capacity—particularly within agencies responsible for nutrition, environment, and local
    governance—and strengthening monitoring, risk screening, and mitigation measures, including safe food handling and
    waste management practices; (ii) improving the application of environmental and social management tools and
    procedures to ensure consistent and effective risk management; and (iii) strengthening grievance redress mechanisms

Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts of the IPF Component


27. The Investment Project Financing (IPF) component will finance analytical work, policy and regulatory support, digital
    systems development, and capacity building to strengthen multisectoral nutrition governance, monitoring and
    evaluation, food safety, supply chains, and local implementation capacity. No civil works are planned under the IPF.
    Environmental and social risks associated with the IPF component are therefore assessed as Moderate, reflecting the
    potential for localized and indirect downstream impacts as policies, systems, and practices are strengthened, as well
    as the need to ensure inclusive participation of vulnerable groups. Social risks relate primarily to stakeholder
    engagement, institutional capacity, and the risk of exclusion if systems are not adequately strengthened. Risks related
    to sexual exploitation and abuse and sexual harassment (SEA/SH) are assessed as low, given the focus on technical
    assistance and capacity building; nevertheless, appropriate mitigation measures will be incorporated, including codes
    of conduct for project personnel, capacity building for implementing agencies, and provisions within the grievance
    redress mechanism to safely receive and address SEA/SH-related complaints.

E. Financing

29. The ALAFIA Program supports implementation of Benin’s National Multisectoral Nutrition and Food Strategy (SNAN
    2026–2030) by complementing government financing and strengthening the public expenditure framework for
    nutrition. The SNAN has an estimated total cost of approximately US$890 million over 2026–2030, of which the ALAFIA
    Program supports a defined slice focused on service delivery, community resilience, and systems strengthening. Phase
    I of the Multiphase Programmatic Approach is financed through a combination of International Development
    Association (IDA) credit and Global Financing Facility (GFF) grant resources, reflecting a balanced financing structure
    that supports results-based implementation while mobilizing catalytic funding.. World Bank financing for Phase I totals
    US$170 million, comprising US$155 million under the Program-for-Results (PforR) component and US$15 million
    under the Investment Project Financing (IPF) component. The PforR supports a defined portion of the Government
    program with a financing envelope of US$285.8 million, complemented by the IPF component for technical assistance
    and systems strengthening. In line with the World Bank’s PforR policy, high-risk and high-value activities are excluded,
    including activities with significant adverse environmental or social impacts and high-value contracts. Government
    resources constitute a substantial share of overall Program financing and are fully integrated within national budget
    processes. While school feeding represents a major cost driver within the SNAN, direct financing of food purchases
    for school meals is excluded from the Program; instead, ALAFIA I focuses on strengthening local production, micro,
    small, and medium enterprises, households, and schools to sustainably improve access to nutritious foods, laying the
    foundation for scale-up under Phase II.

 30. The Program will support a gradual reorientation of public expenditures toward priority nutrition, health, and
     resilience interventions, within Benin’s results-based, medium-term public financial management framework.
     Benin has been engaged since the mid-2010s in a WAEMU-aligned results-based budgeting process, which
