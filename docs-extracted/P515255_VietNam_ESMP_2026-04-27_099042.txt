                                  EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Mekong Delta Climate Resilience and Integrated Transformation Project (MERIT) aims to
enhance climate change adaptation and promote sustainable livelihood development for rural
communities within the project area. The project comprises four main components, of which
Component 2 involves infrastructure investments that may generate direct social impacts,
including to ethnic minority communities (EMCs). Components 1, 3, and 4 primarily focus on
improving technical and technological infrastructure, livelihood development, and
strengthening the capacity of project management units (PMUs) for project implementation,
management, monitoring, and evaluation. Component 3 is the main component prioritized for
integrating activities that support sustainable livelihood development for EMCs within the
project area.
The Ethnic Minority Development Plan (EMDP) has been developed to ensure that EMCs can
fully participate, access information, and equitably benefit from the project, in accordance with
World Bank Environmental and Social Standard 7 (ESS7) and the legal framework of Vietnam.
The EMDP also seeks to safeguard cultural identity, traditional livelihoods, and enhance the
capacity of EMC groups for sustainable development.
Within the project area, EMCs—primarily Khmer communities, alongside a small number of
Hoa and other ethnic groups — live in dispersed settlements and rely heavily on agricultural
production and water resources.
The ESS7 criteria for Indigenous People, in this case referred to as "Ethnic Minorities" possess
the following characteristics: :
       a) self-identification as members of a distinct indigenous social and cultural group and
          recognition of this identity by others;
       b) collective attachment to geographically distinct habitats or ancestral territories and
          to the natural resources in these areas;
       c) customary cultural, economic, social, or political institutions that are separate from
          those of the mainstream society or culture; and
       d) a distinct language or dialect, often different from the official language of the
          country or region.
Based on these four criteria, the Khmer are considered to fully comply as they self-identify as
a distinct ethnic group, possess unique cultural institutions, customs, beliefs, and language, and
have long resided and collective attachment to the project area, thus making ESS7 relevant to
the project.
Regarding the other ethnic groups, the Hoa meet only two criteria, (a) and (d), but do not meet
(b) and (c) because they are a migratory ethnic group across many territories without long-
standing attachment to a specific distinct area; furthermore, their economy is highly developed
and not distinct or separate from the Kinh. Cham and Tay ethnic group meet several
characteristics outlined under ESS7, including self-identification (a), distinct cultural and
religious institutions (c), and use of a separate language (d). Within the project area, Cham and
Tay people migrated to the Mekong for a long time, but they do not live as concentrated
communities, thus, they do not meet the criteria (b). Although the Hoa, Cham and Tay do not

meet all ESS7 criteria, they are still identified as ethnic minority group under the Government
of Vietnam's definition. Therefore, to ensure inclusive and equitable access, while the EMDP
is developed for the Khmer, benefits will extend to the Hoa, Cham and Tay to guarantee their
access to information, consultation, participation, and grievance redress like any other ethnic
minority group in Vietnam.
The positive impacts of the project include improved irrigation infrastructure, enhanced water
management, reduced disaster risks, and support for livelihood development. Potential negative
impacts are mainly short-term during the construction phase, such as localized effects on the
environment, livelihoods, and community safety. The project does not involve land acquisition
or resettlement that would directly affect EMCs.
Proposed mitigation measures include construction management that strictly adheres to
environmental and social safeguard policies under the ESF, protection of culturally sensitive
areas, regular community consultations, and prioritization of local labor. Consultations are
conducted in a culturally appropriate manner, ensuring they are voluntary, fully informed, and
inclusive of vulnerable groups. Results indicate that EMCs generally support the project and
wish to participate in and benefit from project activities.
EMC support activities are mainly concentrated under Component 3, including climate-smart
production training, livelihood development assistance, enhanced market access, and
community capacity building. These activities are designed to be culturally appropriate,
promote gender equality, and ensure long-term sustainability. Under Component 2, while not
affected by land acquisition, EMCs are likely to be impacted during construction activities such
as by dust, noise and other construction-related nuisance described in the Environment and
Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) and mitigated via the Environment and Social Management
Plan (ESMP).
The project will not create a separate budget specifically for the Ethnic Minority Development
Plan; instead, it will focus on integrating these efforts into the sustainable livelihood support
activities throughout the implementation of Component 3.

The implementation of the EMDP is led by central and local PMUs in coordination with
government authorities and EMC communities. A multi-tiered grievance redress mechanism
has been established to ensure transparency, accessibility, and cultural appropriateness.
Monitoring and evaluation are conducted regularly, including both internal and independent
assessments, to track implementation effectiveness and the extent to which EMCs benefit.
Overall, the EMDP ensures that the MERIT project is implemented in an inclusive manner,
without causing significant adverse impacts on EMCs, while contributing to enhanced
resilience and improved sustainable livelihoods for vulnerable populations in the project area.

I.       GENERAL INTRODUCTION
     1. General Information about the Project
1. The Government of Vietnam has requested a loan from the World Bank (WB) to
implement the Climate-Resilient and Integrated Transformation of the Mekong Delta Project
(MERIT). The development objective of the project is to enhance the climate resilience of
agricultural production and rural communities in selected provinces of the Mekong Delta
(MRD) of Vietnam, including Vinh Long, Dong Thap, Tay Ninh provinces, and Can Tho city,
and is expected to be implemented over six years from the commencement date. The specific
objectives of the project include:
•    To develop and improve data systems, disaster monitoring systems, and operational
     management tools for irrigation works and rural clean water supply;
•    To reduce coastal erosion risks;
•    To restore flood retention capacity and environmental conditions in flood-prone areas;
•    To control floods and tidal surges;
•    To support the provision of stable freshwater sources;
•    To promote climate-resilient livelihood diversification and rural economic development.

components as follows:

2.1.   National Legal and Policy Framework on Ethnic Minorities
2.1.1. The Constitution
The Constitution of the Socialist Republic of Vietnam (2013) recognizes the equal rights of all
ethnicities in Vietnam. Under Article 5, the 2013 Constitution stipulates:
   a) The Socialist Republic of Vietnam is a unified nation of all ethnicities living together
      on Vietnamese territory.
   b) All ethnicities are equal, united, and shall respect and assist one another in mutual
      development; all acts of ethnic discrimination and division are strictly prohibited.
   c) The national language is Vietnamese. Every ethnicity has the right to use its own spoken
      and written language, preserve its ethnic identity, and promote its fine customs, habits,
      traditions, and culture.
   d) The State shall implement a policy of comprehensive development and create favorable
      conditions for ethnic minorities to promote their internal resources and develop together
      with the country.
2.1.2. Land Law 2024
Land Law No.31/2024/QH15, dated August 1, 2024, is a critical legal instrument providing
comprehensive regulation of land management and use toward greater transparency, efficiency,
equity, and sustainability, while enhancing decentralization, administrative reform, and the
harmonization of interests among the State, citizens, and investors. The Law introduces several
innovations related to planning, land allocation, land lease, land acquisition, compensation,
support, and resettlement, as well as land access mechanisms for vulnerable groups, including
ethnic minority (EM) communities.
For ethnic minorities, the 2024 Land Law emphasizes priority policies and support to ensure
land access and sustainable livelihoods. Specifically, the State maintains policies for land
allocation, land lease, and recognition of land use rights for EM households and individuals,
particularly in areas with difficult or especially difficult socio-economic conditions. The Law
also encourages the allocation of production and residential land funds for land-deficient EM
households, provides support for vocational transition and livelihood creation upon land
acquisition, and prioritizes access to land-related socio-economic development programs.
Furthermore, the Law strengthens the protection of EM rights during the land acquisition
process through provisions on compensation, support, and resettlement aimed at improving, or
at least maintaining, living conditions compared to those prior to acquisition. Emphasis is also
placed on community consultation, information disclosure, and ensuring the participation of
citizens, including ethnic minorities, to minimize risks and social conflicts while contributing
to the preservation of cultural values and traditional land-use practices of these communities.

2.2.   World Bank Policy on Ethnic Minorities (ESS7)
Environmental and Social Standard 7 (ESS7) aims to ensure that the development process
fosters full respect for the human rights, dignity, aspirations, identity, culture, and natural
resource-based livelihoods of ethnic minorities (defined equivalently to Indigenous Peoples).
ESS7 is also intended to avoid adverse impacts of subprojects on IPs, or when avoidance is not
possible, to implement measures to minimize, mitigate, and/or compensate for such impacts.
ESS7 applies to a distinct social and cultural group as identified in paragraphs 8 and 9 of this
standard. In some countries, such groups are referred to as "Indigenous Peoples" or "Ethnic
Minorities" within the context of Vietnam.
ESS7 applies whenever ethnic minority (EM) people meeting the ESS7 criteria are present or
have a collective attachment to the proposed project area, as determined during the
environmental and social assessment process. This standard applies regardless of whether the
EM community is positively or negatively affected and irrespective of the magnitude of the
impact. ESS7 also applies whether elements of economic, political, or social vulnerability exist,
although the nature and degree of vulnerability will be a primary variable in designing plans to
promote equitable access to benefits or to mitigate adverse impacts.
Under ESS7, the term " Indigenous Peoples" is used in a generic sense to refer to a distinct
                         Indigeno”




social and cultural group possessing the following characteristics in varying degrees:
   (a) Self-identification as members of a distinct indigenous cultural group and recognition
       of this identity by others;
   (b) Collective attachment to geographically distinct habitats or ancestral territories in the
       project area and to the natural resources in these habitats and territories;
   (c) Distinct customary cultural, economic, social, or political institutions that are separate
       from those of the mainstream society or culture; or
   (d) A distinct language or dialect, often different from the official language of the country
       or region.
This standard also applies to EMCs or groups who, during the lifetime of community members,
have lost collective attachment to geographically distinct habitats or ancestral territories in the
project area because of forced severance, conflict, government resettlement programs,
dispossession of land, natural disasters, or incorporation of such territories into an urban area.

                                                                                            Official Use Only
ESS7 also applies to forest dwellers, hunter-gatherers, pastoralists, or other nomadic groups,
subject to meeting the criteria in paragraph 8.

The primary objectives of ESS7 are:

       a. To ensure that the project development process fosters full respect for the human rights,
          dignity, aspirations, identity, culture, and natural resource-based livelihoods of EMCs.
       b. To avoid adverse impacts of projects on EMCs, or when avoidance is not feasible, to
          minimize, mitigate, and/or compensate for such impacts.
       c. To enhance sustainable development benefits and opportunities for EMCs in a manner
          that is accessible, inclusive, and culturally appropriate.
       d. To improve project design and promote local support by establishing and maintaining
          an ongoing relationship based on meaningful consultation with project-affected EMCs
          throughout the project’s life cycle.
       e. To obtain the Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) of affected EMCs in the three
          specific circumstances described in ESS7.To recognize, respect, and preserve the
          culture, knowledge, and practices of EMCs, and to provide them with opportunities to
          adapt to changing conditions in a manner and timeframe acceptable to them.
In accordance with ESS7, this EMDP will be applied to the MERIT Project, providing guidance
on the preliminary screening of ethnic minority communities, the conduct of social assessments,
and the identification of mitigation measures, grievance redress mechanisms, gender-sensitive
considerations, and monitoring arrangements. However, FPIC is not applicable at this stage, as
the Project’s environmental and social impacts have been assessed as not involving land
acquisition, resettlement, or livelihood restrictions affecting ethnic minority communities.
Nevertheless, during the implementation of the EMDP, should any unforeseen impacts arise
that adversely affect ethnic minority communities in terms of resettlement or livelihoods, the
EMDP will be updated accordingly and the FPIC will be obtained, if needed based on ESS7
requirements. As per WB ESS7, Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) is required in the
following circumstances: (a) adverse impacts on land and natural resources traditionally owned
or customarily used or occupied; (b) result in the relocation of ethnic minority communities
from land and natural resources subject to traditional ownership or customary use or
occupation; or (c) have significant impacts on the cultural heritage of ethnic minority
communities, including material aspects that are integral to their identity and/or cultural,
ceremonial, or spiritual aspects of their lives.


III.     SOCIAL IMPACT ASSESSMENT
3.1       Objectives and Methods of Social Impact Assessment
3.1.1. Objectives
The objectives of the Social Impact Assessment (SIA) is to:
(i) Evaluate both positive and negative impacts on the livelihoods and service accessibility of
       ethnic minorities;


                                                                                                16
                                                                                            Official Use Only
(ii) Assess the needs, aspirations, and level of project support within ethnic minority
      communities;
(iii) Identify conditions to enhance project benefits; and
(iv) Propose appropriate mitigation measures to ensure the project is socially inclusive,
      culturally appropriate for ethnic minority communities, and compliant with the ESS7 within
      the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF).
The core goal is to ensure that Khmer, Hoa, and other ethnic minority groups in the Mekong
Delta are not only protected from negative impacts but also benefit from the project in a
culturally suitable manner.
3.1.2. Assessment Methods
(i) Secondary Data Analysis:
This involves collecting, synthesizing, and analyzing available documents such as provincial
and commune socio-economic reports, as well as documentation from other relevant regional
projects (e.g., WB9, JICA, IUCN, FAO, GIZ). Statistical yearbooks and the 2024 results of the
ethnic minority survey across 53 provinces were also incorporated.
(ii) Quantitative Method:
Questionnaires were used to collect quantitative data on income, education levels, and the
degree of dependence on natural resources (rice, shrimp, and wild harvesting) of ethnic minority
households in the project area. A total of 81 households were surveyed using a stratified random
sampling method based on each sub-project and ethnic group. Out of the 81 surveyed
households, 72 were Khmer and 9 were Hoa.
(iii): Qualitative Method:
In-depth interviews: Conducted 25 in-depth interviews with "prestigious individuals" in the
community, such as local ethnic affairs officers, to understand the overall economic, cultural,
and social landscape, as well as the specific beliefs and religions of ethnic minorities in the
project area.
Focus group discussions: Conducted 14 focus group discussions with ethnic minority
households to assess current socio-economic conditions, cultural and religious specificities,
potential project impacts, and community-proposed mitigation measures.
Ethnic minority community consultations: Conducted 14 community consultations. Gather
multi-dimensional information on specific socio-economic and cultural-religious conditions,
the impacts of project implementation on their lives, and their aspirations regarding measures
to mitigate impacts arising from the project construction process.
3.2      Characteristics of Ethnic Minorities in the Project Area
3.2.1 Characteristics of Ethnic Minorities in the Mekong Delta
The Mekong Delta has long been a shared homeland for many ethnic groups, primarily
consisting of the Kinh, Khmer, Hoa, and Cham. According to 2024 statistics, the region is home
to approximately 1.3–1.4 million ethnic minority people, accounting for nearly 8–9% of the
total regional population. Among these groups, the Khmer are the most populous community
with about 1.2–1.3 million people (representing over 90% of the total ethnic minority

                                                                                             17
                                                                                         Official Use Only
population), concentrated mainly in Vinh Long, Can Tho City, Dong Thap, and Tay Ninh. The
Hoa population stands at approximately 200,000–250,000 people, widely distributed across
Vinh Long, Can Tho City, and the former Ca Mau province area. The Cham people have a
smaller population (roughly several tens of thousands) and live primarily in An Giang,
particularly in Tan Chau and An Phu.
EMCs in the Mekong Delta possess a rich spiritual life closely tied to traditional religions and
beliefs. The Khmer follow Theravada Buddhism, with a system of pagodas serving as centers
for socio-cultural activities; the Cham follow Islam, with mosques (masjids) as vital spaces for
religious life; while the Hoa maintain folk beliefs and traditional assembly halls. Festivals such
as Ok Om Bok and Ngo boat racing by the Khmer, as well as community festivals of the Hoa
and Cham, contribute significantly to the region's cultural vibrancy. This long history of
cohabitation has fostered deep cultural intersection among the ethnic groups, forming a spirit
of solidarity and mutual support.
The Khmer are heavily involved in agricultural production, especially rice cultivation and
aquaculture; the Hoa possess strengths in commerce, services, and handicrafts; and the Cham
are notable for small-scale trading, traditional weaving, and livestock farming. However, a
segment of the ethnic minority population, particularly among the Khmer, still faces challenges
regarding livelihoods, education levels, and access to basic services compared to the regional
average.
3.2.2 Characteristics of Ethnic Minorities in Project Provinces
Can Tho City: Can Tho City is diverse, with many ethnic minority groups living together,
primarily the Khmer, Hoa, and a small number of Cham. The total ethnic minority population
is estimated to account for about 8-10% of the city's total population (equivalent to
approximately 330,000 - 420,000 people), concentrated heavily in areas formerly belonging to
Soc Trang and Hau Giang provinces. The Khmer community, with traditional festivals such as
Ok Om Bok and Ngo boat racing, along with the cultures of the Hoa and Kinh people, has
created a rich cultural identity. This contributes to promoting cultural tourism and strengthening
community cohesion during the city's major urban development process in the region.
Dong Thap Province: The ethnic minority community in Dong Thap accounts for a relatively
small proportion, estimated at about 2-3% of the province's total population (equivalent to
approximately 90,000 - 130,000 people). The main ethnic minority groups include the Khmer,
Hoa, and Cham, of which the Khmer are the largest group, distributed mainly in rural and border
areas, linked to agriculture and aquaculture livelihoods. The Hoa are more concentrated in
urban areas and participate strongly in trade and service activities, while the Cham community
is smaller and scattered.
Tay Ninh Province: The ethnic minority community in Tay Ninh accounts for about 1.5 - 2%
of the province's total population (equivalent to approximately 50,000 - 65,000 people). The
primary ethnic minority groups include the Khmer, Cham, and Hoa. Among them, the Khmer
are a significant group, distributed in several rural and boundary areas and linked to agricultural
livelihoods. The Cham community is smaller in scale, maintaining certain unique cultural and
religious traits, while the Hoa mainly reside in urban areas and actively participate in trade and
service activities.

                                                                                            Official Use Only
Ethnic minority communities in Tay Ninh continue to preserve their distinctive traditional
cultural values, as reflected in their languages, beliefs, festivals, and community practices.
Khmer communities are closely associated with Theravada Buddhism and traditional festivals;
Cham communities maintain their unique cultural and religious practices; while Hoa
communities sustain a rich cultural and spiritual life linked to clan associations and traditional
folk festivals.
Vinh Long Province: The province is relatively diverse, with ethnic minority communities
(EMCs) accounting for a comparatively high proportion relative to the regional average,
estimated at approximately 12–15% of the population (equivalent to around 500,000–630,000
people). Among these, the Khmer constitute the largest EMC group, primarily concentrated in
areas formerly part of Tra Vinh province, with livelihoods closely linked to agriculture and
aquaculture, and with a strong presence of Khmer Theravada Buddhist pagodas. In addition,
the Hoa community is mainly distributed in urban areas and economic centers, actively engaged
in trade and service sectors; while the Cham community is smaller in size and more sparsely
distributed.
Ethnic minority communities in Vinh Long continue to preserve and promote a wide range of
distinctive traditional cultural values. Khmer communities are characterized by their pagoda
systems, religious–cultural festivals, and unique language; Hoa communities maintain their
customs, beliefs, and community activities associated with clan associations; while Cham
communities sustain their distinctive cultural and religious practices.

3.3.4. Cultural Life
Khmer pagodas are the hubs for the community's socio-cultural activities. Each pagoda houses
several monks (known as "ong luc") and is headed by a chief monk. Before reaching adulthood,
Khmer youth often enter the pagoda to study and cultivate their character and knowledge.
Currently, there are over 400 Khmer pagodas in the Southern region. These pagodas often teach
production experiences and the Khmer language.

The Khmer people live interspersed with the Kinh and Hoa communities in phums, socs, and
hamlets. Within the project area, they are well-integrated with the Kinh people. The Khmer of
the Southern region possess many unique customs and a distinctive artistic culture. Major
pagodas typically have ensembles for drums, horns, and traditional instruments. Every year, the
Khmer celebrate numerous festivals and traditional holidays.
Major festivals for the Khmer include Chol Chnam Thmay (New Year), Buddha's Birthday,
Sen Dolta (Ancestors' Day), and Ok Om Bok (Moon Worship).
3.3.5. Religion
Both the Khmer and Hoa follow Buddhism. Additionally, they maintain the belief in worshiping
Neak-ta (a guardian spirit of the phums, socs, and families), which is a deeply rooted traditional
cultural feature of the Khmer people.
3.3.6. Marriage
In terms of marriage, the majority of Khmer people marry within their own locality, most
commonly within the same phum, soc, or commune. In the family, the husband or male
members typically act as the head of the household. The Hoa people have marriage relations
and wedding customs similar to those of the Kinh.
c) Economic Activities:
In general, the main livelihood of ethnic minorities remains agriculture, with rice and secondary
crops being common activities alongside aquaculture (shrimp and fish). Ethnic minority groups
develop a comprehensive economy, including raising cattle for plowing, as well as pigs,
chickens, ducks, and fish, and maintaining traditional crafts such as weaving, pottery, and
making sugar from palmyra trees. Additionally, Khmer households have participated in
aquaculture activities within the project area. The Hoa population in the project area is very
small and typically focuses on trade, small services, retail, food processing, or household
businesses.

3.4     Social Impact Assessment for Affected Ethnic Minorities within the Project Area
        3.4.1. Positive Impacts
Improved Agricultural Production Conditions
One of the most distinct positive impacts of the MERIT Project is the enhancement of water
resource management and regulation, thereby improving production conditions for ethnic
minority (EM) communities. Through components such as canal upgrades and the construction
or renovation of irrigation works, the supply and drainage systems will operate more stably,
helping to mitigate local flooding during the rainy season and water shortages during the dry

                                                                                                  24
                                                                                           Official Use Only
season. For Khmer households, who depend heavily on rice cultivation and aquaculture, this is
a key factor in reducing production risks. Reality in the project area shows that many
households are frequently affected by fluctuating water sources, especially amidst increasing
saltwater intrusion; therefore, better water control will enable residents to be more proactive
with crop seasons, stabilize yields, and enhance economic efficiency.
Enhanced Climate Change Resilience
Subprojects within the MERIT framework, particularly salinity control and anti-erosion items,
contribute to increasing the resilience of EMCs against climate change impacts. Minimizing
saltwater intrusion and stabilizing water sources not only protects production land but also
facilitates the maintenance of adaptive livelihood models such as rice-shrimp farming. Evidence
from the Mekong Delta indicates that water control structures play a vital role in minimizing
damage caused by drought and salinity, helping residents maintain more stable livelihoods. For
Khmer households with limited resources, mitigating these environmental shocks is especially
significant in preventing the risk of falling back into poverty.
Improved Waterway Transport and Market Access
The upgrading of canal systems and related structures not only serves production but also
contributes to improving field and waterway transport. This creates more favorable conditions
for transporting agricultural products, input materials, and the general movement of residents.
For the Hoa community, who traditionally participate in trade and small-scale services,
improved infrastructure connectivity opens up opportunities for business expansion and
increased goods exchange. In practice, when transport infrastructure is improved, shipping
costs decrease and market accessibility increases, thereby contributing to the development of
household and local economies.
Job Opportunities and Temporary Income Generation
During the construction phase, the project may create short-term employment opportunities for
local labor. Simple tasks such as material transport, construction support, or other auxiliary
activities can be prioritized for recruitment from communities within the project area. This can
provide some households, including EM and poor households, with an additional source of
temporary income. Although the duration is not long, this supplemental income remains
meaningful for families facing difficult economic conditions.
Increased Stability and Sustainability of EM Household Livelihoods
A long-term positive impact of the project is the increased stability of livelihoods for EMCs.
When risks related to water sources and natural disasters are better controlled, residents can
confidently invest in production, including transitioning to higher-value economic models. This
is particularly important for Khmer households with small-scale production, who are highly
vulnerable to natural fluctuations. Furthermore, a stable production environment facilitates
asset accumulation and improved living standards over time.
Strengthened Participation of EM Communities
During project preparation and implementation, community consultation, information sharing,
and opinion exchanges with local residents are conducted. This is an opportunity for EM
households to express their perspectives, needs, and concerns regarding the project. When
residents are provided with full information and encouraged to participate in the decision-

                                                                                             25
                                                                                         Official Use Only
making process, the level of consensus and support for the project is typically higher.
Simultaneously, this contributes to enhancing the role and voice of EMCs in local development
activities and enhancing their interpersonal skills to help them to integrate confidently and make
positive contributions to the project.
       3.4.2. Negative Impacts
There are no impacts related to land acquisition or assets on land for ethnic minority (EM)
households within the Project. However, there may be some potential negative impacts on EM
households during the construction of infrastructure items under Component 2 of the Project.
These impacts are presented in detail below.
Localized Environmental Impacts: Dust, Noise, and Water Quality
During project implementation, construction activities may generate dust, noise, and waste,
affecting the living environment of EM households residing near the worksites. Specifically,
dredging activities may increase turbidity in canal water, affecting the quality of water used for
aquaculture in the short term. This is a common practical issue recorded in canal dredging
projects, where silt and suspended solids levels rise during construction. However, these
impacts are localized, temporary, and controllable through appropriate environmental
management measures such as site hoarding, construction timing control, and water quality
monitoring.
Temporary Influence on Livelihoods
Dredging and construction activities may disrupt water intake for agricultural production and
aquaculture. For Khmer households dependent on surface water, changes in flow or restricted
access to water for short periods could affect crop schedules or yields. However, these impacts
usually only persist during the construction period and can be mitigated through logical
construction scheduling.
Disruption of Waterway Traffic and Daily Activities
During construction on the canals, the movement of watercraft may face difficulties or localized
interruptions. Some canal sections may have restricted traffic to facilitate dredging or
construction activities. This could affect the transportation of agricultural products, commuting,
or the daily routines of local residents. For EM households living in areas heavily dependent on
waterway transport, obstruction to waterway transportation, leading to localized congestion,
may occur if construction schedules and plans are not widely disclosed and adequately
communicated to local communities in the project area.
Safety Risks during Construction
The presence of machinery, equipment, and construction vehicles may increase safety risks if
not managed well. Construction sites, building materials, or operating vehicles can pose
potential accident risks. These risks are particularly noteworthy for children, the elderly, or
people with disabilities—groups with a lower capacity for self-protection. Therefore, ensuring
safety measures and warning signs at construction areas is essential.
Social Risks Related to Migrant Labor
During the construction phase, the project may mobilize labor from outside the locality. The
presence of migrant workers, if not well-managed and coordinated with local authorities, can

                                                                                               26
                                                                                           Official Use Only
sometimes lead to minor community conflicts. Additionally, concerns regarding security, order,
or other social issues may arise. However, these risks can generally be mitigated through strict
labor management and enhanced coordination with the local community.
Disproportionate Impacts on Vulnerable Groups
The negative impacts of the project, though temporary, may not be distributed evenly across all
population groups. Certain vulnerable groups—such as poor households, female-headed
households, the elderly, people with disabilities, or EM households with limited resources—
may face greater difficulty adapting to changes during the construction period. It should be
noted, however, that the number of vulnerable households in the project area is small, scattered
across only a few communes within the Component 2 subprojects.
       3.4.3. Mitigation Measures
Regarding the impacts during the construction process, they will be mitigated through the
application of the project's environmental and social safeguard measures. Based on the potential
impacts identified above, the following mitigation measures are proposed:
Strengthen Consultation and Information Provision for EM Communities
Throughout the project implementation, meaningful community consultation activities must be
maintained regularly and conducted in a manner accessible to local people. Information
regarding construction plans, schedules for specific items, as well as any changes that might
affect residents' daily lives, must be announced early and clearly. For EMCs, communication
should use simple, easy-to-understand language, potentially integrated through village or
residential group meetings, or via village heads and reputable persons in the community. This
helps residents better understand the project while creating conditions for them to contribute
ideas and report issues arising during implementation.
Limit Impacts During the Construction Process
Contractors must apply technical measures to minimize environmental and social impacts
during the construction phase. For instance, dust should be controlled by periodic water
spraying in active construction areas; work should be restricted during residents' rest hours; and
noise levels must be kept within permissible limits. Dredging activities should be carried out in
sections to minimize the impact on water sources and livelihoods. Additionally, logical
construction scheduling is required to minimize disruptions to waterway traffic and the daily
activities of the community. In addition, it is necessary to have a reasonable construction plan,
implement traffic diversion, publicly announce the construction schedule, and minimize
disruptions to waterway traffic and the daily activities of the community. Specific mitigation
measures are included in the project’s Environment and Social Management Plan.
Ensure Safety for Residents, Especially Vulnerable Groups
Construction areas must be equipped with warning signs, barriers, and appropriate safety
measures. This is particularly crucial for areas near residential zones, schools, or places where
many children live. Contractors also need to disseminate safety regulations to both workers and
residents living around the site. For children, the elderly, and people with disabilities—groups
with limited self-protection capabilities—special attention is required to minimize the risk of
accidents. These measures are part of the ESMP.


                                                                                               27
                                                                                           Official Use Only
Prioritize the Use of Local Labor
To the extent possible, contractors should prioritize recruiting local laborers for suitable tasks
during the construction phase. This not only helps create temporary income for residents but
also contributes to strengthening the bond between the project and the community. For EM or
poor households in the project area, having additional—albeit short-term—employment
opportunities can help partially improve their family's economic conditions.
Labor Management and Social Risk Prevention
Contractors must effectively manage labor, particularly workers from other localities. Workers
need to be informed of local regulations, community codes of conduct, and requirements for
maintaining security and order. Simultaneously, coordination with local authorities is essential
to manage worker housing and daily living activities to mitigate potential social risks. Building
a relationship of respect and cooperation between workers and the local community is also a
vital factor in maintaining a stable social environment within the project area. The project has
prepared Labor Management Procedures (LMP) to manage labor-related risks.
Establishment of a Grievance Redress Mechanism
A transparent and accessible grievance redress mechanism (GRM) must be established so that
residents can report issues arising during project implementation. Local people, including
ethnic minority (EM) households and vulnerable groups, must be clearly informed on how to
submit feedback or complaints. Feedback from the community should be recorded and
processed promptly through coordination between the Project Management Unit (PMU), local
authorities, and contractors. This mechanism ensures that emerging issues are resolved quickly
while strengthening community trust in the project.
Attention to Vulnerable Groups
During project implementation, special attention must be paid to the needs and challenges of
vulnerable groups, such as poor households, female-headed households, the elderly, people
with disabilities, and EM households with limited economic conditions. When organizing
consultation activities or providing information, conditions should be created to enable these
groups to participate and express their opinions. Integrating appropriate support measures will
contribute to ensuring that project benefits are shared equitably and do not exacerbate inequality
within the community. Mitigation measures for vulnerable groups should be elaborated in detail
in the Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP), which is a key document typically
prepared during the project preparation stage. This document forms an integral part of the
Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA), aiming to identify risk mitigation
measures prior to project approval and implementation. The ESMP includes management
measures to be applied throughout both the construction and operational phases of the Project.
In general, if mitigation measures are implemented fully and effectively, the negative impacts
of the project on EMCs and vulnerable groups will be minimized. Simultaneously, the project
can bring many practical, long-term benefits to the local community, particularly in improving
production conditions, enhancing climate change resilience, and strengthening infrastructure
that supports the residents' livelihoods.

VI. IMPLEMENTATION ORGANIZATION
The implementation of ethnic minority (EM) development activities requires the participation
of local, national, provincial, and commune agencies and organizations. The Central Project
Management Unit for Water Resources Projects (CPO) under the Ministry of Agriculture and
Rural Development is the Project Owner and is responsible for the overall coordination of the
Project's EM development action plan.
Central Project Office for Irrigation Projects (CPO):
As the Project Owner, the CPO holds overall responsibility for directing, coordinating, and
supervising the preparation, approval, and implementation of the EMDP in accordance with
Government regulations and Donor requirements. The CPO ensures that EMDP contents are
integrated into the project's master plan; guides and inspects implementation at the subproject
level; synthesizes periodic reports; and coordinates with the World Bank (WB) in monitoring
and evaluating compliance with ethnic minority policies.
Provincial/City People's Committees in the Project Area:
Provincial People's Committees are responsible for organizing the implementation of the
EMDP locally. This includes directing the integration of EM support contents into socio-
economic development plans; ensuring the allocation of necessary resources; supervising the
implementation of impact mitigation measures and development activities; directing
compensation and support work (if any); and coordinating with relevant agencies during the
EMDP implementation process.
Department of Agriculture and Rural Development:
The Department of Agriculture and Rural Development acts as the local technical focal point,
coordinating with the CPO and relevant units to organize the implementation of EMDP
activities. The Department is responsible for mainstreaming EMDP contents into subproject
execution, tracking progress, monitoring the implementation of mitigation measures and
livelihood support, and providing periodic implementation reports.
Provincial Committee for Ethnic Minority and Religious Affairs:
The Provincial Committee for Ethnic Minority and Religious Affairs is responsible for
participating in the consultation, appraisal, and monitoring of EMDP implementation. It ensures
that project activities align with ethnic policies and the cultural characteristics of local EMCs.
Additionally, this agency supports the promotion of meaningful consultation, safeguards the
rights and interests of EM people, and contributes to enhancing the effectiveness of culturally
appropriate development activities within the project area.

   VII.    GRIEVANCE REDRESS MECHANISM (GRM)
The Project’s Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) has been designed to ensure that
complaint submission and resolution processes are culturally appropriate, accessible, and
acceptable to all affected households, with particular attention to Ethnic Minority (EM)
communities. During project preparation, consultations were conducted with local authorities,
EM representatives, and community members to identify culturally, appropriate channels for
raising and resolving complaints. These consultations confirmed that EM communities often
rely on informal institutions, traditional leaders (e.g. village heads, clan elders, respected
persons), and mass organizations as trusted intermediaries in conflict resolution. Accordingly,
the Project GRM integrates both formal administrative procedures and existing traditional and
community-based mechanisms for grievance handling, especially in cases involving EM
specific concerns, customary practices, or collective issues.
The project communication hotline for receiving public feedback on project-related issues will
be maintained throughout the entire implementation period, including the CPO hotline, focal
contact points of commune-level local authorities, and village heads/respected persons within
the community. All issues related to ethnic minority communities will be fully recorded and
reflected in both the internal monitoring and independent monitoring of the Project.
Grievances may be submitted through multiple channels, such as in writing, via hotlines,
contact numbers of responsible focal persons, or online platforms (where available). The receipt
of grievances will be acknowledged by the designated PMU GRM/SEP focal person through
appropriate communication channels. Grievances will then be verified and investigated in
coordination with relevant stakeholders (including local authorities, contractors, and
supervision consultants).
In EM communities, grievances may be submitted not only through formal administrative
channels but also through culturally appropriate entry points, including village heads, clan
elders, respected persons, and representatives of local mass organizations (e.g. Women’s Union,
Farmers’ Union, Fatherland Front). These individuals and organizations will assist EM
complainants particularly elderly persons, women, and vulnerable households in articulating
grievances, documenting complaints, and conveying them to the Commune People’s
Committee (CPC) or the CPO and ICMB10. Verbal complaints submitted through these
traditional or community-based channels will be formally recorded by the CPC or CPO,
ICMB10 and treated with the same validity as written complaints.
The Project's Grievance Redress Mechanism will be implemented in accordance with the Law
on Complaints (Law No.02/2011/QH13, dated November 11, 2011) and the Law on
Denunciations (Law No.25/2018/QH14, dated June 12, 2018). Complaints will be resolved
through two administrative steps before being brought to court.
The GRM applies to individuals or groups directly or indirectly affected by the Project, as well
as those interested in and/or capable of influencing Project outcomes—both positively and
negatively. All complaints and grievances must be properly documented in writing by the CPO,
with copies sent to the commune and provincial levels.
If affected EM people are dissatisfied with the process, compensation, mitigation measures, or
any other issue, they—or their representatives or village elders—may lodge a complaint with
the Provincial People's Committee (PPC) or the CPO and ICMB10 according to the grievance

                                                                                             37
                                                                                         Official Use Only
redress mechanism established in the EMDP. All complaints will be resolved promptly and in
a manner consistent with the culture of the affected EM people. All costs related to EM
grievances are waived for the complainants. The CPO and the independent monitoring
consultant are responsible for tracking the progress of grievance resolution for EM people.
Every grievance case must be recorded in the CPO project files and regularly reviewed by the
independent monitoring consultant.
The steps in the grievance and complaint process are as follows:
First Level – Commune People’s Committee (CPC)
    o    An affected person may submit their complaint to the One-Stop Reception1 under the
         Commune People’s Committee (CPC), either in writing or verbally. The CPC member
         at the One-Stop Reception is responsible for notifying CPC leadership of the complaint
         for resolution. The CPC Chairperson will meet privately with severely affected persons
         and resolve the complaint within 30 days of receipt. The CPC Secretariat is responsible
         for documenting and maintaining records of all complaints handled by the CPC.
    o    Affected people may also submit complaint letters directly to project-level agencies
         such as the CPO/ICMB10 GRM/SEP Focal Points. In case ethnic minority people
         submit complaint letters directly to these agencies/units, the complaints will be
         forwarded to the commune/ward-level People's Committee for resolution in accordance
         with the proper complaint resolution procedures.
Second Level – Provincial People’s Committee (PPC)
    o    If, after 30 days, the complainant receives no response from the CPC or is dissatisfied
         with the resolution, they may submit their complaint to any member of the PPC in
         writing or verbally, or initiate an administrative lawsuit at the District People's Court.
         The PPC has 45 days to satisfactorily resolve the complaints. The PPC Secretariat is
         responsible for documenting and archiving all handled complaints. The affected person
         may still file a lawsuit in court if they wish.
Third Level – Court
    o     If, after 45 days from the date of filing the complaint with the PPC, the complainant
         receives no response or remains dissatisfied with the outcome, they may take the case
         to court for trial. The court's decision will be final. The results of the grievance
         resolution must be sent to the complainant and relevant parties and must be posted at
         the headquarters of the People's Committee where the complaint was resolved. After
         three days, the decision/result must be posted at the commune/ward level, and after
         seven days at the district or provincial level.
