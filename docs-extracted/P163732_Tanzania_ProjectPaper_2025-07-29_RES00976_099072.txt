    I. PROJECT STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING
1.       The Tanzania Sustainable Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Program (SRWSSP) is a US$655 million Program-
for-Results (PforR) operation. The Program was approved on June 26, 2018, with an initial IDA credit of SDR 243.4 million
(US$350 million equivalent) and became effective on July 15, 2019. Additional Financing (AF) of SDR 234.4 million (US$300
million equivalent) and a grant of US$4.931 million from the Energy Sector Management Assistance Program (ESMAP)
were approved on December 14, 2022. Both were declared effective on April 17, 2023. The current Program closing date
is July 31, 2025. As of May 6, 2025, the Program has disbursed 92.5 percent (US$599.18 million equivalent).

    2.     The Program Development Objectives (PDO) of SRWSSP is to increase access to rural water supply and
    sanitation services in participating districts and strengthen the capacity of selected sector institutions to sustain service
    delivery. The Program is in its fifth year of implementation, and both progress towards achieving the PDO and overall
    implementation progress are rated Satisfactory. The Program has three Results Areas and an IPF component. Each result
    area and associated Disbursement-Linked Indicators (DLIs) are summarized as below:
            (i) Results Area 1- Sustainable access to improved water services in rural areas, providing increased access to
                  well-designed, properly constructed, and well-managed rural water supply services (both new and
                  rehabilitated) and sustaining existing water points to ensure they remain functional and provide adequate
                  service levels (DLI #1, 2 and 10).
            (ii) Results Area 2- Increased access to improved sanitation services in rural areas, achieved through promotion
                  of hygiene practices and improved sanitation at household level; construction and rehabilitation of
                  institutional water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) facilities at primary schools and health care facilities; and
                  Community-Wide Sanitation (DLI #3, 4, 5 and 9).
            (iii) Results Area 3 - Strengthening of the capacity of sector institutions to sustain service delivery in rural areas,
                  through better data management for reporting and decision-making; strengthening of technical and financial
                  capacities of Community-based Water Supply Organizations (CBWSOs); and establishment and
                  operationalization of the Rural Water Supply and Sanitation Authority (RUWASA) (DLI #6, 7, and 8).
            (iv) Investment Project Financing (IPF) component (US$20 million) to provide technical support to address
                  capacity gaps and institutional deficiencies.

3.       Several changes were introduced during the discussion of the AF. The Program started with 87 rural districts in
17 regions that have low coverage of water supply and sanitation services, high stunting and poverty rates. In the AF, the
geographical boundary was expanded, and the Program now covers 137 rural districts in 25 regions. Through the AF, some
targets and unit costs were also revised for the additional resources for all Results Areas. Two new DLIs were also added
(DLI 9 and 10) to support WASH facilities in healthcare centers and to promote Public Private Partnerships (PPPs) in water
supply schemes.

4.       More than 10.2 million people have gained access to improved water supply with adequate pressure and closer
to their houses, over five years of verified results under Results Area 1 (Sustainable access to improved water services
in rural areas), which exceeded the end target of 8.85 million. Results of the sustainably functioning public water points
as of June 30, 2025, are 83,202 (out of 80,000 target).

5.     Under Results Area 2 (Sustainable access to improved sanitation services in rural areas), 12.35 million people
have gained access to improved sanitation facilities, which is over the target of 9.04 million.1 Cumulatively, there are a

Results in school WASH and WASH in Health Care Facilities (HCFs) are also making steady progress. To date, 2,593 HCFs
and 1,830 primary schools have been provided with improved sanitation and hand washing facilities through the Program
(program end target after AF is 2,636 HCFs and 1,853 schools, respectively).

6.       Result Area 3 (Strengthen the capacity of sector institutions to sustain service delivery in rural areas) has
continued to progress well. Cumulatively, 11,628 villages were under the management of registered CBWSOs with
improved operation and maintenance (O&M) capacity for water supply services. RUWASA was established in June 2019
with established and operational audit unit and quality assurance and quality control (QA&QC) mechanisms. There has
been improvement in data collection and management by Implementing Agencies. For example, for RUWASA, 118 out of
137 districts managed to submit timely, accurate and complete sector monitoring and evaluation data during the fifth
year of verification compared to 58 in the third-year verification. For the Ministry of Health (MoH), 67 districts submitted
timely, accurate and complete sector monitoring data, similar to year 3 results, and the Ministry of Education, Science and
Technology (MoEST) had 38 districts that all meet the criteria of sector monitoring data.

7.      The Program has been very successful in making progress on most of the DLIs. Table 1 below highlights the results
to date, for all years of verification, across the whole Program. As it shows, verified results for DLI 1, 2, 3 and 6 exceeded
the end targets, while other DLIs 4, 5, and 9 achieved 87 to 99 percent of the targets. On the other hand, DLI 7, 8 and 10
did not meet the target. Nevertheless, these institutional DLIs initiated discussion on critical sectoral discussion, such as
support for fecal sludge management in rural areas, energy tariff for water supply schemes, and data management for the
WASH sector, and they will be continued in a subsequent phase of the Program.

B       Rationale for Restructuring

1.       The proposed Level II restructuring is to reallocate the funds corresponding to the missed targets to the ones
that exceeded the targets. In particular, this restructuring paper seeks approval of the reallocation of budget from DLI 4,
5, 7, and 9 to DLI 1, 3 and 6 and their associated results, so that the results achieved in year 5 can be fully disbursed.

2.      Based on the Results Achievement Notification for year 5, dated July 23, 2025, the achieved and verified results
would be worth SDR 114,304,325.70 of disbursement. The program also has a total of SDR 46,130,913.50 of advance
already paid through two loans (SDR 16,130,913.50 from Ln. 62950 and SDR 30,000,000 from Ln. 72180). The eligible
disbursement after recovering the advance, therefore, would be SDR 68,173,412.20. Table 2 below summarizes the
recovery from each DLI.
