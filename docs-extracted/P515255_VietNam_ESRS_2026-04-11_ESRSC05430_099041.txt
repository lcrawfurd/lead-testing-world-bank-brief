                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The proposed MERIT project helps to operationalize the vision and strategies laid out in the 2022 Mekong Delta
                     Regional Master Plan and address current and future climate change impacts. The project would (i) enhance the
                     information and management tools to support adaptive delta management; (ii) build new infrastructure and repurpose
                     and upgrade existing regional infrastructure to enhance climate resilience and promote more sustainable and climate-
                     smart management of water and land resources; and (iii) facilitate climate-smart livelihoods transition and

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The operation will focus on Vietnam’s Mekong Delta Region (MDR), a low-lying delta plain with mixed soil types and a
                     mosaic of freshwater, brackish, and saline habitats, including extensive wetlands, mangroves, and coastal ecosystems—
                     areas of high biodiversity but also sensitivity to disturbance. Key waterways include the Tien and Hau distributaries and
                     a dense canal network; seasonal flooding delivers sediment and nutrients that sustain fisheries and wetlands, though
                     upstream dams have sharply reduced sediment, contributing to erosion and subsidence. Climate change and sea-level
                     rise intensify salinity intrusion, with dry-season events documented far inland (e.g., 2015–16) that threaten rice and
                     aquaculture systems. The MDR lies within the Mekong international waterway; Vietnam is the lowest downstream
                     riparian. The MDR comprises 5 provinces and Can Tho city, with a majority rural population and notable ethnic diversity.
                     Khmer communities are concentrated in Tra Vinh and Soc Trang and often face socio economic disadvantages if
                     compared to the larger ethic groups, which necessitates greater inclusive engagement and benefit-sharing.
                     Opportunities center on nature based solutions, resilient water management, sediment and habitat restoration, and
                     inclusive design that strengthens agriculture and aquaculture value chains.

                     The environmental risk rating is Substantial. Direct impacts stem from dredging; embankment/riverbank upgrades;
                     wave breakers; and sluices/ship-locks, including UXO hazards. Construction risks include short-term water-quality
                     degradation and benthic disturbance; water/soil pollution from large dredged volumes; erosion and slope instability
                     affecting riverside structures and communities; acidity/salinity mobilization in acid-sulfate and saline soils; OHS
                     exposures during waterway/marine/coastal works; and disruptions to nearby utilities and canal-side structures.
                     Indirect impacts include pressures from sourcing construction materials; temporary disruption to water supply and
                     waterway access/transport; and community health and safety concerns. Climate-vulnerability risks include rising flood
                     and storm-surge hazards, intensifying salinity intrusion, worsening drought/heat, erosion-driven river/canal
                     instability, and deteriorating water quality. Cumulative impacts may include altered hydrology, sediment/coastal
                     change, degraded water quality, salinity intrusion, and ecological/fisheries stress. Potential transboundary effects
                     include cross-border dispersion of turbidity/sediment or contaminants and impacts on shared fisheries and migratory
                     species. Project operations may alter flow regimes and reduce flushing, shifting salinity and concentrating
                     pollutants/nutrients—degrading water quality.

                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The social risk is categorized as Substantial. The main social risks stem from: (i) Land and non-land asset acquisition
                     arising from physical investments (e.g. sluice gates, sea dykes, canal rehabilitation or upgrading, and modernisation of
                     water resources management infrastructure). This may be further complicated where some affected households are
                     classified as encroachers under national regulations, including households belonging to vulnerable groups.; (ii) ethnic
                     minority people (mainly Khmer) in the project area not benefitting the same from the project; (iii) the potential need
                     to develop successful mechanisms to support farmers to transition to the new proposed livelihood adaptation




Public Disclosure
                     models; and (iv) temporary construction-induced impacts due to the disturbance to public service utilities, access
                     restrictions for people in residential areas and potentially their livelihoods. Additionally, the influx from contract
                     workers from other provinces to the project sites during the construction period may result in potential social impacts
                     and risks to local communities and stakeholders. Mitigation plans will be developed in consultation with affected
                     communities to ensure that the social impacts are effectively minimized and addressed. The Social risk rating will be
                     re-visited prior to project appraisal.


                     B. Relevance of Standards and Policies at Concept Stage

                     B.1 Relevance of Environmental and Social Standards

                      ESS1 - Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts                                   Relevant

                      The Borrower will prepare an Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) and an Environmental and Social
                      Management Plan (ESMP) for the project. The ESIAs will scope and assess risks and impacts across the project
                      lifecycle and especially focus on cumulative, transboundary and climate vulnerability risks and address through
                      project design, specific plans, climate informed resilience measures. Social risks include land acquisition, livelihood
                      transition challenges, temporary disruptions, labor influx, and impacts on Khmer EM. Instruments to be developed
                      prior to Project Appraisal include draft ESIA/ESMP, draft site-specific RPs, draft site-specific EMDPs, draft LMP, draft
                      SEP and draft ESCP. Capacity building for the PMU will be conducted during the project preparation and
                      implementation.

                      ESS10 - Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure                                                      Relevant

                      A Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) will be developed, consulted and disclosed prior to project appraisal. Key
                      stakeholders are expected to include project beneficiaries, local communities near investment sites, affected
                      landowners and farmers, ethnic minority groups (notably Khmer communities), relevant government agencies at
                      national, provincial, and local levels (such as land fund development centers, commune's compensation committees),
                      CSOs, farmers’ cooperatives, and vulnerable groups such as the poor, women, children, etc. The engagement
                      approach will consider the needs of ethnic minority groups, poor, vulnerable and disadvantaged communities,
                      language barriers and literacy to ensure that risks are managed, and benefits are accessible to all. There is currently
                      an evaluation of national grievance redress mechanisms underway, which will be reflected in the SEP to be
                      developed, which will outline if there will be reliance on the national GRM and any gap filling measures required.

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                            Relevant

                      The Project will include direct workers, contracted workers, and primary supply workers (providers of construction
                      materials). The project will not likely engage community workers. The workers in Viet Nam are regulated and
                      protected under a labor framework, including Labor Law (2019), Law on Occupational Health and Sanitation (2015),
                      Social Security Law (2014), Civil Servant Law (2019), etc. ESS2 provisions on employment terms and conditions,
                      nondiscrimination, equal opportunity, OHS and anti-SEA/SH will apply in employment contracts. The policies and
                      regulations reflect the principles of ESS2 on fair treatment, nondiscrimination, equal opportunities, workers' rights,
                      prohibition of sexual exploitation, forced labor, child labor, OHS risks. The current evaluation of national grievance
                      redress mechanism will also evaluate if it can be applied in the case of project workers, as is the norm in Viet Nam

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                             Relevant

                      The project is likely to generate and manage significant environmental aspects. Anticipated impacts include short
                      term degradation of surface water quality from dredging, such as elevated turbidity and reduced dissolved oxygen;
                      disturbance of acid sulfate soils and saline soils; and the handling of dredged material across multiple sites. The
                      project will also require sourcing soil, sand, and rock in contexts where such materials may be scarce or
                      environmentally sensitive to extract. Operational changes in hydrology and salinity can influence pollutant dilution
                      and nutrient dynamics, potentially elevating concentrations and eutrophication risk. In addition, ESS3 is engaged to
                      assess opportunities for resource efficiency (for example, energy and water use) and to identify potential greenhouse
                      gas co benefits associated with climate smart agriculture practices and mangrove restoration or enhancement.

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                             Relevant

                      There are risks to communities during construction and operation. Construction activities will increase heavy-vehicle
                      movements and work near populated areas, creating traffic and site safety risks, temporary disruptions to access and
                      public services, and dust and noise affecting residents and businesses. Climate vulnerability (floods/surge,
                      drought/heat, salinity, altered flows/sediment) presents CHS risks and hydrologic changes from dredging and water-
                      control structures may alter drainage patterns and influence local flood risk. The potential presence of unexploded
                      ordnance (UXO) in some areas adds hazards requiring prior identification. During operation, traffic associated with
                      new or rehabilitated infrastructure can present ongoing safety risks.

                      ESS5 - Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement                                   Relevant

                      The proposed investments under component 2—including the rehabilitation of canal systems, embankments,
                      construction of sluice gates, and navigation locks—will require extensive land acquisition, which may lead to both
                      physical and economic displacement of project-affected persons (PAPs). During the pre-feasibility study stage, the
                      project anticipates acquiring more than 100,000 m² of land in Dong Thap, Tay Ninh, Vinh Long provinces and Can Tho
                      city, impacting 352 households; among these, 36 will need to physically relocate. Besides formal landholders,
                      informal users such as those living and fishing along the canals could also be affected. Site-specific Resettlement Plans
                      (RPs) will be drafted, discussed with stakeholders, and made publicly available prior to the World Bank's appraisal.

                      ESS6 - Biodiversity Conservation and Sustainable Management of Living Natural                                    Relevant
                      Resources

                      ESS6 is relevant because the project interacts with both natural habitats and areas that have been previously
                      developed or degraded. While civil works are expected to occur mainly in modified or brownfield settings and initial
                      screening indicates no protected areas or critical habitats at the proposed sites, the activities still present risks with
                      pathways to the critical ecological functions supported in the wider area of influence. Risks will be scoped, and a CHA
                      undertaken if needed. Dredging can disturb aquatic ecosystems by removing benthic fauna, elevating turbidity, and
                      lowering dissolved oxygen. Coastal/estuarine works may alter hydrodynamics, salinity, sediment transport, and
                      ecological connectivity, affecting species movement and habitats. Changes in flow, flushing, and salinity can further
                      impact aquatic ecosystems. Component 3 may trigger habitat conversion (wetlands/mangroves), increased water use
                      and pollution, and soil salinization/degradation.
