                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The operation is the first operation of a simultaneous MPA conceived to allow scalability of World Bank support for local
                     development in northern Mozambique. The MPA will include two overlapping phases to be implemented over 8 years in
                     total, totaling US$250 million. This US$100 million Phase I operation will address the urgent development needs of
                     vulnerable communities, build systems for community participation in local development, and build the capacity of local
                     governments for better planning and efficient service delivery in 22 targeted districts of Niassa, Cabo Delgado, and
                     Nampula provinces. Phase II will be allocated US$150 million to scale-up the community and local development

                     beneficiaries include: (a) local communities in targeted districts; (b) district and provincial authorities; and (c) youth,
                     women, IDPs, returnees and other vulnerable groups in targeted districts. Phase I will cover 22 districts in the provinces

                     (Memba, Erati, Mecuburi, Malema, Ribuae, and Muecate), which have endured recent insurgents’ attacks or that were
                     heavily affected by the 2024 post-elections crisis. In each district, social cohesion activities and community and public
                     infrastructure investments will target localities with the highest poverty rates, low access to public services, and high
                     exposure to natural hazards. Phase I will finance three components and a zero-cost CERC as follows: Component 1:
                     Improving Social Cohesion. The component will finance priority actions aiming to strengthen social cohesion in the
                     targeted districts by mobilizing dialogue platforms, supporting youth and women economic enterprises, and providing
                     GBV and psycho-social support services to women and men. Component 2: Improving access to basic services and
                     climate resilient infrastructure. This component will address the limited delivery of critical basic services by constructing
                     and rehabilitating climate resilient community and public infrastructure, while improving the capacity of the districts
                     and local communities to plan climate resilient infrastructure that responds to the needs of the communities in the
                     short and medium terms. Component 3: Program Management and Coordination. This component will support
                     coordination and management of the operation from fiduciary, E&S risk management, grievance redress, M&E and
                     independent impact evaluation, and communication perspectives. It will also support the operational costs of a Steering
                     Committee (SC, see below), a technical committee, and all necessary stakeholder engagement platforms needed to
                     deliver the operation. Component 4: Contingent Emergency Response. This zero-allocation component will allow for
                     rapid reallocation of uncommitted program funds in the event of an eligible emergency. At the apex of the MPA and

                     entities will support this process to ensure alignment with national standards.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The proposed Project will operate in 22 districts across Cabo Delgado, northern Nampula, and eastern Niassa, areas
                     severely affected by conflict, displacement, and weak state presence. These districts are predominantly rural,
                     characterized by dispersed settlements, limited infrastructure, inadequate basic services, and high dependence on

                    Jan 30, 2026                                                                                                       Page 3 of 10
                              The World Bank

                     natural resources for livelihoods. The coexistence of IDPs, returnees, host communities, and vulnerable youth creates a
                     complex social setting with heightened risks of exclusion, tensions, and differentiated vulnerabilities. To help address
                     these contextual drivers, the project will rely on participatory planning and implementation at locality and district levels,
                     ensuring communities have voice in prioritization and feedback, and supporting trust-building between citizens and
                     local authorities. The environmental context is diverse and sensitive, including coastal zones, riverine systems, degraded
                     forest mosaics, fragile soils, and agro-ecological landscapes. Small-scale community and public infrastructure—such as
                     classrooms, water points, small bridges, community centers, and feeder paths—may be located near wetlands, seasonal
                     streams, and modified natural habitats, requiring careful screening and siting to avoid erosion, vegetation clearance,
                     waste management issues, or disturbance to sensitive ecosystems. Some districts border biodiversity-sensitive areas,
                     requiring robust due diligence. The institutional environment is characterized by limited administrative capacity at
                     provincial and district levels. Project activities will involve labor-intensive public works, community-based construction,
                     and engagement with vulnerable groups in a high-risk FCV setting; consequently, there is a clear nexus between
                     contextual risks and project activities, and some interventions could exacerbate tensions or vulnerabilities if not
                     carefully managed through conflict-sensitive engagement, transparent selection criteria, and responsive GM.

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Borrower’s institutional capacity to manage environmental and social risks and impacts is moderate but uneven,
                     with significant constraints at provincial and district levels, further exacerbated by the fragile and insecure context in
                     northern Mozambique. The lead institutions—the Ministry of Planning and Development (MPD) and the Ministry of
                     Public Works, Housing and Water Resources (MOPHRH)—have prior experience implementing World Bank-financed
                     projects, but their exposure to the Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) varies across institutions and




Public Disclosure
                     administrative levels.

                     The newly established National Administration of Public Works (ANOP), which will lead implementation of community
                     and public infrastructure activities, is still at an early stage of institutional development and does not yet have dedicated
                     environmental and social staff. At subnational level, provincial and district services operate with limited staffing, weak
                     logistical capacity, and inconsistent familiarity with ESF requirements. These constraints are compounded by staff
                     turnover, access limitations, and security-related challenges that affect supervision and field presence.

                     To address these gaps, the project will establish two dedicated Program Implementation Units (PIUs) at MPD/DNPDI
                     and MOPHRH/ANOP, staffed with qualified environmental and social specialists, including expertise in occupational
                     health and safety (OHS) and GBV/SEA/SH risk management. Early in implementation, the Borrower will prepare and
                     operationalize a targeted Environmental and Social Capacity Development Plan focusing on ESF application, screening
                     and supervision of community works, SEA/SH risk mitigation, grievance management, and conflict-sensitive stakeholder
                     engagement. Capacity strengthening will be implemented through structured training, on-the-job mentoring, and
                     practical tools embedded in the ESMF, with phased reinforcement of provincial and district teams and collaboration
                     with civil society organizations

                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                      Substantial
                     The environmental risk of the project is rated Substantial. Although the project will finance primarily small-scale
                     community and public infrastructure works—these activities will take place across a broad geographic area affected
                     by fragility and limited institutional presence. Potential environmental risks include soil disturbance, erosion,
                     improper disposal of construction debris and solid waste, localized air and noise emissions, interference with natural
                     drainage patterns, and risks related to material sourcing, particularly where contractors may rely on unregulated
                     borrow areas or quarries. In addition, the project may involve the rehabilitation or installation of small water supply
                     systems, which could generate risks related to water quality, wastewater management, and resource efficiency.
                     Occupational health and safety (OHS) risks are expected during community works and small-scale construction
                     activities, especially given the likely use of local labor, variable contractor capacity, and existing gaps in OHS
                     management across public works agencies. Community health and safety risks may arise from increased traffic and
                     movement of construction materials, inappropriate site demarcation, or insufficient management of work areas in
                     densely populated village environments. While the project is not expected to support activities in protected areas or
                     critical habitats, some subprojects may be located near wetlands, seasonal streams, or modified natural habitats,
                     requiring screening and mitigation measures under the ESMF. Cumulative environmental impacts are expected to be
                     limited due to the dispersed and small-scale nature of investments. In view of these factors—small-scale but
                     widespread works, moderate intrinsic environmental impacts, weak institutional capacity, operational challenges in
                     FCV settings, and the need to finalize ESF instruments during implementation—the environmental risk is considered
                     Substantial.

                     The social risk of the project is rated Substantial. The project will operate in areas deeply affected by fragility, conflict,
                     displacement, and weak state presence. While project activities are expected to generate important social benefits—
                     including improved access to livelihoods, basic services, and community infrastructure—the operating context
                     presents significant challenges and heightened risks. Key social risks include: (i) labor risks, related to the use of local
                     labor in community works, uneven contractor capacity, and potential gaps in enforcing labor management
                     requirements, occupational health and safety (OHS) practices, and worker grievance mechanisms; (ii) SEA/SH risks,
                     which are elevated in FCV settings characterized by displacement, weakened social protection systems, and limited
                     availability of survivor support services. Labor-intensive public works and increased interaction between workers and
                     communities may heighten these risks if not proactively managed; (iii) community health and safety risks, including
                     localized hazards around worksites, increased movement of construction materials, and potential exposure of
                     communities—particularly women and children—to unsafe construction areas; (iv) exclusion risks, whereby
                     vulnerable groups such as IDPs, returnees, unemployed youth, female-headed households, and remote communities
                     may face barriers to accessing project benefits or participating meaningfully in planning processes, given uneven local
                     government presence and variable community governance structures; and (v) security risks, linked to ongoing
                     insurgent activity in parts of Cabo Delgado and select districts of Nampula and Niassa, which may affect consultations,
                     supervision, continuity of activities, and safe engagement with communities. At this stage, land acquisition needs
                     have not been defined; therefore, ESS5 screening criteria and procedures will be incorporated into the ESMF. Given
                     the high contextual insecurity and institutional capacity.

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                            Relevant

                      ESS2 is relevant because the project will engage direct workers such as consultants and civil servants assigned to the
                      PIUs, indirect workers such as contracted workers employed by small and medium contractors, community workers
                      participating in labor-intensive public works (LIPW), and consultants and technical assistance providers supporting
                      implementation at central, provincial, district, and community levels. Labor-intensive activities may expose workers
                      to OHS risks including physical injuries, unsafe work environments, lack of protective equipment, hazardous manual
                      handling, and risks linked to the movement of materials. Such risks are heightened by limited contractor capacity
                      found in remote districts and the weak enforcement of OHS regulations in northern Mozambique. The project will
                      have an LMP and adopt Codes of Conduct and mandatory SEA/SH and OHS training for all project workers and
                      contractors, with reinforced monitoring arrangements.

                      (e.g., oils, paint containers). In FCV settings, improper storage, transport, or disposal of waste could result in soil

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                            Relevant

                     B.3 Other Salient Features

                       Use of Borrower Framework                                                                                              No
                       The project will not rely on the Borrower’s national environmental and social framework in lieu of the World Bank’s
                       Environmental and Social Framework (ESF). While Mozambique has an established legal and regulatory system for
                       environmental impact assessment, land management, labor, and public health, the current framework does not fully
                       align with the requirements of several ESSs—particularly ESS2 (labor and working conditions), ESS4 (community health
                       and safety), ESS5 (land acquisition and resettlement), and ESS10 (stakeholder engagement). The project will be
                       implemented in fragile and conflict-affected districts where institutional capacity is significantly constrained at all
                       levels (central, provincial, and district). Public authorities, including the newly established ANOP and local planning
                       and infrastructure services, have limited experience applying World Bank ESF requirements and face operational
                       challenges such as restricted access to project sites and staff shortages.


                       Use of Common Approach                                                                                                 No
                       The project will not use a common approach with other development partners. Although development partners are
                       active in northern Mozambique, the proposed operation will be prepared and implemented solely under the World
                       Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) without harmonized or jointly applied safeguard arrangements. No
                       co-financing or parallel financing arrangements require the use of a unified E&S approach.

                     C. Overview of Required Environmental and Social Risk Management Activities

                     C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or
                     required by Appraisal?
                     Environmental and social risk management will be sequenced across preparation and early implementation. Prior to
                     appraisal, the Borrower will prepare and disclose: (i) a draft ESCP setting out commitments, responsibilities, staffing,
                     timelines, and reporting; and (ii) an initial SEP defining early engagement, communications, and grievance
                     arrangements, including provisions for vulnerable groups and SEA/SH-sensitive intake. Following effectiveness, during
                     early implementation, the Borrower will update/adapt the core E&S instruments developed under NCRP/MozNorte (as
                     relevant) to the MozCommunity context, scope, and institutional arrangements. Central among these is the ESMF
                     (including screening procedures, generic ESMP templates, and ESS5 screening procedures), which will guide subproject
                     screening, risk categorization, mitigation measures, and requirements for contractors, including preparation of C-
                     ESMPs. The LMP (integrated into or annexed to the ESMF) will set out labor categories, terms and conditions, OHS
                     requirements for labor-intensive works, worker GRM, Codes of Conduct, and measures to prevent child/forced labor.
                     Where screening identifies ESS5 impacts, site-specific RAPs will be prepared and disclosed prior to commencement of
                     works at the relevant locations. As access and implementation arrangements mature, the Borrower will prepare an
                     updated SEP with district-specific engagement approaches adapted to insecurity and a fully operational project GRM
                     with multiple safe intake channels, including confidential SEA/SH reporting and referral pathways, leveraging civil
                     society partners where government presence is limited.
