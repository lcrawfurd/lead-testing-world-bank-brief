                              B. Introduction and Context

                              Country Context

                                  1. Yemen faces severe and compounding crises, shaped by a decade-long conflict that has fragmented the
                                     country's institutions, economy, and social fabric. Since the conflict began in 2014, Yemen has been divided
                                     between areas controlled by the Houthi (Ansar Allah) movement and those governed by the Internationally
                                     Recognized Government (IRG), with distinct monetary, administrative, and governance structures on both sides.

                                  2. The national poverty rate stood at 48.6 percent in 2014, with recent modeling suggesting it may have reached
                                     as high as 74 percent by 20221. Yemen ranks 184 out of 193 countries on the Human Development Index (2025),2
                                     and sixth out of 179 countries on the Fragile States Index.3 The United Nations Children Fund’s (UNICEF)
                                     Multidimensional Poverty Report indicates that approximately half of all households in Yemen experience
                                     multidimensional poverty, driven primarily by deprivations in housing and nutrition4. The living environment in
                                     Yemen is therefore severely constrained, with 63 percent of households having reported inadequate food
                                     consumption in early 20255, an estimated 19 million people facing food insecurity and needing humanitarian

                                       Yemen stems primarily from affordability constraints, with rising prices and falling incomes preventing households

                               Sectoral and Institutional Context

                                 3. Malnutrition in Yemen is severely undermining human capital development by limiting physical growth,
                                    weakening learning capacity, and diminishing long-term economic productivity. While infant and under-five
                                    mortality rates have improved since 2013, stunting and wasting in children under 5 remain unchanged and
                                    extremely high at 49 percent in 20239 and 17 percent respectively. Eighty-six percent of children are also anemic10.
                                    Less than 12 percent of children aged 6–23 months receive a minimum acceptable diet, and 30 percent achieve
                                    minimum dietary diversity.11 Additionally, only 20 percent of infants are exclusively breastfed for the first six
                                    months of life12 - rates far below international standards. Among women of reproductive age, more than 70

                                    Maternal mortality stands at 118 deaths per 100,000 live births.14 Restrictive gender norms, elevated gender-
                                    based violence (GBV) risks, and barriers to women’s mobility and care have contributed to low healthcare access,
                                    and appropriate childcare and nutrition practices in Yemen. 15 16 17
                                 4. Yemen's health sector has been strengthened through years of external investments, but significant gaps
                                    remain. Over 76 percent of health expenditures are financed through out‑of‑pocket payments or emergency aid18,
                                    leaving service delivery highly vulnerable to declining donor support. The country’s human resources for health
                                    are significantly constrained, with only 12 health workers per 10,000 people (roughly half the World Health
                                    Organization -WHO-recommended minimum) - in some governorates the ratio falls below 0.7 doctors and nurses
                                    per 1,000 people19. Between 35 and 40 percent of medical professionals have left the country since the conflict
                                    began. The majority of those who remain rely almost entirely on international per diem payments funded by
                                    donor projects and may officially earn the equivalent of ~US$50 per month in a government hospital.

                                  5. The rate of unvaccinated children ("zero-dose") jumped from 4 percent in 2022 to 24 percent in 2024 in Houthi-
                                     controlled areas20. Nationally, approximately 1 in 10 children under the age of one year has received no vaccines21,
                                     which is likely to increase the odds of being stunted22. Disease outbreaks are frequent and severe: Yemen carries
                                     the highest cholera burden in the world, with over 93,496 cases and 248 related deaths reported in 202523. The
                                     incidence of cholera, as well as of other preventable illnesses that contribute to malnutrition, is exacerbated by
                                     the country’s limited access to safe water and sanitation. Even before the conflict, water coverage reached only
                                     half the population; today, 59 percent of PHCs lack water, 26 percent lack sanitation24, and household hygiene
                                     awareness remains low25, heightening disease risks. Conflict-driven damage to infrastructure, and recurrent
                                     climate-driven shocks affecting water security and quality2627, continue to undermine the gains achievable through
                                     health interventions.

                                  6. Weak governance, fragmented institutional arrangements, and severe resource constraints continue to limit
                                     integrated health, water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH), and nutrition service delivery in Yemen. Parallel
                                     health systems in IRG- and Houthi-controlled areas undermine coordination, planning, and oversight, while siloed
                                     information systems constrain evidence-based decision-making. Despite these challenges, local institutions in the
                                     health sector demonstrate resilience and continue to operate. The Ministry of Public Health and Population
                                     (MoPHP) in the IRG areas is leading the development of the 2026–2030 National Health Strategy, offering a
                                     platform for improved coordination amid declining external assistance.

                             7. This is the core service delivery component of the project and aims to preserve access to essential health,
                                nutrition, and WASH services across target areas of Yemen. This project builds on the systems and delivery model
                                established under the Emergency Health and Nutrition Project (EHNP) and Emergency Human Capital Project
                                (EHCP). It will support an integrated Minimum Service Package (MSP) along the full continuum of care—spanning
                                community outreach, primary health care facilities, secondary and tertiary hospitals, and therapeutic feeding
                                centers—across targeted governorates that account for the highest number of stunted children, as well as
                                supporting National Blood Transfusion Centers.
                             8. Service delivery will prioritize reproductive, maternal, neonatal, child, and adolescent health; nutrition; WASH
                                commodities; and communicable disease services, with nutrition interventions systematically embedded
                                within routine care. For pregnant women and adolescent girls, the package emphasizes prevention and treatment
                                of micronutrient deficiencies, improved antenatal care quality, and nutrition counseling. For children under five,
                                services focus on prevention and management of acute malnutrition, growth monitoring, treatment of common
                                childhood illnesses, immunization, and promotion of optimal infant and young child feeding practices - particularly
                                during the first 1,000 days. Community health workers, nutrition volunteers, and community midwives will play a
                                central role in extending integrated services to hard-to-reach and underserved populations. To improve service
                                quality, continuity, and readiness, the project will finance essential medicines, vaccines, nutrition commodities,
                                medical supplies, equipment, and operating support for health facilities, alongside structured supportive
                                supervision by district and governorate health teams. A defined subset of facilities will be upgraded to function as




_For Official Use Only_
                                hubs for diagnostics or to provide basic emergency obstetric and newborn care services, reinforcing continuity of
                                care across service levels.
                             9. The component also addresses a critical constraint to service delivery by strengthening WASH infrastructure at
                                primary health care facilities. Investments will focus on reliable, climate-resilient water supply systems, improved
                                sanitation and hygiene facilities, and safe waste management solutions to meet infection prevention and control
                                standards and reduce disease transmission risks. These upgrades are expected to reduce reliance on emergency
                                water trucking, lower operating costs, and enhance the resilience and functionality of frontline health services.
                                Civil works will be conducted in existing facilities only; no new land acquisition is anticipated.
                             10. This component also includes the implementation of social behavior change communication (SBCC activities
                                 through the National Center for Health Education and Information. The approach will focus on delivering unified
                                 messages on health, nutrition, WASH, healthy diets, and social protection through community mobilization,
                                 counseling, radio, social media, and print materials, prioritizing household dynamics that influence prenatal and
                                 child nutrition practices.
                             11. To maximize impact on chronic malnutrition, the project introduces a geographic convergence approach in
                                 selected “Geographic Accelerator Districts,” where health and nutrition service delivery will be deliberately
                                 aligned with complementary World Bank–financed interventions in social protection, community water supply,
                                 food security, and energy. Within these districts, the project will support coordinated service delivery, more
                                 concentrated community delivery of harmonized social and behavior change communication, and mechanisms to
                                 track household-level reach across sectors, strengthening demand for services and reinforcing nutrition-sensitive
                                 outcomes, particularly for children under two and pregnant and lactating women.

                          Component 2: Support to Prevent and Respond to Communicable Disease Outbreaks

                             12. This component strengthens Yemen’s capacity to detect, prevent, and respond to disease outbreaks
                                 nationwide, recognizing the close link between communicable diseases, malnutrition, and health system

                                 fragility. It builds on investments under EHCP and the Pandemic Preparedness and Response Project to sustain
                                 and enhance core public health functions critical to crisis prevention and response.

                             13. The project will support nationwide disease surveillance through continued operation and strengthening of the
                                 electronic Integrated Disease Early Warning System, ensuring that health facilities across all governorates
                                 report timely data on epidemic prone diseases. Surveillance improvements will be complemented by
                                 strengthening district and governorate level rapid response teams and public health emergency operations
                                 centers, enabling faster detection, notification, and response in line with international best practice timeliness
                                 standards.

                             14. Diagnostic capacity will be reinforced through targeted investments in public health laboratories, including
                                 equipment, reagents, quality assurance systems, and training, improving the country’s ability to identify
                                 outbreaks early and guide response measures. Nutrition surveillance will remain integrated within the health
                                 system, generating continuous data on nutritional status, disease patterns, and food security to inform adaptive
                                 service delivery and policy decisions.

                             15. Recognizing Yemen’s extreme vulnerability to climate sensitive and waterborne diseases, the component will
                                 also support emergency water trucking for facilities supported under Component 1 or in areas experiencing an
                                 active outbreak. The component will also integrate water quality testing into the work of the rapid response

                          Component 3: Systems Building for Sustainable Delivery of Health and Nutrition Services
                             16. This component focuses on strengthening national and sub-national institutions and the health workforce to
                                 improve sustainability, quality, and resilience of service delivery in a highly fragmented and
                                 resource-constrained context. It supports a strategic shift from emergency-driven delivery toward stronger
                                 national stewardship and local capacity.
                             17. Institutional strengthening efforts will be guided by a consolidated Systems Strengthening Plan and aligned
                                 with the forthcoming National Health Strategy. The project will strengthen key technical units of the Ministry of
                                 Public Health and Population in IRG-controlled areas, as well as governorate and district health offices, quality
                                 teams at primary health centers, hospitals and laboratories, the National Institute of Public Health, Governorate
                                 Health Institutes, the Yemen Medical and Health Specialization Council and the National Center for Health
                                 Education and Information, to improve planning, budgeting, supervision, service quality oversight, and emergency
                                 preparedness. Support will emphasize practical application of skills, integration into routine functions, and
                                 improved coordination across levels of the health system rather than standalone training.
                             18. Health financing capacity will be strengthened to improve efficiency and support domestic resource
                                 mobilization within a highly constrained fiscal environment, with a focus on sub-national resource pooling and
                                 prioritization of cost-effective nutrition and primary health care interventions. Health information systems
                                 strengthened under predecessor projects will be maintained and consolidated to support evidence-based
                                 planning and performance monitoring.
                             19. The component also makes strategic investments in health workforce development to address acute shortages
                                 and mismatches in skills. Working with national training institutes, universities, and professional councils, the
                                 project will strengthen pre-service, in-service, and postgraduate training, including through learning and blended
                                 modalities suited to a fragile context. Priority will be given to strengthening community-based cadres and critical

                                 guide workforce planning, deployment, retention, and financing over time, with particular attention to rural areas
                                 and women’s participation in the health workforce.

                          Component 4: Project Management
                             20. This component supports fiduciary, administrative, monitoring and evaluation, and project supervision
                                 functions. Key activities include the engagement of independent Third-Party Monitoring Agents (TPMAs) to verify
                                 outputs and outcomes across implementation areas, the conduct of biannual World Bank implementation support
                                 missions, and the preparation of semi-annual progress reports. Capacity building for a Project Technical Unit (PTU)
                                 in the health sector is also envisioned.

                          Component 5: Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC)
                             21. A zero-cost contingent component is included to allow for rapid reallocation of project funds in the event of an
                                 emergency — such as a disease outbreak, natural disaster, or sudden deterioration of the humanitarian situation
                                 — without requiring a formal restructuring. Activation of the CERC is subject to the declaration of an eligible crisis
                                 and compliance with specific disbursement conditions.

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts


                             22. Both the environmental and social risk ratings for this project are assessed as Substantial at appraisal.

                             23. Environmental risks are primarily driven by healthcare waste management, specifically the safe collection,
                                 segregation, storage, transportation, and disposal of medical and infectious waste across supported health
                                 facilities, laboratories, and outbreak response sites, where inadequate disposal infrastructure heightens the risk
                                 of environmental contamination and community exposure to infectious agents. Closely linked risks include
                                 infection prevention control failures at health facilities, improper management of vaccines, laboratory reagents,
                                 and medical consumables along the cold chain and supply chain, and occupational health and safety hazards for
                                 workers engaged in vaccination campaigns, laboratory operations, and waste handling, all requiring specific
                                 protocols, personal protective equipment, and systematic staff training. Sub-component 1.2 civil works for WASH
                                 rehabilitation present localized, temporary environmental risks including dust, noise, and improper waste
                                 management, manageable through site-specific Environment and Social Management Plans.

                             24. Social risks reflect Yemen's deeply fragile, conflict-affected operating environment and the complexity of
                                 implementing arrangements involving two UN agencies across target governorates. Labor risks, including child,
                                 forced, and informal labor within supply chains (notably solar equipment), discriminatory and gender-exclusionary
                                 labor practices, and worker confidentiality, retaliation, and discrimination concerns are cross-cutting across all

                               E. Implementation

                               Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

                                 25. Given Yemen's deeply fragile and conflict-affected operating environment, two United Nations agencies have
                                     been designated as the direct grant recipients and primary implementing entities. Both agencies have existing
                                     country presence, established networks of local implementation partners and nongovernmental organizations
                                     (NGOs), and proven capacity to operate in Yemen's complex environment:
                                     ● United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF): UNICEF leads the delivery of integrated health, nutrition, and WASH
                                         services at the primary care level, including community-level outreach through female community health
                                         workers, SBCC activities, and WASH infrastructure rehabilitation.
                                     ● World Health Organization (WHO): WHO leads service delivery at secondary and tertiary care levels, manages
                                         disease surveillance and outbreak response, and leads institutional capacity building activities.


                                 26. The project will diversify implementation partnerships, and build national capacity through four key
                                     approaches: creating an autonomous Project Technical Unit for oversight to support coordination, monitoring
                                     and quality assurance; strengthening national and sub-national institutions against measurable benchmarks;
                                     training local health workers through pre-service, diploma, and in-service programs; and deploying vetted local
                                     NGOs for service monitoring and logistics in areas where security constraints limit direct UN access. These
                                     measures will be evaluated for effectiveness and are designed to progressively shift implementation
                                     responsibilities toward local partners.

                                     annual reports to the World Bank. Independent Third-Party Monitoring Agents (TPMAs) will be engaged to verify
