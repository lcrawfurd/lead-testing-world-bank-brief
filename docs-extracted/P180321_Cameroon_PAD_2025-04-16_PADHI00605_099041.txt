 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

This Project Appraisal Document (PAD) covers phase 1 of the Cameroon Water Security Program (SEWASH, P180321).

 A. Country Context
1.       Cameroon is strategically located on the Gulf of Guinea and is a lower-middle-income country of approximately
29 million people (40.7 percent in rural areas and 59.3 percent in urban areas in 20231). With a Gross Domestic Product
(GDP) per capita of US$1,734 in 2023,2 the economy is driven by its primary sectors of agriculture and mineral resources.
The GDP growth rate was 3.2 percent in 20233. Cameroon benefits from its location in the Congo Basin, the world’s second-
largest tropical forest zone, which provides an exceptional ecological diversity.
2.      GDP growth has been insufficient to sustainably raise per capita GDP or significantly reduce poverty. The
country's economy recovered from the COVID-19 pandemic with an average annual GDP growth of 3.5 percent from 2021
to 2024. Public debt was 42.0 percent of GDP in June 2024, and is predominantly external, with China as the primary
creditor.
3.      Poverty remains highly concentrated in rural areas with regional disparities. According to the Cameroon
Household Survey (ECAM-5), around 56.3 percent of rural dwellers lived below the national poverty line in 2021-2022
compared with 21.6 percent of urban dwellers. Thus, around seven in ten poor Cameroonians live in rural areas. Moreover,
the poverty rate is significantly higher in the Far North, Northwest, and North regions at 69.2 percent, 66.8 percent, and
61.1 percent respectively, compared with the Center region (home to Yaoundé, the administrative capital) and the Littoral
region (home to Douala, the commercial capital), with poverty rates at 10.8 percent and 8.3 percent, respectively4.
4.      The country faces insecurity due to violence and armed attacks by the non-state armed group Boko Haram in the
Far North and internal crises resulting from sociopolitical demands by English-speaking populations in the North-West and
South-West regions. Cameroon is hosting more than two million forcibly displaced persons, including over one million
internally displaced population from the North-West, South-West and Far North, 413,000 refugees mainly from Central
African Republic and Nigeria, and over 650 000 returnees5. Thus, nine out of ten regions are directly or indirectly affected
by insecurity.
5.      Cameroon’s economy and its population are highly affected by extreme weather events, variations in the
hydrological cycle, and water-related disasters induced by climate change. In the last two decades, floods, droughts, and
landslides occurred more frequently than in the past, with climate models projecting further temperature increases and
more severe floods and droughts in the future. In 2024, climate change-exacerbated floods in the Far North region have
caused significant social and economic impacts6: some 459,102 people (65,945 households), were severely affected by
the floods and at least 38 people died. 56,084 houses were destroyed, 5,278 heads of livestock were lost, and 85,253
hectares of agricultural land were destroyed. More generally, nearly 2 million people are living in water-scarce areas of
the country.7
6.      Cameroon’s vision for an inclusive response to the impacts of climate change is summed up in its slogan:
“transforming climate constraints into development opportunities.8” To address the negative impacts of climate change
and meet the country’s updated Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitment, the Government of Cameroon
(GoC) dedicated a key objective of its National Development Strategy 2020-2030 (NDS30) to the fight against climate

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
7.      Cameroon has significant renewable water resources (273 billion m3/year); however, this endowment is mainly
transboundary surface waters unevenly distributed in space and time. Rainfall is unevenly distributed from a drier North
to a wetter South. Cameroon is drained by three major river systems: Niger, Sanaga, Congo, and several coastal rivers,
which drain into the Gulf of Guinea and the Lake Chad Basin. Internal renewable water resources per capita in Cameroon
declined from 40,809 m3 in 1971 to 10,284.1 m3 in 20209 due to significant population growth and increase in demand.
Despite its abundant water resources compared to global and regional benchmarks, Cameroon has faced a sevenfold
increase in water demand between 2009 and 2021, from 11 billion m3 to 73.3 billion m3.10
8.       Water security is hindered by inadequate infrastructure and insufficient Integrated Water Resources
Management (IWRM). Water security refers to “a society's capacity to have enough water of sufficient quality for survival
and to carry out different productive activities. Therefore, a society with water security can reduce poverty and improve
living standards.”11 Reduced water security due to increasing water demand and erratic weather patterns driven by
climate change is most evident in the northern regions, where eight to nine months of dry season in a year leads to water
shortages and droughts. The three to four months long rainy season comes with increased frequency and intensity of
rainfall and floods in coastal and agro-ecolological zones. Erratic rainfall patterns and rising temperatures due to climate
change have caused local water sources to become unreliable, increasing the water collection burden on vulnerable
communities. Cameroon first introduced IWRM principles in its policy framework in 2009 through the National Action Plan
for Integrated Water Resources Management (Plan d’Action National de Gestion Intégrée des Ressources en Eau,
PANGIRE). PANGIRE aimed to implement IWRM principles in Cameroon which are “an optimal water management,
considering both technical aspects and social goals like meeting basic needs and improving population welfare”. However,
PANGIRE’s operationalization remains too limited to be impactful. There is no mechanism or assigned authority to regulate
and allocate water resources among different uses, namely agriculture, hydroelectricity, domestic, livestock, industry, and
environment. A coordinated and cross-sectoral approach to IWRM at the sub-basin level must involve key stakeholders in
the decision-making process, particularly as water insecurity exacerbated by climate change increasingly drives conflicts
and fuels climate induced displacement. One of the most severe examples occurred in November 2021, when
intercommunity clashes between farmers, fishermen, and herders in the Far North region displaced more than 20 000
people12.
9.       Insufficient access to water and sanitation services and poor hygiene are key drivers of endemic waterborne
diseases such as cholera.13 Cameron does not have an integrated data system to monitor access to water supply and
sanitation (WSS). A 2023 WHO/UNICEF14 Water Study reports that, from 2017 to 2022 access to safely managed drinking
water services stagnated in urban areas at 80 percent and only increased by 10 percentage points in rural areas (from 42
percent to 52 percent, see Figure 1). In the same period, access to sanitation facilities slightly decreased both in urban and
rural areas, as efforts to increase access have not kept up with population growth.15 This is exacerbated by climate change
impacts on health outcomes, in the form of water contamination during floods and higher contaminant loading during

10.      Institutionally, Cameroon’s water sector faces significant challenges, including fragmentation, inadequate
legislation, limited operational capacity, and insufficient cost recovery for operations and maintenance. Drawing on
lessons from previous interventions, the World Bank is implementing a multi-pronged approach to comprehensively
address these issues. Legal reforms are being incentivized through the Cameroon First Fiscal, Inclusive and Sustainable
Growth DPF (DPF1, P175249) and the Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth
Development Policy Financing DPF (DPF2, P180017) to strengthen Water Resource Management (WRM) and improve
irrigation infrastructure maintenance. Additionally, a dedicated Disbursement-Linked Indicator (DLI) under the Improving
Performance, Accountability and Transparency (IMPACT) in Cameroon Program for Results PforR (P505030) will enhance
Cameroon Water Utility Corporation’s (CAMWATER) operational efficiency, including improvements in billing and revenue
collection, thereby supporting its financial sustainability. The proposed SEWASH Multiphase Programmatic Approach
(MPA) complements these efforts, focusing on transformational institutional strengthening and expanding sustainable
water access over 11 years.
11.     The Ministry of Water Resources and Energy (Ministère de l’Eau et de l’Energie, MINEE) oversees the sector.
MINEE’s mandate is the development and implementation of policies and regulations, data collection and dissemination,
and monitoring and evaluation (M&E) of water resources and services. However, many other actors intervene in the sector
at the national level, including the Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development and the Ministry of Environment, among
others (see Annex 2).16 Ensuring coordination among them has been and remains a challenge.
12.     Urban WSS services are the responsibility of CAMWATER. It is a state-owned enterprise entrusted by the GoC,
with the mandate to provide WSS services to people in 216 urban centers and peri-urban areas. However, CAMWATER
only manages to provide water services to 117 urban centers, effectively servicing about 500,000 registered customers,
compared to a national population of 29 million in Cameroon 17. In 2007, the GoC, through the National Water Supply
Company of Cameroon, known as Société Nationale des Eaux du Cameroun (SNEC), which was later replaced by
CAMWATER, entered into a comprehensive 10-year public-private partnership (PPP) delegating the responsibility to
provide water supply services through an ‘affermage’ model to a private operator, ‘la Camerounaise des Eaux’ (led by the
Moroccan national operator). In 2018, the GoC decided not to extend the lease contract (Decree 2018/144), leading to
the transfer of debts of the former SNEC to CAMWATER, worsening its challenged balance sheet. In early 2024, the
government assessed the operational and financial position of CAMWATER. The broad diagnosis confirmed a difficult

14.       A strong system approach is required to effectively manage water resources and deliver inclusive services.
Water security will be achieved by effectively managing water resources with fit-for-purpose institutional and legal
arrangements. Investments should promote efficient use of water, contribute to reducing technical and commercial
losses, and boost cost recovery and financial viability of the service providers. At about 40 percent, Non-Revenue Water
(NRW) is a major issue for CAMWATER. The utility is implementing a metering campaign to improve its water accounting
and billing, and close to 348 km of pipes have been identified as being in urgent need of replacement. Similar to other
utilities globally, CAMWATER is also exploring new solutions to adapt to and minimize the impacts of climate change. This
has driven decision makers to adopt water conservation policies that prioritize leak reduction and a holistic approach to
minimizing unaccounted for losses. However, these efforts require continuous collaboration within the metering and
maintenance ecosystem.

 C. Relevance to Higher Level Objectives
15.      The proposed Cameroon Water Security Project (SEWASH) supports Objective 2 of the Cameroon Country
Partnership Framework (CPF FY25–29)18 “more effective and inclusive service delivery”. It includes activities dealing with
WSS services and IWRM that build the resilience in regions affected by climate change and conflicts, particularly in the Far
North and northern regions of Cameroon, including areas bordering Lake Chad. It is also aligned with the Water Strategy
for Africa West and Central which aims at improving access to water supply and sanitation in the region. The first phase
of the program will extend access to drinking water services for about 875,000 beneficiaries and limited sanitation for
400,000. The entire program is expected to deliver drinking water to around 3.9 million people and basic/limited sanitation
to approximately 2.9 million. This amounts in the MPA regions to an increase in the access rate of 28 percent for drinking
water and 38 percent for basic/limited sanitation over approximately eleven years (See table 2).
16.     The program responds to the government’s request for World Bank support to increase access to WSS in rural
and peri-urban areas. It targets the poorest regions of the country that are also the most vulnerable to water-related
climate change impacts (droughts and floods) and to conflict-related insecurity. The project, aligned with the National
Development Strategy 2020-2030 objectives of improving the protection and use of both freshwater and marine water
resources including through, rationing surface water and groundwater use, best practices and state-of-the-art
technologies. It is also aligned with other sector programs, such as the National Water Policy, approved by the President

 D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach
        (i) Description of the MPA
22.       Rationale for using Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA). The MPA offers the following advantages
compared to stand-alone operations: (a) establishing a strategic, phased long-term partnership that affirms the World
Bank’s commitment to supporting the GoC’s interventions to improve WSS service delivery while ensuring support for
reforms and systemic changes; and (b) supporting a systematic and structured approach for capacity building and
knowledge to incentivize efficiency, promote financial sustainability, and drive water sector transformation. The program
is designed to have a transformational impact, focused on scalability, replicability, and the creation of an effective enabling
environment for sustainable and resilient water resources and water services in rural, peri-urban and urban areas. It builds
on policy and institutional reforms undertaken in recent years, such as the decentralization law adopted in 2019, the
National Water Policy adopted in 2022, and the ongoing revision of the 1998 Water Law. A broad diagnosis of CAMWATER
in 2024 led to the establishment of an action plan, which the project will support. The proposed program will be structured
in three phases that combine capacity building with reforms of the key sector actors (MINEE, CAMWATER, and
municipalities) and the institutional and financial structure of the sector (restructuring of CAMWATER, regulation, water
pricing, and delegation of public services to municipalities or private sector). The first phase of the MPA will ensure that
critical and foundational sector reforms are put in place by using performance-based conditions (PBCs) that incentivize
approval of implementing regulations. The phased approach will also confirm the government’s continued commitment
to reform before moving to subsequent phases.
23.     Over a period of 11 years, the MPA is expected to provide drinking water to about 3.9 million people and
sanitation to 2.9 million people. The program Theory of Change (ToC) is represented in Figure 2. The MPA will also
contribute to water security and the operationalization of IWRM by involving citizens in the decision-making process to
ensure the sustainability of proposed investments. This will support the country in achieving SDG6 targets. Phase 1 will
develop feasibility studies that will facilitate the implementation of the next two phases which will focus on water security,
IWRM and WSS investments in large- and medium-sized cities.
24.   The overall Program Development Objective (PrDO) is to strengthen institutions, improve water resources
management and increase access to water and sanitation services in selected areas of Cameroon.

develop water storage in the country to improve water security.
        (ii) The Learning Agenda
31.      The learning agenda will play a pivotal role in ensuring effective knowledge transfer and capacity building
between program phases, supporting the achievement of sustainable results. The agenda will begin with a baseline
assessment in Phase 1 to identify critical knowledge and capacity gaps among implementing entities such as MINEE,
CAMWATER, and municipalities. This foundational exercise will directly inform program activities by highlighting needs
for capacity building interventions, technical support and institutional strengthening. Importantly, it will serve as a tool
for designing interventions under subsequent phases and as a benchmark for tracking progress across the phases, ensuring
that interventions remain targeted, responsive, and aligned with evolving priorities. Specific activities emerging from the
baseline include tailored training programs and institutional assessments to identify and address gaps in water
management and delivery to equip the institutions for their expanding responsibilities over the implementation period.
The preparation of IWRM plans will also be a key activity in Phase 1. These plans will provide an opportunity to engage
stakeholders, foster partnerships with academic institutions, and lay the foundation for collaborative water resource
governance. The insights gained during the preparation of these plans will be essential for designing more effective
interventions in subsequent phases, ensuring the program’s long-term success.

of transformative results in service delivery, utility management, and financial performance. By creating a structured
pathway for continuous improvement, the learning agenda underpins SEWASH’s broader objectives of improving water
security and increasing access to water and sanitation services in Cameroon.

 II. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

33.      Despite the access gaps and the socioeconomic impact of poor services, investments in water and sanitation
are low in Cameroon. Significant investment is needed to extend access to water and sanitation to the entire population
and reach the 2030 Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 6 targets (about US$11 billion estimated on the JMP/WHO access
figures), with more than half the needs being in the three northern regions, the East and West regions. SEWASH represents
the World Bank’s reengagement in the water sector in Cameroon. It will focus on (i) supporting the improvement of the
institutional framework for IWRM; (ii) strengthening the capacity and systems of the main actors (MINEE, CAMWATER,
and municipalities) to implement IWRM and improved WSS services; (iii) piloting the operationalization of IWRM in
selected sub-basins of the Benoue River basin; and (iv) supporting investments to improve access to WSS services,
essentially in the selected sub-basins for greater impact addressing attendant climate change-exacerbated droughts and
floods.
34.     The project will seek to strengthen the entire value chain for water service delivery, starting from improving the
capacity of key institutions and supporting reforms. It will support the reinforcement of IWRM, service delivery, operation
and maintenance capacities, and evidence-based decision-making at the national and local level to effectively improve
water security and access to WSS services for the poorest people in selected sub-basins.

 E. Lessons Learned and Progress on Learning Agenda
51.        The project builds on lessons learned from previous World Bank-financed projects related to WSS in urban
and rural areas, including (i) the Cameroon Urban and Water Development Project (P084002), closed in 2015, which
increased access to basic infrastructure and services in about 100 urban centers in the country, including water supply
with a special focus on low-income settlements; and (ii) the Cameroon Sanitation Project (P117102), closed in 2017, which
improved the planning and management of the country’s sanitation sector as well as provided access to sanitation. The
Cameroon Sanitation Project supported mostly rural municipalities in preparing and approving 17 local sanitation plans,
provided 116,564 people with access to improved sanitation facilities, and delivered hygiene education to 466,555 people
in targeted municipalities and settlements. Taking the lessons from all these former World Bank-financed projects,
SEWASH in its first phase is focusing on drinking water investments and on providing public latrines in peri-urban and rural
areas in three of the poorest regions of the country. Former projects were focused mainly on cities without demonstrating
an increased access at scale to drinking water for the population. The project proposes simple and robust infrastructures
in rural and peri-urban areas which would allow easy maintenance and facilitate their sustainability. Phase 1 also focuses
on rural and peri-urban areas where the access to basic WSS services is the lowest in the country.

different approach. Learning from the past PPP intervention, the MPA will first focus on operationalizing the legislation

 IV. PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY
 A. Technical, Economic and Financial Analysis
       (i)   Technical Analysis
61.      The project aims to tackle Cameroon’s water sector challenges: The biggest challenge in the WASH sector is poor
access to safe drinking water and sanitation facilities, especially among the rural populations of the North, Far-North,
Adamawa, North-West, South-West and East regions. Inadequate hygiene practices affect family health and nutrition and
contribute to the high mortality rate of children under 5 (79 per 1000 live births according to DHS, 2018). Cameroon’s
WASH sector faces challenges due to population growth, urbanization, and climate change, with notable gaps in water,
sanitation, and hygiene services between urban and rural areas. The project analysis highlighted three key areas that need
addressing. (i) To strengthen the policy dialogue: in addition to the two implementing agencies (MINEE and CAMWATER)
the steering committee will include representatives of MINEPAT and MINFI. This way the project and its expected
outcomes will be fully integrated in the governmental planning process and counterpart funds will be granted. (ii) To
strengthen the operationalization of IWRM and improve stakeholder involvement: the two sub- basin management
plans developed in the Benoue basin will benefit from the creation of sub basin-committees. These committees will involve
local communities (including women). They will contribute to the decision-making process for the selection and the
prioritization of the measures to be included in the sub-Basin plan. (iii) The construction of the water supply systems and
of the institutional latrines in about 100 peri-urban and rural areas have benefited from feasibility studies prepared by
                                                                                                                    Page 16
          The World Bank
          Cameroon Water Security Project (P180321)


AfDB, including environmental notice. The 40,000 new connections for Douala and Yaounde have feasibility studies
approved by MINEE.
62.       The operation is aligned with the climate adaptation and mitigation goals of the Paris Alignment. The MPA is
fully aligned with the global framework of the Water Security and Climate Adaptation Global Challenge Program (Water
GCP). The project is consistent with Cameroon’s Updated NDC, National Adaptation Plan, and will also contribute to the
CCDR’s recommendation for improved infrastructure planning to support resilience and efficient water use. Assessment
and reduction of mitigation risks: Proposed activities financed by the project are on the Universally Aligned List and do not
pose the material risk of having a negative impact on the country’s low-GHG emissions mitigation pathway. While
Cameroon contributes only 0.25 percent of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, its mitigation target in the updated
NDC is a conditional pledge to cut 35 percent of GHG emissions by 2030. Contributing project activities include the
construction of solar-powered water standpipes and climate-proofing of household latrine systems. Furthermore,
feasibility studies to implement priority actions in IWRM plans will include reforestation, sustainable forest management,
small water storage infrastructures, integration of solar pumps in pumping stations, low-emission technologies, and
wetland restoration. Assessment and reduction of adaptation risks: The main climate risks likely to have a material impact
on the operation’s activities and the achievement of its PDO are drought, floods, and erratic rainfall. Activities supporting
institutional capacity strengthening, research, and implementation under Components A, B, and C are at low risk from
being impacted by climate hazards. These activities, including improving IWRM and access to WSS services, are in fact
essential to building climate resilience of the sector. The water supply and sanitation facilities under Component C are
likely to be at risk from climate hazards including droughts and floods, however, these risks will be managed through the
incorporation of climate considerations in the infrastructure design based on the feasibility studies that will be conducted.
Therefore, the residual adaptation risks will be reduced to an acceptable level.
63.     The operation takes a systematic approach in strengthening the climate resilience and low-carbon development
of Cameroon’s water sector. Cameroon is one of the world’s most vulnerable countries to climate change, ranking 155th
out of 187 countries in the ND-GAIN index19, and Cameroon’s water insecurity is increasingly driven by climate change.
•    Subcomponent A1 supports the institutional reforms critical for implementing IWRM and WSS-related regulations and
     will address climate-induced water insecurity by integrating climate adaptation measures such as
     hydrometeorological assessments and climate-resilient infrastructure planning in master plans. Subcomponent A2
     focuses on building technical and institutional capacity to deliver climate-resilient water services. This includes
     conducting feasibility studies for phase 2 and 3 of the water supply systems, and training staff on climate-resilient
     water services, as well as quantifying NRW and reducing system losses through the installation of smart water meters.
•    Component B primarily focuses on promoting water security and increasing climate resilience in two sub-basins
     located in semi-arid regions of the country that are highly vulnerable to climate-induced drought and floods. The
     IWRM aims to enhance the watershed management and promote nature-based solutions, such as reforestation, to
     reduce water losses and soil erosion, therefore strengthening drought resilience and mitigating flood risks. This
     component will develop a comprehensive water security diagnostic that includes the climate change vulnerability
     analysis and introduces climate-resilient measures in water resources management.
•    Component C will reduce water losses including NRW through the installation of meters. A total of 40,000 water
     meters will be installed in Yaoundé and Douala, and these meters have been proven to reduce water consumption by
     up to 46 percent, contributing to significant GHG reduction.
•    Subcomponent C1 will finance the construction of 2,200 water standpipes (US$92 million) that aims to address the
     climate-exacerbated water scarcity issues in the rural areas of the Adamawa, North, and Far North regions and build
     the adaptive capacity of the local water system. Among the 2,200 standpipes, at least 1,100 of them will be powered
     by solar pumps. The small water supply systems will incorporate climate-resilient design, including the use of

              (ii)       Economic and Financial Analysis
64. The project will produce economic benefits for existing and new water users by providing new or improved
services. The expected benefits, which far outweigh the investment costs, include (i) beneﬁcial health impacts derived
from WSS investments; (ii) increased production and productivity within economic sectors, particularly in agricultural and
livestock, based on improved WRM and water supply; and (iii) a boost in economic growth and poverty reduction due to
the overall economic benefits of improved WSS. Three different methodologies were used to estimate the economic and
financial impact of the project (see Annex 4), based on different studies and evidence: (i) the first focused on the health
impact and its derived economic benefits; (ii) the second assessed the growth impact of investment in water and
sanitation; and (iii) the third is an overall economic assessment of the project. However, these different results cannot be
added up since they overlap in some areas and do not span over the same period.
65. The total net benefits derived from health impacts arising from the project (net present value [NPV]) range from a
minimum of US$127.3 million to a maximum of US$254.1 million, five years after the investment is made. The internal
rates of return (IRRs) range from 39 percent to 65 percent, implying that the project is worth undertaking, since this is
significantly higher than the discount rate of 8 percent used. The project will have an impact on the growth in the three
regions. The net present value is estimated at US$40.8 million, due to its impact on the country’s productivity.
66. Overall economic benefits of improved water and sanitation induced by the project have been evaluated according
to SIWI20 (2004) report. This report mentioned that the economic benefits range from US$3 to US$34 per US$1 invested
on drinking water and sanitation, depending on the region and technologies applied. Based on this hypothesis, the overall
economic benefits derived from the project’s investment, will have an NPV ranging from US$450.2 million up to US$750.7
million and an IRR ranging from 40 percent to 53 percent.
67. A technical and economic analysis has been undertaken for CAMWATER based on its financial statements of
202321. CAMWATER's current production capacity is around 1,055,741 m3/day, while estimated demand exceeds
1,700,000 m3/day, with the commissioning of the Drinking Water Supply Project for the city of Yaoundé and its environs

D. Environmental and Social
82.      Environmental Policies. The project environmental risk is rated Substantial. Although the main, long-term impact
of the project is expected to be positive, there are several short-term adverse risks and impacts associated with activities
that need to be addressed under Components B and C. These activities can have potential impacts and risks for the
physical, biological, and human environment such as (i) destruction of plant cover (plantations); (ii) disturbance of aquatic
fauna; (iii) accidental spillage of hydrocarbons and waste oils (maintenance of machinery); (iv) risk of degradation of soil
structure through compaction (machinery traffic); (v) risk of groundwater contamination by chemical pollutants; air
pollution; noise pollution; risk of respiratory illness due to air pollution (dust and exhaust emissions); (vi) risk of damage
to cultural remains in the event of accidental discoveries during excavations; (vii) risk of work-related accidents; (viii)
occupational health and safety risks; (ix) disruption to the normal peace and quiet of local communities; and (x) risk of
disruption to road traffic during the works and to access to local residents. However, measures resulting from the
implementation of similar projects in similar contexts and scientific and technical knowledge exist to address these risks
and impacts. But, in addition to these risks and impacts related to project activities, it is important to note that the project
will be implemented in the three northern regions prone to drought, the advancing desert and flooding, and in the cities
of Yaoundé and Douala, which are characterized by poorly structured neighborhoods that will make the works phase
challenging due to space constraints for organizing safe work areas. While the MPA is not expected to negatively impact
protected areas and natural or critical habitats, some project activities will be implemented in rural and peri-urban areas
with high biodiversity conservation value and/or ecosystem services. Given that the specific sites where the project
activities will be implemented are not clearly defined, the project has prepared an Environmental and Social Management
Framework (ESMF) which identifies the risks associated with the various project interventions, defines the mitigation
measures to be implemented and defines the institutional monitoring arrangements to be made before, during and after
the implementation of the project activities to eliminate or mitigate the adverse environmental impacts. The ESMF also
includes criteria and procedures to ensure that all sensitive ecosystems are identified and treated accordingly. Watershed
Management Plans will pay attention to biodiversity surveys and include Biodiversity Management Plans. The ESMF was
consulted and disclosed on December 16, 2024.
83.      Social policies. Social risks associated with this project are related to resettlement compensation, population
influx in project areas, the marginalization of vulnerable groups and security risks. The extension or rehabilitation of water
supply and sanitation in rural areas, small towns, Yaoundé, and Douala is not expected to cause significant physical
displacements, resulting in minimal resettlement compensation. The project will primarily focus on rehabilitating existing
water supply systems that are no longer functional or adapted to the production capacity of the national water service
provider (CAMWATER). Potential extensions of water supply systems are also not expected to require significant
resettlement compensation as they will be installed within the right-of-way of existing roads, which is part of CAMWATER's
deployment strategy. This significantly reduces the risk associated with resettlement compensation. The project will
involve small-scale activities carried out by local municipal councils, which are unlikely to generate labor influx associated
to civil works. Consequently, the risk associated with community health and safety, such as tensions between project
communities and migrant workers, is expected to be minimal. The risk of excluding marginalized groups has been
mitigated by the project design as it is planning to improve access to potable water in public entities such as health centers,

 V. GRIEVANCE REDRESS SERVICES

87.     Grievance Redress. Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by a project
supported by the World Bank may submit complaints to existing project-level grievance mechanisms or the World Bank’s
Grievance Redress Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed to address project-
related concerns. Project affected communities and individuals may submit their complaint to the World Bank’s
independent Accountability Mechanism (AM). The AM houses the Inspection Panel, which determines whether harm
occurred, or could occur, as a result of the World Bank non-compliance with its policies and procedures. It also includes
the Dispute Resolution Service, which provides communities and borrowers with the opportunity to address complaints
through dispute resolution. Complaints may be submitted to the AM at any time after concerns have been brought directly
to the attention of World Bank Management and after Management has been given the opportunity to respond. For
information on how to submit complaints to the World Bank’s GRS, visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information
on how to submit complaints to the World Bank’s Accountability Mechanism, visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.

 VI. KEY RISKS

88.    The overall project risk rating is Substantial. Key risks as well as mitigation measures to minimize their potential
impact have been identified.
89.      Political and Governance risk is rated as High. Political and governance risks are heightened by Cameroon’s
sociopolitical crisis in the Northwest and Southwest, and Boko-Haram attacks in the Far North. Upcoming elections in
October 2025 may reduce the pace of the reforms. These challenges threaten stability and may divert resources from
reform. The authorities have taken essential steps to curb the adverse social impacts of shocks by extending social safety
net programs and providing targeted subsidies for fertilizers to improve crop yields. Prior actions under Cameroon Second
Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth DPF will contribute to improving decentralization, social inclusion
and digitalization of Civil Registration systems, as well as to addressing the drivers of fragility and conflict and reinforcing
the social contract.
90.       The Macroeconomic risk is rated as Substantial. Risks stem from Cameroon’s reliance on oil and raw materials
exports, as well as imports of key goods like fuel and rice. These dependencies heighten vulnerability to external shocks,
impacting fiscal stability. Low global commodity prices could cut export earnings and government revenue, potentially
forcing reductions in capital and social spending. High prices for food, energy, and fertilizer may keep inflation high, adding
to fiscal pressures via increased subsidies and tax exemptions. Monetary tightening in advanced economies could worsen
the economic outlook and debt sustainability. Declines in exports and tax revenue, coupled with weak State-Owned
Enterprises (SOEs) governance, add further fiscal and debt risks, threatening program implementation by reducing the
government’s fiscal space. While these risks cannot be fully mitigated, fiscal controls and tax reforms—including reduced
fuel subsidies—partially address them. Additionally, new tax and customs measures under the national budget, supported
by the International Monetary Fund (IMF)’s Extended credit Facility – Extended Fund Facility (ECF-EFF) program, the World
Bank’s Cameroon Second Fiscal Sustainability, Inclusive and Sustainable Growth DPF and IMPACT PforR, anchor prudent
policies to help manage these risks. The government is implementing measures under the Public Financial Management
Reform Strategic Plan for 2024-2027.
91.      Sector Strategies and Policies risk is rated Substantial. In recent years, several institutional reforms have been
approved and will contribute to improving sector strategies and policies. In 2019, the Decentralization Law was approved;
it allows delegation of public water services to municipalities. In November 2022, the National Water Policy was approved;
it aims to contribute to the country's sustainable development, by providing appropriate solutions to water-related

                                                                                                                         Page 24

problems, considering the effects of climate change, and from a management perspective, promoting a sustainable use
of water resources and associated environmental resources. Owing to the ongoing cycle of reforms, the risk may be rated

regime. The revised water law was finalized by MINEE in May 2024, sent to the Prime Minister in June 2024, still awaiting

                                                           ANNEX 4: Economic and Financial Analysis


Introduction
1.      The program is expected to provide significant benefits with increased access to safe drinking water and
sanitation services/facilities in rural and peri-urban areas. Based on a literature review, the expected benefits from
investment in water and sanitation are the following:24
    a. Improved WSS and water resources management have beneﬁcial health impacts.
    b. Improved water resources management and water supply contribute significantly to increased production and
        productivity within economic sectors.
    c. The overall economic benefits of improved WSS far outweigh the investment costs and can boost countries’
        economic growth and contribute greatly to poverty eradication.
2.      The evaluation has been based on different studies and evidence using three different methodologies to estimate
the economic and financial impact: (a) the first methodology was l focused on the health impact and its derived economic
benefits, (b) the second methodology assessed the growth impact of investment in water and sanitation, and (c) the third
methodology is an overall economic assessment of the project. However, these different results cannot be added up since
they overlap in some areas and do not span over the same period.
Main Assumptions:
3.      The project will cover 875,000 people in basic drinking water services (650,000 in three northern regions and
225,000 in Douala and Yaoundé) and 400,000 people in limited sanitation services. There will be an overlap between those
that are covered by drinking water services and those covered by limited sanitation services, but the extent of this overlap
is not known yet. In the absence of precise data on the number of people who will benefit from both drinking water and
sanitation, it will be assumed that 50 percent of those covered by limited sanitation will also be covered by drinking water.
Therefore, the following coverage is assumed:
          • limited sanitation only: 400,000
          • Basic drinking water alone: 875,000
          • Both limited sanitation and drinking water: 200,000.

Health Impacts and Subsequent Economic Benefits
4.       Over recent decades, compelling evidence has been gathered demonstrating that signiﬁcant and beneﬁcial health
impacts are associated with improvements in access to safe drinking water and sanitation facilities (Waddington et al.
2009). These impacts are detailed in the table below. There are three main economic benefits related to health impacts
of improved water and sanitation services.25
a) Savings related to seeking less health care. Health care savings are estimated as a function of treatment-seeking rates,
    medical practices, and unit costs of medical services. Medical practices include the types of treatment given for a
    disease and the rate of in-patient admission or referral.
b) Savings related to productive time losses from disease. Productivity losses are estimated based on disease rates, the
    number of days absent from productive activities, and the unit value of productive time. Given the stringent data

                                                 ANNEX 7: Gender Analysis

1.    The Gender Inequality Index ranked Cameroon among the most unequal countries globally: 141 out of 162 in
2019 with an index of 0.560 (UNWOMEN Cameroon). Women hold 17 percent of leadership roles in government
ministries, and 34 percent of seats in Parliament. Gender gap in education is particularly large in rural and northern
regions (WB, 2024). Young girls were twice as much out of education, employment or training in 2021, as boys did:
30.4 percent vs. 14.7 percent. Women only composed 32.3 percent of graduates from tertiary STEM programs in 2017,
and 25.2 percent from engineering. Women are, overrepresented in informal, low-paying sectors and have limited
ownership of asset, access to credit and land ownership: only 13.6 percent owned a dwelling alone or jointly, vs. 32.1
percent for men, i.e a gender gap of 18 percent (UNDP, 2021).
3.    Women disproportionately bear the burden of water collection, spending hours daily, which impacts their
education, income, and health. Only 52 percent of the Northern population has access to safe drinking water compared
to 82 percent in urban areas (World Bank 2021). Women’s economic marginalization compounds the issue, as limited
access to credit particularly hinders investment in water solutions for female-headed households, and long walks for
water expose women and girls to safety risks and further increase their vulnerability.
4.     Inadequate sanitation disproportionately affects women and girls, increasing health risks related to
reproductive and menstrual health, dignity, mobility and school attendance. The maternal mortality ratio in 2020 was
438 per 100,000, the 21st highest globally. Poor menstrual hygiene impacts long-term health, dignity, and education.
In peri-urban areas, cultural stigmas around menstruation, overcrowding in informal settlements, financial barriers and
lack of gender-segregated facilities exacerbate sanitation issues, increasing risks of gender-based violence around open
defecation sites especially at night.
5.    Women are underrepresented in managerial, skilled and technical roles in the water sector. In CAMWATER,
women hold fewer than 32 percent of senior staff positions and 20 percent of leadership roles, and the Ministry of
Water Resources and Energy similarly lacks gender-balanced representation (CAMWATER Statistical Yearbook, 2022).
Gender biases in recruitment and promotion practices favor men, perpetuating stereotypes that associate technical
and leadership roles with male competencies (GWP-Cmr Report, 2023). Women also have lower enrollment in capacity-
building programs, limiting their career advancement. The lack of gender-disaggregated data, lack of role model and
champions, hinders efforts to address disparities and discourages enrollment in STEM studies. Excluding women from
leadership roles results in policies that fail to meet their unique needs, perpetuating systemic inequalities in water and
sanitation access.
6.      Lower participation in decision-making and control of water resources and land compounds gaps in access. Land
ownership largely determines access to water and women own 16.4 percent of land compared to 83.6 percent for men.
Although land ownership laws are formally gender-neutral, Cameroon's succession laws allow for patrilineal control of
property, and discriminatory practices and social norms disadvantage women. Family law also introduces unequal
treatment, as husbands manage household property. Limited access to financing restricts women's ability to purchase
land, undermining their influence in community water management. This exclusion perpetuates economic dependence
and limits their ability to secure private water sources or advocate for community resources that meet their needs,
perpetuating economic marginalization, affecting agricultural productivity and eventually subsistence.
