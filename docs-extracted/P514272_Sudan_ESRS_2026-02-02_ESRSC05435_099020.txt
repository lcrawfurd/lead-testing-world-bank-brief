                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The proposed STARR Project aims to strengthen agricultural and livestock systems, revitalize seed security, and promote
                     private sector–led value chain development by delivering improved seeds, fertilizers, animal feed, and veterinary
                     services; rehabilitating rural infrastructure; and expanding access to finance and markets for SMEs and farmer
                     cooperatives. Together, these interventions would build a stronger agricultural sector that enhances food security,
                     creates jobs, and supports more resilient livelihoods. The project will comprise three components: Component 1:
                     Revitalizing Agricultural and Livestock Systems The project will boost productivity of key crops and livestock through
                     improved inputs, extension services, and veterinary support. It will rehabilitate community-based infrastructure,

                     focusing on irrigation systems, rainwater harvesting structures, and livestock watering points. Solar-powered irrigation
                     pumps will enhance water availability, especially in drought-prone areas. Additional efforts include repairing canals,
                     restoring storage facilities to reduce post-harvest losses, and improving rural market access via feeder roads and
                     bridges. Component 2: Strengthening Seed Systems This component aims to ensure long-term access to quality seeds by
                     restoring and expanding community seed banks and supporting local cooperatives with training on seed management. It
                     will revive research and development for improved seed production and build capacity for seed certification aligned
                     with global standards. Investments in local seed enterprises will increase the availability of climate-resilient varieties,
                     reducing reliance on external sources and improving recovery readiness. Component 3: Enhancing Value Chains and
                     Private Sector Engagement The project will strengthen agricultural and livestock value chains to address food insecurity
                     and promote recovery. It will mobilize private investment and empower SMEs, cooperatives, and farmer associations to
                     manage input supply, processing, and logistics. Activities will include upgrading irrigation systems, transport, storage,
                     and post-harvest processing, financed through matching grants and supported by technical assistance. This approach
                     will stabilize food supply, lower prices, and create jobs for vulnerable groups, especially youth and women. Digital
                     Market Platform linking farmers, SMEs and markets The project will expand and operationalize the Mahsul digital
                     platform to improve market access and value chain integration for farmers, cooperatives, and agri-SMEs. Mahsul,
                     launched in 2024 under the ongoing Sudan Thabat Project, currently operates in three states (Northern, River Nile and
                     Kassala) and connects farmers to markets through transparent B2B transactions. By reducing intermediaries and
                     promoting price transparency, the platform will stabilize prices and enable vulnerable communities to access affordable
                     food commodities through organized selling points. Geographical Targeting Target localities, communities, and
                     agricultural cooperatives will be identified using a formula established under the ongoing Sudan Thabat Project, based
                     on clearly defined criteria including security conditions, agricultural and livestock potential, access to water resources,

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     Sudan is a country affected by fragility, conflict, and violence (FCV) with interdependent drivers of fragility, namely (i)
                     regional imbalances and social exclusion; (ii) a dysfunctional economy; (iii) the securitization of governance; (iv) natural
                     disasters; and (v) weak institutional capacity. Sudan is facing an unprecedented crisis, with ongoing conflict since April
                     2023 triggering the world’s fastest-growing displacement and severe economic contraction. As of October 2025, more
                     than 11.7 million people have been forcibly displaced. Sudan now has the largest number of internally displaced
                     persons (IDPs) globally, and the humanitarian situation is compounded by recurrent outbreaks of disease, the
                     destruction of critical infrastructure, and widespread food insecurity.
                     The conflict has sharply undermined agricultural production and disrupted the entire agrifood value chain. Access to
                     inputs, credit, and rural infrastructure is severely constrained, with high transport costs and fragile seed supply. Many
                     farmers have been displaced, fields left uncultivated, agriculture and irrigation equipment looted or destroyed, and
                     seed production and storage disrupted. The Project will start targeting localities, communities, and agricultural
                     cooperatives in Gezira and Sennar states due to the improved security situation and high agricultural potential in these

                     services, and complementarity with other development partner interventions. There are significant vulnerable

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The institutional capacity of Sudan is weak, driven by prolonged instability, a legacy of non-transparent institutions, and
                     widespread capture by vested interests. In October 2021, the military took over Sudan’s Government, which triggered
                     the World Bank’s Operational Policy (OP) 7.30 - Dealings with De Facto Governments, halting disbursements in all World
                     Bank operations and preventing the processing of new operations, effective October 25, 2021. Thus, the STARR Project
                     will be implemented through a Third-Party Implementation (TPI) arrangement. At this stage, the Third-Party
                     implementing entity is not identified. However, it will build on the successful TPI experience of the Sudan THABAT
                     Project where reputable UN agencies and international NGOs were able to deliver results at scale, mobilize technical
                     expertise, and maintain operational flexibility despite challenging conditions. The STARR Project will adopt a similar TPI
                     mechanism, appraising recipients/implementing partners with proven capacity in agricultural recovery, community

                     The TPI will establish a Project Implementation Unit (PIU) to carry out day-to-day management and implementation of

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                           Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                      Moderate
                     The Project inherent environmental risk is rated ‘Moderate’. Project activities are not anticipated to cause conversion
                     of natural habitats, or generation of large-scale pollutants given their small-scale nature and the rehabilitation
                     activities being limited within the existing irrigation schemes. The construction and rehabilitation of the small-scale
                     irrigation schemes may involve small-scale site clearance and removal of vegetation, resulting in soil erosion due to
                     exposure of soil surfaces to rain and wind during site clearing, and excavation activities. Construction or rehabilitation
                     activities, though small, could cause noise, air, and water pollution, and increase project workers and communities’


                    Feb 02, 2026                                                                                                         Page 4 of 9
                              The World Bank

                     exposure to health and safety impacts. Potential occupational health and safety (OHS) risks may also result from the
                     construction and rehabilitation of the irrigation schemes and other infrastructures. Although the irrigation schemes to
                     be supported by the project will be small-scale, they could use agrochemicals, though not to be financed by the
                     Project, that could pollute water and soil and cause risks to humans from mishandling and absence of protection.
                     There are also potential environmental, health, and safety risks from project activities including waste management
                     related to the disposal of used batteries. If handling and management of waste is not done properly, it could be
                     indiscriminately discharged into the environment and cause damage to the community and the ecosystem. No TA
                     that will have downstream environmental and social risks, such as feasibility studies, design, and preparation of
                     strategic studies, is anticipated in this Project. However, any TA activities providing for capacity-building as well as
                     training shall be undertaken in compliance with the requirements of the ESF.
                                                                                                                                       Substantial
                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The inherent social risk of the STARR Project is rated ‘Substantial’. Social risks include (i) exclusion of ethnic minorities
                     (indigenous peoples) and vulnerable populations from project activities and benefits, (ii) security threats (including
                     UXO), (iii) gender-based violence (GBV) and sexual exploitation and abuse/harassment (SEA/SH), (iv) weak community
                     engagement due to remoteness and inaccessibility of some areas, and (v) threats to cultural heritage. Land
                     acquisition and displacement risk is not anticipated at this stage. The contextual risk in Sudan is high mainly due to
                     the FCV context of the country and the ongoing conflict that continues to result in large number of IDPs. The STARR
                     Project will be implemented in conflict-affected localities and IDP hosting areas, which are volatile and highly prone to
                     instability and conflict situations. As a result, there will be security risks for staff deployed to provide services for the
                     conflict-affected communities while moving from area to area. In addition, there might be tensions or conflicts




Public Disclosure
                     between the IDPs and the host communities that may impact the timely implementation of Project activities and
                     delay the delivery of benefits. The majority of the peope lack a sustainable source of livelihood, or income sources,
                     and want to maximize their life-saving opportunities and to meet their basic survival needs by moving to project
                     locations where they believe that good benefits will be available which could lead to conflict. There is also the risk of
                     inadequate monitoring, supervision, and timely redress of complaints, which could impact implementation. Social
                     risks might be exacerbated due to contextual factors and the remoteness of project areas.

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                                Relevant

                      Potential labor risks include child and forced labor, risks related to inappropriate working conditions and
                      Occupational Health and Safety (OHS), discrimination, and risks of UXOs to workers. The TPI will prepare LMP and it
                      will include requirements of ESS2 and available national labor laws. The LMP will be prepared and disclosed prior to
                      the commencement of subproject activities. It will summarize procedures to address labor issues including, but not
                      limited to: (i) child labor and forced labor; (ii) contracts of employment and terms and conditions of employment (iii)
                      protection of wages including fair treatment, non-discrimination, and equal opportunity (iv) OHS issues and
                      mitigation measures (v) labor influx and associated risks, including SEA/SH (vi) security provisions for workers (vii)
                      code of conduct and (viii) GRM for workers.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                                 Relevant

                      Although the revitalizing of irrigated agricultural and livestock production systems under Component 1 are not
                      expected to have significant risk profiles due to their minor scale, the ESMF shall include procedures for screening
                      small-scale irrigation subprojects to identify those with impacts on surface and groundwater, including assessment of
                      current and planned uses of water within the watershed, assessing short- and long-term cumulative impacts on
                      communities, other users, and the ecosystem services. The project prohibits the use of funds to purchase pesticides.
                      Nonetheless, some farmers may choose to use agro-chemicals, so the ESMF requires TPI and its implementing
                      partners to provide information and training on acceptable and unacceptable pesticides, and will encourage farmers
                      to comply with international standards of pesticide use, storage, and disposal. The ESMF includes an IPMG, which the
                      implementing partners must apply through their use of the ESMF screening procedure.

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                                 Relevant

                      Construction and/or rehabilitation activities could cause noise, vehicle emissions, traffic accidents, dust, and soil and
                      water pollution with an impact on the communities. Other community health and safety risks include (i) SEA/SH, and
                      (ii) security risks including risks of UXOs. The SEA/SH risk will be managed by implementing the SEA/SH Action Plan,
                      including a COC as outlined in the ESCP. The SEA/SH Action Plan will include mitigation measures such as regular
                      consultations with women and girls on project-related risks, signing of the COC for all project staff with specific
                      clauses on SEA/SH, establishing SEA/SH sensitive GRM. TPI will carry out security risk assessment and will prepare a
                      security risk management plan (SRMP). A conflict-sensitive approach to overall project implementation will be

                      The people who meet the ESS7 requirements in Sudan include pastoralists and agro-pastoralists, who constitute

                      (IFAD), there are over 500 ethnic groups in Sudan speaking over 400 languages. In Gezira and Sennar states, there are

                      ESS8 - Cultural Heritage                                                                                         Relevant

                      The Project will finance the rehabilitation and/or construction of community-based agricultural and rural
                      infrastructure; irrigation infrastructure; livestock watering points; repair of canals, headworks, and control structures;
                      rehabilitation and restoration of storage facilities including grain silos and warehouses; rehabilitation of feeder roads,
                      bridges, and water crossings under Component 1. These activities will involve excavation that could have an impact
                      on cultural heritage. Risks to tangible cultural heritage will be addressed as follows: (i) subprojects likely to be
                      implemented within, or in the vicinity of, a known cultural heritage site will be eliminated through the ESMF
                      screening procedure; and (ii) unexpected encounters with cultural heritage artefacts during subproject
                      implementation will be addressed by applying a Chance Find Procedure that will be part of the ESMF.
