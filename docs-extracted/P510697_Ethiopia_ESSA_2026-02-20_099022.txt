Executive Summary

Ethiopia: Second Community Action for Landscape Management Program for Results (CALM-2) consists
of two major result areas: Participatory Watershed Management and Land Administration and Management
in Non-Rangeland Areas, with a total of eleven disbursement-linked indicators. The World Bank has
proposed the CALM Program with Program-for-Results (PforR) financing, aiming to strengthen
government systems and institutional capacity for implementation, shifting from input-based to results-
based approaches with defined accountability.

As part of the preparation and appraisal of the CALM 2 program, the World Bank has updated and consulted
on an Environmental and Social Systems Assessment (ESSA). This update was conducted to assess the
capacity of the implementing agency i.e., Ministry of Agriculture (MoA), particularly its Natural Resource
Management and Land Administration and Use Directorates, in managing environmental, social, and safety
risks associated with sustainable natural resource management. Specifically, the ESSA aimed to: (i) identify
potential environmental and social (E&S) risks and impacts associated with the Program; (ii) assess the
strengths and weaknesses of the legal, institutional, and implementation frameworks; and (iii) recommend
measures to strengthen national and regional systems and capacity to deliver the PforR sustainably. The
ESSA was conducted within the framework of the six Core Guiding Principles of PforR financing: (i)
General Principle of E&S Management; (ii) Natural Habitats and Physical Cultural Resources (PCR); (iii)
Public and Workers Safety; (iv) Land Acquisition and Loss of Access to Natural Resources; (v) Indigenous
Peoples and Vulnerable Groups; and (vi) Social Conflict. The assessment methodology included desk
reviews, key informant interviews, focus group discussions, community consultations, and institutional
analysis. The updated ESSA will be disclosed in country and at the World Bank’s external website for
garnering feedback, consulted in a stakeholder's workshop, incorporate the feedback from the public
disclosure and the stakeholder's workshop and disclose the revised version before appraisal.
Summary of Key Findings

Applicable Policy, Legal, and institutional Framework aspect: Ethiopia has a reasonably robust legal
and institutional framework for E&S management of development projects and programs. The E&S
performance of the ongoing CALM program is currently rated as satisfactory. Few proclamations and
regulations have been repealed and replaced by new since the ongoing ESSA for CALM has been prepared
and approved. These include: (i) Labor Proclamation (Proc. No. 1156/2019), which replaces Labor
Proclamation No. 377/2003, (ii) Expropriation of Land Holdings for Public Purposes, Payments of
Compensation and Resettlement of Displaced People (Proc. No. 1336/2024) for amended the Expropriation
of Land Holdings for Public Purposes, Payments of Compensation and Resettlement of Displaced People
Proclamation (Proc. No. 1161/2019), and (iii) Expropriation and Valuation, Compensation and
Resettlement Council of Ministers Regulation (Reg. No. 472/2020), which replaces Regulation No.
135/2007. In addition, the federal EPA has recently (since April 2025) replaced the former EIA
Proclamation (Proclamation No. 299/2002) with the newly enacted ESIA Proclamation (Proclamation No.
1371/2025)

Beneficial Environmental and Social Impacts of CALM 2 The CALM 2 program is expected to deliver
substantial environmental and social benefits by scaling up participatory watershed management and land
administration activities. Environmentally, it will improve vegetation cover, reduce soil erosion, enhance
water availability, and regenerate biodiversity. Socially, it strengthens community ownership, supports
livelihood opportunities for youth and women, and improves tenure security through Second-Level
Landholding Certificates (SLLCs). These interventions foster long-term sustainability, empower vulnerable
groups, and reinforce local institutions and social cohesion.

Potential Adverse Environmental Risk: While most anticipated risks are site-specific and reversible,
CALM 2 faces challenges due to limited E&S management capacity at woreda level. Poor watershed

Analysis of ESMS of implementing agencies: The Ministry of Agriculture (MoA) has a strong track

CALM, PSNP, LFSDP, ELRP, and other major initiatives, demonstrating institutional capacity and
commitment to sustainable development. To meet the Program Action Plan (PAP) requirements under
CALM, the MoA developed an Environmental and Social Management System Guideline (ESMSG.
Environmental and Social (E&S) Risk Management in the CALM 2 Program is overseen by the Ministry

one E&S specialists (at the NRM dirctorate), one E&S specilists (at the RLUA dirctorate), Gender

reporting of E&S risks.

Assessment against core principles: The ESSA analysis indicates that all six core principles of PforR
financing are applicable to the CALM 2 Program for reasons described in the following sections.

    •   Core Principle 1: General Principle of E&S Impact Assessment and Management: this principle
        is applicable due to the materiality of environmental and social (E&S) risks across its components.
        Ethiopia has a solid legal and institutional foundation for E&S management, recently strengthened
        by the 2025 ESIA Proclamation, which aligns with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social
        Framework (ESF). The Ministry of Agriculture (MoA) and Environmental Protection Authorities
        (EPAs) have demonstrated capacity in managing E&S risks in Bank-financed projects. However,
        systemic capacity gaps persist—especially at the woreda level—including understaffing, limited
        technical expertise, weak enforcement of ESMPs, and inadequate logistics and budget. Leadership
        awareness and staff turnover further constrain compliance. Opportunities include leveraging two
        decades of E&S enforcement, building CALM 2 systems, and scaling through existing grievance
        redress mechanisms (GRMs), decentralized extension services, and cross-program learning. Risks
        of non-compliance, unmanaged impacts, and weak stakeholder engagement remain substantial if
        capacity and coordination are not strengthened. As part of the mitigation measures, deploying full-
        time E&S staff at all levels, especially in new woredas, and updating ESMSG with clear procedures
        and safety measures should be prioritized.

   •   Core Principle 2: Environmental Considerations—Natural Habitats and Physical Cultural
       Resources (PCRs): Core Principle 2 is applicable due to the potential presence of natural habitats
       and undocumented PCRs in rural watershed sites. Ethiopia’s ESIA legal framework and
       institutional mandates (e.g., ARCCH) support biodiversity and cultural heritage protection. Past
       watershed interventions have not shown major adverse impacts. Nonetheless, awareness and

       guidelines lack explicit filters for sensitive areas, and coordination between EPA and the Ministry
       of Culture and Tourism is weak. Opportunities lie in integrating biodiversity and PCR safeguards
       into screening tools, leveraging indigenous knowledge, and building local capacity. If unaddressed,
       risks include inadvertent damage to cultural assets and ecological degradation. Training zonal and

       offices and promoting community participation in screening are the key issues to be implemented
       under this core principle.
   •   Core Principle 3: Public and Worker Safety CALM 2 watershed activities involve manual labor
       in challenging terrain, triggering occupational health and safety (OHS) risks. Ethiopia’s legal
       framework supports worker protections, and past Bank-financed projects show no major accidents.
       However, enforcement is weak in remote areas, with limited PPE use, safety briefings, and first-
       aid access. Updating ESMSG with watershed-specific OHS guidance, ensure first-aid readiness,
       train local staff and communities, and allocating resources for inspections and safety equipment are
       the main issues to be implemented as part of the mitigation action.
   •   Core Principle 4: Land Acquisition and Access Restrictions: CALM 2 activities may affect land
       tenure, access rights, and livelihoods especially through area closures and land certification. While
       Ethiopia’s legal framework and SLLC systems promote tenure security and community
       engagement, institutional capacity and grievance mechanisms remain weak. Risks include
       exclusion of customary users, inadequate compensation, and social conflict. The main
       recommended action includes strengthening staffing and training, improving GRMs and
       compensation protocols, engaging communities through participatory mapping, and ensuring
       inclusive planning and digital access to land services.
   •   Core Principle 5: Indigenous Peoples and Vulnerable Groups: CALM 2 must ensure culturally
       appropriate and equitable access to benefits for vulnerable groups including women, youth, people
       with disabilities, and the chronically ill. While the program promotes inclusion through
       participatory watershed management and land certification, gaps remain in consultation, gender
       equity, and safeguard implementation. Risks include exclusion, elite capture, and limited access to
       digital services. The priority actions include strengthening inclusive consultations, recruiting social
       specialists, improving GRMs, supporting women’s participation, and adapting digital tools for
       underserved communities.
   •   Core Principle 6: Social Conflict CALM 2 promotes equity and cohesion through participatory
       watershed management and land certification. However, risks of conflict over land use, benefit-
       sharing, and vulnerable group inclusion persist especially in areas affected by political instability
       and weak grievance systems. Strengthen GRMs, train staff in conflict-sensitive approaches, engage
       local leaders, support vulnerable groups, and improve coordination across agencies to prevent
       disputes and ensure inclusive participation are the recommended actions.

1     Introduction
1.1    Background
Land degradation remains a critical challenge in many developing countries, including Ethiopia, which
accounts for about 8% of global land degradation (Habtamu, 2010). To address this issue, Ethiopia
introduced watershed management in the 1980s as a strategy to restore natural resources and enhance land
productivity. Since 2010/11, the government has implemented large-scale, community-based participatory
watershed development programs across four regions. Despite these efforts, land degradation continues to
impose significant economic costs, estimated at 2–3% of agricultural GDP annually. To mitigate these
impacts, the Climate Action through Landscape Management (CALM) Program was launched for a five-
year period (FY20–FY24), aligned with the third phase of the Ethiopia Strategic Investment Framework
(ESIF-3). The Project Development Objective (PDO) of CALM is to promote the adoption of sustainable
land management practices and expand access to secure land titles in non-rangeland rural areas. Building
on these achievements, the Government of Ethiopia, in partnership with the World Bank, is preparing the
Second Community Action for Landscape Management (CALM 2) program. CALM 2 will support the
National Strategic Investment Framework for Sustainable Landscape Management (ESIF 2, 2024–2038)
and contribute to long-term landscape restoration efforts. Implemented under the World Bank’s Program-
for-Results (PforR) financing approach, CALM 2 will include an Investment Project Financing (IPF)
component funded by IDA, the Nordic Development Fund (NDF), and the Scaling Climate Action by
Lowering Emissions (SCALE) initiative. The program aims to expand the geographic coverage of CALM,
introduce new forms of support, and increase the number of beneficiaries, reinforcing Ethiopia’s sustainable
development agenda.

1.2    Rationale for Updating the ESSA
In line with the PforR financing requirements, the World Bank has updated the ongoing CALM ESSA to
assess institutional arrangements and capacity within the proposed program for managing E&S impacts and
risks in a manner consistent with the Operational Policy for Program-for-Results (PforR) financing. This
policy establishes core principles and key planning elements to ensure that PforR operations are designed
and implemented to maximize E&S benefits while avoiding, minimizing, or mitigating potential harm.
PforR adopts a risk management approach for E&S management, tailoring process requirements to the
program context. For the proposed operation, the Bank evaluates at the program level the borrower’s
authority and organizational capacity to achieve E&S objectives in relation to the range of impacts and risks
that may arise.
Accordingly, the World Bank has updated this ESSA using existing CALM ESSA and other documentation
and field-based assessments. The review focuses on the robustness of government systems within the
program's scope, assessing their capacity to plan and implement effective environmental and social
management measures at federal, regional, and woreda levels. Specifically, this updated ESSA evaluates
compliance with the six core principles of PforR financing.

country’s existing systems for environmental and social management in relation to the proposed PforR
activities at both national and regional levels. For each activity, the following steps were undertaken:

2     Program Description
2.1    Program Development Objectives and Key Results
The proposed Second Community Action for Landscape Management (CALM 2) program’s goals are to
reverse the process of land degradation in the Ethiopian highlands and strengthen smallholders’ tenure
security. CALM 2 builds on the country’s proven track record in addressing land degradation and
enhancing climate resilience through participatory watershed management and strengthened land tenure
systems. Tackling land degradation will improve agricultural productivity, create jobs and raise rural
incomes. Improving tenure security will increase smallholders’ incentives to adopt sustainable land
management practices, and reduce local conflicts over land rights and access to resources. Combining
these two approaches increases smallholders’ livelihood security. The Government of Ethiopia (GoE) has
requested support for the second phase of the Ethiopia Strategic Investment Framework for Sustainable
Landscape Management (ESIF 2). GoE's Sustainable Land Management Program (SLMP), active since
2008, aims to combat land degradation, a major issue costing 2-3 percent of agricultural GDP annually.
ESIF-2 will support long-term landscape restoration efforts from 2024 to 2038. The Program supports
priorities in the Global Challenge Program on Forests and the ‘Livable Planet’ Scorecard, focusing on
enhancing resilience, creating more and better jobs, strengthening food security, improving land
management, and reducing GHG emissions. Building on collaboration with IFC, it advances the WBG’s
AgriConnect initiative by (i) supporting smallholders’ access to technology and infrastructure that will
improve productivity and incomes; (ii) supporting government policies that seek to attract private
investment into agriculture, and (iii) expanding access to finance for, and participation in, agri-value
chains.
The Program Development Objective (PDO) is to expand area under sustainable land management
practices, secure land tenure and increase market linkages in highland areas. Progress toward the
achievement of the PDO will be measured through the following indicators:
   • Area under Sustainable Landscape Management practices (hectare)
   • New or better jobs (number)
   • Beneficiaries with enhanced resilience to climate risks (number)
   • Area of landholdings in targeted areas with strengthened security of tenure registered in the
        National Rural Land Administration Information System (NRLAIS) (hectare),
   • Net greenhouse gas emissions (Metric ton CO2eq per year - tCO2eq/yr).

2.2    Description of the Scope and Thematic Boundaries of the Program

The proposed CALM 2 program builds on successful investments and an ongoing Program for Results
Operation (CALM, P170384), scaling one of Africa's largest landscape restoration and land tenure security
initiatives. In total (including CALM), this has now covered 4.8M hectares, 700 highland woredas, 8,000
watersheds, reaching over 11 million beneficiaries. These interventions have delivered annual reductions
of 3.3 million tons of CO₂e through forest protection, climate-smart agriculture, soil and water conservation,
and 80,000 hectares of tree planting, resulting in a 9 percent increase in vegetation cover. Land rights have
also been strengthened, with 9.5 million households receiving certificates—80 percent including women—
and 34,000 landless youth obtaining legal documents. To sustain these results, CALM 2 will strengthen
private sector engagement and financing to reduce long-term dependence on donor support.
Building on the existing CALM, the second PforR program enables a smooth continuation of the
verification and payment systems already in place. This continuity deepens impact and scales up ongoing
innovations. The complementary IPF support provides targeted assistance that the PforR instrument cannot.
It helps address specific technical and managerial capacity constraints so that implementation remains
effective. The DLIs under the PforR component create strong incentives for expanding landscape
restoration and land administration interventions. The IPF component supports the required technical and
procurement assistance. The proposed Program entails support for sustainable landscape management and
land administration, as in CALM, and expands to other areas of ESIF 2 including participatory forest
management, value chain development and land certification of communal forests and grazing lands.

Ethiopia’s legal and policy framework vests ownership of land in the state and the peoples, with rural users
including peasants, semi‑pastoralists, and pastoralists holding use rights that can be leased and bequeathed
but not sold; federal and regional land administration laws operationalize holding, certification, dispute
resolution, and expropriation/compensation (notably Rural Land Administration and Use Proclamation No.
456/2005 and the Expropriation Proclamation No. 1161/2019 as amended by No. 1336/2024). Within this
framework, the CALM Program‑for‑Results (PforR) aims to increase adoption of sustainable land
management practices and expand access to secure land tenure in non‑rangeland rural areas, with land
tenure supported under Results Area 2 through system actions such as Memorandum of Understanding
(MoUs), training, monitoring (MELA), and rollout of the National Rural Land Administration Information
System (NRLAIS), as well as disbursement‑linked indicators (DLIs) that incentivize certification,
registration, and timely updating of transactions. Under the PforR, large‑scale Second‑Level Landholding
Certificates (SLLCs) have been issued and registered digitally in NRLAIS across program woredas, with
functioning monitoring systems and intergovernmental MoUs that promote consistent transaction
recording; the program has also achieved a high share of landholdings registered to women, individually or

jointly, though regional disparities persist (for example, lower shares in Amhara and Tigray), underscoring

areas, it has established participatory watershed institutions—Community Watershed User Cooperative
Societies (CWUCS)—as the governance backbone for communal resources and sustainable land

including random CWUCS sampling and field/geospatial checks, creating a quality‑assurance platform that

rule‑making and mitigated disputes, with related operations (e.g., RLLP) documenting reductions in
boundary disputes and improved confidence in rental markets following certification. At the same time,

risks that can exacerbate grievances and complicate verification and registration; the CALM‑to‑CALM‑2

continued data‑backed verification. Overall, the CALM PforR land tenure package mass certification,
digital registries, MELA, and DLIs for transaction updating has strengthened tenure security, narrowed
gender gaps, and reduced disputes within a legal framework well aligned to country systems; as CALM‑2
scales, formalizing communal tenure linked to CWUCS governance and maintaining strong grievance
redress and verification mechanisms should further reduce historical sources of conflict while sustaining
progress on inclusion and tenure security. Three broad elements of Government of Ethiopia (GoE)

planning units given the physical and social characteristics of these rangelands and (b) provisions for
issuance of communal pastoral land rights are at an earlier stage of development. Third, expenditures

Advancing Landscape Restoration and Climate Resilience. CALM 2 builds on the country’s proven track
record in addressing land degradation and enhancing climate resilience through participatory watershed

innovative approaches such as communal land certification, participatory forest management, payment for
ecosystem services, integration with value chains, and carbon financing, while expanding restoration efforts
to include forested areas. By mobilizing local communities and embedding ownership in planning and
implementation, the program aims to restore degraded landscapes, improve natural resource management,
and secure land tenure and resource rights, thereby increasing agricultural productivity and delivering
substantial climate co-benefits. Through the digitization of land administration and the promotion of
sustainable investments, CALM 2 seeks to ensure long-term sustainability, unlock new economic
opportunities, enhance social cohesion and peace and empower rural households to invest in sustainable
land management practices.
Site Selection and Assesment: the GoE leveraged the Investment Prioritization Tool (IPT) to identify
watersheds with better ecosystem and economic potential for CALM 2 intervention, explicitly factoring in

11 Program target regions that cover about 5,640 major watersheds. The top 20 percent of major watersheds

situated in the highlands. These watersheds will undergo further selection processes at the regional level to
narrow them down to the final Program target. The criteria for further refinement will be determined by
each region and may differ based on priorities set by individual regions. Leveraging the IPT ensures that
resources are directed to where they are most needed and impactful, enhancing efficiency, effectiveness
and transparency.
Potential areas for participatory forest management (PFM) were identified across 25 CIF-targeted
woredas through a multi-criteria screening that considered forest extent, biodiversity opportunities, and
sediment transport risk. This process resulted in 14 priority woredas in Amhara, Oromia, and South
Ethiopia, with a combined intervention area of 206,011 hectares and a program target of 150,000 hectares.
Importantly, ten of these woredas overlap with CALM 2’s prospective sustainable land management (SLM)
zones, allowing for integrated restoration efforts and more efficient technical support. In parallel,
afforestation and reforestation (A/R) priorities were set by overlaying forest maps with degradation data,
identifying approximately 89,500 hectares of degraded land between forest patches as potential corridors,
with 22,500 hectares targeted for restoration. This integrated approach is designed to enhance ecosystem
recovery, strengthen biodiversity, and improve habitat connectivity for wildlife.
Enhancing Rural Livelihoods through Market Linkages and Ecosystem Service Incentives: the
program will strengthen market-driven value chain linkages by connecting rehabilitated watersheds and
certified land users with priority agribusiness sectors, including coffee, soybean/feeds, malt barley,
horticulture, and cotton. The operation will facilitate market access and business development through
targeted support to cooperatives and unions, capacity building in business planning and management, and
strategic engagement with private sector partners. Key interventions will include farmer mobilization,
agronomic training, provision of inputs, field demonstrations, and the introduction of small-scale
technologies to enhance value addition. By embedding these activities within CWUCSs and revolving
funds, the program will localize market benefits, promote inclusive growth, and translate sustainable land
management and secure tenure gains into diversified and resilient rural incomes.
The operationalization of Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) will be piloted as an innovative
mechanism to incentivize sustainable land and natural resource management. Key interventions in
ecosystem services will include farmer mobilization, agronomic training, provision of inputs, field

Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Program Design: Previous and ongoing World Bank support,
under the CALM and RLLP programs, has demonstrated that combining land restoration with the provision
of land tenure security is a powerful means to improve livelihood security and build resilience to climate
impacts. Land tenure security incentivizes smallholders to make considerable investments in time and labor
required to rehabilitate degraded farm and forest land. This in turn provides the basis for improving yields
and greater livelihood security. The current CALM program has demonstrated that PforR provides an
effective way to finance land restoration programs by directly linking financing to tangible, measurable
results rather than to inputs or expenditures. CALM’s focus on outcomes, such as restored land, improved
soil health, and stronger land governance, has ensured both accountability and measurable impact. By using
and strengthening Ethiopia’s own systems, CALM has built institutional capacity and local ownership,
which are essential for sustaining land restoration gains. PforR’s flexibility allows for context-specific,
innovative approaches while also incentivizing policy and governance reforms that address the root causes
of degradation. Previous World Bank support has focused on restoring private land. This has left significant
areas of ‘communal forests’ (often highly degraded scrubland) untouched by both restoration efforts and
initiatives to ensure secure communal land rights. The proposed program will fill this gap by including
communal forest land for both restoration and land tenure security initiatives. Increasing land productivity
creates opportunities for linking smallholder farmers with agribusinesses. This can significantly improve
their incomes and create broader job opportunities. By fostering these connections, the program aims to
attract private sector investment, create jobs and improve incomes, and reduce reliance on donor funding.
Restoring landscapes not only benefits the environment but also enhances carbon sequestration. This
provides an opportunity to tap into carbon credit markets and provides opportunities for communities to
receive payments for environmental services, such as increased groundwater storage, reduced siltation and
improved downstream stream flows. These sorts of payments can reward community groups for their land
restoration efforts. If successful and scalable, this approach could lead to greater financial independence
from donor funding, creating a more sustainable model for both environmental conservation and ongoing
livelihood security.

Federal and Regional Institutions for SLM
At the federal level, existing institutional mechanisms will provide oversight, policy direction, and
coordination of SLM initiatives developed used during the previous ESIF (2016-2023) will be continued

3   ESSA Methodology and Process
The updated ESSA was undertaken to ensure alignment with the World Bank’s PforR financing core
principles and key planning elements for environmental and social management. This approach aims to
effectively manage program risks and promote sustainable development. The gaps identified through this
process, along with recommended actions to address them, will directly contribute to improving the
effectiveness and performance of MoA’s institutional structures and related Implementing Agencies (IAs)
under CALM 2.
To achieve these objectives, a combination of methods was applied to generate comprehensive data:
Sample Selection: Woredas/districts in each regional state were selected based on their performance and
experience in public mobilization, public works, watershed management activities, and prior involvement
in Bank-financed projects. Regional focal persons were consulted for input, and district-level contacts were
established. Accessibility and travel feasibility were also considered. Watershed management and mass
mobilization sites were chosen in consultation with district and kebele officials to ensure diversity in
performance and agro-ecological context.

4     Assessment of Potential E&S Benefits, Impacts and Risks
4.1    Beneficial E&S Impacts of the Program
Expanding watershed management on communal lands indeed has dual benefits—environmental
rehabilitation and social and livelihood gains for youth and women—and to realize these while avoiding
unintended downstream impacts, community-based actions should be coordinated with broader micro-/sub-
watershed and catchment plans. Evidence from ongoing CALM and related programs (SLMP/RLLP/PSNP)
shows that communal land restoration linked to youth and women groups has delivered both ecological
recovery and enterprise opportunities, while soil and water conservation measures (terraces, trenches,
micro-basins), gully rehabilitation with check dams and revegetation, and establishment of enclosures have
increased ground cover, reduced erosion, and improved infiltration, often enabling fodder production
through cut-and-carry systems; independent evaluations report substantial biophysical gains such as large
reductions in soil loss and sediment export alongside halved gully volumes and improved vegetation, which
are consistent with positive downstream outcomes. Tenure security through Second Level Landholding
Certificates, including joint titling for women and digitization via NRLAIS, complements communal
actions by incentivizing household investments in sustainable land management. Because hydrological and
social externalities cross local boundaries—small community-led structures can alter flow timing,
groundwater recharge, and water availability downstream—Ethiopia’s Community-Based Participatory
Watershed Development guidelines and Environmental and Social Management Framework/Systems
require watershed-level planning, screening for downstream user effects and sensitive habitats, equitable
water allocation mechanisms, site-specific ESMPs, and participatory monitoring, all of which help align
local interventions with broader hydrology and social dynamics. In practice this means deriving community
micro-plans from approved watershed management plans and multi-year development plans with explicit
upstream–downstream analyses; rigorously applying ESMF/ESMS to screen and manage risks; maintaining
inclusive governance and tenure incentives that link maintenance obligations to benefits for youth and
women; monitoring cumulative biophysical outcomes (e.g., NDVI, erosion/sediment models) to adaptively
manage interventions; and strengthening coordination across CALM PforR, RLLP, and PSNP through
watershed coding reconciliation and shared verification so upstream and downstream investments are
coherent and sustainable.
As the case with the ongoing program, CALM 2 is also expected to be environmentally friendly and socially
acceptable. It is to provide benefits to the nation in general and to the beneficiaries of the proposed Program
in particular. The proposed program being a national development agenda in the natural resource
management sector, particularly focusing on watershed development and land administration and use
sectors. It is immensely benefiting the country in terms of soil and water conservation and reducing land
degradation. Given the nature and scale of the proposed program, both positive and negative impacts could
emanate from the participatory watershed management and land administration and use program activities.
The E&S benefits will be derived from participatory watershed management program and provision of
SLLCs.

The proposed CALM 2 program is going to support investments in field-based projects and programs for
promoting and scaling up the ongoing CALM PforR which includes community-based watershed
management and provision of the SLLCs. The implementation of CALM 2 program is anticipated to
enhance the community-based watershed development works in a more organized manner with strong
participation and growing sense of ownership by the communities. The CALM 2 program will further
encourage the establishment and strengthening of WUAs and support the issuance of SLLCs to the
communities in the program implementation areas, both of which aim in enhancing the organizational
capacity and sense of ownership of the communities in watershed development works. The CALM 2
Program will therefore strengthen further the positive E&S impacts gained through the on-going
participatory watershed management projects as well as provision of SLLCs and will contribute towards its
long-term sustainability.

4.1.1 Beneficial Environmental Impacts of CALM 2
In most of the sub-watershed areas where soil and water conservation activities were carried out, the
environment has benefited through improvement in vegetation cover, soil, water and biodiversity
regeneration. Implementation of the CALM 2 program is also expected to ensure the sustainability of these
beneficial E&S impacts through preparation and execution of better watershed development plans with the
participation of WUAs, fostering equity benefit sharing, and enhancing the sense of ownership.
Moreover, as the current trends show, expansion of the watershed management activities done on
communal lands will have dual potential beneficial impacts. In addition to the environmental rehabilitation
and restoration benefits, the transfer of these rehabilitated and reclaimed communal lands for unemployed
youth and women groups has become an important dimension in reducing unemployment in many rural
kebeles. The unemployed youth and women groups are made to continue maintaining and conserving the
communal lands while making benefit from the grasses, beekeeping and perennial fruit trees planted to
stabilize the Soil and Water Conservation (SWC) structures. Therefore, implementation of the CALM 2
program will further enhance additional E&S benefits through the support it provides for expansion of
watershed management works on communal lands.
Largely, the overall environmental impacts of the participatory watershed management activities are
positive. The positive environmental impacts of the participatory watershed management activities include,
but not limited to, the following aspects:
    •   Improvement in vegetation cover of the environment,
    •   Improving carbon sequestration,
    •   Improvement in reducing soil erosion and enhancing soil fertility,
    •   Improvements in reducing surface runoff and flooding,
    •   Increase in surface and ground water recharging capacity,
    •   Increasing ground and surface water availability, and
    •   Improvements in biodiversity regeneration.

Communities appear to have realized that the positive environmental impacts attained by the participatory
watershed management far outweigh the negative impacts, if any. The improvements in soil and its fertility
were reported to be due to the increase in vegetation cover and moisture availability. The vegetation cover
improvement is also associated with reduced soil erosion, increased infiltration and soil moisture which
support vegetation growth. The physical and biological soil and water conservation structures have
contributed to reducing runoff, increasing infiltration and enriching the springs and smaller tributaries that
supply water to rivers. The reduced runoff has also contributed to reduced flooding and siltation.

In the watershed development through community mobilization, afforestation was implemented in three
ways, i) planting seedlings of exotic and indigenous plants on degraded lands, homesteads and farm borders,
ii) closing degraded bare lands for natural rehabilitation from soil seed bank iii) combination of the two. As
a result of these reforestation interventions made on the watersheds, improvements in both plant and animal
biodiversity are reported to be among the positive environmental outcomes. The observations revealed that
it is not only the growth in biomass but also the areas have become green. The changes in vegetation cover
have a positive impact on climate change by lowering the atmospheric temperature.

    i.     Sense of ownership increased
Communal resources in each watershed used to be considered as everyone’s resources. As a result, they

    ii.       Expanded livelihood opportunities, especially for youth and women

    iii.      Enhanced capacity of households for natural resources management
 Watershed development intervention is known to involve people with diverse knowledge, skills and

4.2.1 Potential Environmental Related Adverse Impacts and Risks
 i. Risks of Weak or Absence (for newly added Woredas) of E&S Management Capacities
Experience from the ongoing CALM Program shows that many woreda-level environmental experts still
lack sufficient capacity to manage environmental and social (E&S) risks. Overall, implementing agencies,
such as regional Bureaus of Agriculture and Natural Resources and their zonal and woreda offices
(especially in newly added program woredas), as well as environmental regulators, including REPAs and
woreda environmental protection offices, currently have limited E&S management capabilities. This
situation presents a potential risk for the proposed CALM 2 Program: subprojects may proceed without
adequate safeguard measures, and watershed development plans and other activities with potential adverse
E&S impacts may not be properly screened, reviewed, or approved. To mitigate these risks, continuous
capacity building in E&S management is essential for both implementing agencies and regulatory bodies.
ii. Risks as Part of Watershed Development Plan and Prioritization of Works
Sustainable land management practices are in principle planned and carried to rehabilitate and conserve

careful planning and proper prioritization of the watershed development works. Such gaps in planning
watershed development can cause risks of creating new flood directions and erosion, damage to downstream
Soil and SWC structures due to improper prioritization of works, covering farm fields with receding pond
water or conversely drying up downstream ponds/irrigation waters because of excessive retention of water

treatment and the resulting changes in land use types can also affect the environment through long term

whether between kebeles, woredas, or zones—must be conducted jointly to avoid environmental risks that

5.5.1 Summary of E&S Assessment Procedures under CALM
The Environmental and Social Management System (ESMS) in Ethiopia, as mandated by legislation and
implemented under the CALM program, aims to promote sustainable development through informed
decision-making and enforcement of E&S assessment requirements for development projects. The system
provides clear procedures to minimize, avoid, or offset anticipated impacts and develop mitigation
measures. Overall, Ethiopia’s environmental system aligns with the World Bank’s PforR core principles
and attributes. The ESMS is widely applied across most regional states and Woreda administrations, though
implementation and enforcement vary. Audit and Annual Performance Assessment reports indicate that
participating Woredas generally have functioning ESMS frameworks to ensure subprojects meet E&S
requirements. However, coverage needs to expand to newly participating Woredas, which currently lack
internalized ESMS structures. Operational effectiveness remains uneven, and integration of Environmental
and Social Management Plans (ESMPs) with engineering designs is often inadequate.
E&S Screening Requirements
All subprojects undergo E&S screening to determine whether a full Environmental Impact Assessment
(EIA), preliminary EIA, or no assessment is required. Screening occurs early in project planning and
includes consultations. Additional screening using standardized formats is conducted for annual subproject
plans. The World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) classifies risks as high, substantial,
moderate, or low, while Ethiopia’s National EIA guidelines categorize projects under Schedules I, II, and
III. These systems differ in approach, with the Bank considering factors such as project scale, sensitivity,
institutional capacity, and reputational risks. Enforcement of EIA requirements by regional and local
authorities has been uneven but is improving with awareness. Environmental compliance audits are
increasingly practiced.

Impact Analysis and Cultural Heritage
Projects requiring EIAs include alternative analysis, typically covering site, technology, design, and the “no
project” option, though depth is limited, please also see Annex v for the lists of exclusion. Sites of cultural
significance are avoided, and chance finds during construction trigger immediate reporting and halting of
work. No such incidents have been reported to date. Program activities do not involve land-use changes.
Mitigation, Monitoring and Reporting Requirements
Impact identification and implementation measures focus on direct and indirect effects. However,
cumulative and transboundary impacts are rarely encountered. Preliminary EIAs often recommend generic
mitigation measures, though improvements are noted. Site-specific ESMPs are prepared during watershed
planning, and implementation is the responsibility of project proponents and Woreda E&S focal persons.
However, lack of detailed mitigation plans and inconsistent application remain challenges. Observed gaps
include inadequate safety measures for workers and limited public safety provisions. Monitoring, primarily
conducted by implementing agencies, needs strengthening. While most Woredas conduct annual audits
against ESMPs, systematic processes are inconsistent at the local level. The MoA compiles E&S
performance reports from the respective Woreda and regional agricultural offices and submits them to the
WB team on a quarterly basis.
Overall, while Ethiopia’s ESMS framework and the CALM ESMSG are robust and generally effective,
gaps persist in uniform implementation, integration of ESMPs, detailed mitigation planning, and
monitoring. Strengthening institutional capacity, expanding ESMS coverage to new Woredas, and
improving operational consistency are critical for enhancing environmental and social risk management
under CALM 2.

Core Principle 1: General Principle of E&S Impact Assessment and Management
   Program E&S management procedures and processes are designed to (a) promote E&S sustainability
   in the Program design; (b) avoid, minimize or mitigate adverse impacts; and (c) promote informed
   decision-making relating to a program’s E&S effects.

   Program procedures will:
    ▪ Operate within an adequate legal and regulatory framework to guide E&S impact assessments at
       the program level
    ▪ Incorporate recognized elements of E&S assessment good practice, including (a) early screening

5.5.3 Analysis of the ESSA against the Core Principles
Based on the key findings of the assessment, this section presents an analysis of the consistency of
government’s E&S management systems with the World Bank core principles of PforR financing. System
consistency is analyzed against each core principle by adopting Strength, Weakness, Opportunities, and
Threats/Risks (SWOT) analysis approach.

Core Principle 1: General Principle of E&S Impact Assessment and Management
           a. Applicability:
One of the focus areas of the Program is increasing the total area under restoration in selected watersheds,
which implies expanding the sustainable landscape management practices in the watersheds. The overall
purpose of the landscape development practices is to rehabilitate and conserve the degraded natural
resources with expected positive outcome for the environment. Risks derive from watershed development
activities being not well planned; with unintended and unpredicted adverse impacts on farmlands, natural
habitats, wetlands, physical cultural resources, etc. Current practices in the woredas of all visited regions
show that the process of site selection and prioritization for watershed development follows similar patterns
as stipulated in the Community Based Participatory Watershed Development Guideline issued by the MoA
(2005). During the selection and prioritization of the watersheds criteria, like existing severity of land
degradation and potential beneficial use of the watershed after treatment, are considered. The appropriate
conservation technologies required for treatment of the prioritized watersheds are also determined by NRM
professionals in the woreda. Based on the ESSA field assessment and ongoing monitoring of CALM, it was
observed that generally the prioritized micro-watersheds conservation activities are selected based on the
level of high degradation of natural resources. This implies that communities and local authorities are
accumulating experiences from their practices in planning, selection, and prioritizing the treatment works
of watersheds involved. However, there is still need to confirm that these experiences are carried forward
and strengthened further during the CALM 2 program by ensuring that comprehensive watershed
management plans that also adress potential E&S risks/impacts which may occur during the watershed
development works are prepared and implemented. This will require Watershed Development Plans to be
screened, reviewed and cleared (approved) by the competent Environment protection authorities prior to its
implementation. Thus, Core principle-1 on E&S Impact Assessment and Management will be required to
ensure that watershed development activities are well planned and supported and guided by adequate
watershed development plans that adress E&S impact considerations.

In addition, main issues from the perspective of potential E&S risks/impacts could be related to lack of
controlling free grazing, difficulty to enforce bylaws, limited support for vulnerable groups in watershed
development and SLLCs-women, elderly and unemployed and landless youths and inequitable and delayed
benefits from watershed, inadequate participation in participatory watershed management, limited
empowerment of women in the watershed development, risk of female household heads and disabled
persons losing their land that they have leased to sharecroppers as well as workers and community safety
risks and in some cases forced labor and fatigue towards participatory watershed management. Besides,
participatory consultation, planning, management and monitoring, resource use and management were not
well established. Moreover, the CALM 2 program component on improving the robustness and quality of
service delivery in land administration is to expand the coverage of second level land certification. The
establishment of National Rural Land Administration Information System (NRLAIS) and its connection
with the regional land use administration offices is likely to require additional IT facilities. The main issue
from the perspective of potential E&S risks/impacts could be related to the disposal of used IT facilities
(e.g., computers, laptops, printers, etc.), surveying equipment (e.g., GPS), lack of enforcing land use plan,
political instability and conflicts (due to replacement and wrong spell of names, incorrect land measurement
and sometimes problem of locating the right landholding). The potential E&S risks of program activities
related to these aspects will need to be adressed and hence applies Core Principle-1.

            a) Strengths
The Country has adopted the key principles of E&S management and sustainable development at different
levels of its main legislations and policies including in the FDRE Constitution and Environment Policy of
Ethiopia (EPE). The EPE (1997) is aimed at guiding sustainable social and economic development of the
country through the conservation and sustainable utilization of the natural, man-made and cultural resources
and the environment at large.
The Country has replaced the previous EIA Proclamation No 299/2002 with the newly enacted ESIA
Proclamation No. 1371/2025, since April 2025 which defines the fundamental requirements and procedures
for E&S impact assessment. The ESIA proclamation provides the legal and regulatory framework that
guides E&S impact assessments applicable at National and Regional levels. This new proclamation
introduces several enhancements and is more closely aligned with the World Bank’s Environmental and
Social Framework (ESF) procedures and requirements. Among the key improvements introduced under the
ESIA Proclamation (Proclamation No. 1371/2025) are the following:
    • The impact of a project shall be assessed on the basis of the size, location, nature, cumulative effect
        with other concurrent impacts or phenomena, trans-regional effect, duration, reversibility or
        irreversibility or other related effects of the project, whereby this is more aligned with the principle
        of proportionality under the WB’s ESF,
    • Any government organization that initiates public instrument (any government policy, law, strategy
        and program) shall have the obligation to conduct Strategic Environmental and Social Assessment
        (SESA) on the public instruments which are likely to cause significant E&S impacts,
    • The Authority or the relevant Regional Environmental Agency shall make any E&S impact study
        report accessible to the public and solicit comments on it. The Authority or the Regional
        Environmental Agency shall disseminate the ESIA report through public notice and accept
        comments for 15 working days,
    • The implementation of the Federal ESIA proclamation EPA is also supported by EIA procedural
        guideline (2003) and ESMP preparation guideline (2004) (until the required ESIA guidelines will
        be prepared and issued) which provides the essential procedural steps for carrying out ESIA and
        the required minimum contents of an ESMP. The EIA procedural guideline requires that prior to
        the commencement of ESIA studies, pre-screening consultations, screening and scoping exercises
        need to be done in order to Categorize the proposed project into one of the three schedules of
        activities (Schedule I, II or III). The EIA procedural guideline has consisted in its annexes the list
        of project types that are categorized under the schedules I to III. On the other side, the ESMP
        preparation guideline requires ESMPs to consist of summary of identified impacts, proposed
        mitigation and enhancement measures, institutional arrangements for implementation of the
        mitigation and monitoring measures, and preliminary cost estimates to ensure mitigation and
        monitoring measures are adequately funded for implementation. These guidelines are widely
        applied not only by the EPA at Federal level, but by all regional EPAs.
The Country has environmental protection institutions at Federal, Regional and Woreda levels mandated to
enforce the ESIA Proclamation and other environmental management related policies and legislations. EPA
is the Federal environment institution responsible to enforce the ESIA law at National level. There are also
Regional Environment institutions in the proposed CALM 2 Program target regions responsible for
enforcing the ESIA system. The Regional Environment Protection Agencies have organizational structures
that go down to the Zone and Woreda levels. Whereas the Federal EPA is responsible for enforcing the
ESIA law on development projects licensed by the Federal Government and on those that will have trans-
regional impacts, the regional environment protection bureaus are generally responsible for enforcing the
ESIA law on development projects licensed by their respective regional authorities. Within the regional
environment protection authorities, though some variation exists between regions, the woreda level offices
are usually mainly responsible for carrying out environmental performance monitoring of approved project
ESMPs and for review and approval of some moderate to low-risk subproject activities.

Core Principle 2: Environmental Considerations – Natural Habitats and Physical Cultural Resources
       Applicability
The applicability of Core principle 2 is generally related to the reasons given for applicability of Core
principle 1 in the preceding sections. Most often, watershed sites are situated deep in rural areas and away
from the rural villages where farm fields, open communal lands, and other territories that may adjoin natural
habitats, wetlands, places of unknown physical cultural resources could be present. The current practices
of site selection for watershed development largely focus on prioritizing degraded lands without due
attention given to other aspects like conservation of physical cultural resources in the watersheds. The
potential environmental risks of program activities related to these aspects will need to be adressed and
hence applies Core Principle-2.

The ESIA Proclamation (2002) and the EIA procedural guideline (2003) are consistent with Core Principle

undertake an environmental impact assessment to identify the likely adverse impacts of projects and
incorporate the means of their prevention or containment and submit the environmental impact study report
to the Authority (i.e., EPA) or the relevant regional environmental agency. Moreover, the kind of adverse
impacts a project proponent is required to assess include any change to the environment or to its component
that may affect flora, fauna, natural or cultural heritage, or in general, subsequently alter environmental,
social, economic or cultural conditions. Thus, the Federal proclamation on ESIA has provisions by which
it considers the issues of conserving natural habitats and physical cultural resources which are the main

Core Principle 3: Environmental Considerations – Public and Worker Safety
       Applicability
Core Principle 3 is applicable to the CALM Program in the sense that individual worker safety issues are
of concern during participatory watershed management activities. Depending on the nature of the watershed
sites communities that may be engaged on activities such as digging with hand tools, carrying materials,
and working on sloppy grounds can expose them to safety hazards. There is a potential for occurrence of
injuries and accidents (non-fatal and/or fatal) while at work during the participatory watershed management
activities. Temporary facilities like tents and first aid kits are also supposed to be availed voluntarily by the
local kebele authorities to provide temporary shelter for babies whose moms are working in the watershed.
However, during the field assessment in all the regions, no such first aid kits and tents were seen availed
during the work at the watersheds. Besides, participatory watershed management has created a sort of forced
labor and double burden on women in which they carry out participatory watershed management activities

Core Principle 5: Social Considerations – Indigenous Peoples and Vulnerable Groups
       Applicability
Core Principle 5 emphasizes the importance of ensuring that program benefits are culturally appropriate
and equitably accessible, with special attention to the rights and interests of underserved groups and the
needs of vulnerable groups. In the context of CALMII ESSA, this principle applies where underserved
groups and Vulnerable groups are present within the program area. Free, prior, and informed consultations
are required if underserved groups are potentially affected, to determine whether there is broad community
support and to ensure their participation in benefiting from customary resources or indigenous knowledge—
with their consent.
In Ethiopia, there are people who are vulnerable and underserved that need special attention. In the 1997
Cultural Policy of Ethiopia and the 2014 Social Protection Policy, vulnerable groups are identified as
children, women, older people, people with disabilities, and the chronically ill people.
Within CALMII program, particularly in watershed development and SLLCs, there are vulnerable sections
of the community that need attention. For example, SLLCs offer land rights to vulnerable groups such as
elderly and female-headed households, enabling them to rent out unused land for better returns, while also
creating access opportunities for landless youth. Although the SLLC manual has been revised to better
address tenure rights for women and vulnerable groups, implementation at the local level in progress.
Gender equity and social inclusion require further work, starting with improved monitoring of intervention
impacts on vulnerable women and adaptive mitigation measures that support their long-term benefits,
including participation in Watersheds Users Cooperatives. Participatory watershed management can
impose labour burdens, particularly on women, without offering the option to opt out While, even though
stakeholders during ESSA consultations could not report the presence of Gender Based Violence (GVB),
the DHS (2016) study in the three regions consulted for this assessment revealed the existence of
GBV/SEA/SH and underscoring the need for proactive measures . CALM ESSA inputs highlight the need
for a clear definition of vulnerability, regular assessment of welfare program impacts, and protocols to
address GBV and other social risks. Legal and institutional frameworkssuch as equal opportunity policies
and representation mechanismsare critical for ensuring inclusion in planning, implementation, and
monitoring. However, gaps in meaningful consultation, targeting, and inclusion persist, which can hinder
access to culturally appropriate benefits. Addressing these gaps through strengthened stakeholder
engagement, improved grievance mechanisms, and targeted capacity-building is essential for the effective
application of Core Principle 5 in CALM ESSA.

Core Principle 6: Social Considerations – Social Conflict
   Applicability
The program delivers substantial social benefits to local community members, particularly those residing
within watershed areas. It promotes equitable for vulnerable groups through participatory watershed
management, enhancing their access to resources and strengthening the social cohesion of the local
community unit during participatory watershed management. Collective engagement can also advance a
sense of ownership towards watershed resources, support the rehabilitation of degraded landscapes, and
improve security tenure via SLLCs. Despite these positive outcomes, there is potential for complaints and
conflict among local communities participating in watershed management and receiving SLLCs. Issues
may arise around basic resource sharing and the adequacy of support for vulnerable groups. In rural
Ethiopia, where livelihoods are closely linked to land, land-related conflicts including disputes over use
rights, boundaries, and errors in land registration are prevalent and can undermine productive activities.
Additional sources of conflict include inequitable benefit sharing, difficulties in enforcing bylaws, and
damage to soil and water conservation structures.

Moreover, social tensions in certain regionssuch as Oromia, Tigray, and Amhara significantly affect the
program’s capacity to deliver services. Political instability in these areas may restrict the movement of
experts and impede implementation, especially for activities related to land. While these disturbances are
not directly attributable to the program, they nonetheless impact its execution. Therefore, Core Principle 6
is considered applicable.

            a. Strengths
 • The National proclamations are applicable in all regional states of Ethiopia. The existence of the legal
   frameworks is an opportunity that can serve as a basis to address complaints and conflicts related to
   program implementation.
 • In connection with conflict resolution and grievance redress mechanisms, if misunderstandings and
   disputes arise (e.g. implementing agencies and affected parties) during program implementation, there
   is established GRM system at the community level to resolve complaints as well as the traditional
   grievance redress mechanism at the community level. If the complainant is dissatisfied with the
   decision of the arbitration committee, the case may be referred to woreda and to the High Court. In
   most regions, household participation in watershed development activities is voluntary, and
   communities have been well consulted and informed throughout the process.
 • The program’s participatory approach to watershed management has fostered social cohesion within
   local communities and strengthened women’s rights to landholding. This inclusive model promotes a
   sense of ownership and helps mitigate social tensions by involving diverse stakeholders in decision-
   making.
 • The program has also generated off-farm and non-farm livelihood opportunities particularly for youth
   and women such as apiculture and livestock fattening. These improvements help reduce economic
   grievances that often contribute to social conflict.

   benefit-sharing and the inclusion of vulnerable groups, aiming to prevent exclusion and reduce the risk
   of social conflict stemming from perceived or actual inequities
 • The program recognizes the importance of capacity building, including hiring environmental and
   social development experts and strengthening Environmental and Social Management Systems
   (ESMS) to support effective implementation, monitoring, and reporting. These efforts are intended to
   address social risks, including conflict.

6     Recommendations and Actions
6.1     Institutional Capacity Related Risks
The MoA has gained substantial experience in implementing World Bank-financed projects and programs,
including both IPF and PforR modalities. Through the ongoing CALM program, the MoA and several
Program Woredas have strengthened their institutional capacity, notably through the establishment and
operation of the ESMS. However, the current assessment has identified several key areas that require further
improvement: (i) Although the NRM and RLAU Directorates within the MoA have each assigned one E&S
development specialist, and Gender mainstreaming focal person to the CALM program, these E&S staff
members are reportedly overburdened due to additional responsibilities. While the BoANR has appointed
an E&S specialist with environmental/natural resources background for watershed management, the
BoLAU does not have a social development specialist to manage social related issues including the GRM
of the Program. Moreover, many E&S focal persons designated by the program Woredas lack the necessary
qualifications and experience, and are also overloaded with extra assignments, (ii) The assessment further
reveals limited capacity, particularly at the regional and Woreda levels, exacerbated by frequent staff
turnover. These have resulted in the preparation of lower quality E&S risk instruments, challenges in
addressing recommended actions from E&S audit reports, insufficient attention to OHS-related risks and
grievance redress mechanisms, a tendency to underestimate E&S risks; coupled with weak accountability
and enforcement mechanisms, misconceptions and low prioritization of E&S risk management, budget
constraints, and inadequate follow-up, reporting, and documentation. Most of these issues are primarily
attributed to capacity constraints at the Woreda level.
6.2     Potential Adverse E&S Risks and Impacts
Similar to the ongoing CALM program, the anticipated E&S risks associated with CALM 2 are expected
to be site-specific and limited in scale. Potential adverse E&S impacts may arise from activities such as
sustainable landscape management practices, now encompasses reclaimed natural forest areas (including
afforestation and reafforestation), cooperatives supported to participate in existing and emerging agri-value
chains, (including coffee, soybean/feeds, malt barley, horticulture, and cotton); and smallholder
participation in payments for ecosystem services (PES) and emerging carbon markets; participatory and
inclusive planning and implementation of watershed development interventions; provision of Second Level
Landholding Certificates (SLLCs) to rural landholders; and the operation of the NRLAIS for the registration
and management of SLLCs. These adverse impacts are primarily linked to land-related issues during
watershed development and the issuance of SLLCs. Risks have been identified by assessing existing and
potential vulnerabilities within the E&S context, the program’s strategy and sustainability, as well as
institutional complexity and capacity. Mitigation measures include building the capacity of implementing
partners to promote inclusion and participation and strengthening accountability and grievance redress
mechanisms. Overall, the anticipated E&S impacts are expected to be minimal and not large-scale, though
they may affect individuals through loss of assets such as structures, crops, trees, or portions of their land.
Additional risks include community and occupational health and safety (OHS) concerns, and the
management of physical cultural resources (PCR) as part of watershed management and livelihood support
subprojects. Most adverse effects are expected to be reversible, with no impact leading to irreversible or
permanent negative change. For a detailed discussion of E&S risks and impacts, please refer to section 2.2
above.

Relevant Policy, Legal and Institutional Framework of Ethiopia’s E&S Management Systems
This section discusses relevant national and regional environmental Policies, strategies and legislations
applicable to CALM 2 program that needs to be considered during program implementation phases. The
discussion below provides a list of the key relevant environment legislations and key institutions that are in
charge of the implementation. The relevance of these requirements to CRLM 2 is assessed with due
consideration of the requirements and guidelines of PforR financing.

Applicable Policies and Strategies forming the National E&S Management System
a. The Constitution
1. The constitution of the Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia had been issued in August 1995 with
several provisions, which provides basic and comprehensive principles and guidelines for environmental
protection and management in the country. The concept of sustainable development and environmental
rights are presented in Articles 43, 44 and 92 of the Constitution.
Article 43- The Right to Development
    •   The Peoples of Ethiopia as a whole, and each Nation, Nationality and People in Ethiopia in
        particular have the right to improved living standards and to sustainable development.
    • Nationals have the right to participate in national development and, in particular, to be consulted
        with respect to policies and projects affecting their community.
Article 44- Environmental Rights
    •   All persons have the right to a clean and healthy environment.
    •   All persons who have been displaced or whose livelihoods have been adversely affected as a result
        of State programs have the right to commensurate monetary or alternative means of compensation,
        including relocation with adequate State assistance.
Article 92- Environmental Objectives
    •   Government shall endeavor to ensure that all Ethiopians live in a clean and healthy environment.
    •   The design and implementation of programs and projects of development shall not damage or
        destroy the environment.
    • People have the right to full consultation and to the expression of views in the planning and
        implementations of environmental policies and projects that affect them directly.
    • Government and citizens shall have the duty to protect the environment.
Article 40: Land and Natural Resource
In relation to land and natural resources, the Constitution under Article 40 proclaims that land and natural
resources are commonly owned by the people of Ethiopia and shall not be subject to sale or other means of
exchange. It stipulates the rights of Ethiopian farmers and pastoralists to obtain land for cultivation and for
free grazing without payment and the protection against eviction from their possession.
Article 42: Rights of Labor
Article 42(2) stipulates that ‘workers have the right to a healthy and safe work environment’, obliging an
employer (be it government or private) to take all necessary measures to ensure that workplace is safe,
healthy and free of any danger to the wellbeing of workers.

Article 41: Economic, Social and Cultural Rights
Article 41 of the Constitution states that every Ethiopian has the right to access publicly funded social
services. Sub Article 5 of the same article stipulates, the state, within available means, should allocate
resource to provide rehabilitation and assistance to physically and mentally disabled, the aged and to
children who are left without parents or guardians.
Regional states constitution: Regional states have their own constitution upholding the federal
constitution in its entirety and constituting their regional particulars. All the regional state constitutions
have addressed land and natural resources management and environmental protection. The regional states
constitutions state that:
    •   The regional governments are entrusted to administer land and natural resources in the name of the
        people and deploy for the common benefit of the same;
    •   The regional governments and all citizens of the regions are responsible for the conservation of
        natural resources and the environment;
    •   Concerned communities shall be given opportunity to express their opinions in the formulation and
        implementation of policies in relation to the environment

b. Environment Policy of Ethiopia
The first comprehensive statement of Environmental Policy of Ethiopia was approved by the Council of
Ministers in April 1997 that was based on the policy and strategic findings and recommendations of the
Conservation Strategy of Ethiopia. The policy is aimed at guiding sustainable social and economic
development of the country through the conservation and sustainable utilization of the natural, man-made
and cultural resources and the environment at large. The overall policy goal is to improve and enhance the
health and quality of life of all Ethiopians and to promote sustainable social and economic development
through the sound management and use of natural, human-made and cultural resources and the environment
as a whole so as to meet the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future
generations to meet their own needs. The Environmental Policy provides a number of guiding principles
that require adherence to the general principles of sustainable development. In particular, the need to ensure
that Environmental Impact Assessment:

    •   Considers impacts on human and natural environments
    •   Provides for early consideration of environmental impacts in project and program design
    •   Recognizes public consultation processes as essential to effective management
    •   Includes mitigation and contingency plans
    •   Provides for auditing and monitoring
    •   Is a legally binding requirement

The Government of Ethiopia has recently initiated to update the Environmental policy of Ethiopia. The
technical committee under the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change was formalized to be
in charge of updating the National environmental policy to fulfil the gaps identified in addressing climate
change and other environmental issues.

c. Climate Resilient Green Economy
The Climate Resilient Green Economy (CRGE, 2011) is Ethiopia's overarching framework and a national
strategy towards a green economy. The Green Economy Strategy is believed to provide an opportunity to
promote sustainable development in Ethiopia. Currently, it builds on an investment plan of over 60
initiatives that are, or can be, turned into financed projects. For this to happen, there is a strong need to
reform the economy. The CRGE is envisioned to be the main driver for this transformation. The CRGE has

three complementary objectives: i) fostering economic development and growth, ii) ensuring abatement
and avoidance of future GHG emissions; and iii) improving resilience to climate change. To achieve these
objectives, CRGE sets out to tap into international climate finance, seize opportunities for innovation and
new technologies, and create competitive advantages via sustainable resource use and improving
productivity.
d. The Agriculture Sector Policy and Strategy
The policy objectives are:
    •   To substantially enhance the production and productivity of agricultural sector for improvement of

e. Food Security Strategy
This strategy addresses both the supply and the demand side of the food equation - that is, availability and
entitlement respectively from both a national and household level perspective. Within this, particular
attention is focused on the diversity of food production zones in Ethiopia (i.e. areas with adequate moisture,
moisture deficit and pastoral) to tailor options and strategies accordingly.
The three basic pillars on which the strategy rests are:
            To increase the availability of food through increased domestic production.
            To ensure access to food for food deficit households; and
            To strengthen emergency response capabilities.

f. FDRE National Occupational Safety and Health Policy and Strategy
The National Policy and strategy on Occupational Safety and Health (OSH) was endorsed by the FDRE
Council of Ministers in July 2014. The OSH policy and strategy was prepared to implement the rights of
Labour as stipulated in article 42(2) of the Constitution and also implement the requirements of
International Conventions on Occupational Health and Safety (No.155) to which Ethiopia is a signatory.
The overall objective of the national OSH Policy and strategy is to avoid, prevent or minimize occupational
and health hazards by providing effective OSH services in all working places and thereby contribute to the
socioeconomic development of the Country.

The guiding principles of the National OSH policy and strategy are stated as the following:
    a.   Occupational Safety and Health Services are basic rights of workers
    b.   Occupational Safety and Health Services are necessary in all working places
    c.   Occupational accidents and health hazards can be prevented
    d.   Tripartite and bipartite cooperation and coordination are key instruments for the national OSH
         policy and strategy implementation.

Proclamations, Regulations & Guidelines forming the National Environmental Management System
a. Environmental and Social Impact Assessment Proclamation (Proclamation 1371/2025)
The Country has replaced the previous EIA Proclamation No 299/2002 with the newly enacted ESIA
Proclamation No. 1371/2025, since April 2025, which defines the fundamental requirements and
procedures for E&S impact assessment. The ESIA proclamation provides the legal and regulatory
framework that guides E&S impact assessments applicable at National and Regional levels. The New
Proclamation reflects the country's evolving development priorities, aligns with international best practices,
and integrates broader social considerations into development planning. The New Proclamation
significantly broadens the assessment scope beyond environmental impacts to include social, economic,
and cultural factors. Unlike the Previous Proclamation, which narrowly defined environmental impact
assessments, the New Proclamation adopts an integrated approach. This shift emphasizes the importance
of holistic development, recognizing that environmental sustainability must be considered alongside social
and economic dimensions in project planning and approval. Overall, this new proclamation introduces
several enhancements and is more closely aligned with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social
Framework (ESF) procedures and requirements. Among the key improvements introduced under the ESIA
Proclamation (Proclamation No. 1371/2025) are the following:
 •    The impact of a project shall be assessed on the basis of the size, location, nature, cumulative effect
      with other concurrent impacts or phenomena, trans-regional effect, duration, reversibility or
      irreversibility or other related effects of the project, whereby this is more aligned with the principle
      of proportionality under the WB’s ESF,
 •    Any government organization that initiates public instrument (any government policy, law, strategy
      and program) shall have the obligation to conduct Strategic Environmental and Social Assessment
      (SESA) on the public instruments which are likely to cause significant E&S impacts,

             Draft Environmental and Social Systems Assessment for CALM 2 Program: Ethiopia

 •   The Authority or the relevant Regional Environmental Agency shall make any E&S impact study
     report accessible to the public and solicit comments on it. The Authority or the Regional
     Environmental Agency shall disseminate the ESIA report through public notice and accept comments
     for 15 working days,
 •   The Authority or the relevant Regional Environmental Protection Agency may, to the extent that its
     capacity allows, support the implementation of a project destined to provide any environmental
     rehabilitation or pollution prevention or clean up or green development and technology,
 •   The Authority or the relevant Regional Environmental Agency shall monitor the implementation of
     an authorized project in order to evaluate compliance with all commitments made by, and obligations
     imposed on the proponent during authorization,
 •   An E&S audit made by a proponent shall be carried out by a certified consultant. The Authority or
     the Regional Environmental Agency shall also audit the implementation of the project at least once
     in two years. The Authority, if it is conducting the audit by itself, shall engage competent regional
     authority where the project is to be implemented,
 •   Any person dissatisfied with the authorization or monitoring or any decision of the Authority or the
     relevant Regional Environmental Agency regarding the project may submit a grievance notice to the
     head of the Authority or the relevant Regional Environmental Agency, as may be appropriate. The
     decision of the Head of the Authority or relevant Regional Environmental Agency shall be issued
     within 30 days following the receipt of the grievance,
 •   Any proponent or public instrument initiator who implements a project or public instrument without

     from it, may in addition to any penalty it may impose, order the convicted person to restore or in any
     other way compensate for the damage inflicted.
Environmental and Social Impact Assessment Procedural Guidelines Series (Series 1 and 2)
In order to facilitate the implementation of Environmental Impact Assessment Proclamation (Proclamation
299/2002), the then MoEFCC (EPA) had formulated four procedural guidelines, namely, Review Guideline
Series 1: Guidelines for Review Approach; Review Guideline Series 2- Guidelines for Contents and Scopes
of Report; Review Guideline Series 3- Checklist of Environmental Characteristics and Review Guideline
Series 4- Review Criteria. These widely applied draft environmental impact assessment guidelines were
under review to enhance the documents in light of the experiences gained so far and to publish it for official
use after endorsement by the Ministry. The review process is still ongoing and yet to be completed during
the current 2018/2019 fiscal year. Review Guideline Series 1 and 2 will be elaborated to a certain extent
here and any further updates made to the documents will apply after official publication of the reviewed
guidelines.
A) Procedural Guideline Series 1 -Guidelines for Review Approach
This guideline pointed out roles and responsibilities of the former MoEFCC (now called EPA) and Regional
Environmental Agencies, the proponent, consulting firm, interested and affected parties, and the licensing
agency. In the guideline, the EIA processes and requirements, and comprehensive description of the EA
process has been stated. It also outlined projects which may have adverse and significant environmental
impacts, and may, therefore, require full EIA (Schedule 1), projects whose type, scale or other relevant
characteristics have the potential to cause some significant environmental impacts but not likely to warrant
an environmental impact study (Schedule 2) and projects which would have no impact and does not require
environmental impact assessment (Schedule 3).
B) Procedural Guideline Series 2 - Guidelines for Contents and Scopes of Report



                                                     76
             Draft Environmental and Social Systems Assessment for CALM 2 Program: Ethiopia

This guideline among others indicates structure and content of the Environmental Impact Study Report and
describes the contents including the administrative, legal and policy requirements, assessment and
mitigation measures. The guideline indicates the following main types of mitigating measures, which need
due considerations:
    •   Preventing, reducing or minimizing impacts before they occur;
    •   Eliminating an actual impact over time by incorporating appropriate maintenance measures during
        the life of the project;
    • Rectifying an impact by repairing, rehabilitating or restoring the affected environment;
    • Compensating for an impact by replacing or providing substitute resources or environments as well
        as contingency plans in case of emergencies;
    • Maximizing beneficial impacts through specific additional actions
c) Directive No.2/2014 (2006 EC): Directive on issuing “professional competence certificate to consultants
and firms providing service in Environmental Impact Assessment, Environmental Audit and Climate
Change fields”
The Directive has been issued by the MoEFCC (now called EPA) and has been in force for the last four
years. It has become an important milestone in the development of the EIA system in Ethiopia. The directive
stipulates that EIA and Environment Audits should be conducted by professional consultants and firms that
are registered and certified for their competence by the Federal Environment, Forest, and Climate Change
Commission. EIAs and Environment Audits prepared by unregistered and certified firms will not be eligible
for review and approval. The Regional EPFCCs have also started applying the stated directive of MoEFCC.
Directive no.2/2014 is also among the guidelines put under review by the MOEFCC and is being updated.

of an Environmental Management Plans (EMP) for proposed developments in Ethiopia and institutional

Vii. Proclamations on public and workers safety- The Labor Law
Ethiopia has issued proclamations in the effort to improve employment relations and outcomes, protect
child labor exploitation, and maintain proper occupational health and safety. The transitional government
of Ethiopia has issued Labor Proclamation No. 42/1993. This proclamation was amended and replaced with
Labor Proclamation No. 377/2003. The Labor Proclamations have had detailed provisions pertaining to
workers’ suspension and protects their rights. Besides, there are other labor related proclamations such as
the provisions of the Employment Exchange Service Proclamation (Proclamation No. 632/2009) and the
Right to Employment of Persons with Disability (Proclamation No. 568/2008) enacted to govern the
relations between employers and employees.

The Labor Law protects Children against Child Labor abuse. Under the provisions of the Revised Family
Code (2000), a child or minor is defined as “a person of either sex who has not attained the full age of
eighteen years”. Proclamation No. 377/2003, Article 89 prohibited employment of less than 14 years. The
proclamations states “It is prohibited to employ persons under 14 years of age”. It is also prohibited to
employ young workers which on account of its nature or due to the condition in which it is carried out,
endangers the life or health of the young workers performing it. "Young worker" means a person who has
attained the age of 14 but is not over the age of 18 years (Article 89 Sub-Article 3).
Proclamation 377/2003 is also the prevailing law protecting public and workers safety. The proclamation
covers health and safety at work, harmonious industrial relation and minimum workplace standard and
addresses workplace vulnerability. Article 92-93 of the proclamation defines obligation of employers and
employees in work place including assignment of safety officers and health committee.

The Labor Proclamation mandates employers to protect occupational safety, health and create better
working environment for their workers. Article 92 states that “An employer shall take the necessary
measure to safeguard adequately the health and safety of the workers…” The proclamations have details
about the safety and health of workers. For instance, it forces employers to i) take appropriate steps to
ensure that workers are properly instructed and notified concerning the hazards of their respective

1.3 National Policies, Regulations, and Guidelines for Social Impact Assessment and Management
a. The National Social Protection Strategy of Ethiopia
Ethiopia has formulated National Social Protection policy in 2012 with a general objective to create an
enabling environment in which citizens (including special need and other vulnerable segments) have
equitable access to all social protection services that will enhance their growth, development. Ethiopia’s
social protection policy is a central public policy component for addressing poverty, vulnerability and
inequality. The following are among the objectives of Social Protection Policy of Ethiopia:
    2. Protect poor and vulnerable individuals, households, and communities from the adverse effects of
        shocks and destitution;
    3. Increase access to equitable and quality health, education and social welfare services to build
        human capital thus breaking the intergenerational transmission of poverty;
    4. Guarantee a minimum level of employment for the long term unemployed and under-employed;
    5. Enhance the social status and progressively realize the social and economic rights of the excluded
        and marginalized;
The strategy has designed instruments to reach long and short term objectives including conditional and
unconditional social transfer, expansion of public works; providing technical support and financial services;
mandatory social insurance and community based health insurance; establishment of social work system,
services for PWDs, the elderly and mobility constrained persons; enhancing abuse and exploitation
prevention communication, provide protective legal and policy environment, support for survivors of abuse
and exploitation and drop in centers and hot lines.
b. The National Policy on Ethiopian Women (1993)
It underlines the need to establish equitable and gender sensitive public policies that empower woman,
especially in education and property rights, and engaging them in decision making. Improving healthy
working conditions, ensuring access to basic services, protecting woman from harmful traditional practices
are among the emphasized key issues.
c. Gender mainstreaming strategy and guideline (2010)
This strategy was adopted at policy, program and project level by government and development parteners
to ensure the out comes of development to be shared equally between men and women; both men and
women enjoy equal opportunities, status and recognition.
The ratification of the Family Law and amendements made to the criminal code significantly support to
fight abuses committted against woman and children. Proclamation No, 377/2003 gives special attention
to woman and young workers. The proclamation provides protection for woman in general and pregenant
woman in particular from hard work and long hours. The law clearly states that women should not be
discriminated against as regards to employment and payment on bases of her sex. Gender norms in
Ethiopia vary widely depending on geographic location, ethnicity, and religion, especially related to
property ownership, inheritance, and the division of assets after divorce. However, the new Family Code

                                                     80
             Draft Environmental and Social Systems Assessment for CALM 2 Program: Ethiopia

has changed all that. Passed in 2000, it gives equal rights to women in marriage and it requires all assets be
divided equally among both partners in the case of a divorce. By now, all the states in Ethiopia have
approved this new Code. Ethiopia is one of many developing countries implementing gender policy
reforms, especially regarding women’s equal access to assets and resources.
d. The Development and Change Package (2007)
It envisions to build democratic society where women are equal participants and beneficiaries of economic,
social and political life of the country. Widespread awareness creation of women to actively participate in
the development process; organizing and associate women to address challenges they face; capacitate
women to solve problems and fight demeaning perceptions & fight for their rights; facilitate linkages and
support among created associations and organization; and enable women to benefit economically and
socially.
e. The Federal rural land administration proclamation No.89/1997
Federal rural land administration proclamation stated that women, orphans and disabled persons can use
hired labor on their land or use other options without compromising their rights. In addition, the legislation
has also provided orphans inherited land-use rights through their legal guardians.
Article 6 of part two of the Proclamation (right to hold land) states that: “The working system that gives
priority to women, disables and orphan children shall be executed during the time of land distribution”. It
is also reiterated under article 9 on procedures for land provision that: “where the land to be distributed is
not available to all petitioners with equal magnitude of land holding problem it shall priory be caused to be
given to orphan children, disables, women and youngsters who join the new life of independence,
consecutively”.
Article 5 on access to and use of grazing land states that: “Women pastoralists have equal rights with men
to access and use grazing lands” and Article 9 on access to rural lands and use right “ Women who intend
to be engaged in raising animals, crop farming; and natural resource development activities have the right
to access to and use farm and grazing lands free of charge and (ii) Rural women whose husbands are away
from the locality and are in other places to render government services; or to do other work shall not lose
their right of use over the joint/communal holding”.
f. National Rural Land Administration and Use (Proclamation No. 456/2005)
The Rural Land Administration and Use Proclamation (Proclamation No. 456/2005) defines the state
ownership of rural land and the tenure rights of the land occupant, including rights to "property produced
on his land", rights of inter-generational tenure transfer, and rights of exchange land and limited leasing
rights. Provisions are made for the registration and certification of tenure rights. Part three of the
proclamation presents regulations relating to the use of rural land, particularly as it relates to soil and water
conservation and watershed management. The rural land administration and land use laws are to be
implemented by the regional states.
Land holding right gives the right to use the land for agricultural purposes as well as to lease it and, while
the right remains in effect, bequeath it to family members, as well as the right to acquire property thereon,
by labor or capital, and to sell, exchange and bequeath the same. The proclamation also addresses
environmental concerns, including non-compliance with directives on environmental protection. An
important feature of this proclamation is that it stipulates rural land use and restrictions based on proper
land use planning, providing for the proper use of various types of land, such as slopes, gullies and wetlands,
as well as the utilization of rural land for villages and social services. In addition, it is envisaged that the
proclamation will create a sense of ownership among the vast majority of the rural population and enable
them to take initiatives and collectively engage in environmental management activities.

1.4 Applicable International Conventions Endorsed by Ethiopia
Ethiopia has ratified several international/multilateral environmental conventions and many of the
principles and provisions in those conventions have been well addressed in the national environmental
policies and regulations. Accordingly, Article 9(4) of the constitution of the Federal Democratic Republic
of Ethiopia provides that once an international agreement is ratified through the accepted or established
procedure, it automatically becomes an integral part of the law of the land. Consequently, the convention
and the protocol are the laws of this land. Therefore, the following international conventions and protocols
are relevant to the proposed P for R operation;
UN Framework Convention on Climate Change: It provides a framework for international cooperation
to combat climate change by limiting average global temperature increases and the resulting climate change
and coping with its impacts. The objective of this convention is to stabilize greenhouse gas concentrations
in the atmosphere at a level that will prevent dangerous interference with the climate system. Ethiopia
ratified this convention through proclamation No. 97/1994 on May 2/1994. This convention takes into
account the fact that climate change has trans-boundary impacts.
The United Nations Conventions to Combat Desertification: The objective of the convention is to
combat desertification and mitigate the effects of droughts in countries experiencing serious drought and
desertification, particularly in Africa. Ethiopia has ratified the convention through its proclamation No.
80/1997.
Convention on Biological Diversity: The convention on biological diversity has three goals. These are:
     • Conservation of biodiversity;
     • Sustainable use of the components of biodiversity; and
     • Fair and equitable sharing of the benefits arising from the use of genetic resources.
Cartagena Protocol on Bio-Safety to the Convention on Biological Diversity: It aims to ensure the safe
handling; transport and use of living modified organisms (LMOs) are resulting from modern biotechnology
that may have adverse effects on biological diversity, taking also into account risks to human health.
Convention for the Protection of the World Cultural and Natural Heritage Paris, 23 November 1972
Kyoto Protocol to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change: Legally binds
developed country Parties to emission reduction targets.

Annex III: Grievances Redress Mechanism (GRM)
Under the ongoing Program, the Grievance Mechanism (GM) operates through kebele and woreda committees and is
complemented by the Ethiopia Independent Ombudsman (EIO); the most common grievances relate to Result Area 2
land administration especially parcel and boundary disputes while Result Area 1 complaints concern community
watershed activities, including targeting and benefit-sharing, site selection, free-grazing restrictions, and program
communication. Reporting across 2024–2025 shows strong functionality: for RA1, one period recorded 567
grievances, all resolved, with later reporting indicating a cumulative 2,218 RA1 grievances resolved; for RA2, typical
periods show around 1,537 land-related cases submitted, of which approximately 960 were resolved locally and the
remainder referred to land courts. Notably, GM resolution contributed to confirmation of land tenure rights for at least
135 women, evidencing inclusion outcomes.

Ongoing CALM PforR Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM)
The World Bank financed ESPES PforR has supported the establishment of a national GRM, and all regional
governments have established their respective GRM structures down to woreda level with focal persons located in the
Kebeles. Operation of this GRM is based on a manual approved by the Ethiopian Institution of the Ombudsman (EIO
- which is accountable to the Parliament of Ethiopia), which provides capacity building for regional and woreda GRM
offices with continued support from the ESPES. At the woreda level, assigned GRM officers report to the woreda
administrator, who in turn provides regular consolidated reports to the GRM office at the regional level. The head of
the regional GRM office is accountable to the chief administrator, and provides regionally consolidated reports to the
EIO. While the rollout and functionality of the GRM is variable, it is a robust and functional mechanism setting
procedures for the receipt, registration, response and documentation of complaints. The CALM P for R will draw on
and strengthen this mechanism by using it to provide data on Program-related complaints in regional reports to the
MoA, and in the MoA’s regular reporting of Program implementation.

World Bank Grievance Redress System

Communities and individuals who believe they have been adversely affected by a project supported by the World
Bank may submit complaints through existing project-level grievance mechanisms or the Bank’s Grievance Redress
Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that all received complaints are promptly reviewed to address concerns related to
specific projects.

Affected communities and individuals may also submit complaints to the Bank’s independent Accountability
Mechanism (AM), which encompasses both the Inspection Panel and the Dispute Resolution Service. The Inspection
Panel assesses whether harm has occurred or may occur due to non-compliance with Bank policies and procedures,
while the Dispute Resolution Service offers an opportunity for resolution between communities and borrowers.

Complaints may be submitted to the AM at any time after concerns have been raised directly with Bank Management
and following Management's response. For details on how to submit complaints to the Grievance Redress Service
(GRS), please visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For guidance on submitting complaints to the
Accountability Mechanism, visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.

Background information: The consultation was conducted with a total of seven participants, including the CALM

Summary of the consultation: In the consultation, it was indicated that Implementation is currently focused on RA#1,
with regional experts directly engaging communities. However, support at the woreda and cluster levels is limited,
and focal people are either absent or overwhelmed with other responsibilities. Bureau of Agriculture and Natural
Resources (BoANRs) have hired one environmental and one social development specialist, there are no dedicated
environmental and social (E&S) focal persons at the woreda or cluster levels. Supervisors often juggle multiple roles
and lack direct accountability for CALM 2 activities since he was assigned by regional government to oversee
activities conducted by the different sectors.

At the community level, Development Agents (DAs) are involved in some activities, including environmental and
social screening, but they require additional support and incentives. Regional experts lead implementation efforts,
though structured support from lower administrative levels. Gender mainstreaming efforts face challenges due to the
absence of a dedicated budget, although Water User Cooperatives (WUCs) bylaws do include provisions for
occupational health and safety (OHS), non-discrimination, and the prevention of sexual harassment and exploitation
(SH/SEA). Despite these limitations, it was indicated that several achievements have been noted. An Environmental
and Social Management System (ESMS) operational manual has been developed and disseminated across all levels,
and a memorandum of understanding has been signed between MoA and BoAs to operationalize the ESMS at the
woreda level. A functional Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) has been established at the community level, with
three of the seventeen watershed cooperative committee members serving as GRM representatives, including one
woman. Training has been provided for community members and committees, and technical sessions on ESMS and
safety management have been conducted, although overall capacity remains low.

Environmental and social screening processes are in place, with subprojects categorized based on risk levels.
Screening reports are reviewed and endorsed by the regional Environmental Protection Authority (EPA) before
physical activities commence. Stakeholder consultations are conducted during both the screening and Environmental
and Social Management Plan (ESMP) preparation stages. However, several gaps persist. The absence of dedicated
E&S focal persons at woreda/cluster levels undermines accountability. Incident reporting mechanisms, particularly
for SH/SEA cases, are ineffective, and grievance committees have not been established at nursery sites for the worker.
Safety resources such as personal protective equipment (PPE) are unavailable, only first kits are available the WUCs,
leaving health extension workers to manage safety concerns.

Monitoring and auditing systems also require improvement. No E&S audit report has been communicated by
consultants, and concerns have been raised about the accuracy and transparency of consultant reporting. Nonetheless,
in the discussion it was mentioned that EPA demonstrates strong capacity and plays a key role in subproject
certification and approval. To address these challenges, the assessment recommends hiring dedicated E&S experts at
the woreda level, strengthening ESMS-related logistics and training, allocating budget for gender mainstreaming, and
assigning incentivized focal persons at regional. Enhancing EPA’s audit capacity, improving incident reporting
systems, and ensuring the availability of PPE are also critical steps toward reinforcing E&S risk management under
the CALM 2 project in Dire Dawa.

Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM), which is accessible to stakeholders. Community labor guidelines and Water
User cooperatives (WUCs) bylaws now include provisions for occupational health and safety (OHS), non-
discrimination, and the prevention of sexual harassment and exploitation (SH/SEA). Land governance systems have
also shown positive developments, including inclusive kebele-level committees, the issuance of joint Second-Level
Landholding Certificates (SLLCs), and the protection of land rights for vulnerable groups.

Despite these achievements, the consultation team identified several gaps and challenges remain. There is limited
awareness and understanding of E&S responsibilities among focal persons, and reporting quality is inconsistent.
Technical training on ESMSG and safety protocols is lacking, and focal persons are often overburdened with other
duties, necessitating incentives to enhance their engagement. Access to disputed areas for monitoring remains a
challenge, and there is a need to recruit E&S experts with social development background at the RLUDA regional
level. While E&S monitoring mechanisms exist, they require strengthening, and incident reporting systems
particularly for SH/SEA are not functioning effectively.

To address these issues, the team has recommended continuous technical training on E&S risk management and safety
management, improved quality and consistency of E&S screening reports, and the hiring of additional social
development experts. Strengthening incident reporting systems and raising community awareness are also essential.
Furthermore, providing incentives to focal persons and enhancing access to remote or disputed project areas will be
critical to ensuring the effectiveness of environmental and social risk management under the CALM 2 program.

    Harari Program Implementation team
Background information: In this region, both Regional Authorities (RAs) implemented 19 watersheds across 19
kebeles out of a total of 74, involving 5 woredas out of 9. The consultation was conducted with 18 regional-level
environmental and social (E&S) and gender experts, coordinators, and the Director of the Bureau of Agriculture (BoA)
from both RAs, along with other relevant experts, including the head of the agriculture office from Erar woreda, E&S
and gender focal persons, and additional technical staff.

Summary of the consultation: Discussions covered the status of the Environmental and Social Management System
(ESMS), which is established and operational during the implementation of the parent CALM PforR program. The
participants confirm that the Bureau of Agriculture and Natural Resources (BoANR) has hired one full-time
environmental and social development specialist and assigned a focal person for the gender mainstreaming activities.
The E&S, Gender, and GRM focal persons have been assigned at the Woreda level. However, capacity gaps remain
due to heavy workloads on the assigned focal persons, and RLUA offices in the regions have not yet hired E&S
experts. Gender mainstreaming efforts are hindered by the absence of a dedicated budget, and while focal persons are
required at both woreda and regional levels, only partial assignments have been made.

Furthermore, it was discussed that, the E&S operational manual has been developed and disseminated across all
implementing agencies at the regional and woreda levels to address environmental and social risks, including those
related to physical cultural resources, natural habitats, and community safety. An MoU between the MoA and BoAs
is in place to operationalize the ESMS at the woreda level. A Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) has been
established, with committees formed at the watershed level comprising 17 members, including three GRM
representatives (one female). However, budget constraints and the lack of logbooks remain challenges. For land use
and administration issues, traditional mechanisms such as “Shimigilina” are used at the kebele level, while woreda
RLUA offices handle escalated cases. Although reporting mechanisms exist, they require further strengthening.

Also, the consultation covered program implementation processes, including stakeholder engagement during E&S
screening and the preparation of site-specific instruments such as ESMPs. The regional EPA conducted timely reviews
and endorsements of screening reports. The capacity building has been provided particularly for cooperatives and
experts at the woreda and community levels but frequent staff turnover and limited budgets for soft skills have affected
continuity. Additional awareness-raising and refresher training are needed, especially for CALM 2. Awareness of the
E&S operational manual among management and staff across administrative levels also needs to be enhanced.

While grievance procedures are in place, no GBV/SEA/SH incidents have been reported. Reporting mechanisms
therefore need strengthening to receive and handle GBV/SEA/SH cases effectively. Discussions on Watershed Users
Cooperatives (WUCs) highlighted their benefits and the importance of land rights registration in supporting watershed
