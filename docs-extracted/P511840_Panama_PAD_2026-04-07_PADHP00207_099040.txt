 I.   STRATEGIC CONTEXT

A. Program Strategic Context

1.       Effective land administration plays a pivotal role in fostering job creation by establishing the legal and
institutional foundations necessary for private sector growth. Secure property rights and well-defined land laws are
essential for enabling private investment. As emphasized in the World Bank’s 2025 Development Committee paper, land
and property rights are integral to the broader job’s agenda, particularly under the first pillar of the strategy: foundational
infrastructure. Clear and predictable land governance reduces investment risk, facilitates access to credit, and empowers
individuals and businesses to engage in productive activities. Land administration systems support the development of
agribusiness, infrastructure, and urban planning, sectors identified as engines of job creation. Land administration is a
strategic lever for economic growth and employment generation.

2.       Recent macroeconomic evidence underscores the importance of advancing land and cadastral reforms as a tool
to strengthen the labor market and fiscal sustainability in Panama. The country has one of the lowest tax-to-GDP ratios
in the region, at 13.1 percent in 2022, compared to the regional average of 21.5 percent and the Organization for Economic
Co-operation and Development (OECD) average of 34.0 percent1. Unlike most countries, where property taxation or taxes
on goods and services are the main source of revenue, in Panama, contributions to the Social Security System (Caja de
Seguro Social, CSS) are the most significant source. According to a World Bank report (2024), public revenues have
remained around 17–18 percent of GDP in recent years, while public spending has exceeded 21 percent, leading to
persistent fiscal deficits and increasing public debt, which is estimated to reach 59.1 percent of GDP by 2024. This
structural gap between revenues and expenditures limits fiscal space and heightens dependency on external financing.

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

3.       The land administration sector of Panama operates with a dual system. The National Land Administration
Authority (Autoridad Nacional de Administración de Tierras, ANATI) serves as the national cadastral authority,
responsible for the technical, geographic, and administrative management of land information, while the Public Registry
of Panama (Registro Público, RP) ensures legal certainty by recording and safeguarding all deeds and legal transactions
related to both public and private lands. In practice, this dual system can function effectively only when cadastral, legal,
and fiscal information flows seamlessly across institutions. However, these critical linkages face significant challenges.
ANATI’s datasets do not fully interoperate with either the Public Registry or the General Directorate of Revenue
(Dirección General de Ingresos, DGI); updated property valuations are not consistently available for fiscal purposes; and
property transactions, particularly in cases involving parcel subdivisions, remain slow. Meanwhile, the DGI within the
Ministry of Economy and Finance (Ministerio de Economía y Finanzas, MEF) maintains robust taxpayer information
through the Unique Taxpayer Registry (Registro Único de Contribuyentes, RUC), but this system remains siloed from
cadastral and registry data, constraining the ability to link properties to taxpayers, enforce compliance, or detect evasion.
Collectively, the lack of integration across the cadastral (ANATI), legal (Public Registry), and fiscal (DGI) domains
constitutes a central structural constraint to modern, efficient, and transparent land governance in Panama.

4.      Panama’s cadaster has historically been oriented toward fiscal objectives and the mass titling of land—
especially state-owned vacant parcels—rather than serving as a comprehensive geospatial backbone for public
administration. The establishment of ANATI by Law 59 of 2010, unified mandates that had previously been dispersed
across several independent institutions, including the National Cadaster Directorate and the General Directorate of
Revenue (DGI) within the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), the Agrarian Reform Directorate under the Ministry

foundational dataset capable of supporting territorial planning, public service delivery, environmental management, or
fiscal policy. Instead, it has remained largely an intermediate and episodic product, shaped by regularization and mass
titling campaigns, without the continuous updates, systematic maintenance, or robust interoperability required for
integrated land administration.

5.      As a result, cadastral processes have evolved to be predominantly reactive and demand‑driven rather than
systematic. This approach has led to long updating cycles and persistent data gaps, particularly in the recording of
undeclared physical and economic improvements, undermining data accuracy and the credibility of downstream uses
such as valuation and taxation. Institutional and technological capacity constraints further limit the ability to process
large volumes of data, scale mass updates, and deliver services efficiently.

6.       From a fiscal perspective, there are various exemptions for the Transfer Tax on Movable Goods and Services
(Impuesto a la Transferencia de Bienes Muebles y Servicios, ITBMS) and the Property Tax (Impuesto de Bienes
Inmuebles, IBI), the main one being the restriction on the mass updating of cadastral appraisals until the year 2034
(Law 401 of 2023). An updated multipurpose cadaster can significantly enhance the mobilization of domestic public
revenues, particularly through property tax, improving both equity and efficiency in taxation. By enabling better
identification of assets and taxpayers, it also boosts the collection of indirect taxes, which are easier to administer and
less distortionary. Additionally, property formalization stimulates access to credit and private investment, which under
the right conditions reduces reliance on external financing and foreign direct investment. A multipurpose cadaster would
also help to reduce reliance on external financing, which currently represents more than 56 percent2 of GDP and over 82
percent of total public debt. This high level of exposure to foreign currency debt increases fiscal vulnerability to external
shocks. By enhancing the State's capacity to generate sustainable and predictable domestic revenues, fiscal sustainability
is strengthened, a key factor in the country’s risk assessment methodologies used by agencies such as S&P and Moody’s.

7.       ANATI possesses solid technical capacity and extensive experience in mass land titling, supported by a robust
legal framework. To ensure efficient management and effective support for municipalities and indigenous territories,
ANATI must continue investing in technological modernization, process standardization, and staff capacity-building. A
key institutional actor is the National Geographic Institute “Tommy Guardia” (Instituto Geográfico Nacional Tommy
Guardia, IGNTG). As an entity under ANATI’s authority, IGNTG benefits from established technological infrastructure,
strong technical expertise, and experience in inter-institutional collaboration. Nevertheless, IGNTG needs to further
strengthen its technical capacity to respond more rapidly and effectively to the growing demands of the geospatial
market.

8.       Panama’s National Multipurpose Cadaster program, adopted through ANATI Resolution No. 791 of November
22, 2025, is a cornerstone reform aimed at transforming the country’s land administration ecosystem into a modern,
integrated, and efficient framework. This government program seeks to establish a coherent governance structure
through regulatory reforms and inter-agency coordination, ensuring that cadastral, registry, and fiscal systems operate
seamlessly. It combines upgrades in geodesy and cartography with Information and Communications Technologies (ICT)
modernization to deliver accurate spatial data and digital services nationwide. Within this enabling policy framework,
priority actions for the proposed PforR include densifying the geodetic network, acquiring high-quality imagery and
cartography, and upgrading ICT systems—led by ANATI and IGNTG qualified personnel—to efficiently process and

    C. PforR Program Scope

(i) Summary of government program

12.     Panama’s National Multipurpose Cadaster Program (2025–2035), led by the National Land Administration
Authority (ANATI), aims to modernize and integrate the country’s cadaster into a unified, digital, interoperable, and
multifunctional system. Accordingly, with an estimated investment of US$250 million over ten years, the program will
strengthen land tenure security, enhance property tax administration, support sustainable territorial planning, and
provide reliable geospatial information for public policy and climate resilience.

13.    The program encompasses the modernization of the legal and regulatory framework governing cadastral
management, the upgrading of the national geodetic network and base cartography, and the expansion of systematic
cadastral formation, updating, and maintenance across rural, urban, and expansion areas. It also establishes national
standards for interoperable cadastral data to be shared with key institutions, including the Public Registry, the DGI,
municipalities, and environmental and territorial planning agencies.

(ii) Program Boundary and Results Areas

14.     The PforR will support the most critical components of the Government’s broader initiative to establish a
National Multipurpose Cadaster System, including the governance scheme, institutional arrangements, regulatory
framework, procedures and instruments, information systems, and data model required to maintain up‑to‑date cadastral
data that can be integrated with the public registry and are interoperable with other territorial information systems.

15.       A central pillar is the development and implementation of the Integrated Cadastral–Registry Information
System (SICAR), enabling the integration of legal and cadastral data, institutional interoperability, and citizen access.
The program further promotes the multipurpose use of cadastral information to support public policies related to land
titling, fiscal equity, climate adaptation, environmental management, and urban and territorial planning. Finally, it seeks
to reduce the country’s land titling backlog through inclusive and gender-responsive mass regularization processes,
prioritizing vulnerable and Indigenous communities. Program implementation will adhere to the institutional processes

16.     The Program will enhance geospatial data management in line with internationally recognized principles for
integrated geospatial information management. The adoption of common standards and interoperable frameworks will
strengthen institutional coordination, promote cross‑sectoral data reuse, and position cadastral information as a core
national geospatial asset supporting sustainable development and evidence‑based decision‑making. These data will
support key public functions, including fiscal management (such as property valuation and taxation), land titling, and
land‑use, urban, and territorial planning.

17.      Selected Program Areas will be defined by ANATI based on the following selection criteria: (i) for municipalities,
those with potential to increase local revenue and those where projects of strategic importance are being implemented
in line with the Government’s Strategic Plan; and (ii) for Indigenous Territories, land‑titling‑related activities will be based
on: (a) a formal request from the competent Indigenous Authority, certified population data, government recognition,
and other applicable regulatory requirements; (b) complete and reliable technical information, including analysis of
historical and current occupation, assessment of overlaps, and a cadastral evaluation; (c) evidence of meaningful
participation in land‑related matters, identification of potential land‑related conflicts, and the existence of dispute
resolution mechanisms; and (d) government prioritization, with preference given to areas aligned with the Government’s
Strategic Plan.

18.     Results Area # 1: Development of the national multipurpose cadaster management system. This results area
focuses on establishing the core architecture and institutional capability required to build and sustain Panama’s National
Multipurpose Cadaster Management System. This results area addresses fundamental constraints, such as outdated

geospatial infrastructure, fragmented cadastral registry information, and limited digital capacity, by investing in the
modernization of the country’s geodetic and cartographic foundations while simultaneously strengthening ANATI’s ability

19.      Geodesic and cartographic support. Aims to modernize Panama’s foundational geospatial infrastructure to
ensure accurate, reliable, and nationally consistent territorial information. This component strengthens and updates the

surveying, mapping, and land tenure regularization processes. In parallel, the acquisition of high-quality imagery and
modern cartographic production will enable ANATI to generate updated base maps essential for land administration,
environmental management, risk assessment, and planning. The information provided includes climate-related data,
allowing for better climate-informed decision making, that could result in for example, avoiding deforestation or not
building in flood-prone areas. Together, these actions establish the technical backbone required for standardized data
capture, improved service delivery, and long-term sustainability of the national geospatial ecosystem.

20.      IT strengthening and capacity building. Focuses on equipping ANATI with the digital foundations and institutional
capabilities required to operate a modern, integrated land administration system. This includes the development of the
LADM Panama profile5, which will standardize cadastral registry data across institutions, and the creation of the SICAR.
Complementary investments will provide technological equipment for ANATI’s central and regional offices, enabling
consistent, real-time data management and service delivery. The Program will also develop a Geographic Information
System for one Indigenous Territory to inform scalability and support culturally relevant, participatory land governance.
To ensure sustained institutional performance, the Program will support the design and implementation of inclusive
training programs for land professionals, municipal staff, Indigenous authorities, and women, strengthening local and
national capacity to maintain and use the system. The training will incorporate the use of climate-related information for
decision-making or management. Finally, a comprehensive business plan for ANATI, including the National Geographic
Institute Tommy Guardia (IGNTG), will define operational, financial, and service delivery strategies to ensure the long-
term sustainability of the digital cadaster ecosystem.

21.     Results Area 2: Improve access to cadaster and land tenure services in selected program areas. This results area
focuses on addressing longstanding limitations in data availability, municipal capacity, and the regularization of land rights.
The Program advances a comprehensive set of activities—including digitalizing paper-based cadastral records, conducting
systematic cadastral surveys, diagnosing formal and informal tenure for both men and women, and carrying out mass land

cadastral processes to support tenure regularization in Indigenous territories.

(iii) Program Beneficiaries

25.      Direct beneficiaries include individuals whose property rights are inventoried, formalized, and registered,

local development.

26.      Indirect beneficiaries include government institutions, the private sector, and the general public. These groups
will benefit from improved access to a comprehensive property information system, enhanced land tenure security to
support territorial and land‑use planning, increased property tax collection, improved conditions for agricultural and
infrastructure investment, and broader economic growth driven by a more transparent and efficient land administration
system. Key institutional beneficiaries include the National Authority for Land Administration (ANATI), the National
Geographic Institute “Tommy Guardia” (IGNTG), the Public Registry, the General Directorate of Revenue (DGI) under the
Ministry of Economy and Finance, and selected Program Areas. These institutions will gain direct access to the
multipurpose cadaster’s core data attributes and interoperable systems, enabling improved land‑use planning,
agricultural development, soil conservation, landslide risk reduction, restoration of degraded lands, and disaster risk
management.

F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Program Design

30.      In addition to global experience, the Panama PforR incorporates key lessons from long running land
administration programs supported by the World Bank in Nicaragua and Colombia, both of which demonstrated that
multipurpose cadaster modernization requires a long-term approach supported by strong institutional leadership
and sustained technical capacity. From Nicaragua, Panama adopts the insight that land administration reforms succeed
when they are phased, when systems such as SICAR are built around local technical capacity, and when municipalities
and land agencies are empowered through clear mandates and coordinated execution. These lessons underscore
Panama’s focus on institutional strengthening of ANATI, sustainability through the adoption of the LADM Panamá
profile and SICAR core, and structured engagement with municipalities and the Public Registry. Nicaragua’s emphasis
on social inclusion, community engagement, gender considerations, and alternative conflict resolution mechanisms
directly informs the Program’s approach to diagnostics, mass regularization, and Indigenous territory pilots.

31.    Meanwhile, Colombia’s experience reinforces the need for a solid regulatory and institutional framework to
manage the complexity of multipurpose cadaster implementation across multiple actors. Panama mirrors Colombia’s
emphasis on clear leadership by positioning ANATI as the central authority and policy steward of the system.
Colombia’s focus on technical robustness—modern geodesy, updated cartography, interoperability aligned with LADM

 standards, and CORS densification—shaped the Panama PforR’s Results Area 1 and its DLIs linked to foundational

 III. PROGRAM IMPLEMENTATION

     A. Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

32.     The National Land Council (Consejo Nacional de Tierras, CNT) will serve as the Program’s Steering Committee,
acting as the highest body for strategic governance, high‑level decision-making, and results of oversight. It will be
composed of key sectoral ministries (Ministerio de Vivienda y Ordenamiento Territorial - MIVIOT, MEF, Ministerio de
Desarrollo Agropecuario - MIDA, Ministerio de Gobierno - MINGOB, Ministerio de Ambiente - MiAmbiente), the Office of
the Comptroller General of the Republic (Contraloría General de la República - CGR), the Public Registry, and the relevant
general administrators (Autoridad de Turismo de Panamá - ATP, and ANATI). In its role as Board of Directors, the CNT will
ensure the intersectoral coordination required for technical and regulatory coherence, alignment of institutional
mandates, and resolution of inter‑agency bottlenecks. The CNT will operate under its current internal regulations,
ensuring technical quorums and the participation of actors with the necessary technical authority for the approval of
Program-related decisions.

33.     Program Implementation. The General Administrator of ANATI will lead Program implementation and be
responsible for overall operational planning, technical coordination, and institutional management. The Program Director
will coordinate the work of ANATI’s technical directorates, the IGNTG, and regional offices to ensure effective
implementation. The General Administrator of ANATI will designate the unit responsible for liaison with the National
Authority for Government Innovation (Autoridad Nacional para la Innovación Gubernamental, AIG) on the planning and
investment of ICT solutions. ANATI will coordinate the preparation, approval, and implementation of the Program
Operational Manual (POM), ensure achievement of the Disbursement‑Linked Indicators (DLIs), and report on Program
progress to the National Land Council (CNT) and the Bank. During implementation, ANATI will assess its staffing capacity
to meet increased Program demands, including the hiring or appointment of key financial management, monitoring and
evaluation, technical, and environmental and social specialists, and will deliver targeted training as needed.

34.       Technical Team. ANATI’s Technical Directorates, together with IGNTG, constitute the core technical team
responsible for the development and implementation of the National Multipurpose Cadaster System. The technical team
will comprise: (i) the National Directorate of Cadastral Information and Appraisal; (ii) the National Directorate of Cadastral
Surveying; (iii) the National Directorate of Titling and Regularization; (iv) the Directorate of Mass Adjudication; (v) the
Directorate of Indigenous Lands and Municipal Assets; (vi) the IGNTG; (vii) information systems and technology staff; and
(viii) planning, monitoring, and evaluation staff. These units will operate under standardized technical protocols, quality
assurance criteria, and data control procedures. ANATI’s regional offices will support the technical team and will be
responsible for territorial implementation, coordination with communities and local authorities, operation of regional
User Service Centers, and supervision of field service providers.

35.     Social and Environmental Management. ANATI’s social units, including the National Directorate of Mass
Adjudication, the National Directorate of Indigenous Lands and Municipal Assets, the Public Relations Office, the Equal
Opportunities Office, the Legal Advisory Office, and the User Service Center, will lead the Program’s social management
functions. These include community engagement, social inclusion, grievance and inquiry management, public
communications, and conflict prevention and resolution. Given that ANATI does not have a dedicated environmental unit,

ensure the timely and adequate availability of funds required to implement Program activities and achieve the intended
results.

IV. PROGRAM ASSESSMENTS SUMMARY

 A. Technical

(i) Strategic relevance, technical soundness

46.      The Program is strategically relevant and technically sound. It operationalizes national priorities and Country
Partnership Framework outcomes through a standards‑based, sequenced approach that modernizes geospatial
foundations, digitizes and integrates cadaster‑registry data, and scales service delivery in Program areas. At the core of
the Program is SICAR, a next‑generation digital platform to be developed based on the Land Administration Domain Model
(ISO 19152). SICAR will serve as the backbone of land information management by providing standardized and
interoperable data structures that ensure consistency, traceability, and real‑time integration with fiscal and territorial
planning systems. This is complemented by a structured CORS densification plan, high‑resolution base mapping, and
streamlined tenure regularization, which are essential for accurate cadastral surveying and the reduction of boundary
disputes, with a positive impact on the land market and the business environment. The approach reflects international
good practice while being tailored to Panama’s institutional context, strengthening legal certainty and transparency and
laying the foundation for improved revenue mobilization, inclusive land governance, and evidence‑based decision‑making
across sectors.

47.     The Program is expected to stimulate job creation and strengthen private-sector participation along the land
administration value chain. According to ANATI, the operation will generate approximately 1,200 direct jobs, including
positions for topographers, geodetic technicians, and other professionals required to achieve the Program’s targets. This
estimate excludes indirect employment expected to be generated by private firms engaged in cadastral activities across
participating municipalities. To ensure workforce sustainability and quality, ANATI plans to partner with universities to
train professionals in cadaster and land administration–related fields. In addition, collaboration with AMPYME (Autoridad
de la Micro, Pequeña y Mediana Empresa de Panamá) will support the creation and financing of new local companies to
meet the growing demand generated by the Program.

48.      The PforR aligns with the Maximizing Finance for Development (MFD) approach and contributes
towards Private Capital Enable (PCE) corporate scorecard indicator. Activities under Results Area 2 are considered MFD-
enabling by establishing the institutional and technological foundations needed to reduce transaction times and costs,
lower investment risks, and unlock private capital flows. Improved cadastral information and strengthened property rights
are critical to fostering private-sector investment. Enhanced land data reduces risk and transaction costs, improves access
to finance, enables more efficient land and real estate markets, supports infrastructure and urban development, and
increases fiscal revenues. Through streamlined cadastral procedures, the Program will reduce barriers to formal real estate
transactions and support the development of secondary markets. The availability of standardized land market
information, together with the implementation and interoperability of the SICAR platform, is expected to increase both

50.      Panama’s climate vulnerability is closely linked to rapid and often unplanned urbanization, weak land
management, and insufficient integration of climate risk into urban planning. The country faces high exposure to multi-
hazard events, especially flooding, which is the most frequent and impactful natural disaster, affecting over 170,000
people between 1980 and 2020. Urban expansion, particularly in Panama City, has led to the loss of critical ecosystems
such as mangroves and wetlands, which naturally mitigate flood risk and coastal erosion. In the Tocumen basin alone,
mangrove coverage declined by 60 percent between 2000 and 2015, exacerbating flood frequency and severity. Poorly
planned land use, construction in floodplains, and inadequate drainage systems have increased the vulnerability of
infrastructure and marginalized populations, while also degrading vital ecosystem services. The government has
recognized these challenges and is advancing reforms to strengthen disaster risk management, update building codes,
and integrate climate adaptation into territorial planning. Nature-based solutions, such as restoring mangroves and
riparian vegetation, are being promoted alongside structural measures and improved governance, but implementation
gaps remain. Effective urban and land management, including risk-informed zoning, resilient infrastructure investment,
and inter-municipal coordination, is essential to reduce exposure, protect vulnerable communities, and ensure sustainable
urban development in the face of intensifying climate hazards.

51.      Paris Alignment. The proposed PforR is aligned with the goals of the Paris Agreement on both Adaptation and
Mitigation. The operation is consistent with Panama’s comprehensive legal climate framework, its National Determined
Contributions (NDCs) under the Paris Agreement, and its adaptation policy instruments, including the National Climate
Action Plan (Plan Nacional de Acción Climática, PNAC) 6. Panama’s first updated NDC (2020) commits to mitigation and
adaptation across 10 strategic sectors, including forest restoration reaching 50,000 hectares by 2050, and 22 strategic
adaptation commitments that strengthen planning, risk management and information systems, and climate-resilient
infrastructure7. Given that Panama remains a net GHG sink—thanks to forest removals—its land sector is central to climate
action and resilience. While deforestation has slowed, it remains a key driver of natural capital loss; maintaining Panama’s
sink status and meeting 2030/2050 commitments depend on safeguarding intact landscapes, reducing deforestation, and
scaling restoration and afforestation through initiatives such as the PNAC measures, the Alliance for One Million Hectares,
and forestry actions mandated by Executive Decree 135 (2021)8.

55.      Gender. There is limited information on women’s land tenure in Panama. State institutions, including the National
Land Administration Authority (ANATI), the Agricultural Census, and the Ministry of Agricultural Development, do not have
updated, sex‑disaggregated data readily available to researchers. The limited information that can be collected is
fragmented and not linked across institutions in a way that facilitates data analysis. Each institution produces its own
statistics without mechanisms to cross‑reference data, which hinders the development of differentiated, evidence‑based
policies related to land use and land tenure9. Prevailing social norms tend to underrecognize women’s tenure rights,
limiting their participation in cadastral surveys, technical workshops, and training programs that are critical for
understanding and asserting property rights. These structural barriers are compounded by persistent data gaps in
cadastral systems, weak incentives for formalization, and women’s limited access to information, resources, and legal
processes—constraints that are especially pronounced in contexts of high informality and among rural and vulnerable
populations. While 38.2 percent of women hold tenure over occupied private housing and 41.3 percent of agricultural
producers are women, 31 percent of agricultural holdings remain without a property title10. These figures highlight a
central finding of the assessment: women play a significant role in land use and agricultural production, yet they are
disproportionately exposed to informality and weaker tenure security, linking productive participation directly to legal
vulnerability.

56.      The PforR will strengthen women’s land rights by incorporating sex‑disaggregated indicators in the Results
Framework to monitor women’s access to cadastral and land tenure services. The Program includes a PDO‑level indicator
on the number of women receiving property titles (individual or joint), and tracks women’s participation in technical
training and capacity‑building activities. At the systems level, the Program recognizes that further improvements in data
architecture are needed and incorporates actions to progressively strengthen joint‑ownership recording through SICAR
and updated cadastral protocols and operational guidelines. By linking improved data generation to measurable results
on women’s land tenure security, the Program establishes a practical foundation for monitoring inclusion over time and
for advancing more equitable, evidence‑based land policies in Panama.

57.     International evidence shows that when women’s legal rights to housing, land and property (HLP) are
strengthened and effectively implemented—through reforms and enforcement that recognize ownership, joint titling,
inheritance, and marital property regimes—women’s economic agency and ability to use formal financial services
increase. The Program responds to the historic under‑reporting and the lack of sex‑disaggregated information by
strengthening data models and cadastral infrastructure so that women’s rights—formal or informal—are consistently
captured and traceable. Incorporating sex‑disaggregated data fields and joint‑ownership recording significantly increases
women’s visibility and foster incidence within cadastral systems. Yet global and regional experience shows that data
improvements alone are not enough. The Program therefore ensures that women have a direct and unrestricted link to
the cadastral database—holding rights and responsibilities in their own name without needing to prove marital status or
dependency. This is reinforced through joint‑ownership protocols, systematic verification of women’s rights during field
operations, and user‑centered service delivery that reduces information barriers and strengthens women’s effective
access to cadastral services.

58.     The Program acknowledges that women face information gaps, lower digital literacy, and procedural barriers
that limit their ability to access and use SICAR. Drawing on lessons from Colombia’s Multipurpose Cadaster PforR—where
tailored training and gender‑sensitive protocols improved women’s effective use of digital cadastral systems—the
Program integrates a results chain that expands women’s digital access through inclusive SICAR training, user‑centered
support, and outreach designed to account for safety, low literacy, and limited digital skills. These actions strengthen
women’s ability to access cadastral information and fully benefit from digital services.

(iv) Economic justification and financial sustainability

61.     The Program is economically justified, fiscally sound, and financially sustainable. A multi-dimensional evaluation
shows that benefits from systematic cadastral surveying, land titling, and institutional strengthening exceed costs over
the long term. Using total costs of US$ 95.15 million (World Bank loan + government contribution), the Program’s Net
Present Value is US$ 209.3 million at an 8 percent discount rate, with a 23.9 percent Internal Rate of Return (IRR). Public
involvement is justified because cadastral systems have public good characteristics and face persistent market failures.
Secure tenure and complete cadastral coverage create spillover benefits, better land markets, fewer disputes, improved
planning, and stronger fiscal capacity, which private actors do not fully capture, leading to underinvestment. The Program
addresses these failures through coordinated, large-scale interventions, especially important in rural and peri-urban areas
with weak private demand for formalization.

62.      The economic analysis shows net positive welfare gains from higher land and property values due to cadastral
surveying and titling in prioritized municipalities. About 25,000 parcels will be affected through new titles and the
resolution of pending cases. The valuation assumes untitled properties are 20 percent less valuable in rural areas and 40
percent less valuable in urban areas. Over 30 years, the resulting increase in property values exceeds the total Program
costs of US $ 95.15 million, confirming efficient public resource use.

63.     The fiscal analysis evaluates whether improved cadastral coverage and valuation can increase property tax (IBI)
revenues enough to offset Program costs. Incorporating around 25,000 new properties and updating existing valuations—
without changes to tax rates or enforcement—yields gains based on an average annual IBI payment of US$ 109 per
property (2022 data). The projected 13 percent increase in collections from updated and new properties would generate
the equivalent of a 30.35 percent rise in IBI revenues in targeted areas, sufficient to recover the Program’s investment
over time.

64.      The financial analysis shows that ANATI and the Public Registry can become financially sustainable. For ANATI,
resolving 75 percent of pending coastal and maritime concession cases can generate US$ 20.9 million annually. With stable
institutional costs and a 30 percent revenue increase from other services, ANATI would be self-sustaining. For the Public
Registry, expanded formalization produces estimated annual revenues of US$ 34.9 million once fully realized, meaning
government revenues from the Program would cover its costs.

B. Fiduciary

65.      The Fiduciary Systems Assessment (FSA) reviewed ANATI’s fiduciary capacity and performance over fiscal years

systems, with the implementation of some strengthening measures, would provide reasonable assurance that the

achievement of results remain. Those risks relate to the inclusion of key Program activities in the Government Investment

increase its operations and investment budget. Credible mitigation measures to address the identified risks have been
discussed and agreed upon with ANATI and the Government of Panama (MEF and CGR). To facilitate implementation of
the Bank’s Anti‑Corruption Guidelines and ensure timely reporting, monitoring, and cooperation in relation to credible
allegations of corruption, one of the following arrangements will be in place: (i) a Memorandum of Collaboration between
the Bank and the CGR General, or (ii) alternative arrangements acceptable to the Bank. Additionally, ANATI will develop

(i) Key fiduciary risks

66.     After the application of proposed mitigation measures, the residual overall fiduciary system risk to the
achievement of the PDO is Substantial. From a financial management perspective, weaknesses across the Public Financial
Management (PFM) cycle and ANATI’s limited prior experience with large-scale, externally financed operations represent
substantial risks that would affect Program implementation and achievement of development objectives. Key risks stem
from unpredictable budget formulation and approval processes, weak budget credibility, and the need to absorb a four
to fivefold increase in investment resources, all of which are likely to result in implementation delays and slower
disbursements. ANATI’s internal control framework, including internal audit, is not yet adequately prepared to support
the scale and complexity of activities envisaged under the PforR, particularly large IT and geospatial procurements.
Continued reliance on CGR ex‑ante controls is expected to further slow implementation and delay disbursements. In
addition, ANATI has not been regularly audited by the CGR and lacks established external audit arrangements for the
Program. While institutional capacity is adequate for routine operations, it may be insufficient for a Program of this
magnitude, requiring strengthened staffing, processes, and agreed mitigation measures, including MEF commitment to
provide timely and sufficient budget allocations.

67.     From the procurement perspective, the following are the main risk factors identified: (i) although the national
public procurement system is generally robust, inefficiencies identified in recent studies and in this FSA indicate that, in
general and in particular for the case of ANATI, the procurement cycle from process launch to contract signature and
implementation, can take several months due to budgetary, legal, and internal control requirements; (ii) ANATI’s
procurement of goods and services appears fragmented, with limited consolidation of demand and insufficient planning
or aggregation; and (iii) while many Program activities have been previously undertaken by ANATI and an identifiable
market exists, ANATI should ensure that the market is willing and able to respond to the scale and complexity of Program
needs.

(ii) Risk mitigation measures

68.     Procurement risks. The mitigation measures encompass: (i) streamlining ANATI’s procurement procedures and
strengthening internal processes to address identified inefficiencies; (ii) consolidating demand through more strategic and
coordinated procurement planning to achieve economies of scale and improve transactional efficiency; and (iii)
conducting market analyses to assess supplier capacity and market readiness for Program‑financed activities.

69.      Financial management risks. The Program Action Plan (PAP) incorporates measures to mitigate fiduciary risks,
including aligning procurement planning, budget allocations, and cash‑flow management with Program requirements.
These measures are intended to reduce the risks of delayed execution, under‑absorption, and misalignment between
approved budgets and implementation needs. However, given that the most critical risk relates to the timely and sufficient
allocation of budgetary resources, sustained and structured dialogue with the MEF, including the National Budget
Directorate (Dirección de Presupuesto de la Nación, DIPRENA), Public Accounting, and Public Finance, will be essential
throughout Program implementation.

 71.      The PforR will finance activities with potential environmental and social risks, including procurement and
 installation of CORS stations and ICT equipment; minor renovations of existing ANATI and IGNTG facilities; and
 construction of CORS monuments, which may affect natural habitats. The Program will also support land titling,
 requiring verification to avoid overlap with non‑adjudicable areas such as forests and environmentally sensitive zones.
 Socially sensitive activities include cadaster surveys and tenure diagnostics; development of a GIS for an Indigenous
 territory; capacity building for women, Indigenous peoples, and municipalities; awareness campaigns; establishment of
 a permanent cadaster consultation mechanism; and preparation of guidelines on gender equity, Indigenous land rights,
 and alternative dispute resolution.

 (i) Key environmental and social risks

 72.      The environmental and social risk rating for the Program is assessed as Substantial. From the environmental
 perspective, the PforR will not finance the titling of individual or collective tenure rights that overlap with mangroves,
 wetlands, and/or Natural Protected Areas, including their buffer zones. In the case of forests, however, the Program
 may finance the titling of individual tenure rights that overlap forest areas, which could potentially lead to forest
 conversion; therefore, compliance with applicable land‑use regulations will need to be strengthened. Other relevant
 environmental risks include: (i) temporary nuisances related to dust generation, vibration, and noise during small‑scale
 infrastructure rehabilitation works; (ii) the generation, inadequate management, and improper disposal of
 non‑hazardous and hazardous solid waste, including e‑waste from equipment installation; and (iii) occupational health
 and safety risks affecting the project workforce. These risks and impacts are expected to be low, temporary, and
 site‑specific, and can be effectively mitigated through the application of readily available measures.

 73.      From the social perspective, key risks include: (i) increased boundary and tenure disputes in the absence of
 proactive social screening and mediation mechanisms, which could delay implementation and exacerbate local
 tensions; (ii) barriers to access and participation for rural, Indigenous, and Afro‑descendant populations due to
 language, cultural, documentation, cost, and connectivity constraints, limiting equitable access to cadastral and titling
 services; (iii) community resistance, misinformation, or mistrust arising from the lack of culturally appropriate,
 transparent communication and consultation, potentially leading to grievances or conflict; (iv) the exclusion or
 under‑recognition of women as co‑owners and rights holders if joint titling and gender‑sensitive verification are not
 systematically applied, increasing the risk of loss of patrimonial rights and long‑term tenure insecurity; (v) limited
 institutional capacity within ANATI to manage social risks, grievances, and conflict resolution, as well as to coordinate
 effectively with relevant agencies (e.g., MiAmbiente, the Viceministry of Indigenous Affairs, and Indigenous authorities);
 and (vi) data gaps and insufficient sex‑disaggregated information in cadastral systems, constraining informed
 decision‑making, monitoring of inclusion outcomes, and timely course correction.

 V. KEY RISKS

77.     The overall risk rating for the Program is assessed as Moderate, with four risk categories rated Substantial:
fiduciary, institutional capacity, environmental, and stakeholder risks.

78.      Fiduciary Risk (Substantial). This risk is assessed as Substantial, reflecting weaknesses across PFM cycle and
ANATI’s limited experience with large‑scale, externally financed operations. Institutional capacity risk is also Substantial,
given the scale and pace of reforms, the introduction of new digital systems and procedures, uneven capacity across ANATI
and partner institutions, and the need to ensure long‑term financial and institutional sustainability beyond Bank support.
To mitigate fiduciary risks, the PAP includes actions to streamline ANATI’s procurement processes, strengthen internal
controls, consolidate demand through coordinated planning, and conduct market analyses. As the most critical risk relates
to the timely and adequate allocation of budgetary resources, mitigation measures include sustained dialogue between
ANATI and MEF.

79.      Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability (Substantial). This risk is assessed as Substantial,
reflecting the scale, novelty, and accelerated pace of the reform agenda. The operation entails the introduction of new
digital systems and procedures, expanded institutional mandates, and increased coordination requirements across ANATI
and partner institutions, with uneven capacity at regional and municipal levels. In addition, the long‑term sustainability of
reforms will depend on securing adequate financing for system maintenance, operation of the geodetic network,
continuous data updating, and staff retention, as well as on the institutionalization of new workflows and governance
arrangements. Mitigation measures include a phased implementation approach, DLIs linked to capacity‑building results,
preparation of a business plan for ANATI to support financial sustainability, and the adoption of a standardized national
LADM profile and technical procedures prior to scaling, which together are expected to reduce institutional capacity risks
over time.

80.      Stakeholders and Environmental and Social Risks (Substantial). These risks are assessed as Substantial due to the
social sensitivity associated with inventorying property rights through the cadaster and implementing land tenure
regularization, including in municipalities and Indigenous territories. In this context, misperceptions or inadequate
communication could lead to resistance, implementation delays, or localized conflict. Mitigation measures include a
proactive and differentiated stakeholder engagement strategy emphasizing early and transparent communication;
culturally appropriate engagement and participatory mapping; respect for Indigenous Peoples’ collective governance
structures; transparent eligibility criteria; alternative dispute resolution mechanisms; and accessible grievance redress
systems for populations with informal or insecure tenure.
