1. Introduction
1.1 Introduction
The Pacific Region Preparedness, Adaptation and Resilience Program – Samoa Project (PREPARE) is
financed by the World Bank (WB) and aims improve the capacity of the Government Samoa to prepare
for and manage the impacts of natural hazards effectively, to strengthen the resilience of select urban
areas, and in case of an eligible crisis or emergency, respond promptly and effectively to it.
PREPARE is being implemented by the Ministry of Finance (MOF), Ministry of Works, Transport and
Infrastructure (MWTI) and the Ministry of Natural Resources and Environment (MNRE). MoF is the
executing agency. The Project Management Division (PMD) of the (MWTI) will carry out the overall
implementation role for the Project.
PREPARE is being prepared under the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), which
was effective on October 1, 2018. In line with ESF guidelines, projects such as PREPARE must comply
with ten Environmental and Social Standards (ESS). Per ESS 10, the Implementing Agencies (IA) should
provide stakeholders with timely, relevant, understandable, and accessible information, and consult
with them in a culturally appropriate manner, which is free of manipulation, interference, coercion,
discrimination, or intimidation. This Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) has been prepared to meet the
requirements of ESS 10, Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure, and to satisfy a Project
financing requirement. It is supported by the following Project documents: Project Appraisal Document
(PAD), Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP), Environmental and Social Management
Framework (ESMF), Project Operational Manual (POM) (to be developed), and other specific plans that
have been or will be prepared for the Project. The draft environmental and social (E&S) instruments will
be disclosed on MWTI’s website and will be further workshopped with the IAs and other key
stakeholders to ensure full understanding of the Project’s E&S requirements including stakeholder
engagement, communication, and the grievance process.
This SEP outlines the program for stakeholder engagement, including public information disclosure and
consultation throughout all Project stages; communication methodologies amongst Project
stakeholders; and includes a Grievance Redress Mechanism (FGRM) by which people affected by any
project-related activity can raise concerns or lodge complaints.
1.2 Project description
The Project aims improve the capacity of the Government Samoa to prepare for and manage the
impacts of natural hazards effectively, to strengthen the resilience of select urban areas, and in case of
an eligible crisis or emergency, respond promptly and effectively to it.
This includes developing and implementing early warning systems; procuring and installing hazard
monitoring and communication equipment; design and construction of a new meteorological facility and
a sister satellite office (Upolu); construction of a Seismic Operations Centre (Upolu); construction and
retrofit of firehouses (Upolu and Savai’i); pilot waterfront upgrading in Salelologa, combined with
coastal protection and community spaces; settlement infrastructure and access roads to basic services,
stormwater drainage, and coastal protection (Salelologa); multifunctional public spaces, including parks,
green spaces, and floodplain management spaces (Salelologa), development of Salelologa Township
Plan, Sustainable Master Plan and related Strategic Environmental and Social Assessment.
The Project comprises the following five components:
Component 1: Hazard Monitoring and Early Warning Systems

Activities under Component 1 will invest in global, regional, and country-level decision support systems,
instrumentation, and capacity and institutional strengthening for Multi Hazard Early Warning Sysytem
(MHEWS), supporting both climate-related and geophysical hazards. This includes strengthening the
“upstream” end of effective MHEWS through investments in observations and forecasting, including for
the Global Basic Observation Network (GBON).
Component 2: Emergency Preparedness and Response
Activities under Component 2 will enhance national- and community level response capacities to better
prepare for, and respond to, future disasters. This will be achieved through supporting the
“downstream” end of effective MHEWS, particularly infrastructure designed to national climate-resilient
building design standards, investments in dissemination and communication, and EP&R at the country
and community levels.
Component 3: Resilient Infrastructure
Activities under Component 3 will invest in national and local activities to reduce the exposure and
vulnerability of priority assets to climate and natural-related hazards. This will be achieved through the
risk-informed town planning, strengthening of critical public facilities, including to current energy/water
efficiency and natural hazard resilience standards, physical climate adaptation measures, and
institutional strengthening related to infrastructure resilience.
Component 4: Project Management
Activities under Component 4 will support Project management and implementation support: (i)
recruitment of consultants to provide support on Project engineering, environmental and social
management, procurement, financial management, and monitoring and evaluation and contract
management; (ii) monitoring, review, and evaluation of the project; and (iii) capacity building and
training including for operations and maintenance. Technical specialists would be funded under other
components.
Component 5: Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC)
Activities under Component 5 will support a Contingent Emergency Response Component. The CERC
will provide a mechanism: (i) for quick disbursements to meet the immediate liquidity needs of the
Recipient following an eligible disaster event in order to finance critical imports; or (ii) to finance pilot
adaptive social protection measures, emergency response, recovery and reconstruction works and
associated services.
1.3 Objectives of the Stakeholder Engagement Plan
The overall objective of this SEP is to define a program for stakeholder engagement during
implementation of the Project, including public information disclosure and consultation throughout the
entire Project cycle. The SEP outlines the ways in which MNRE and MWTI communicate with
stakeholders and will establish a mechanism that people can raise concerns, provide feedback, or make
complaints about project-related activities. The SEP specifically emphasizes methods to engage groups
considered most vulnerable and that are at risk of being left out of Project benefits.
The SEP is a living document and will be updated early during Project implementation by the MWTI
Project Management Division (PMD) safeguards team once Project activities are further defined. The
SEP will be updated thereafter throughout the Project implementation period, as new stakeholders are
identified.

2. Stakeholder Identification & Analysis and Methodology
Stakeholders will vary depending on the scope of work of a particular Project along with the
involvement of any additional agency or association that will assist throughout the Project life. Project
stakeholders are identified as individuals or groups, government ministries and other entities that may
be impacted by or have an interest in the proposed projects.
Projects stakeholders can be divided into the following categories:
    •   Project Partners – stakeholders that contribute to the execution and implementation of a
        project.
    •   Affected Parties: persons, groups and other entities within the Project Area of Influence (PAI)
        that are directly influenced by the Project and/or have been identified as most susceptible to
        change associated with the Project, and who need to be closely engaged in identifying impacts
        and their significance, as well as in decision-making on mitigation measures;
    •   Other Interested Parties: individual/groups/entities that may not experience direct impacts
        from the Project but who consider their interests as being affected by the Project and/or who
        could affect the Project and the process of its implementation in any way;
    •   Vulnerable Groups: persons who may be disproportionately impacted or whom the Project
        poses significant impacts and disadvantage compared with any other groups due to their
        vulnerable status. This group will require special engagement to ensure their equal
        representation in the consultation and decision-making process associated with the Project.

2.1.1 Disadvantaged/Vulnerable Individuals or Groups
It is vital to understand the different aspects of impacts of how the project could impact the affected
people if the impacts are weighed more to one disadvantage or vulnerable individual or group. This may
be due to the disadvantages or vulnerable individuals or groups are restricted from having a voice to
express their concerns about the Project or even finding it hard to understand the impacts of a project.
Therefore, it is crucial to ensure that awareness is carried out as well as stakeholder engagement with
disadvantage or vulnerable individual groups for equality and fairness. Factors that contribute to
vulnerability can be from an individual’s origin, sex, gender, age, ability, health condition, economic
deficiency, and financial security.
The engagement between the stakeholders and disadvantaged or vulnerable individuals and groups
requires the application of specific measures and assistance focusing on making aspects of the Project
clear so that they can understand how the project will impact them. Thus, the engagement requires
their participation in project related discussions so that they are aware of all inputs to the overall
process equal to those of the stakeholders.
Within the Project, the vulnerable or disadvantaged groups take into consideration and are not limited
to the following:
    •   Women
    •   Elderly
    •   Youth
    •   Female-headed households
    •   Illiterate people
    •   People with disabilities (PWDs)
    •   Vulnerable groups working in the informal economy, who are mostly women.
Vulnerable groups within the communities affected by the Project will be further confirmed and
consulted through dedicated means, as appropriate. Specifically, there will be consultations that are
women-only as well as efforts to seek specific consultations with women the disabilities and those will
diverse if they are present in the communities.
2.2 Verification of Stakeholders Representatives
While the Project will seek to undertake broad and periodic consultation and information dissemination
with the Project stakeholders it will also utilize stakeholder representatives (e.g. Sui o le Nuu/Village
Mayor, Matai/village chief, village councils, village women’s councils, district council, and a youth
representative).
The Project will identify persons in the above listed stakeholder groups who can act as legitimate
representatives. Thereby, the individuals who have been entrusted by their fellow group members are
to uphold their group's interests throughout the process of engagement with the Project. This can be
provided by a community representative, who can provide helpful information about the local settings.
The community representative can also act as the main conduit for community consultation of the
Project as well as a primary communication between the Project and the targeted communities with
their already established networks and/or committees.
Verification of stakeholder representatives (i.e., the process of verifying that they are indeed legitimate
or and genuine advocates of their local communities) is considered an important task as these verified

persons will act as the focal point between the local communities and the Project team. Verification
process is undertaken by simply talking informally to a random sample of the community members and
heeding their views on who they might represent their views more effectively.
Specific stakeholder verification for all involved NGOs, agencies and/or government ministries will be
conducted prior to any engagement. These stakeholders are determined based on the Project scope of

the Project. The IAs will determine who these stakeholders are and invite a representative; whomever

Ministries.

3. Stakeholder Engagement Program
3.1 Purpose of Stakeholder Engagement
The Project’s stakeholder engagement program will be implemented over the Project life-cycle with the
following objectives:
    •   Providing timely and appropriate information during Project preparation, implementation and
        closure to enable informed participation and feedback;
    •   Disclosing information and obtaining stakeholder input on the expected Project benefits and any
        potential adverse impacts and proposed mitigation measures;
    •   Facilitating open and continuous communication and consultation between the Project's many
        stakeholders and communities;
    •   Encouraging equal participation of all affected groups in the consultation process; and
    •   Addressing stakeholder concerns or grievances in a timely manner.
3.2 Stakeholder Engagement Principles
Best practice approaches will include the Project applying the following set of principles of meaningful,
inclusive and timely stakeholder engagement:
    •   Transparency and life-cycle approach: public consultations are vital as it sets a platform for
        both the stakeholders and community to conversate openly on their respective views and
        opinions, free from any external manipulation, interference, coercion or intimidation. Primary
        consultations are ideal to be conducted initially before Project design phase to ensure
        community views are considered and portrayed within the design where and if applicable;
    •   Informed participation and feedback: Project information will be widely distributed amongst all
        involved stakeholders; opportunities are provided for communicating stakeholders’ feedback
        and thereafter will be analyzed and address any concerns and comments;
    •   Inclusiveness and sensitivity: stakeholder identification is undertaken to provide better
        communication support and build effective relationships. The participation process for the
        Project is inclusive. All involved stakeholders are encouraged to participate in the consultation
        process. Equal access to information is to be provided to all stakeholders. Sensitive
        stakeholders’ needs are considered the key underlying the selection of engagement methods.
        Special attention is given to the vulnerable group, in particular women, youth, elderly and
        cultural sensitivities.

3.4 Engagement during Project implementation

3.4.1 Methods, tools and techniques for engagement
Consultations will be further undertaken, including with council leaders, NGO/CSOs, community groups
and communities, as Project activities and locations are further defined during Project implementation.
Public and community meetings, workshops and site visits will be facilitated by the relevant divisions
within the IAs, with assistance provided by the MWTI-PMD.
The MWTI-PMD will ensure regular updates periodically with key Project updates and reports on the
Project's environmental and social performance. The information on feedback and grievance redress
mechanism (FGRM) will also be provided through the MWTI web page.
The following engagement tools may also be developed for the Project:
    •   Social media page(s)

3.4.2 Strategy for Engaging Vulnerable Group
The Project will ensure that awareness raising and stakeholder engagement with disadvantaged or
vulnerable individuals or groups take account of such groups or individuals’ particular sensitivities,
concerns and cultural sensitivities and ensures a full understanding of Project activities and benefits.
Vulnerability may stem from a person’s origin, gender, age, health condition, economic deficiency and
financial insecurity, disadvantaged status in the community (e.g. minorities or fringe groups),
dependence on other individuals or natural resources etc. Engagement with vulnerable groups and
individuals will require the application of specific measures and assistance designed to facilitate their
participation in Project-related decision making so that their awareness of, and input to, the overall
process is commensurate to those of the other stakeholders.
When engaging with women, youth, people living with disabilities and other vulnerable groups including
residents of remote and under-serviced areas, the Project will identify and employ appropriate
Information, Education and Communication (IEC) strategies to reduce barriers to participation and
ensure their views are heard and incorporated in activity planning and prioritization. Such arrangements
could include:

5. Feedback and Grievance Redress Mechanism
The Feedback and Grievance Redress Mechanism (FGRM) is a platform created under the projects to
receive and address complaints issued by affected persons or community members who have been
impacted by any specific Project activities whether it is social or environmental related.
PREPARE’s FGRM has been established in accordance with the World Bank requirement and acts as a
transparent mechanism that aims to record, manage and resolve any complaints, concerns and
grievances in a timely, effective and efficient manner and ensure that a satisfactory resolution is
reported back to the complainant. Furthermore, the FGRM also builds trust and cooperation as an
integral component of broader community consultation that facilitates correct action.
Overall, the benefits of having a FGRM includes:
    •   Provision of avenues to the affected people for making a complaint or resolving any dispute that
        may arise throughout the Project life;
    •   Ensures that suitable and mutually acceptable redress actions are identified;
    •   Identify and address potential negative impacts, unanticipated issues;
    •   implemented to the satisfaction of complainant; and
    •   Avoid the need to resort to judicial proceedings.


5.1 Description of FGRM
The MNRE and MWTI as the implementing agencies will be the complaints administrators in charge of
managing and monitoring the FGRM for each of their respective components. The focal points for

MWTI-PMD is responsible for providing coordination support amongst relevant ministries to ensure

The IAs will liaise with APs whenever necessary regarding the issues/requests and ensure they are

grievances or requests to be recorded. The contractors will then communicate the issue to the
supervision team/consultant who will thereafter relay the matter to the IAs. This process is taken in the
case that a contractor is the contact point or receiver of the complaint.


5.2 FGRM Procedure
The 30 Day – FGRM Process includes the following steps:
1. Issue raised and received - Submission/Lodging of Complaint. (2 Business Days)
The issue is raised by an affected person or the affected community member. Submission can be done
orally, in writing, through social media or in person. The issue is then registered on the standard form by
the receiver.
2. Issued Processed (5 Business Days)
Recipient (either contractor or IA ) records the complaints in the FGRM spreadsheet for record keeping.
Responsible party is then informed and assigned the responsibility and issuant of the acknowledge letter
to the complainant.
3. Issue is assessed (10 Business Days)
The recorded issue then under goes the grievance assessment process. This step requires the contractor
and/or the IA to address and resolve the issue that has been lodged within the allocated time. The
committee is able to intervene should the issue be severe, serious or one that may cause any delayed.
4. Complainant Resolution Process (10 Business Days)
Once the issue is meet with a resolution, the grievance data base is then required to be updated.
5. Complainant Notified (3 Business Days)
After appropriate actions are executed by the contractor and/or the IA, the complainant is then
informed of the proposed resolution including actions taken or actions that will be undertaken. The
grievance register is updated regarding any progress made.
Escalation
Complainants can request the grievance be escalated if they are not satisfied with the process or the
proposed resolution. This would involve escalation from contractor to IA or from IA to committee.

                                                                                                    Page | 23
                             PREPARE (P507749) – Stakeholder Engagement Plan | June 2025




Legal Recourse
This course of action is not considered part of the 30 days process given it is solely reliant on the
complainant if they are displeased with the outcome or resolution. In such cases, the complainant is
informed that a legal avenue can be taken to obtain a more satisfactory outcome and resolution.
The legal recourse will allow the complaints to seek and lodge a formal complaint to the Ministry of
Police or through a legal representative within the country. The Ministry of Police or the legal
representative will represent the complaints throughout the entire legal process under the local
judiciary system. However, the Safeguards committee along with the IAs will work assiduously to
resolve these issues to prevent matters from escalating to such a level.

Village members from the Fa’asaleleaga district were invited for a one-day consultation on the

   •   Communities recognized that there are growing weather, ocean, geological and climatic changes

Consultation Summary Salelologa Township Plan, Apia, 2 August 2024

Members of the Government of Samoa were invited for a half-day consultation on the development of
the proposed Salelologa Township Plan for Salafai/Savai’i. The consultation was spearheaded by MWTI
with the support of government officials from Upolu and Savai’i, and the World Bank team.

Theme 1: Hazards and Urban Planning

The following main points capture the results of the discussions:

    -   It is important that current plans and ideas are incorporated well into the development of the
        township. This is for example the case for the expansion of the National University of Samoa, for
        which a plot of land has been allocated. There are ample opportunities to incorporate Building
        with Nature, green-grey measures, and other climate resilient measures into the development of
        the new campus, contributing to the overall vision of the proposed township.
    -   It was noted that there are several opportunities to re-develop tourism in the area. These include
        infrastructure development such as water/riverfronts, coastal walks, etc. However, a limiting
        factor could be land ownership.
    -   There is a growing need to raise awareness of risk(s) and their perception(s) to specifically
        inhabitants that are residing on non-government owned land. This will enable people to make
        informed (building) decisions, regardless of land ownership. Examples include building houses on
        higher land, utilizing gravitational drainage, etc.
    -   When considering the township, it is critical to consider the broader context of Sālelologa and
        the surrounding region of Savai’i; the township will not exist in a vacuum, but will rely on upgrades
        to regional infrastructure (water supply, wastewater, emergency services, port facilities, etc.)
        and attention to the local commercial economy.
    -   It was noted that the southeastern half of the township site (bordering the sea) had been subject
        to a previous conservation agreement establishing a nature preserve in that area. Participants
        supported the maintenance of natural buffers where possible.
    -   King-tide flooding into the roadway was reported at several locations along the coast, including
        the corridor between the Wharf and the new township area.

Theme 2: Institutions and Governance

The following main points capture the results of the discussions:

    -   From the institutional frameworks discussion, the Mission identified that there are several
        policies for which different ministries have overlapping mandates, and they have different
        regulations which can be confusing. One example is with setbacks from roads and another is with
        minimum building floor level for constructions within flood prone areas.. MNRE has one set of
        guidelines while PUMA has another. The contradictory regulations need to be identified and
        harmonized so that people are not confused. Given that so much development is ad hoc also
        complicates things and makes it hard to enforce policies in urban areas. The Sustainable
        Management Plan (SMP), which has only been done once previously, in Vaitele, is a way to create
        a new planning framework for an area such as Sālelologa. While Community Integrated
        Management Plans are useful because they identify a list of important actions, the Plan doesn't
        prioritize them, nor does it oblige the government or the community to implement them, and so
        it cannot guide a staged process of urban development. The SMP, however, is a document that

                                                                                                    Page | 35
                      PREPARE (P507749) – Stakeholder Engagement Plan | June 2025


    can be more prescriptive, contains design standards and guidelines, provides provisions for
    zoning (such as mandatory set-backs) and should be used in Sālelologa. The implementation of
    a SMP on government would be straightforward as there is no risk of conflict with customary land
    owners.
-   In order to a) respond to local culture (observe local tradition and voices), and b) obtain buy-in
    for an urban development plan the use of by-laws is very important. There are already a few by-
    laws in place in Sālelologa (regarding waste collection and preventing cutting down trees) but
    further by-laws can be drafted and passed locally. By-laws are respected by PUMA as they express
    the wishes of the village or local community, they offer a great opportunity to create plans and
    regulations with local participation. The Ministry of Women, Community and Social Development
    oversees the local by-laws, and the village (local community) enforces them. A process of
    community engagement to gather their ideas and discuss them for creating by-laws (or receive
    inputs and validation) will help support the effective enforcement of a plan given the importance
    of buy-in of local land owners and lease holders. Consultation will be critical to get people on
    board.
-   Design standards for the township should integrate the needs of disabled people, and consider
    those of marginalized groups. More awareness is needed in terms of addressing the needs of
    these groups.

Overview of stakeholder workshops in Apia and Salelologa 24-28 February 2025
The two workshops in Apia and Salelologa involved key stakeholders that involved members of
government from over 30 government agencies. These included the Ministry of Works, Transport and
Infrastructure (MWTI), Ministry of Finance (MOF), Samoa Water Authority (SWA), Electric Power
Corporation (EPC), Ministry of Natural Resources and Environment (MNRE), Samoa Fire and Emergency
Services Agency (SFESA), Land Transport Authority (LTA) and Ministry of Lands and Survey (MLS) among
others. Key elements of the draft report were reproduced as the primary workshop materials and
participants were organized into three groups (corresponding to the three prioritized recommendation
packages) to closely review the proposed measures, sub-components, and schematic concepts.

The primary objectives of these workshops included the following -

   •   Presentation and validation of key components of the technical analysis: This included sharing the
       results of the rapid and qualitative analysis of hazard risks, recommendations for resilient
       urbanisation, enabling policies and institutional arrangements, potential social, environmental
       and livelihood impacts, as well as operations and maintenance needs. Budget requirements and
       suggested implementation timelines were also shared.
   •   Identification of next steps: This included outlining clear and actionable next steps to support
       near- and long-term implementation of the identified resilient investment options.

Some of the key themes noted during the conversations at the workshops included the following -

   •   There is a need for improved clarity and transparency in land ownership and associated rights
       within the Township. This is particularly important with regard to maintaining the rights of
       customary landowners in accordance with national law.
   •   Improved inter-agency coordination to ensure the development of a well-balanced Township
       Plan. This will prevent the duplication of efforts and facilitate the integrated development of
       resources and infrastructure for the people of Savai’i.
   •   Emergency services infrastructure should include facilities for pre-hospitalization and evacuation
       within Salelologa. This must include improving transportation and communication networks
       between Salelologa, the district hospital in Tuasivi, and Upolu.
   •   Plans to develop parks, open spaces and enhance overall walkability must include measures to
       enhance overall safety and accessibility, while mitigating the impacts of climate change (extreme
       heat and flooding).
   •   It will be important to coordinate between statutory and customary governance frameworks in
       all stages of planning, implementation, operations and maintenance of all resilient investments.
       The insight and support of the village councils and matais will be critical to ensure the long-term
       success of any resilient investment in the area.

Detailed Feedback from participants.

suggested that urban planning measures be implemented to encourage the development of the local

walking in the car-dependent town.

proposed park location is within land that is owned by the government. Anecdotal references were made

4. Location of the stormwater outfall in the context of the larger township catchment area: Participants
from LTA and MNRE highlighted that the proposed bioswale is located in what is likely the only feasible
outfall location for the entire New Township catchment area. As such, broader considerations must be
made to ensure that the water discharged at the outlet does not harm the local marine ecology. This
should include integrating water filtration and treatment measures for stormwater (which often mixes
with urban runoff) and addressing the current gaps in solid waste management. Participants also
suggested integrating provisions to increase water retention in upland developments in the Township
Plan and building codes. This would reduce the anticipated volume of urban runoff at the outfall and
mitigate the potential associated negative impacts.

5. On-street parking instead of bike lanes: Some participants expressed scepticism regarding the proposed
bike lane network, given that this mode of transportation is not widely used in the community or
considered viable given the extreme heat and humidity, but many participants in the Salelologa workshop
supported the idea. On-street parking was suggested as a more useful alternative in the current
development context. In the long-term, this space can easily be repurposed into bike/bus lanes should
the demand for low-carbon public transportation increase.

6. Operations and maintenance: Participants expressed the need for a focused strategy to finance and
develop the capacity for operations and maintenance work, especially lessons learned from the Apia
waterfront play space facilities following the handover. Representatives of LTA highlighted that resources
are limited in relation to the maintenance of street lighting, traffic signals, signage, and planting along
roadways. Representatives from MWCSD and PUMA suggested the possibility of integrating community
labour in operation and maintenance works.

Consulting the local village councils and matais will be critical for the implementation and monitoring of
such a program.

 7. Inter-agency coordination and realistic implementation timelines: Participants stressed the need to
integrate lessons learned from the coordination and implementation processes associated with similar
initiatives in the recent past.

Referencing the work done at the Apia waterfront park, participants emphasised the need to take into
account longer procurement periods due to existing institutional processes and political frameworks, as
well as building in contingencies for emergency halting of works due to climate-based hazards (high
velocity winds, tropical storms, storm surge, earthquakes and tsunamis) or community-wide health
concerns (like COVID-19).
