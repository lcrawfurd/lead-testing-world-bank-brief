 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

Introduction

1.      Mali faces multifaceted problems that are delaying its economic development including water insecurity,
aggravated by climate change, natural resource degradation, fragility, and rapid population growth. The country is highly
vulnerable to climate change impacts, such as increasing severity and incidence of floods and droughts, that decrease food
security and limit agricultural production, upon which most of the population depends. Poor water resource management,
aggravated by limited development of these water resources, further impact already fragile livelihoods, worsening
economic and human capital outcomes. Poor coordination among institutions responsible for management and use of
natural resources, including water, increases the country’s vulnerability to climate change impacts.

2.       Well-coordinated, integrated water-related interventions are needed to increase resilience to climate
variability, reduce fragility, and strengthen the foundations of socioeconomic development in Mali. The proposed
project is designed to enhance climate resilience by increasing water security in selected cities and their corresponding
sub-basins. The main goal of the project is to enhance resilience to climate change–exacerbated drought risk by helping
the government increase sustainable access to drinking water services in an extension of the Kabala program1 in Bamako
and secondary centers while securing water resources in the corresponding sub-basins through activities implemented by
local community organizations. Through harmonization across the World Bank portfolio and with other partners, a
coordinated approach to water security will encompass water resource management, ecosystem preservation, irrigation,
optimization of rain-fed agriculture, flood management, and access to properly managed sanitation.

 A. Project Strategic Context

3.       Mali faces internal and external shocks that hamper progress toward inclusive, sustainable development. Its
Human Development Index is low, because of the weakness of its education, health care, and social protection systems.
Climate change, rapid demographic growth, and insecurity have increased its vulnerability and fragility. A vast landlocked
country of 22.4 million people, Mali has an average annual population growth of 3.3 percent (2009-2022) and a median
population age of 21.42 Most of the population lives in the south, close to the Senegal and Niger rivers, with 57 percent in
rural areas and 12 percent in the capital city of Bamako. The latest estimates for 2023 suggest that Mali’s gross domestic
product (GDP) per capita is US$897 (current prices). Forty-five percent of the Malian population was living under the national
poverty line in 2021/22, concentrated in rural areas.3 Access to basic services and infrastructure is limited and highly unequal,
with extremely marked regional disparities. In 2021/22, poverty rates were highest in Sikasso (57.1 percent), where
households depend on cotton farming, and high in Ségou (53.2 percent) and Mopti (57.5 percent), where families mostly
depend on rainfed sorghum and millet cultivation. In Kayes, where households depend on remittances and livestock, poverty
is lower (41.4 percent), and the lowest poverty rates are in Bamako (15.8 percent) and Kidal (4.9 percent).4 Mali ranks 153
out of 166 countries on the Gender Inequality Index, reflecting significant challenges in reproductive health, empowerment,
and economic status of women.5

4.      Mali is among the world’s most vulnerable countries to climate change—ranked 180 out of 187 countries in 2022
on the Notre Dame Global Adaptation Initiative Country Index,6 and there has been little structural change in the economy
over the last three decades to increase resilience to climate shocks. Most climate scenarios project that temperatures in
the Sahel will be at least 2°C higher in 2040 than in 2021, and it is projected that rainfall patterns will become more
irregular, with oscillations between very wet and very dry years. Droughts and floods are likely to become more extreme.7
More severe droughts will decrease food security in economy reliant on agriculture and put water utilities under stress as
water sources dry up. More-extreme floods are likely to damage critical infrastructure, including agricultural and urban
water supply networks. Mali has already experienced the consequences of climate change, with major floods in the
Senegal and Niger rivers in 2020 affecting 80,000 individuals, 6,000 houses, and 7,000 tons of food.8 In at least one project
area, the San district, monthly precipitation decreased significantly between 1989 and 2019.9 In 2024, severe flooding
across the country caused 30 fatalities, affected 47,374 people, and led the government to declare a state of national
disaster in August.

5.       Such climatic shocks hamper efforts to reduce poverty by limiting economic growth, especially in the
agricultural sector, compounding political instability and insecurity. Agriculture and low-productivity services dominate
the economic and employment landscape, whereas manufacturing is limited and concentrated in agro-industries and
cotton ginning. Gold and cotton dominate exports, exposing the economy to commodity-price and climatic shocks. Two-
thirds of the country is arid and semiarid and exposed to desertification, and overexploitation and degradation of natural
resources has increased vulnerability to climate change. With only 14 percent of land suitable for agriculture, landscape
restoration to boost drought resilience is crucial. It is projected that, unless urgent investments in climate adaptation are
made, Mali’s annual GDP will have shrunk by roughly 10.7 percent by 2050, with 2.2 million additional people in poverty,
mainly because of shocks related to climate change.10

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

Water resource challenges

6.      Mali receives an estimated 415 billion m3 of precipitation per year, with renewable water from the Niger,
Senegal, and Volta River basins accounting for about 71.4 billion m³ per year.11 Four international river basins shape the
country's hydrology: the Niger, Senegal, Volta, and Comoé-Bia-Tanoé. Mali also contains two major transboundary aquifer
systems: the Taoudeni Basin (or Taoudeni-Tanezrouft Aquifer System) and the Iullemeden Aquifer System. Temporal and
geographic water variability, exacerbated by changing rainfall patterns, contributes to economic water scarcity. With
annual renewable water resources of 6,290 m³ per capita, Mali's agricultural water consumption (4.23% of renewable
water) does not exceed sustainable water resources at the national level. However, extreme variations in water availability
between regions and seasons, exacerbated by climate change and inadequate management, lead to glaring inequalities in
access to water, exacerbating water insecurity. Insecurity has restricted movement, disrupted economic activities and
limited access to fields and pastures, leading to the abandonment of arable land.

7.      Climate change and human activities are also degrading the quality of surface waters, particularly through
increased runoff and sediment transport.. Changes in rainfall patterns, gold mining in riverbeds, deforestation, soil
erosion, and pollution from point and non-point sources are degrading raw water quality, significantly increasing water
treatment costs in cities such as Bamako.12 Lack of knowledge about and limited monitoring of the country’s water
resources hinder sound decision-making regarding sustainable, climate-resilient water investments.

Water supply and sanitation

8.        Although access to drinking water continues to increase, overall service quality remains poor. Eighty-three
percent of the population has access to water (94 percent in urban areas, 74 percent in rural areas), but only 62 percent
of services qualify as improved (JMP 2022)13. Because of years of climate shocks, compounded by poor water service
management (e.g., underinvestment, failure to anticipate high population growth, poor service provider performance in
rural areas), water supply services fail to deliver an adequate amount of good-quality water. In addition, insecurity makes
it difficult to develop basic services such as water and electricity. Water supply is the responsibility of the Ministry of
Energy and Water (Ministere de l’Energie et de l’Eau-MEE), which delegates urban asset management to the Malian
Potable Water asset holding Company (Société Malienne de Patrimoine de l'Eau Potable, SOMAPEP-SA) and the Malian
Drinking Water Management Company (Société Malienne de Gestion de l’Eau Potable, SOMAGEP-SA), whereas rural water
is the responsibility of the National Directorate of Hydraulics (Direction Nationale de l’Hydraulique, DNH) and
municipalities. The urban water perimeter covers 90 centers but lacks the resources to upgrade their systems. With 65
percent water coverage,14 several neighborhoods in Bamako experience water shortages that an increase in demand and
drought, heightened by climate change, could exacerbate. Shortages due to low pressure and limited water network are
acute in the oldest neighborhoods. To address these challenges, the government launched the Kabala program in 2013
with seven donors to increase water production and expand services in unserved neighborhoods of Bamako, but
incomplete funding limits service delivery.

9.       People who have been internally displaced because of climate shocks and insecurity add pressure to already-
inadequate water and sanitation services. Mali has approximately 400,000 internally displaced persons, with many residing
in project areas such as Mopti. Approximately 28 percent of the population in these areas lacks access to basic water and
sanitation services, underscoring a critical need. The needs of displaced people and the pressure on existing facilities, are
not being met by the government's response, which may not be adequate.

10.      The financial performance of the water sector is weak. Expansion of the urban sector zone in 2018 to include 72
additional secondary centers with low coverage (31 percent, compared with 64 percent in the original 18 centers),15 has
hindered the country’s ability to meet the Sustainable Development Goals and to charge adequate tariffs for services, 16
exacerbating the sector’s financial situation. Mali's water tariffs, which have remained unchanged since 2004, are among
the lowest in West Africa, are insufficient to cover operating expenses, and do not allow for necessary investments in
climate resilience. In 2020, a World Bank-funded study on the financial viability17 of the sector recommended debt relief
in the short term to stabilize the sector, and the introduction of structural policies in the medium and long term, such as
(i) socially acceptable tariff adjustments; (ii) subsidy compensation; and (iii) payment of the reform costs promised to
SOMAPEP-SA when the assets of the former Eau et Energie du Mali were separated.. SOMAPEP's precarious financial
situation and level of debt is a major constraint on increasing urban service coverage and improving performance.

11.      Non-revenue water (NRW) and energy costs pose significant challenge to the water sector’s financial
sustainability and contribute to increased greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions.. With current network efficiency at less than
62 percent, which is considered very poor compared to the efficient range of 80 to 85 percent, there is a need for more
water abstraction, which in turn further exacerbates the drought crisis. The financial model developed to monitor the
sector’s financial viability identified tariff adjustment as a way to increase water usage efficiency and enhance the financial
sustainability of the water utility sector but these measures have not been implemented by the government. According
to SOMAGEP-SA, the main reasons behind the increase in NRW between 2018 to 2024 were (i) the commissioning of the
new Bamako (Kabala) Water Treatment Plant, which puts pressure on the older network, causing numerous water leaks;
(ii) water production that generates no revenue in towns experiencing insecurity (e.g., Kidal); and (iii) poor network quality
in the 72 secondary centers added to the urban perimeter in 2018. SOMAGEP-SA has a dedicated NRW department with
staff who know the network well and are familiar with strategies for detecting leaks, metering, and establishing district
metering areas. The project will support the reduction of NRW through a program of network rehabilitation, sectorization,
and installation of production and distribution meters.

12.     Only 20 percent of households nationally have access to safely managed sanitation services. 18 Access to
sanitation has progressed at a much slower pace than access to water and is lower than in many countries in the region.
Through the Bamako Urban Resilience Project (PRUBA, P171658),19 the World Bank is developing two major fecal sludge
treatment plants, 800 sanitary blocs, and 20,000 household latrines. Implementation of these investments will generate
lessons to define future sanitation sector engagement.

 II. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

13.      Given vulnerability to climate change and other water security challenges that Mali faces, an integrated approach
is crucial to sustain economic development and reduce fragility. The proposed project will enhance drought and flood
resilience by supporting sector development through a comprehensive water-management approach focused on
increasing access to drinking water as a continuation of the Kabala investment program in Bamako and selected cities
while protecting associated water resources at the sub-basin level. The project will support important climate-resilient
water investments for water supply, water source conservation, and operational and institutional measures to enhance
sector performance such as reducing NRW and priority reforms. The project will not only address immediate water access
and quality concerns, but also lay the groundwork for long-term resilience and contribute to water-related job growth and
economic activity. In supporting these activities, the project will help build a stable, sustainable future for the country;
reduce the risks of water-related conflicts; and support economic growth in vulnerable regions.

C. Project Beneficiaries

15.   The main project beneficiaries are households in the project area (Bamako, Bafoulabé/Mahina, Dioila,
Mopti/Sévaré, Ouélessébougou, San) and rural populations benefiting from ecosystems restored through nature-
based solutions (NBSs). It is estimated that at least 497,000 people will benefit directly from project investments,
approximately half of whom are women and 19.2 percent youth. Categories of beneficiaries include:

   •   227,000 people with access to at least basic water services
   •   270,000 people who will benefit from improved service quality
   •   30,000 people in rural areas near project implementation cities who will benefit from ecosystem restoration

D. Project Components

16.    The proposed project is structured around four components contributing to water security. Project locations
were selected using a rigorous process with key criteria such as maximizing the number of people to be provided with
access, as well as focusing on cities vulnerable to climate change (drought, floods), level of water service, readiness of
technical studies, and security situation. Most of selected activities have advanced detailed studies. For secondary
centers, alternative centers are identified to anticipate possible security challenges that could hinder implementation.
The project is described in detail in Annex 3.

17.     Component 1: Water supply in Bamako and selected secondary centers (US$87 million equivalent): This
component will ensure safe access to drinking water in the face of climate change–exacerbated droughts and floods, by
supporting development of efficient, well-performing drinking water services in Bamako and selected secondary towns.
The component will leverage technical studies already conducted with the support of the French Development Agency
(Agence Francaise de Developpement) in Bamako and secondary centers of Mopti, San, and Sévaré. Special attention
will be paid to the most vulnerable and underserved neighborhoods on the left bank of the Niger River in Bamako, which
are also most exposed to floods and drought. The component will consist of water supply systems in secondary centers
(US$47 million), a water supply system in Bamako (US$28 million), and management of NRW (US$12 million).

18.    Subcomponent 1.1: Water supply systems in secondary centers (US$47 million): This subcomponent will
construct water supply systems to enhance drought resilience by increasing access to water supply services in selected
secondary urban centers through the construction of water supply systems in three selected secondary centers: Mopti,
San, and Sévaré. The works to be carried out include construction of water supply systems in secondary urban centers
including (e.g., boreholes for production of 24,400 m3/day, tree pumping stations with mixed energy (solar/grid), four
2,000-m3 elevated water tanks and one 1,000-m3 ground-level water tank, water distribution networks, water
transmission pipes, social household connections, community standposts) and implementation of protection zones in
compliance with national legal requirements upstream of the water production boreholes in the targeted centers.

19.     Subcomponent 1.2: Water supply systems in Bamako (US$28 million): This subcomponent will increase drought
resilience by constructing and supervising water supply systems in Bamako, mainly a 9-km transfer from Djikoroni Para
to Missira, a pumping station at Missira, a 6-km transfer from Missira to Kati-Sud, a 2,000 m3 elevated water tank, the
distribution network, and household connections.

 20.     Subcomponent 1.3: Management of non-revenue water (NRW (US$12 million): This subcomponent will enhance

 21.    Component 2: Enhancing conservation of water sources (US$8 million equivalent): Infrastructure investments in

 water from the Niger and Senegal rivers that is tapped for the selected secondary centers, including through dialogue and
 coordination with key stakeholders; (v) implement NBSs in collaboration with local communities to support soil and
 riverbank stabilization in selected areas; (vi) establish and implement immediate protection and close protection areas;
 and (vii) restore landscapes to preserve and enhance the natural recharge of identified aquifers and the application of
 NBSs to contribute to climate change mitigation.

 F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

 25.      Need for flexibility due to security challenges. Project implementation in Mali requires state presence and
 flexibility, for example through the possible change of intervention areas and in implementing activities through
 nongovernmental organizations or communities in the case of an increase in insecurity. The project will intervene in some
 areas affected by insecurity (Mopti and San) to indicate the presence of the state. The project also has flexibility to
 intervene in alternative secondary cities without restructuring if the security situation deteriorates in the selected areas.

 26.    Water supply services should be developed in response to demand. During implementation of previous project
 (PEMU), it was difficult to change the initially estimated number of social household connections in certain cities, even
 though demand for connections was less than planned. To avoid such situations, the planned household connections will
 not be earmarked for particular locations but will follow demand in the project area. It has also been noted that public
 standposts have been abandoned in favor of house connections since the networks were densified. Thus, the project will
 provide prepaid public standposts (public water points for which people pay ahead for the service through a vending
 machine) that can be relocated based on the needs of the area to avoid locking communities in as the network evolves.

 27.    The anchoring project management and implementation unit (PMIA) within SOMAPEP-SA guarantees
 sustainability and ownership, improves performance, and enables building on lessons learned. The project

losses, and network inefficiencies. These measures are intended to increase cost recovery and revenue for the utility,
ultimately narrowing the financial gap of the sector. The project team will continue policy dialogue with the government
to allow for development of alternative solutions to sustain the financial viability of the urban water sector.

IV. PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY

A. Technical, Economic and Financial Analysis (if applicable)

43. Technical Analysis. As an extension of the Kabala program in Bamako, the proposed project will use technologies
for water supply systems that are well established and have been used effectively in previous water supply projects. The
water investment program that the proposed project supports originates from a priority investment program that
SOMAPEP-SA designed with the assistance of SOMAGEP-SA to restore water services in critical areas of Bamako and
other targeted secondary centers and to maximize the impact of the facilities built under the earlier phases of the Kabala
program and those supported by a previous multi-donor Kabala program. The proposed rehabilitation and expansion of
water production facilities, feeder pipes, storage and pumping facilities, water distribution networks, and service
connections is designed to increase the quantity of water available to existing customers and the quality of service. The
social household connections and standpost programs will be conducted in parallel through expansion of secondary and
tertiary networks. The proposed project will help provide sector actors with the tools to create financial viability and
increase their capacity to conduct their missions. SOMAPEP-SA and SOMAGEP-SA have demonstrated their capacity to
implement these programs, including managing expansion projects in close coordination with identification of
connection beneficiaries. The government has conducted technical studies for project Subcomponents 1.1, 1.2, and 1.3
in Bamako, Mopti, San, and Sévaré with support from a donor. The World Bank team reviewed these reports, which
would expedite subsequent stages and accelerate implementation during the first year of the project. Because the
investments under Component 2 will be defined in the first year of project implementation, it is expected that the
implementation phase of these investments will start in the second and third years of the project.

limit the viability of water sources. The project will build flood and drought resilience for users by providing stable, safe
drinking water supply systems that are themselves resilient to the impacts of climate change. Climate-resilient
measures to be used to protect project investments include placing key infrastructure outside flood zones and areas
prone to landslides and using materials that can withstand extreme temperatures. Drought resilience will be ensured
by using diverse water sources, including surface water and groundwater. Investments in water infrastructure such as
tanks and water distribution networks will consider recommendations from the Resilient Water Infrastructure Design
Brief to ensure design that is resilient to drought and flood.

Economic and financial analysis

47. Project benefits. World Bank support will generate significant economic and financial benefits that will enhance
the quality of the drinking water service. This will have immediate effects on service continuity while guaranteeing
sufficient water for more than 500,000 subscribers. The intervention will generate several other benefits, the most
important of which are reduction in water rationing, additional water volume sales resulting from greater water
availability thanks to reduction in technical losses, consumer surpluses due to the alternative reduction in the price of
water due to network extension and social household connection, reduction in water fetching time for women and
girls, and operations and maintenance savings. In addition, improvements in conservation of water sources will involve
30,000 beneficiaries in reforestation and riverbank stabilization and restoration activities.

48. Rationale for public sector financing. In the most recent Country Opinion Survey in Mali (conducted in June/July
2023), stakeholders surveyed identified water supply and sanitation infrastructure a high priority for World Bank Group
support to Mali. The public sector's contribution to ensuring development of the drinking water sector is crucial for
several reasons: rapid urbanization is exacerbating the existing structural deficit observed in the supply of drinking
water services, which justifies continued public financial support for the sector's development; SOMAPEP-SA’s
revenues are insufficient to sustain a viable self-financing policy of water infrastructure; private sector involvement in
financing drinking water infrastructure is limited, as the involvement of several traditional donors in the Kabala
program (including World Bank, European Investment Bank, French Development Agency, African Development Bank)
underscores; and development of the informal sector is unregulated, leading to a resurgence in water security
problems. In light of the above-mentioned development challenges, a strong commitment from and involvement of
the public sector and related agencies can increase access to water infrastructure and services. This project is designed
to build on previous and ongoing interventions and help the government provide sustainable water services and
thereby improve living conditions.

49. Added value of World Bank support. The significant increase in capacity, technical expertise, coordination and
use of global knowledge required to provide adequate water infrastructure and services in Bamako and secondary
centers justifies the World Bank's support. In addition to building on key achievements and lessons learned from
previous and ongoing projects, the World Bank's added value will include direct technical expertise and implementation
support from a multidisciplinary team combining expertise from various relevant sectors. Given Mali's significant water
infrastructure deficit and the challenge of achieving the Sustainable Development Goals, World Bank support is a key
element of its strategic and programmatic approach to supporting sustainable development.

61. Gender. There are significant gender gaps in terms of efforts to gain access to water services and decision-making
power in households and communities. Women, especially in rural areas, are responsible for water collection and
spend up to eight hours per day on household chores, including up to one hour collecting water. This reduces their
time for income-generating activities and education, perpetuating poverty and gender inequality. Women also have
limited decision-making power over design and location of water infrastructure, which often exacerbates their
disproportionate burden in water collection and increases safety risks, including exposure to gender-based violence
when traveling to distant or unsafe water points. To address these challenges, the project will implement two key
actions. First, women’s input will be actively incorporated into planning and design of water infrastructure, particularly
in selection of locations for household connections and standposts. This will be achieved through structured
consultations with women’s groups, ensuring their participation in decisions regarding water infrastructure placement
to enhance accessibility and safety. Designing infrastructure with women’s input will help meet their needs, reducing
the distance they must travel and enhancing their security. Second, the project will prioritize construction of water
points closer to homes, particularly in areas where women are typically responsible for water collection. By decreasing
the distance to water sources, the project will reduce the time women spend collecting water, freeing up their time
for income-generating activities, education, or other productive uses and increasing their safety by minimizing the
risks associated with traveling to distant water points. To monitor progress, the following indicators will be used:
percentage of water infrastructure (e.g., household connections and standposts) designed in consultation with
women and reduction in average time women spend in water collection, with a target of 30 percent for both. These
actions are expected to increase women’s participation in water management, reduce their labor burden, and increase
gender equality.

V. KEY RISKS

  64. The overall risk of the project is rated Substantial because the political and governance risks are High, and the
  macroeconomic, sector strategy and policy, fiduciary, E&S, and stakeholder risks are Substantial. Technical design of
  project or program, institutional capacity for implementation, and sustainability risks are rated Moderate.

  65. The political and governance risk is rated High. Since the August 2020 and May 2021 events in Mali, the political
  landscape has remained unstable. The decentralization process has progressed inconsistently, and the project is
  designed to mitigate associated risks by encouraging implementing agencies (SOMAPEP-SA, SOMAGEP-SA, DNH) to
  engage in inclusive dialogue with regional and subregional entities and beneficiaries, including vulnerable groups, to
  ensure smooth implementation of project activities. Addressing political and governance risks through project
  measures presents significant challenges, which justifies maintaining the residual risk level as High. The unpredictable
  security situation in the Sahel may divert government attention from development programs, which may affect
  prioritization of water projects. The project is designed to be flexible, enabling it to adapt to uncertainty and volatility
  during implementation, and it aligns with the Prevention and Resilience Allocation strategy. A Security Management
  Plan has been prepared for the project.

   66. The macroeconomic risk is rated Substantial. The country is facing a challenging macroeconomic environment
   and outlook because of high security-related spending, supply and demand disruptions, and its exit from the Economic
   Community of West African States (ECOWAS) in July 2024. The pressures that weak budgetary management and the
   reduction in agricultural (e.g., in Ségou) and mining production linked to the risk of insecurity exert aggravate
   macroeconomic instability. The economic outlook is subject to significant downside risks related to the security crisis,
   the electricity supply crisis, and the withdrawal from ECOWAS. In addition, the tightening of financing conditions in
   the subregional market increases the cost of financing on the regional market given Mali’s high gross financing needs,
   and the long-standing risk of further climatic shocks remains. These challenges present immediate risks to the project
   with regard to funding compensation for project-affected people. The government has requested (ref: L/N
   Cds/fbd/586-24 du 21/11/2024) an exception to be able to fund compensation from IDA as a mitigation measure. A
   memo was accordingly prepared and approved by the RVP on December 26, 2024)

   67. The sector strategy and policy risk is rated Substantial. The project is aligned with key sector strategies and
   policies for water resource management and water supply, but continued delay of the measures that the World Bank
   has recommended to improve water supply sector financial equilibrium could jeopardize SOMAPEP-SA’s and
   SOMAGEP-SA’s ability to take on more projects and realize the objectives of the project. Recent and ongoing dialogue
   with the Ministry of Energy and Water indicated willingness to implement the tariff study recommendations. The NRW
   reduction and use of solar energy will contribute to reduce operation cost, increase the sector revenue and therefore
   improve the financial viability of the utilities.

   68. The fiduciary risk is rated Substantial despite the experience that SOMAPEP-SA gained in Bank operations with
   PEMU (Kabala) and the ongoing PRUBA. The project design, with multiple agencies, combined with the current country
   context, presents some challenges from a fiduciary standpoint. Proposed mitigation measures include the renewal of
   the previous PEMU project team located within SOMAPEP-SA, which already has a procurement function run by a
   tried and tested unit with experienced fiduciary and safeguards experts familiar with World Bank procedures (financial
   management, procurement, internal and external audit, safeguards, M&E), and draft of the PIM, including financial,
   administrative, accounting, technical, and safeguards procedures.

   69. The E&S risks are rated Substantial. The activities proposed under Components 1 and 2 may entail risks to the
   health and safety of local populations in densely populated areas of several districts of Bamako and some secondary
   towns, risks of land acquisition and loss of private property, and businesses, and risks of disruption of social activities
   such as movement of pedestrians and cars and parking. Mitigation measures are included in the ESIAs/RAPs prepared
   for Mopti/Sevare and San.

    procurement procedures defined in the legal documents. The sampling is risk based and considers the project

    plan.

ANNEX 3: DETAILED PROJECT DESCRIPTION

46. The proposed project is structured around four components, based on identification of investments, most of which
have benefited from detailed studies. The project followed a rigorous selection process with key criteria such as maximum
number of people to be provided with access, cities vulnerable to climate change (drought/flood), level of water service,
readiness of technical studies for the centers, and security situation as appropriate. For secondary centers, alternative
centers are identified to anticipate possible security challenges that may hinder works implementation. The project
components are as follows:

47. Component 1: Water supply in Bamako and selected secondary centers (US$87 million equivalent): This component
will ensure safe access to drinking water in the face of climate change–exacerbated droughts and floods by supporting
development of efficient, well-performing drinking water services in Bamako and the selected secondary towns (Mopti,
San, Sévaré). The component will leverage technical studies29 already conducted with the support of the French
Development Agency in Bamako and the secondary centers. Special attention will be paid to the most vulnerable and
underserved neighborhoods on the left bank of Bamako, which are also most exposed to floods and droughts. Activities
under this component will be phased, initially targeting development of drinking water services in urban areas for which
technical studies are already available. In addition to infrastructure investments, technical assistance will be provided to
the Malian Water Assets Holding Company (Société Malienne de Patrimoine de l'Eau Potable, SOMAPEP-SA) and the
Malian Drinking Water Management Company (Société Malienne de Patrimoine de l'Eau Potable, SOMAGEP-SA) to
improve the overall performance of the sector, in particular its financial viability. These water supply systems will improve
the quality of water services by bringing a consistent, drought-resilient water source to water-scarce areas. The systems
will also enhance flood resilience by ensuring that installed infrastructure is sited outside flood zones and areas prone to
landslides and heatwave and wildfire resilience by using materials that can withstand extreme temperatures.24 Solar
power will be used wherever possible as a first option for treatment and pumping energy needs to reduce related
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and avoid carbon lock-in. When solar is not feasible to cover all energy needs, grid
connections will be prioritized over diesel generators or other fossil fuel sources, noting that the electric grid is subject to
interruptions. Component 1 will also lower the amount of non-revenue water (NRW), and facilitate the operation of water
systems, reducing energy consumption and thus GHG emissions and thereby increasing sector revenues. It will be
structured as follows: water supply systems in secondary centers (US$47 million), water supply system in Bamako (US$28
million), and management of NRW (US$12 million). 48. Subcomponent 1.1: Water supply systems in secondary centers
(US$47 million): This subcomponent will enhance drought resilience by constructing water supply systems in the selected
secondary centers to increase access to water supply services. Works include construction of boreholes for production of
24,400 m3/day, three pumping stations, four elevated 2000-m3 water tanks and one 1,000-m3 ground-level water tank,
the water distribution network, water transmission pipes, and social household connections and community standposts.
SOMAPEP-SA will implement this subcomponent, which will include implementation of protection zones, including
necessary legal constraints, upstream of the water production boreholes in the targeted centers, according to article 24
of the 2002 Water Act. If the security situation deteriorates, the project will assess the possibility of relocating to
alternative secondary cities.49. Subcomponent 1.2: Water supply systems in Bamako (US$28 million): This
subcomponent will construct and supervise works of water supply systems in Bamako, mainly a 9-km transfer from
Djikoroni Para to Missira, a pumping station at Missira, a 6-km transfer from Missira to Kati-Sud, a 2,000-m3 elevated water
tank, the distribution network, and household connections, to increase drought resilience by increasing access to water
services. SOMAPEP-SA will implement this subcomponent. Because the African Development Bank is supporting the
transfer of water from Baco Djicoroni to Missira, it will be important to ensure synergy of action with the African
Development Bank. This subcomponent will also include exploratory activities to anticipate the need for diversification of

50. Subcomponent 1.3: Management of NRW (US$12 million): This subcomponent will enhance drought resilience by
improving the quality of water supply services, reducing energy use through network loss reduction, improving metering
for households and large consumers (companies, administration), procuring equipment to ease leak detection, replacing
poor segments of the network, increasing capacity to map networks, and establishing district metering areas. The target
for NRW is 5 percent, which corresponds to more than 24,000 m3/day for Bamako alone. Establishment of district metering
areas will help manage water pressure to prevent contaminants, such as pathogens or chemicals that can enter through
cracks in the system, especially in areas with intermittent supply. Managing pressure reduces outbreak of waterborne
diseases by maintaining pipe integrity, minimizing leaks, and protecting water quality. This subcomponent will also help
National Directorate of Hydraulics (Direction Nationale de l’Hydraulique, DNH), SOMAGEP-SA, and SOMAPEP-SA to
improve their data-sharing system on water abstraction and water quality. This will increase detection of overabstraction,
flood-related turbidity, and other supply challenges, increasing drought and flood resilience. The subcomponent will also
help SOMAGEP-SA improve the operability of water supply systems and support climate change mitigation through NRW,
which will reduce GHG emissions and energy costs.

51. Activities under this subcomponent include network rehabilitation works to replace 100 km of poor segments in
Bamako and three secondary cities (Bafoulabé/Mahina, Dioila, Ouélessébougou); implementation of a district metering
area in Bamako for efficient metering and water supply network management; renewal of household and distribution
meters for Bamako, Bafoulabé/Mahina, Dioila, and Ouélessébougou; and installation of production meters in
Bafoulabé/Mahina, Dioila, and Ouélessébougou.

52. Bamako’s water supply system generates almost 60 percent of the utility’s revenues and has the least-efficient water
supply network because of the increase in production and water pressure that affect the old network on the left bank of
the Niger River. It is estimated that 38 to 40 percent of the water is lost. Bafoulabé/Mahina, Dioila, and Ouélessébougou
were transferred to the utility’s perimeter in 2018 without any support to update their systems to urban standards. The
planned actions will increase network efficiency and metering of the water produced and distributed to consumers.

53. Activities to be financed include technical assistance to SOMAPEP-SA and SOMAGEP-SA to enable the sector to achieve
financial viability and improve technical and commercial performance, including customer management over the entire
urban perimeter (Bamako and secondary centers). This technical assistance will build on the Utility of the Future tool that
the World Bank developed, which begins with an assessment of utility performance and service provision that yields a
100-day action plan for the service provider to implement. Discussions with SOMAPEP-SA and SOMAGEP-SA are ongoing
for development of these action plans. The project could then support implementation of this action plan and associated
measures through this subcomponent. This subcomponent will support SOMAGEP-SA in Bamako, Bafoulabé-Mahina,
Dioila, and Ouélessébougou.

54. Component 2: Enhancing conservation of water sources (US$8 million equivalent): The infrastructure investments
under Component 1 are critically interconnected with the upstream and downstream aspects of the water resources they
use, and the changing precipitation regime affects them greatly. For these water sources, this component will support
comprehensive water conservation activities to ensure the maintenance and enhancement of ecosystem services that
protect against flooding and erosion and allow for enhanced natural aquifer recharge (generating a drought-resilient water
supply). These efforts will build on the main institutional water resources management functions at the national and local


                                                                                                                     Page 39
          The World Bank
          Mali Water Security Project (P181538)


levels as outlined in the second phase of the National Program for Integrated Water Resources Management (2023–2026).
Key activities will include enhanced climate-resilient planning regarding current and future uses of these water sources
and supporting DNH and other institutions by supplying data and tools for secure water resource management. Focusing
on the cities targeted under Component 1, this component will increase DNH’s understanding of water resources and their
competitive uses and the durability of this resource in times of water scarcity induced by climate change. This includes
strengthening the monitoring network for water resource quantity and quality, including at raw water production sites, in
part to improve flood and drought early warning systems, and effectively communicating the results of this monitoring to
decision-makers and the public. A dialogue will be initiated to begin the journey toward establishing a financially
sustainable water resource monitoring solution. By emphasizing consolidation of knowledge and building consensus
around resource availability, the project aims to highlight opportunities linked to investments at the sub-basin scale,
encompassing groundwater and surface water. This approach will enable DNH to engage actively in dialogues regarding
international rivers and transboundary aquifers. The project will reinforce water allocation tools on the Niger River in
collaboration with the Niger Basin Authority, ensuring effective management and equitable distribution of these vital
resources in the face of greater water variability due to climate change.

55. This component will address questions of turbidity in Niger River water tapped for Bamako and Mopti and in Senegal
River water tapped for Bafoulabé and Mahina. A comprehensive evaluation of these basin-level dynamics will be
conducted during project implementation, involving thorough dialogue and coordination with key stakeholders,
particularly local water committees. To mitigate the impact of solid transport in the rivers, this component will implement
nature-based solutions (NBSs) to help stabilize soil in selected areas and riverbanks (which will also increase flood
resilience by reducing erosion potential), in close cooperation with the Mali Landscape Restoration Project (P177041).
Activities will be conducted in collaboration with local communes and small mining operators working within the
riverbeds.

56. For water abstraction facilities tapping groundwater resources in the Airport Zone, Kati, and Dogodouma for Bamako
and in San and Ouélessébougou, this component will establish and implement protection zones, which will include
establishing immediate protection areas within which the focus will be on minimizing risks of contamination of the water
well.25 Project activities will concentrate on measures to safeguard the physical integrity of wells and prevent pollutants
from contaminating the abstracted water. Larger than immediate protection areas, close protection areas are broader
areas where climate change, especially heavy downpours, threaten groundwater quality and consequently public health.26
In these areas, efforts will be directed toward minimizing risk of point or accidental contamination of groundwater. In
addition to the protection zones, this component will focus on preserving and enhancing natural recharge of aquifers
experiencing adverse impacts of changing rainfall patterns by restoring landscapes in the aquifer recharge areas and using
NBSs, such as reforestation, construction of terraces, and use of contour bunds, which will sequester carbon, helping
mitigate climate change. These targeted approaches will ensure sustainable management and protection of vital
groundwater resources, contributing to long-term drought-resilient water security for the communities that these
facilities serve.

and adherence to the World Bank’s activation policy, the contingency emergency response component would be
triggered.
