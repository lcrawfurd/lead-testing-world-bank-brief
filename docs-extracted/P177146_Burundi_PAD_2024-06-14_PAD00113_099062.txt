    I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT


    A. Country Context
      1. Burundi is a country in the process of improving the quality of life of its population. Burundi is one of the poorest
          and most densely populated countries in the world, with a population density of 442 people per square kilometer.1
          Burundi had an estimated per capita Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of US$267 in 2023.2 Almost 62.1 percent of its
          13.2 million people (as of 2023)3 live below the international poverty line of US$2.15/day, and 75 percent of the
          population live in multidimensional poverty (as of 2021/2022).4 Burundi ranks 187 out of 191 countries on the UN
          Human Development Index in the 2021/2022 report, descending two places since 2015. The revised National
          Development Plan 2018–2027 is part of the vision 2040 (emerging country) and 2060 (developed country) of the
          President of the Republic, with the objective of improving living standards of its population and propelling its
          socioeconomic development.
      2. The country has an undiversified economy heavily reliant on public services and agriculture. Economic growth is
          estimated at 2.7 percent in 2023, from 1.8 percent in 2021.5 Growth is projected to increase to 3.8 percent in 2024.
          Burundi’s main economic driver is services, contributing 50.5 percent of GDP growth in 2023, followed by agriculture
          at 31.7 percent.6 Bujumbura, the economic capital of Burundi, where 61 percent of the urban population lives, could
          play a key role supporting the economic drivers in the country. Agribusiness jobs are increasingly concentrated in
          urban areas. Consumer services account for the majority (47 percent) of employment in Bujumbura, and
          employment related to other agricultural value-addition activities are mainly found in peri-urban areas.7 However,
          inadequate infrastructure and services and poor resilience to shocks hinder the growth and job-generating potential
          of these economic activities.
      3. Burundi remains largely rural, with a 14 percent urban population; however, it is one of the countries with the
         fastest growing urban populations in the world. Burundi experienced a 5.7 percent growth in urban population
         between 2000 and 2019, and it is estimated the urban population will double by 2050.8 Approximately 58 percent
         of the urban population lives in unplanned areas, often under-resourced and without basic services. The adequate
         provision of basic services, conflicts over land ownership, exposure to disaster risks, and refugee return represent
         major challenges in urban areas, as more people move to cities in search of livelihood opportunities, housing, and
         access to services. Between 2010 and 2020, unplanned urban and built-up areas doubled in the country, increasing
         from about 5,000 ha (0.2 percent of the country’s total area) to about 10,000 ha (0.4 percent), with expanded built-
         up land area contributing to increased risks of flood and erosion. Urban access to basic sanitation is 41 percent, and
         less than 2 percent is connected to a sewer system. Around 40 percent of urban households fetch water more than
         30 minutes away from their home, impacting the lives of women and putting them at risk of Gender-Based Violence
         (GBV).
      4. Burundi is highly vulnerable to natural hazards and climate-related events, with large implications for people,
         infrastructure, and ecosystems. Burundi ranks as the 22nd most vulnerable country and the 20th least ready to

           terrain and extensive river systems, the country is prone to storms, torrential rains, regular landslides, and
           flooding, primarily due to the rise of Lake Tanganyika. Urban areas like Bujumbura, the largest city and former

           mostly due to the rise of Lake Tanganyika.10
       5. Climate risk is a threat multiplier, keeping communities across Burundi’s hills in a vicious cycle of fragility and
          vulnerability to poverty, land degradation risks, livelihood loss, and unemployment.11 The 2022 Risk and
          Resilience Assessment (RRA) identified unplanned, rapid urbanization, coupled with land scarcity in the context of
          demographic growth and increasing intensity of climate events, as the key drivers of fragility, conflict, and violence
          in Burundi. Limited opportunities for upward mobility in an undiversified economy, within unplanned urban areas
          which lack services and infrastructure, create a vicious cycle of segregation, especially among disenfranchised
          groups. The sustainability of livelihoods is being compromised by dwindling amounts of arable and productive land
          per capita in the context of demographic growth and increasing poverty, the increasing intensity of climate events,
          and influxes of returnees.
       6. Climate shocks and disasters exacerbate existing inequalities and have a disproportionate impact on women and
          girls.12 Women are often more affected by disasters due to a combination of limited access to information and lack
          of voice and agency, assets, financial protection, and emergency services. Women tend to be underrepresented in
          urban planning and disaster preparedness and emergency agencies. Women's participation in elective positions
          averages 31 percent, and representation in non-elective political and technical positions averages 30 percent.13 Not
          having women involved in decision-making may result in women’s needs not being met. Due to the lack of access
          to intangible assets among women (such as small business or income-generating activities), it is likely that a larger
          share of women’s assets is at risk of disaster impacts. If affected, women have lower access to coping mechanisms
          to respond to shocks compared to men. For example, there is very little access to agricultural credit for women
          heads of household, since they do not have the collateral security required for the granting of agricultural credit
          (land titles, livestock ownership, etc.).14

       Bank’s urgent support to address life threatening and flooding emergencies in Bujumbura. Given the scale, the
       complexity, and the absorptive capacity challenges in the country, the World Bank has proposed to provide support
       in an incremental manner. Specifically, this project will address (i) immediate emergency interventions under
       Subcomponent 1.1, aimed at responding to the current flooding and at mitigating impacts on people and critical
       infrastructure in Bujumbura; and (ii) medium- to long-term climate-resilient infrastructure interventions under
       Subcomponent 1.2.

 C. Sectoral and Institutional Context
    10.Bujumbura is the most populous urban area in the country, with an estimated population of about 1 million,
       concentrating 71 percent of the total urban growth in the country between 2014–2019. Bujumbura concentrates
       a large proportion of total urban employment across Burundi—accounting for 61 percent of jobs in industry, 70
       percent in services, and 74 percent in construction.15 Bujumbura hosts the greatest number of internally displaced
       people in the country. While official statistics from September 2020 point to about 30,000 displaced individuals in
       both Bujumbura City (Bujumbura Mairie) and the neighboring Bujumbura Rural, the numbers are likely to be
       higher.16

    11.Bujumbura is acutely affected by floods from rising lake levels, river overflows, and pluvial flooding worsened by
       climate change. The city is bounded by Lake Tanganyika, the largest and deepest of the Rift Valley lakes in the west
       and one of the shoulders of the hilly North Tanganyika-Kivu Rift in the east. Several streams and rivers, which drain
       into the lake, including the Ruzizi, contribute to the flooding in the city and surrounding areas such as Gatumba,
       which is particularly vulnerable when the lake level rises.17 Hydrological modeling carried out during project
       preparation—adjusted to simulate climate change impacts and considering demographic growth—forecasts an
       escalation in flood magnitudes in Bujumbura. Specifically, for frequent events (2- to 10-year return periods),
       increases in rainfall can lead to about a 10 to 20 percent increase in river flows; for larger, less frequent events (25-
       to 100-year return periods), river flows could see a 12 to 30 percent increase. Climate models predict an increasing

    35.The value added of the World Bank’s support comes in part from global operational experience in urban
       resilience, especially in Fragile and Conflict-affected Situations (FCS), and financing capacity. The World Bank is
       well-positioned to tailor approaches to Burundi based on comparable work in many other FCS in Africa and
       elsewhere that have addressed urban resilience in a context of weak institutions.40 Given the enormous
       development needs in the country, the World Bank is among the only financiers with the capacity to invest at the

 F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design
    38.Lessons from past and on-going World Bank-financed urban resilience projects on flood risk reduction have been
       integrated. Projects such as the Niger Integrated Urban Development and Multi-sectoral Resilience Project
       (P175857), the Dakar Stormwater Management and Climate Change Adaptation Project 2 (P175830) and Msimbazi
       Basin Development Project (P169425) have focused on targeted flood risk reduction combined with improving
       capacity for risk-informed urban planning. They have shown that targeted interventions combined with flexible

      52.The project’s technical design maximizes investment impacts in the prioritized catchment basins in Bujumbura
         by focusing on the flood-affected areas in February – April 2024 and “low-regret” measures. The approach was
         validated by a large section of beneficiaries. Technical recommendations for urban flood resilience were
         developed during project preparation through the detailed flood risk assessment and investment plan, based on
         exposure and vulnerability to floods in Bujumbura and neighboring Gatumba. The flood risk assessment modeled
         the flood risk drivers and average annual losses across a range of flood return periods and future climate
         scenarios. A baseline flood hazard analysis was completed to demonstrate the current and future projected flood
         risk considering existing defenses and mitigation measures. The risk model combined the probability and impact
         of flooding by intersecting the outputs from the flood hazard model with data representing exposure and
         vulnerability of the population, buildings, and critical infrastructure. Future scenarios were also modeled,
         representing different potential future climatic conditions, land-use and population scenarios and identifying
         low-regret measures, that offer high benefits across the range of future climate scenarios. Based on the assessed
         flood risk, the most feasible combination of investment approaches to minimize economic, physical, and human
         damages and losses due to climate change-exacerbated flooding, and by catchment were modeled and identified,
         for uptake within this project.
      53.The three catchment areas of Mutimbuzi, Ntahangwa and Ruzizi were selected and investments were
         prioritized based on three criteria: (i) urgency by addressing the flood risks of today, leading to reduction in the
         current impacts and the expected annual damage, the expected number of socially vulnerable households
         exposed to flooding and critical infrastructure exposed to a 1 in 100-year hazard; (ii) long-term outcomes, where
         investment efficiencies are based on benefit cost analyses through the appraisal period; and (iii) alignment, by
         promoting low-regret and social just investments that align with wider development plans. The investments
         selected present the most impactful combination of structural and non-structural measures, modeled against the
         existing flood hazard baseline, looking at results of each investment on flood risk reduction for a range of flood
         return period scenarios, lake-level rise scenarios, and hazard interdependencies by catchment. All construction
         and rehabilitation activities will ensure that the built infrastructure is resilient to future climate shocks, by
         applying build-back-better principles,45 and will use LIPW approaches when possible, to create local jobs within
         the benefitted communities.

      Economic Analysis
      55.An economic analysis carried out as part of project preparation indicates that the investments are economically
         viable. As the current parallel rate is twice as much as the official rate, a shadow exchange rate was used to factor
         in the exchange rate distortion. The project shows a Net Present Value (NPV) of US$75 million discounted at 6
         percent and an Economic Rate of Return (ERR) of 18 percent over 20 years. An adjusted shadow price was also
         considered where the rate used was based on the real price of construction materials, as this best reflects the
         benefits expressed in terms of avoided damages. When using the Burundi francs (BIF) adjusted shadow exchange
         rate, the economic analysis remains viable with an NPV of BIF137 billion with an ERR of 14 percent. The project
         will reap intangible and tangible benefits in terms of the reduction in asset damages, improved well-being, and
         forgone socioeconomic activity during future flood events. The project economic analysis considers the costs and
         benefits accruing from the EWS, build-back-better and DRM investments and LIPW over 20 years, assuming a
         return period of 10 years as well as O&M expenditures to sustain the assets.
      56.A sensitivity analysis was carried out that shows robust results. The project remains viable under a pessimistic
         scenario with an NPV of US$35 million and BIF 40 billion, respectively, and an ERR of 15 and 11 percent,
         respectively. Detailed analysis is available in the project files including results for the official, adjusted parallel,
         and parallel exchange rates used in the analysis.

   million that directly addresses Burundi’s vulnerabilities, particularly to climate change-exacerbated flooding and
   supports the country’s low-GHG emissions development pathways. Sixteen percent (US$18 million) will support the
   design and construction of immediate to medium-term emergency interventions to address the ongoing impacts of
   climate change-exacerbated 2024 floods and lakeshore erosion (Sub-component 1.1). Seventy-five percent (US$85
   million) will support the design and construction of low regret measures combining grey and green infrastructure
   to address the burgeoning impacts of climate change-induced flooding, as well as strategic studies for future urban
   services and flood risk management investment (Sub-components 1.2 and 1.3). These investments will be designed
   and constructed using climate-resilient design criteria that account for increases in rainfall intensities, peak flows
   and velocities as a result of climate change. Component 2 investments account for seven percent (US$7 million) of
   the total investment costs and aims to enhance the urban resilience of Bujumbura by supporting low-carbon,
   informed land management and building resilience of the low-regret flood infrastructure investments by supporting
   institutional capacity for operation and maintenance. This component also seeks to further build resilience in
   Bujumbura by supporting the upgrade of water infrastructure to maintain service continuity and improve system
   efficiency, towards reducing water loss and enhancing resource management (Sub-component 2.3). Additionally,
   through Sub-component 2.1 the project supports Bujumbura in building its adaptive capacity through investments
   and institutional strengthening to support an early warning and emergency response system. Emergency
   investments to protect critical infrastructure and the development of a drinking water supply master plan are
   pivotal in ensuring that water systems and supplies are resilient to climate variability and extreme weather.
   Safeguarding essential services and promoting sustainable resource management are pivotal in ensuring that water
   systems and supplies are resilient to climate variability and extreme weather, safeguarding essential services and
   promoting sustainable resource management (Sub-component 2.3).
Gender
70.The project will reduce identified gender disparities in terms of access to information and lack of voice in EWS
   and EP&R and will minimize the risk of adverse gender impacts due to project activities. Project activities will be
   designed to reduce the exclusion of women and youth in access to information and the lack of voice in emergency
   services, and ensure adequate gender-disaggregated indicators are included in the results framework to monitor
   and evaluate results. The project seeks to assure a gendered approach to community resilience, and women’s
   empowerment is a key factor to achieving it. The focus aims to reduce imbalances; promote transparency,
   accountability, and participation in decision-making, also building the capacity of public institutions and civil society
   organizations (CSOs); and promoting gender equity. The project will support increased involvement of women in
   disaster risk management committees by involving women as focal points. An indicator is included in the Results
   Framework to capture this expected result.

 V.     GRIEVANCE REDRESS SERVICES

       72.Grievance Redress. Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by a project
          supported by the World Bank may submit complaints to existing project-level grievance mechanisms or the
          Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed in
          order to address project-related concerns. Project-affected communities and individuals may submit their
          complaint to the Bank’s independent Accountability Mechanism (AM). The AM houses the Inspection Panel,
          which determines whether harm occurred, or could occur, as a result of Bank non-compliance with its policies
          and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution Service, which provides communities and borrowers with the
          opportunity to address complaints through dispute resolution. Complaints may be submitted to the AM at any
          time after concerns have been brought directly to the attention of Bank management and after management has
          been given an opportunity to respond. For information on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s Grievance
          Redress Service (GRS), visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to submit complaints to the
          Bank’s Accountability Mechanism, visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.

 VI. KEY RISKS

      73.The overall risk rating of the project is Substantial. The key risks and mitigation measures are discussed below.
      74.Political and Governance risk is Substantial. The government has a stated decentralization policy with milestones
         for the 2025 legislative, municipal, and general election. Implementing the decentralization agenda will be complex
         and require substantial resources. Local government institutions at the level of the Bujumbura urban area are
         expected to change, with the greater Bujumbura area consolidated into larger communes and without an
         overarching city-level administration (Mairie de Bujumbura). The project will mitigate these risks through its flexible
         design to respond to the different paces of change and the possible scenarios, and to ensure technical assistance
         to support central, provincial/city, and local governments to strengthen their mandate in climate-resilient urban
         management.
      75.Macroeconomic risk is High. While Burundi's public debt remains sustainable, it faces risks from external
         imbalances, such as large current account deficits, insufficient reserve buffers, and a substantial parallel exchange

76.Sector Strategies and Policies risk is rated Substantial. This risk is derived from the early stages of several
   institutional reforms, regulations, and tools critical for urban resilience, such as planning and management tools
   that reduce flood exposure, as well as the recent set-up of a national disaster risk management platform. These
   risks are mitigated through a strong focus on building the capacity of government institutions and the private sector
   in urban planning and flood risk assessment, the preparation of urban planning tools and policy instruments, and
   support to national platforms that promote resilience to climate change.
77.Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability risk is Substantial. The capacity to implement and
   coordinate project activities is limited as demonstrated by the track record to implement World Bank-financed
   projects on time and on budget. The project is therefore increasing the staffing of the PIU through competent locally
   recruited consultants. International TA for the PIU as well as national and local beneficiary institutions have been
   embedded under the implementation arrangements. Considerations for O&M are built into the project design,
   leveraging communities’ involvement in infrastructure maintenance and sustainability of EP R systems, and
   defining the mandates of local governments and sector institutions for the assets built under the project.
78.Fiduciary risk is Substantial. The fiduciary risk assessment is driven by the inherent fiduciary risk at the country
   level, the few large potential contracts under the project, and the FM and procurement capacity of the recently
   established PIU. The risk mitigation measures focus on the quality of the personnel recruited for the PIU as well as
   training and capacity development, funds flow mechanisms with oversight by beneficiaries, and the application of
   good practices in procurement, internal control, and audit. Technical assistance will be provided to mitigate risks,
   along with the other measures detailed in Annex 1.
79.Environmental and Social risk is Substantial. The risk rating is driven primarily by the likely physical or economic
   displacement of people in a relatively dense urban setting and the expected scope of civil works, as well as the weak
   capacity of the PIU. The selection and design of project investments will be based on a principle of minimizing
   negative social impacts, with local community consultation. The PIU will be strengthened through environment and
   social specialists, a GBV SEA/SH specialist as well as community development specialists, who will support the
   implementation of the ESF instruments, monitor risks and ensure mitigation actions. A sufficient budget is included
   to implement the agreed actions listed in the ESCP.

  2. Burundi is highly vulnerable to the impacts of natural hazards and climate change. Burundi ranks as the 19th most

  3. Floods followed by epidemics, storms, and droughts are the most prevalent natural hazard occurrences in Burundi.

  12. The project responds directly to the World Bank Climate Change Action Plan 2021 – 2025 and the World Bank
      Burundi Country Partnership Framework (FY19-FY24) concerns regarding vulnerability to the effects of climate
      change. The CPF emphasizes the need to step up support for climate mitigation, adaptation, and DRM, which are all
      key priorities of the project; and identifies climate change as a major constraint to addressing fragility.
  13. In the wake of the ongoing significant impact of the 2024 floods, the project aims to enhance emergency
      responsiveness and strengthen immediate resilience to climate change through targeted structural measures
      included in the emergency response interventions component (Sub-component 1.1). This sub-component is
      specifically tailored to address the impacts of ongoing climate change-exacerbated floods (pluvial, fluvial and lake),
      landslides and lakeshore erosion currently affecting Burundi. The introduction of an emergency response sub-
      component is essential for building resilience to climate-change exacerbated flooding and associated hazards in the
      immediate to medium term until the longer-term climate-resilient infrastructure (under Sub-component 1.2) is
      implemented. Under this sub-component emergency interventions include: (i) emergency erosion protection along
      Lake Tanganyika to protect critical water infrastructure; (ii) rehabilitation of river banks and construction of check
      dams to control flood waters, reduce flow velocities and reduce channel erosion; (iii) river and channel cleaning to
      increase capacities to carry peak flows; and (iv) pilot road paving to fortify existing road infrastructure against future
      flooding in order to ensure vital connectivity is maintained during extreme events.
  14. The project aims to build long-term resilience to climate change through investments in both structural and non-
      structural measures, to mitigate the impacts of natural disaster and climate-related risks from floods (pluvial, fluvial
      and lake), landslides and other erosion process in Bujumbura and Gatumba. Physical interventions under sub-
      component 1.2 include low-regret measures designed and constructed to primarily address the climate change-
      exacerbated flooding, inter alia: (i) upstream catchment and channel restoration to attenuate fluvial risk, reduce
      erosion and siltation of storage/drainage facilities; (ii) lakeshore restoration and protection along Lake Tanganyika to
      mitigate lake flooding and prevent erosion; (iii) river bank restoration and protection, with differentiated interventions
      considering the two main rivers in Bujumbura; (iv) integrated urban drainage in prioritized areas of the cities, including
      conveyance management of channels and bridges; (v) nature-based solutions (NBS), specifically swales and upstream
      check dams to support infiltration and manage pluvial flood risk, and (vi) protecting and rehabilitating at-risk critical
      infrastructure, such as water service infrastructure and roads/bridges in hotspot areas. These interventions were
      supported by the findings of an urban flood risk technical assistance that adopted climate-resilient design criteria for
      hydrologic and hydraulic design of the interventions66. Sub-component 1.3 will finance a flood management pre-
      feasibility study that will use a basin-wide approach to prioritize future investments for flood risk reduction in support
      of urban resilience and for urban water and sanitation in Bujumbura. Investments for future urban drainage and flood
      management infrastructure will be prioritized based on past flooding severity and locations offering the best return
      on investment in minimizing economic disruptions; and aims to minimize resettlement and adhere to build-back-
      better (BBB) principles.
