                          B. Introduction and Context
                          Country Context
                          1. South Africa (SA) has ambitious development goals, but slow growth and weak job creation continue to hold
                          back progress toward shared prosperity. Economic growth averaged just 0.7 percent over the past decade.
                          Unemployment exceeds 30 percent, with 46 percent of 15–34-year-olds unemployed. After earlier gains, poverty has
                          risen since 2011 and now affects 68 percent of the population at the $8.3 upper-middle-income poverty line.
                          Investment has averaged about 15 percent of GDP over the last decade—roughly half the upper-middle-income
                          country average—constraining growth and contributing to a widening infrastructure gap.

                          2. Deteriorating infrastructure is a central barrier to unlocking growth and jobs. Two reinforcing dynamics -
                          declining performance and declining investment - have pushed the electricity, freight logistics, and water sectors into
                          significant strain. Power shortages, rail and port inefficiencies, and unreliable water services raise firm costs, reduce
                          productivity, and undermine competitiveness, with disproportionate impacts on small firms and low-income
                          households that lack alternatives (e.g., rooftop solar, generators, reliable private transport). Climate change further
                          pressures already aging and overstretched systems.

                          3. SA has deep financial markets, with institutional investors holding assets equivalent to roughly 160 percent of
                          GDP, yet they do not efficiently allocate long-term finance to infrastructure without significant government

                                                                                                                                          Official Use Only

                          guarantees. Private participation remains limited, with many projects requiring 100 percent government guarantees.
                          Yet, infrastructure needs are vast—estimated at US$254–329 billion between 2022 and 2030. Given constrained fiscal
                          space, the public sector cannot meet these needs alone. Infrastructure-related guarantees already amount to almost 9
                          percent of GDP in 2024/25, underscoring the urgency of market-based de-risking that mobilizes private capital without
                          further increasing contingent liabilities.

                          4. Weaknesses in the enabling and regulatory environment - especially in SOE-dominated network sectors -
                          significantly constrain investment. Limited competition, operational inefficiencies, governance gaps, and financial
                          fragility among SOEs and municipalities undermine performance in electricity, transport and water. These shortcomings
                          are rooted in unclear sector policies, weak and fragmented regulatory frameworks, and inefficient institutional
                          processes. Poor creditworthiness and gaps in the sector-regulatory architecture further deter private investment, even
                          as the government begins opening infrastructure sectors to greater private involvement.

                          5. These constraints are compounded by a thin and under-prepared pipeline of bankable projects. Many
                          sponsors—national departments, municipalities, and SOEs—lack the institutional capacity, technical skills, and
                          familiarity with appraisal frameworks needed to develop high-quality, investment-ready proposals. In combination with
                          an imperfect enabling environment and underperforming foundational infrastructure, this weak pipeline continues to
                          constrain investment and, ultimately, SA’s ability to generate higher growth and better jobs despite ample liquidity in




_For Official Use Only_
                          the financial system.

                          6. The infrastructure bottlenecks reflect structural and sectoral inefficiencies - most notably weak SOEs,
                          fragmented governance and regulatory processes, and suboptimal use of public funds for private capital
                          mobilization. SOEs dominate critical sectors, yet unclear policies and fragile regulatory settings constrain their ability to
                          prepare or procure viable projects. The resulting bottlenecks in energy, transport, and water impose economy-wide
                          costs estimated at 2–3 percent of GDP annually, while private capital remains on the sidelines despite strong underlying
                          project economics. This has forced government to finance most infrastructure on its own balance sheet and to
                          backstop SOE obligations.

                          7. SA is committed to a just energy transition toward a low-carbon, climate-resilient economy. The Just Energy
                          Transition Investment Plan and Just Transition Framework identify investment needs of approximately US$100 billion
                          for 2023–2027, aligned with the updated NDC. Given constrained fiscal and debt conditions and elevated
                          policy/regulatory risks, private finance has not filled the gap. SA’s Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer
                          Procurement Programme (REIPPPP) mobilized private investment at scale with National Treasury guarantees to
                          backstop Eskom offtake risk, but this has expanded contingent liabilities to ZAR 650 billion (about US$36 billion) in
                          FY2024/25—roughly 90 percent in the electricity sector—creating fiscal sustainability concerns. Bridging the gap will
                          require coordinated public and private financing alongside reforms to reduce perceived risks.

                          8. Substantial infrastructure investment is also critical for competitiveness. Infrastructure has forward linkages to
                          green industries and backward linkages to renewable energy equipment manufacturing and critical minerals - vital for a
                          just transition, industrial competitiveness, and jobs. The investment backlog raises industry costs, while potential trade
                          disruptions with the United States and the EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism pose additional challenges to
                          the trade competitiveness of South African industries.

                          Sectoral and Institutional Context
                          9. SA’s infrastructure bottlenecks stem from structural and sectoral inefficiencies. This includes: weak SOE
                          performance, fragmented governance and processes, and limited use of public resources to mobilize private capital.

                          SOEs dominate energy, transport, and water but face operational inefficiencies due to unclear sector policies, weak
                          regulation, poor governance, and financial stress. A thin pipeline reflects weak project readiness and sponsor capacity
                          (departments, municipalities, SOEs) to prepare robust proposals and navigate appraisal. Poor creditworthiness of
                          SOEs/municipalities, inadequate regulatory architecture, and inefficient processes deter private participation. Despite a
                          sophisticated domestic financial sector capable of mobilizing capital and mitigating FX risk, infrastructure exposure
                          remains below potential due to sector-specific shortcomings and delivery bottlenecks.

                          10. To address these constraints and build an investable pipeline, the Government is streamlining infrastructure
                          delivery and advancing sector reforms through Operation Vulindlela, focusing on electricity, water, and freight
                          transport for their growth impact. Sectoral reforms focus on improving governance and structural inefficiencies and
                          introducing markets and competition, aiming to enhance sector financial sustainability. Infrastructure delivery system
                          reforms include modernizing PPP regulations (national, provincial, with municipal reforms underway), rationalizing
                          institutions, and strengthening core functions: harmonized project prioritization, consolidated preparation via a new
                          Infrastructure Financing and Implementation Support Agency (IFISA) which will become operational by April 1, 2026,
                          and faster, centralized appraisal through a reformed Budget Facility for Infrastructure (BFI) to improve project quality,
                          bankability, and agility, and to crowd in private capital.

                          11. SA’s electricity sector is shifting toward greater competition and financial sustainability to support energy




_For Official Use Only_
                          security and decarbonization. The system’s reliance on aging coal plants constrains reliability and emissions reduction.
                          Key reforms include the ERAA (effective January 2025) and Eskom’s unbundling with the National Transmission
                          Company of South Africa (NTCSA, July 2024). NERSA has approved regulations to enable private participation, and a
                          wholesale market is planned for 2026. Private investment has already flowed into generation and storage: REIPPPP and
                          BESIPPPP mobilized about US$16 billion through 2024, adding 8.3 GW of renewables and 1.7 GW of storage under
                          sovereign guarantees. IRP 2025 projects 33 GW of new capacity by 2030 (27 GW from renewables and storage). South
                          Africa’s CIF Accelerating Coal Transition Investment Plan carries a total financing envelope of US$2.6 billion (US$500
                          million from CIF in concessional/grant financing), leveraging MDB and private capital.

                          12. Transmission is the principal bottleneck to scaling renewables, with congestion—especially north–south—
                          limiting connections needed ahead of coal decommissioning. The TDP 2024 estimates about US$25 billion in
                          investment by 2034, to be financed via Eskom/NTCSA balance sheets and Independent Transmission Projects (ITPs). To
                          bolster ITP bankability, the framework draws on ERAA principles, DEE’s 2025 Transmission Infrastructure Regulations,
                          NERSA’s forthcoming Cost Recovery Rules, and standardized contracts. Given NTCSA’s nascent credit profile and the
                          phase out of sovereign guarantees, additional credit enhancement is likely required to attract private investment.

                          13. In addition, growing investment needs in generation, distribution and storage infrastructure for the energy
                          transition require private sector participation. The IRP 2025 aims to add, for the next five years, 27 GW of renewable
                          energy and storage capacity, for which private capital mobilization will account for a substantial portion, but without
                          proper mitigation of NTCSA’s offtake risk, the market appetite may be constrained. South Africa is also taking forward
                          reform agenda in the distribution sector, which is also exposed to increasing distributed renewable energy and
                          operational inefficiency with substantial investment needs. Risk mitigation measures for creditworthiness of
                          distribution companies are critical to mobilize private capital to fill the financing gap in the distribution sector.

                          14. Similar to the electricity sector, the transport and logistics sector dominated by Transnet SOE has also been
                          undergoing changes prompted by poor performance and legacy issues which have hurt the economy. South Africa’s
                          freight logistics system—centered on Transnet’s state-owned rail and port operations—has struggled with declining

                                                                                                                                         Official Use Only
                                The World Bank

                          performance that has shifted freight from rail to costlier, more polluting roads, undermining export competitiveness.
                          Aggregate rail volumes fell from 226 million tons in 2017/18 to 151 million tons in 2023/24, with export coal volumes at
                          their lowest since 1993 and iron ore at a decade low. Container terminals in Durban and Ngqura ranked among the
                          worst globally, while Transnet’s financial health deteriorated to a net loss of about US$500 million in 2021. The failures
                          are attributed to underinvestment, poor maintenance, theft and vandalism, and operational inefficiencies, costing the
                          economy an estimated 7.4 percent of GDP in 2022.

                          15. In response, the government adopted a Freight Logistics Reform Roadmap in December 2023, supported by the
                          World Bank, to unbundle Transnet and strengthen regulation. The Transport Economic Regulator Act (June 2024) and
                          an official rail network statement (December 2024) established a framework for competitive, non-discriminatory
                          access, with the regulator (TER) overseeing slot allocation and pricing, access and compliance, and dispute resolution.
                          By September 2025, eleven private operators were granted rail slots, and RFIs were issued for five PPP concessions for
                          main lines and the Durban and Cape Town ports. Transnet received R98 billion (about US$6 billion) in rescue financing
                          in 2024–2025, conditional on improvements in balance sheet strength, operational efficiency, workforce engagement,
                          and governance.

                          16. Looking ahead, restoring rail and port performance to 250 million tons per year will require US$10–12 billion—
                          funding needs that cannot be met by the public sector alone. Mobilizing private capital at scale is therefore essential




_For Official Use Only_
                          to clear maintenance backlogs, renew and maintain rolling stock, and protect assets against theft and vandalism, with
                          priority on security‑critical corridors. To crowd in private investment, the government must strengthen delivery
                          capacity, streamline procurement to reduce delays and risk, and break the vicious cycle in which underinvestment
                          reduces availability, erodes revenues, and further constrains investment. Well-structured PPPs, transparent tariff and
                          access regimes, and bankable concession frameworks will be key to aligning risk‑return profiles and unlocking
                          long‑term financing.

                          17. The water supply and sanitation sector is undergoing significant reforms to enable private sector participation
                          amid severe service gaps. Currently, 46 percent of households lack safely managed water supply and 49 percent lack
                          safely managed sanitation, with the most acute needs in informal settlements and rural areas. Non-revenue water loss
                          stands at 41 percent—both technical and financial—costing municipalities about R10 billion annually. These
                          vulnerabilities contributed to Cape Town’s “Day Zero” crisis (2015–2018), when drought, underfunded and aging
                          infrastructure, high consumption, and climate change combined to reduce water levels, causing an estimated economic
                          impact of 3.4 percent of provincial GDP and 37,000 jobs lost.

                          18. To address these challenges and mobilize private capital, the government has advanced several critical reforms.
                          It established the National Water Infrastructure Agency by consolidating the Water Trading Agency and the Trans-
                          Caledon Tunnel Authority—South Africa’s main water financing body—to drive PPPs for large water infrastructure. The
                          Water Services Amendment Bill, brought on October 3, 2025 before Parliament, requires all Water Service Providers to
                          be registered and licensed, including private operators. Additionally, the Department of Water and Sanitation created
                          the Water Partnerships Office in 2023 to promote PPPs across programs such as non-revenue water reduction,
                          wastewater reuse, and desalination. GSA has taken several steps to support a more conducive environment and a
                          systematic review of the infrastructure delivery landscape.

                          19. SA’s financial sector is large and sophisticated—around 300 percent of GDP—with banks at 118 percent and
                          institutional investors at roughly 160 percent (pension funds ~92 percent, insurance ~68 percent), yet institutional
                          investors allocate only about 5–7 percent of assets to domestic infrastructure, far below peer exposures of roughly

                          listed equities, and foreign holdings. To unlock more local capital, National Treasury has advanced enabling policies;
                          Regulation 28 was amended to permit up to 45 percent of institutional portfolios in infrastructure, and tax incentives
                          are under consideration for vehicles such as infrastructure debt funds and infrastructure REITs.

                          20. Sector reforms must be complemented by credible de-risking solutions until sector and SOE reforms build a
                          strong performance track record. Today, private capital mobilization (PCM) at scale for infrastructure requires market
                          instruments to mitigate and price risk, in order to phase out from existing practices of cost-free sovereign guarantees,
                          concentrating financing in programs like REIPPPP and straining fiscal and contingent liabilities. The government is
                          exploring market-based de-risking—potentially via specialized credit-guarantee vehicles drawing on global
                          experience—to crowd in institutional investors, and with an estimated US$573 billion in institutional assets, a one
                          percentage point reallocation to infrastructure could mobilize about US$5 billion.

                           Key Results
                          22. The success of the first phase of the Program will be measured through three Project Development Objective
                          (PDO) level indicators.

                             I.     The first PDO level indicator is the amount of capital mobilized for resilient infrastructure finance. The baseline
                                    is zero and the end target is USD 4.5 billion (after 5 years).
                            II.     The second PDO level indicator is the number of direct and indirect jobs created within the scope of the
                                    resilient infrastructure (related) projects. The baseline is zero and the end target is 240,000 (after 5 years).
                           III.     The third PDO level indicator is the amount of GHG emissions reduced. The baseline is zero and the end target
                                    is 34 Mt CO2e per year (after 5 years).

                           D. Project Description
                          23. The Project will have 3 components.

                          24. Component 1: CGV Capitalization (US$368 million, of which IBRD US$325.97 million, Livable Planet Fund
                          US$12.03 million and Clean Technology Fund US$30 million). This component will fund the GSA equity participation

                          million), and (c) tier of callable capital for up to 30% (up to US$ 150 million). The IBRD-loan will be used for GSA’s equity
                          participation in the CGV and will cover the junior capital tier (first loss). In addition to the GSA, mobilizing concessional
                          climate or just transition financing sources, including potentially CIF ACT, will be sought to co-finance the junior
                          tranche. The remaining part of the initial capital of US$ 300 million is expected to be subscribed by domestic, regional,

                           27. CGV will initiate operations in its first phase with a capital structure of US$ 500 million (of which the Project of
                           which IBRD will finance initially US$96 million), that can leverage up to four times its capital base, mobilizing US$ 2

                          30. The environmental and social category is High risk (FI-I). The risk rating has been assigned due to the anticipated
                          downstream financial exposure associated with the large infrastructure developments, facilitated by guarantees from
                          the CGV, that have a potential to cause significant adverse environmental and social risks and impacts that are
                          considered diverse and irreversible if not properly mitigated. The exact pipeline projects and locations are not yet
                          known and will only be defined during project implementation. However, this is a “specific purpose portfolio” since the
                          use of guarantees can be tracked to specific investments. The MPA aims to establish a Blended Finance Platform to
                          mobilize private capital, deliver infrastructure services, reduce carbon emissions, and create jobs. The first phase,
                          through an IPF will support establishing a CGV to demonstrate effective leverage and mobilization of private capital for
                          critical infrastructure including in the electricity, transport, water and social sectors, while subsequent phases will
                          support upscaling of the CGV. Component 1 of the IPF (first phase of the MPA) will support the capitalization of the CGV
                          through NT. Component 2 will support technical assistance were needed and further development of the project
                          pipeline implemented by private sector.

                          31. Environmental and social risks are largely associated with the downstream project pipeline activities which the
                          CGV is likely to support. This includes: (i) capacity of private and public sector to assess and manage environmental,
                          health and safety (EHS) risks and impacts, (ii) complex contractor and supply chain EHS management, (iii) poor
                          compliance and enforcement of EHS requirements project developers and contactors, (iv) loss of biodiversity and
                          natural habitats (marine and terrestrial), (v) increased air emissions including greenhouse gasses, (vi) soil and water




_For Official Use Only_
                          pollution, (vii) generation of large quantities of hazardous and non-hazardous waste, (viii) fatalities and disabling
                          injuries due to construction related occupational health and safety hazards and (ix) community health and safety risks
                          including traffic related incidents, exposure to construction hazards such as dust, noise, hazardous substances, (x) life
                          and fire safety risks due poor structural integrity and public space safety considerations, (xi) poor emergency
                          preparedness and response planning,(xii) labor influx; (xiii) excluding vulnerable groups from the decision-making
                          processes; (xiv) challenges in ensuring adequate stakeholder engagement and grievance redress mechanism; and (xv)
                          SEA/SH risks.

                          32. Risk associated with the initial phases of the project will largely relate to labor and working conditions for
                          members of the Project Implementing Unit (PIU) that will be established within DBSA. The PIU will be staffed with an
                          environmental, health and safety specialist and a social specialist within the timeframe set out in the ESAP. The
                          PIU/IFISA will be responsible for overseeing the establishment of the CGV and facilitating the development of CGV
                          operational policies and procedures including an ESMS to assess, manage and monitor portfolio E&S risks. The CGV will
                          be a non-bank financial entity structured as a private corporation and will be managed by an independent and

                          38. The CGV will be established as a private, non-life insurance company in South Africa, managed by an
                          independent professional team. GSA will hold a minority stake, with other initial shareholders including several

                          at the SARB/PA by March 31, 2026 to operate as a non-life insurance company. This includes preparing a business plan,
                          governance structure and operating model. In the third step, the management team will work towards implementation
                          of the CGV and the potential shareholders of the CGV will formally secure their equity participation in the second
                          quarter of 2026 as they will receive formal commitment from the Board of their respective Development Financial
                          Institution. In the fourth step, it is expected that the CGV will be fully operational and licensed to issue guarantees by
                          the second half of 2026.
