    I. PROJECT STATUS AND RATIONALE FOR RESTRUCTURING

A. Program Background

      1. Nigeria’s Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) Sector is in a state of emergency. According to the 2021 Water,
      Sanitation and Hygiene National Outcome Routine Mapping (WASHNORM) survey, approximately 60 million Nigerians lack
      access to basic drinking water services, with 38% of improved water points being non-functional and 30% failing after the
      first year. Piped water access has declined significantly, from 32% in 1990 to just 7% in 2019. Poor sanitation and hygiene
      practices are widespread, with 48 million Nigerians practicing open defecation. Only 11% of schools and 6% of healthcare
      facilities have basic water supply and sanitation services1. Inadequate access to WASH services has severe implications for
      public health, economic development, and overall quality of life. In Nigeria, inadequate WASH leads to 73% of the total
      burden of enteric infections and over 255,000 preventable deaths each year. Nigeria invests an average of 0.32% of its gross
      domestic product (GDP) annually in the WASH sector, which is much lower than the African average of 0.7%. To achieve the
      Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) by 2030, at least 1.30% of GDP investment is required. There is also a shortage of
      skilled personnel to manage and maintain services, and environmental challenges such as water pollution and climate
      change affect the quality and availability of services. Social and institutional barriers, such as limited community
      participation and transparency, significantly impact the sustainability of WASH programs and projects.

      2. In response to this critical situation, the Government of Nigeria (GoN) launched the National Action Plan (NAP) for the
      Revitalization of Nigeria’s Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene Sector in 2018 to serve as a 13-year strategy aimed at ensuring
      universal access to sustainable and safely managed WASH services by 2030. The Sustainable Urban and Rural Water Supply,
      Sanitation, and Hygiene (SURWASH) Program (P170734) was designed to support the implementation of the NAP. The
      Program was approved on May 25, 2021, and became effective on January 14, 2022. With a total financing envelope of US$
      700 million (IDA Credit No. 6882-NG), the Program Development Objectives (PDO) are to increase access to water,
      sanitation, and hygiene services and to strengthen sector institutions in participating states of Nigeria. The program focuses
      on two results areas:

            a. Results Area 1 (RA 1): Strengthened sector policies and institutions for improved services. This area focuses on
               enacting necessary policy reforms and enhancing the capacity of institutions required for effective and sustainable
               service delivery. It aims at improving the governance, management, and accountability mechanisms within the
               WASH sector.

            b. Results Area 2 (RA 2): Improved access to water supply, sanitation and hygiene services. This area supports an
               integrated package of investments to expand access to and the use of WASH services in urban and rural areas and
               small towns. It promotes a Local Government Area (LGA)-wide approach to WASH service delivery, addressing
               critical gaps simultaneously in WASH, and within communities, public institutions, and public places.

      3. The SURWASH Program is currently being implemented in seven states: Delta, Ekiti, Gombe, Imo, Kaduna, Katsina, and
      Plateau. These states were selected by the Federal Ministry of Water Resources and Sanitation (FMWR&S) based on

   eligibility and readiness criteria through a fair and transparent process. The program leverages financing from the World
   Bank using the Program-for-Results (PforR) instrument to support and incentivize infrastructure investment and the
   strengthening of Policies, Institutions, and Regulations (PIR). Additionally, a US$ 60 million Investment Project Financing
   (IPF) component was included to provide technical assistance (TA) to address implementation gaps. Allocations under the
   PforR and IPF components are notional, with final allocations determined based on performance.

   4. SURWASH was designed as a national platform to accelerate progress towards universal WASH access, leveraging a
   competitive, performance-based approach to drive results and sector transformation. It provides a unified framework to
   scale up investments, strengthen institutions, and harmonize policies and standards across states through a platform
   design, while ensuring flexibility to adapt to local contexts and evolving sector needs. This approach positions SURWASH
   not just as a project, but as a scalable, country-led mechanism to promote and support achieving universal, sustainable
   WASH access. The program was designed to allow additional States to be incorporated over time. Currently, States such as
   Abia, Anambra, Bauchi, Edo, Jigawa, Kwara, Niger, Oyo, Taraba, and the Federal Capital Territory (FCT) receive TA from
   FMWR&S through the IPF to address weaknesses and achieve readiness for investment under the PforR.

   5. The program aimed to benefit an estimated 6,100,000 people by providing basic drinking water services, 1,400,000
   people with access to improved sanitation services, and 2,000 schools and healthcare facilities with improved WASH
   services. Additionally, it targeted 500 communities to become open defecation free and aimed for seven states to achieve
   their PIR targets. The current Program closing date is June 30, 2027.

B. Program Status

   6. The SURWASH Program has completed four years of implementation, with Program Year 4 concluding at end‑2025.
   Results from Program Year 4 are currently under verification by the Independent Verification Agent (IVA). As such, the most
   recent verified results available at the time of restructuring are those from Program Year 3 (2024).

   7. Based on the verified Year 3 results, the Program has demonstrated both measurable progress and persistent
   challenges. SURWASH experienced significant delays in its early years, mainly due to the need to socialize the PforR
   approach, slow budget releases, and capacity constraints at both federal and state levels. Nevertheless, performance
   improved markedly between Program Year 2 and Program Year 3, as reflected in the following verified outcomes:

        a. The number of people provided with basic drinking water services increased from 58,585 in Year 2 to 268,800 in
           Year 3, reflecting a substantial scale-up in verified infrastructure and service delivery.
        b. Households with improved sanitation facilities rose from just 1 in Year 2 to 1,824 in Year 3.
        c. The number of schools and healthcare facilities with improved WASH facilities increased from 22 in Year 2 to 123
           in Year 3.
        d. Payments for results to states increased by 168% from Special Drawing Rights (SDR) 9,561,537 to SDR 25,619,932
           from Year 2 to Year 3, indicating improved program execution and achievements and highlighting the acceleration
           in verified results and associated disbursements.

   8. Despite these gains, the Program remains off‑track in relation to its overall targets. The PDO and Implementation
   Progress (IP) ratings remain Moderately Unsatisfactory, and SURWASH continues to be classified as a problem project due

9. These gaps are largely explained by delays in operationalizing the Program’s platform approach, which was intended to
scale results through the onboarding of additional states meeting eligibility and readiness criteria. Early implementation
challenges, including the learning curve associated with the PforR instrument, slow budget releases, and delays in mobilizing
technical assistance and completing assessments for prospective states, slowed the expansion of the Program beyond the
initial cohort. These challenges also revealed underlying institutional and systems capacity constraints at both the federal
and state levels, which limited the Program’s ability to scale as originally envisaged. As a result, implementation has
remained limited to the initial seven states, constraining the scale and pace of results and contributing to the Program’s
overall underperformance.

10. To date, approximately US$19.4 million out of the US$60 million Investment Project Financing (IPF) allocation has been
disbursed. At the federal level, IPF resources have supported the development of sector guidelines, the revision of the NAP,
and the update of the National WASH Policy. IPF funds have also provided operational support to the Partnership for
Expanded Water Supply, Sanitation and Hygiene (PEWASH), the Clean Nigeria Campaign (CNC), the National Water
Resources Institute (NWRI), and the National Action Plan Coordination Office (NAPCO). In addition, the IPF has financed the
recruitment of the IVA, responsible for verifying achievement of Disbursement-Linked Indicators (DLIs) across the program.
At the state level, IPF funds have been mostly used to run Program Coordination Units (PCUs) and to hire individual technical
assistants for environmental and social safeguards (E&S) and monitoring and evaluation (M&E). At both federal and state
levels, the majority of IPF resources have supported routine operations, individual consultancies, and verification, rather
than broader capacity-building or transformative TA.

11. The Midterm Review (September–October 2024) confirmed that while foundational reforms, institutional structures,
and policy frameworks are being established, operationalization and sustainability remain key challenges. Field verification
and supervision missions continue to reveal gaps in infrastructure quality, reliability, stakeholder engagement, and post-
construction maintenance. Weaknesses in budget execution, maintenance systems, and community engagement highlight
the need for stronger institutionalization of service delivery responsibilities to ensure that investments translate into
reliable, equitable, and sustainable WASH services. At the same time, the Program has demonstrated that where states
internalize the results‑based approach and strengthen implementation capacity, performance improves. The MTR therefore
underlined the need to restructure the Program to enable substantial acceleration, timely onboarding of new states, and
targeted reforms to achieve the PDO and deliver sustainable impact.

12. The proposed restructuring, together with intensified turnaround actions already underway, including increased
hands‑on technical assistance and strengthened verification arrangements, directly addresses the core design and
implementation constraints that contributed to the project’s problem status. These measures provide a credible basis for
restoring performance and set the stage for improved PDO and IP ratings following approval of the restructuring.

C. Rationale for Restructuring

   13. Building on the conclusions of the MTR, on November 20, 2024, the Honorable Minister of Water Resources and
   Sanitation sent a call for expression of interest to all non-participating states, inviting them to express interest and share
   documentation by December 23, 2024, to assess their eligibility and readiness to join the Program. Given both the urgency
   to address core design and implementation bottlenecks identified during implementation and the time required to
   complete the necessary assessments for program expansion, it was recommended to process the required program changes
   through two Level II restructurings. The first restructuring, completed in early 2025, focused on improving performance
   among the initial seven states. It introduced scalable performance frameworks and simplified the assessment of reforms
   and institutional improvements. It realigned the sanitation DLIs requirements with the national sanitation strategy and
   protocol. These adjustments delivered tangible results: disbursements increased, and states began progressing on key
   indicators for water supply, sanitation, and institutional WASH facilities.

   14. The assessments undertaken to inform this second restructuring, building on the implementation challenges
   encountered during the early years of the Program, revealed deeper systemic constraints that needed to be addressed. The
   majority of implementing agencies (IAs) in participating or prospective states were assessed to be operating at a
   “Foundational” level of capacity, with limited ability to deliver sustainable results or scale up investments. As a result, the
   platform approach envisaged under the original program design which assumed a higher level of institutional readiness and
   systems maturity could not function as intended. The Program’s incentive structure required recalibration to place greater
   emphasis on foundational reforms, institutional strengthening, and service delivery systems. Compounding this, the original
   program design relied on unit costs established in 2015, which had neither been reviewed nor adjusted for inflation, market
   changes, or evolving implementation realities. This misalignment affected both the pace and feasibility of achieving results
   at scale. During implementation, gaps were identified in the execution of environmental and social risk management
   actions, particularly regarding the Program Action Plan (PAP) requirement for the IVA to conduct quarterly reviews. This

action was not consistently implemented, highlighting the need for better alignment with the program’s verification

15. The Federal Ministry of Finance (“The Borrower”) submitted a second restructuring request through an official letter
dated November 25, 2025. The request was informed by the findings of the different assessments and aimed at recalibrating
the program’s ambitions and approach to:

     a. Prioritize foundational reforms and institutional strengthening, as highlighted by the scorecard findings, and
        establishing these as prerequisites for any scale-up.
     b. Adjust targets, timelines, and resource allocations to reflect the actual readiness and capacity of states.
     c. Update unit costs to reflect current market realities, ensuring that results-based financing remains both realistic
        and motivating.
     d. Expand the program to include additional states meeting eligibility and readiness criteria, while capping the total
        number of states supported by the program at fourteen (14). This cap is informed by the revised Program
        Expenditure Framework (PEF), which defines the level of expenditures that can be supported through Program
        Year 6 (2027). The limit ensures that resources remain aligned with the program’s expenditure trajectory and that
        quality implementation and sustainable results are maintained.
     e. Refocus the IPF TA component, which has primarily been used for program coordination operations and hiring
        individual consultants, with limited impact on accelerating implementation or addressing key gaps. A significant
        portion (US$17.5 million) originally set aside for new states remains unallocated due to delays in onboarding new
        states. Given the time remaining, streamlined Program Coordination Unit (PCU) structures, and more centralized
        large-scale TA provision, the projected need for IPF funds is now US$45 million.

16. This shift from rapid expansion to building strong foundations reflects the lessons learned over the past four years of
implementation (three program years completed and results verified). This second restructuring of the SURWASH Program
addresses the root causes of underperformance and aligns incentives with realistic, evidence-based milestones. It is
expected to unlock the program’s potential, accelerate progress, and position SURWASH as a credible national platform for
achieving universal access to sustainable WASH services in Nigeria.

17. As part of the restructuring, the allocation to improved WASH services has been reduced to better align resources with
the program’s current capacity and implementation realities. It is important to note that this reduction does not reflect
diminished commitment or interest in expanding access. Rather, the program is prioritizing the strengthening of
institutional and implementation capacity to ensure that future investments in WASH can be sustained and scaled
effectively. By focusing first on building robust systems and capabilities, the program aims to create a solid foundation that
will attract additional funding from other donors and private sector partners, leveraging the SURWASH platform for greater
impact over time. These adjustments reflect a sequencing decision rather than a reduction in ambition, prioritizing
sustainability and institutional readiness to enable scalable access gains beyond the current Program period.

18. The SURWASH program does not have any outstanding audit or financial reports.

II. DESCRIPTION OF PROPOSED CHANGES

  19. The present restructuring is proposing the following changes:
       a. Capping at fourteen (14) the number of states participating in the Program. The original program design
           envisioned SURWASH as a national platform open to all eligible states which achieve readiness criteria. The
           restructured program shifts from an open-ended platform to capping participation at fourteen (14) states, to
           ensure that program resources are focused on states most likely to deliver results within the program period. The
           ultimate objective remains that States participate based on their ability to meet the defined eligibility and
           readiness criteria. This approach maintains a flexible, performance-driven platform, allowing states to move
           between the investment (PforR) track and the technical assistance (IPF TA) track according to their performance.
       b. Increasing flexibility in the Program Expenditure Framework (PEF) by removing rigid state‑specific allocations.
           The restructuring shifts the PEF from fixed, state‑specific allocations to a more flexible framework that supports
           the Program’s operation as a national platform. Rather than pre‑allocating resources by state, the revised PEF
           organizes allocations by expenditure and entity types across the Program, as set out in the amendment letter to
           the Financing Agreement. This approach allows the Program to better manage risks arising from uneven
           implementation progress across states and evolving priorities, while supporting sector‑wide objectives and
           enabling more adaptive planning. The revised PEF is informed by an assessment of historical budget execution in
           the fourteen states currently meeting eligibility and readiness criteria, as well as federal‑level expenditures, with
           projections through Program Year 6 (2027).
       c. Extending the Loan Closing Date by one year, from June 30, 2027, to June 30, 2028, to provide sufficient time for
           the Governments to complete all activities planned for Program Year 6 (2027), and to ensure that results achieved
           in 2027 can be independently verified and disbursements processed in early 2028, in line with the implementation
           and verification schedule.
       d. Cancelling a portion of the loan amount to align total financing with anticipated results and disbursements
           through Program Year 6 (2027), as well as with the revised scope and expenditure needs under the updated PEF.
           The proposed cancellation reflects a recalibration of the Program’s ambitions to match implementation capacity
           and achievable targets. The specific amount to be cancelled, expressed in SDR, is set out in the amendment letter
           to the Financing Agreement, and is reflected in the revised withdrawal table attached thereto.
       e. Refocusing and reducing the IPF allocation to align with the revised scope of the Program and remaining
           implementation period. The IPF will continue to support technical assistance and capacity building for federal and
           state Ministries, Departments, and Agencies (MDAs), but will be strategically refocused toward targeted technical
           assistance with higher catalytic impact. This includes support for project preparation, feasibility analysis, risk
           mitigation, and upstream work to develop a pipeline of bankable investments. The refocused IPF will also prioritize
           Early Childhood Development (ECD) and other cross‑sectoral priorities, creditworthiness and private sector
           participation, and the rollout of a national WASH Management Information System (MIS). The revised IPF
           allocation is set out in the amendment letter to the Financing Agreement.
       f. Reallocating Loan Proceeds Across DLIs to reflect the revised program scope and increase the emphasis on
           institutional reforms and systems strengthening. Unit payments for DLIs 3 and 4 have been updated to better
           match current cost estimates and implementation realities, while ensuring that incentives remain motivating for
           states and agencies. Analysis revealed that other categories of expenditures for service providers, including

   Responsiveness Score (DLI 3.1), and (3) Sanitation Service Quality and Delivery Score (DLI 5.1).
g. Recalibrating the ceiling on advances to participating States. Given that SURWASH is a capital‑intensive PforR,
   access to advance funds remains important to enable participating states to pre‑finance eligible expenditures,
   accelerate implementation, and sustain momentum toward results achievement prior to verification and
   disbursement. Under the restructured Program, the ceiling for advances is set at 25 percent of the value of the
   DLIs eligible for advances (DLIs 3, 5, and 7), consistent with the Program’s disbursement architecture and fiduciary
   framework. Based on the revised DLI structure, the applicable ceiling for advances under the Program is SDR
   28,643,470, as set out in the amendment letter to the Financing Agreement. This approach ensures that advance
   financing remains closely linked to results, preserves fiduciary discipline, and continues to support timely
   implementation by participating states.
h. Revising the Results Framework to reflect the expanded program scope, updated targets, and the new closing
   date. Key changes include adjustments to outcome and intermediate indicators, revised annual and endline
   targets, and updated definitions and verification protocols for DLIs 2, 3.1 and 5.1 to align them with the
   restructured program’s focus on institutional strengthening, systems improvement, and service delivery. The
   revised Results Framework, including all updated indicators and targets, is presented in Annex 1 to this
   restructuring paper.
i. Updating the PAP to address gaps identified during implementation, specifically by aligning the requirement for
   the IVA to conduct quarterly reviews with the program’s verification exercise. This change is intended to ensure
   consistent implementation of E&S risk management actions. Details of the revised PAP are provided in Annex 2.
j. Revising the Definition of Operating Costs in the Financing Agreement to allow performance-based incentives for
   civil servants, in line with the value-for-money guidelines agreed between the Recipient and the Association. This
   represents a change from the current Financing Agreement, which explicitly excludes civil servants from any
   incentives.
k. Risks. As part of the restructuring, the Program’s risk profile has been adjusted to reflect the refocusing on
   institutional performance, revised incentives, and strengthened implementation discipline. While overall risk
   remains Substantial, the restructuring recalibrates key risk drivers particularly those related to political
   commitment, institutional capacity, fiduciary management, stakeholder engagement, and security by
   concentrating resources on demonstrated readiness and performance and reinforcing oversight and verification
   arrangements. The updated risk ratings and corresponding mitigation measures are reflected in the Systematic
   Operations Risk‑Rating Tool (SORT).
