 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

 A. Country Context

1.      The Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), the largest and third-most populous country in Sub-
Saharan Africa, is rich in natural resources. With a total land area of 234 million ha, the DRC’s size is
equivalent to that of Western Europe. It has a low population density of 16.1 person per km 2 and an
estimated 99 million inhabitants, 1,2 of which 54.4 percent live in rural areas.3 The country has vast natural
resources, including the world’s third-largest hydropower potential, the second-largest tropical forest
area globally, mineral endowments that are among the richest and most diverse in the world, and more
than half of all freshwater resources in Sub-Saharan Africa.4

2.       The DRC is one of the world’s poorest countries, with a per capita gross domestic product (GDP)
of US$577,5 roughly one-third of the Sub-Saharan African average (US$1,633). Based on the international
poverty line of US$2.15 a day, 64.6 percent of the population lives in poverty.6 While there has been a
slight decrease in the poverty line—which stood at 69.7 percent in 2012, the number of poor is growing,
with 20 million more poor in 2020 than in 2003. Poverty reduction is hampered by fragility, weak
governance, and high population growth, on average 3 percent annually from 1961 to 2020, while GDP
growth rate was merely 1.6 percent over this period. Economic growth picked up to 8.6 percent in 2022,
driven by mining sector investment and exports. However, growth in non-mining sectors was modest,
slowing down to 3 percent in 2022. The humanitarian situation remains precarious: half of all children
have not received routine immunizations, over 5.2 million people are forcibly displaced, 27 million people
are food insecure, and four Ebola outbreaks have occurred in the past three years.7 In 2020, the Human
Capital Index score was 0.37, which is below the average in Sub-Saharan Africa.

3.        Conflict and violence continue, particularly in the DRC’s part of the Great Lakes Region of
Africa,8 contributing to persistent fragility and instability. Over the past decade, continuing violence has
affected nearly 40 million Congolese.9 Women and girls are especially affected as widespread conflict-
related sexual violence compounds other forms of gender-based violence (GBV). Cycles of conflict have
hampered reform implementation, service delivery, and private sector growth. The three main themes
driving fragility and conflict identified in the World Bank’s 2021 Risk and Resilience Assessment (RRA) are:
(a) elite capture and resource extraction perpetuating a nonresponsive system of governance and a poorly
redistributive economy; (b) an increasingly younger population lacking prospects for social mobility due
to trauma and exclusion; and (c) the interaction between local, regional, and international conflict systems
based on the competition for access to land, minerals, and other natural resources.

4.      Decentralization is enshrined in the 2006 Constitution. However, many key government
functions and financial resources have yet to be transferred to provinces. A sound public financial
management (FM) system is lacking, and provinces still lack proper collection of resources, procedures,
systems, and staff for budget management and execution. In the period 2017–2021, the annual domestic
budget execution rate in the water sector has been around 14–34 percent, while the execution of
investments at provincial level remains negligible (0–4 percent).10 This slow progress on decentralization
severely constrains the ability to deliver services and develop infrastructure for the DRC citizens.

5.      Significant gender gaps exist in the DRC. The DRC ranks 149 (out of 153 countries) as measured
by the Global Gender Gap Index from the World Economic Forum. Gender gaps exists across all pillars of
the World Bank Group (WBG) Gender Strategy,11 and national-level indicators of the World Bank’s Africa
Human Capital Plan12 show that girls and women in the DRC are highly disadvantaged in terms of economic
empowerment, agency over life choices, and access to services.13 Women face discrimination regarding
labor force participation, land, and property rights, and are less likely to have skilled jobs, accounting for
only 24 percent of professional and technical workers.14 One-quarter of STEM15 graduates are female, and
only 9 percent of graduates from construction and engineering programs are female.

6.       Women and girls in the DRC experience high levels of violence. According to the Demographic
and Health Survey, more than half of all women in the DRC have experienced physical violence since the
age of 15, and over one-quarter reported they had been victims of physical violence over the past 12
months.16 Levels of acceptability of partner violence are highly elevated, reflecting the internalization of
violence against women as an acceptable norm. This level of violence, higher than Sub-Saharan African
regional and global averages, represents a critical barrier to women’s and girls’ full participation in the
social and economic space.17

7.      Climate change poses a serious threat to the country’s weak infrastructure and livelihoods—
and hence growth and development. Due to the vast resources of the Congo basin and a high average
annual rainfall of over 1,500 mm per year, the DRC is generally water rich. The total volume of freshwater
withdrawn by major economic sectors is 0.2 percent of the total resource endowment. Long-term
forecasts show more intense and frequent rainfall and an increase in daily mean temperature of 1.5–2.5°C
by 2050. This would lead to more frequent flooding, increased erosion, and prolonged dry spells. Climate
change impacts and vulnerabilities already pose significant challenges and risks in key sectors such as
agriculture, urban development, water supply and sanitation, electricity, and human health. Progressing
climate change will exacerbate water-related risks, hence the need for climate-resilient infrastructure and

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
8.       The poor state of access to basic infrastructure services is a major constraint to sustainable and
inclusive growth. Decades of conflict, underinvestment, and lack of connectivity across the DRC’s vast
territory have resulted in large gaps in access to water supply and sanitation (WSS) services and electricity
supply. Poor access represents a major barrier to doing business, hampers market development, and adds
significant costs to projects. The government has initiated a reform process for key state-owned
enterprises (SOEs) responsible for service delivery, including the national water utility (Régie de
Distribution d’Eau, REGIDESO). These reforms principally affect urban services, leaving significant
governance gaps in rural areas where local service provision models are to be strengthened. 18
9.      The DRC has, after Nigeria and Ethiopia, the third-largest population in Sub-Saharan Africa in
terms of people without water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH). As of 2020, 48 million people (53
percent) lack basic water access, 76 million people lack basic sanitation (84 percent), and 73 million people
lack basic hygiene services (81 percent). Recent household surveys19 show that access in rural areas is
lower than in urban areas: 19 percent have access to basic water supply, 9 percent have access to basic
sanitation, 18 percent practice open defecation, and 15 percent have access to basic hygiene services. 20
Not only is access higher in urban areas, but wealthy households are more likely to have access to water
supply (66 percent versus 14 percent) and basic sanitation (30 percent versus 6 percent) than those in the
bottom quintile of the total population.

10.     There are significant disparities in water supply, sanitation access, and prevalence of open
defecation, with hotspots in the central provinces and large numbers of people without services in the
eastern provinces.21 The Water Supply and Sanitation Access Program using Multiphase Programmatic
Approach (MPA) targets nine provinces22 that are among the provinces identified in the WBG Country
Partnership Framework (CPF) for the DRC (FY22–26, Report No. 168084-ZR), as focus areas for
implementation of operational engagements over the CPF period. These nine provinces represent 45
percent of the total population, hosting 50 percent of the people without basic water supply, 47 percent
of the people without basic sanitation, and 45 percent of people practicing open defecation.23 Phase 1 of
the MPA targets four out of these nine provinces that represent 18 percent of the total population, hosting
31 percent of the people without basic water supply, 23 percent of the people without basic sanitation,
and 33 percent of people practicing open defecation.

12.      In a context of state fragility and poor governance, three major institutional weaknesses affect
the sector: institutional fragmentation, weak capacity, and a bias toward investing in urban water
services.30 The institutional framework for the water sector is highly fragmented and poorly coordinated.
The Ministry of Water Resources and Electricity (Ministère des Ressources Hydrauliques et Electricité,
MRHE) is the key government institution for the water supply sector, including developing water sector
policies, planning, and overseeing project implementation, although a national policy on public water
supply services has not yet been approved. Under the tutelage of the Ministry of Rural Development
(Ministère de Développement Rural, MDR), the National Office of Rural Hydraulics (Office National
d'Hydraulique Rurale, ONHR) assumes tasks for the development of rural water schemes, although this
entity is not mentioned in the 2015 Water Law. The Water Law introduced the establishment of the Water
Regulatory Agency (Autorité de Régulation du Service Public de l’Eau, ARSPE) responsible for economic-
and service-level regulation and the development of water public-private partnerships (PPPs), and the
Congolese Water Authority (Office Congolais des Eaux, OCE) responsible for WRM functions, although
both entities are not yet operational. While OCE’s mandate is operational — set up basin committees,
develop basin plans, manage water allocations, and collect water resources taxes, the Department of
Water Resources (Direction Ressources en Eau, DRE) under the MEDD remains responsible for policy and
water resources regulatory functions and hosts the hydromet database.

d'Eau Potable, ASUREP).32 There is no register of ASUREPs, and often rural water committees operate
informally without technical support or oversight.

15.      While sanitation services, or the collection, treatment, and disposal of fecal waste, are absent

MEDD’s Sanitation Department (Direction d'Assainissement, DAS) is responsible for sanitation planning
and programming and, building on previous programs,34 collaborates with the Ministry of Public Health,
Hygiene and Prevention (Ministère de Santé Publique, Hygiène et Prévention, MSPHP), notably its
Department for Hygiene and Public Health (Direction Hygiène et Salubrité Publique, DHSP). The
government intends to develop a framework law for sanitation35 that would require bylaws to define
institutional and governance roles for liquid sanitation and fecal sludge management (FSM).

16.       The GoDRC has prepared a 2020–2030 National Program for Water Supply, Sanitation and
Hygiene (Programme National Eau, Hygiène et Assainissement, PNEHA). It lays out investments required
to reach the ambitious access targets of 80 percent for urban water, 60 percent for rural water, 70 percent
for sanitation, and universal access in schools and health centers by 2030. The PNEHA, budgeted at US$8
billion, has several subprograms and involves all relevant ministries. However, annual joint sector reviews
and tracking of results and expenditures, facilitated by the National Action Committee for Water Supply,

are not taking place.

17.     Public spending for WASH is low and budget execution rates are compromised due to the
incomplete decentralization process, leaving the sector dependent on donor financing. An analysis of
expenditure data over the 2017–2021 period reveals that domestic public expenditure on WASH was only

18.      The water and sanitation sectors are critical to implement the Government’s response to
climate change. As indicated in the Nationally Determined Contributions document,37 the government
has committed to reducing its greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 21 percent from 2021 to 2030—a
reduction of around 650 million tCO2e compared to business-as-usual emissions. Targeted sectors for GHG
emissions reduction include agriculture, forestry, land use, and energy. The operation of water production
and distribution is often among the largest energy consumers in small towns and rural areas and
accelerating the use of solar-powered solutions for water system operations is part of the mitigation
response. The government’s 2022–2026 National Adaptation Plan to Climate Change 38 identifies the
following adaptation priorities: (a) conservation of forest ecosystems and biodiversity; (b) integrated
agriculture, fishing, livestock, and rural development; and (c) water and sanitation, including reducing
flood risk, and drought and erosion impacts. Low-income communities, often most affected by floods and
droughts, are the most vulnerable to the climate-induced impacts of disrupted water and sanitation
systems.39

D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach

Rationale for Using MPA

28.     Policy, governance, financing, and capacity challenges are complex and will require an
adaptative and programmatic approach to accelerate access to basic WSS in the DRC. The MPA signals
a strong and long-term commitment of the World Bank to support the GoDRC toward universal access
and will allow the progressive implementation of policies and systemic change, alongside phased
implementation. The MPA allows the GoDRC to address the universal access agenda with a national
platform approach for scaling up WASH services, supported by its development partners and backed by a
credible financing roadmap. While the GoDRC’s 2020–2030 PNEHA is the overarching planning
framework, the MPA aims to progressively leverage domestic financial resources, crowd in development
partner financing, and in its latter phases, enable private capital mobilization under a long-term program,
while focusing on the sustainability of water investments.

29.     The MPA allows for the gradual rollout of investments across nine provinces and—if relevant in
the latter phases—would allow the flexible use of different instruments. Phases 1 and 2 will build the
foundational implementation capacities of the provincial governments, including Performance-Based
Conditions (PBCs) to support reforms, with Phase 2 being a geographic scale-up to additional provinces.
Phases 3 and 4 of the MPA would serve to scale up impact and expand the number of targeted local
governments (ETDs) within the nine provinces. All phases are foreseen to utilize the Investment Project
Financing (IPF) instrument given the current low level of domestic expenditures on WASH and the
hampered provincial budget execution (see table 1). Depending on the budget execution under a national
WASH program and the associated risks of using country systems, the use of a Program-for-Results (PforR)
instrument will be considered during preparation of Phases 3 and 4.

36.      Phase 1 of the MPA focuses on increasing access in the provinces of Kasai, Kasai Central, Kasai
Oriental, and Kwilu. These provinces have both high absolute numbers of unserved people, including high
levels of open defecation, and were identified as priority by the Government. They offer immediate
synergies with the World Bank’s support to provincial decentralization under ENCORE project and AGREE
project. Additional five provinces have preliminarily been selected for Phase 2—Ituri, Kongo Central,
Lomami, Nord Kivu, and Sud Kivu, provinces in central as well as eastern DRC, the latter affected by conflict
and violence. Phase 3 will scale up within the ‘Phase 1 provinces’, and Phase 4 within the ‘Phase 2
provinces’. A total of US$250 million of donor co-financing and US$150 million of government financing
is tentatively foreseen under Phases 3 and 4.

37.      The approval of Phase 2 will be contingent on satisfactory progress under Phase 1 and will
include early implementation lessons in its design. Several aspects will be considered to proceed with
Phase 2, including: (a) well-performing implementation agencies at the central (CEP-O) and provincial level
under Phase 1, (b) disbursements and interim results achievement in line with projections, (c) Phase 2
provincial implementation mechanisms agreed and set up in advance, and (d) completion of baseline and
feasibility studies for water investments under Phase 2. The four phases are overlapping, each with a
duration of 5.5–6 years. Phase 2 will be used to prepare for Phases 3 and 4 and evaluate whether a PforR
instrument would be feasible. Each phase includes a province-led validation process to select ETDs, based
on criteria such as unserved people, levels of accessibility, and the presence of larger settlements to
increase efficiency of implementation.

Learning Agenda

40.      A robust learning agenda is central to the MPA’s design and implementation and will help
mitigate technical design risks. The learning agenda of the proposed MPA focuses on stengthening impact
and efficiency from programmatic service delivery for WSS. Robust monitoring systems will be put in place
to validate and adapt approaches, combined with support to provinces for peer learning exchanges, as
well as South-South knowledge exchanges (under Subcomponent 3.1). Partnerships with other knowledge
partners for the GoDRC are foreseen, notably with USAID, such as with the Rural Evidence and Learning
for Water (REAL-Water)46 and the WASH Partnership for Learning and Sustainability.47 Through the Global
Water Security and Sanitation Partnership, specifically the City-Wide Inclusive Sanitation (CWIS) 48 and the
Utility of the Future initiatives,49 regional and global learning opportunities will be enhanced.

E. Lessons Learned

64.       The project design incorporates the following lessons learned from past and ongoing WSS and
other sector projects. First, reform efforts require high level of commitments at the national as well as at
the provincial level. Hence, the project introduces several PBCs that reward key reform milestones, either
led by the Prime Minister’s Office (PBC#1) or by the provincial governments (PBC#2, PBC#5). Second, in a
large, complex country with governance challenges, political volatility, and conflict, a focus on
decentralized implementation is appropriate and capacity-building activities need to be mainstreamed. 58
The implementation arrangements of the MPA, with its rural focus and ambition to achieve scale, have
been carefully considered and a decentralized model is proposed. The project supports the establishment
of PPIUs, putting in place risk mitigation and a PPIU capacity-building program as well as a sectoral capacity
building. Third, project coordination arrangements will be anchored in relevant line ministries and
supported by country-based World Bank task team leaders. MRHE’s Project Coordination Unit (PCU), CEP-
O will be responsible for project coordination, involving a national technical coordination team, composed
of staff in relevant line ministries and entities. Key members of the World Bank task team are based in the
DRC to ensure close implementation support.

65.      Pursuing parallel and well-coordinated public and private approaches coupled with institutional
strengthening have proved to be effective in accelerating access and are followed under the project. In
the rural water sector, given past challenges in sustainability in the DRC and globally, the project builds
on lessons from private sector engagement in Senegal, Benin, India, and Tanzania. The project addresses
these in several ways: (a) the inclusion of transaction advisory to help clustering of perimeters to develop
attractive contracts (affermages); (b) the careful design, including performance indicators for IDA-
financed DBO contracts to attract regional and domestic private sector; (c) the development of a local
market for maintenance services for solar pumped rural water systems managed by ASUREPs;59 (d)
capacity development of the project owner (maître d'oevrage), that is, in the DRC, the Régies Provinciales;
and (e) the strengthening of independent regulation through ARSPE with cost-reflective tariffs framework.

66.     Lessons from World Bank-financed projects and other partner initiatives60 on rural sanitation
service delivery in Africa are reflected in the MPA design, using market-based approaches for urban
areas incentives for results. In Kenya, experiences with sanitation marketing and partnership with global

84.      Baseline studies for the selected ETDs will inform the prioritization of subproject investment

project activities. It includes: (a) project cost under Subcomponents 1.1, 2.1, and 2.2, including estimated
replacement and O&M costs; and (b) all measurable benefits, including benefits from reduced time spent
collecting water and accessing sanitation, reduction in incidence of associated diseases, and improved
school attendance of girls. Other non-quantifiable benefits are improved health and well-being and
increased safety and dignity, specifically for women and girls.

90.      Overall, the project has a strong economic justification with a net present value of US$2.23
billion and an economic internal rate of return of 91.2 percent (excluding GHG emissions). This analysis
assumes a discount rate of 6 percent, 50 years of asset life of water supply systems, and a 30-year project
life horizon. Project interventions are expected to reduce net GHG emissions; thus, the impact of GHG
emissions on the cost-benefit analysis results are positive (although small). A sensitivity analysis was
conducted, including for time savings, delay in implementation, and discount rate, and it is concluded that
the project design is robust.

91.      A financial analysis has been carried out to assess the viability of typically sized water supply
subprojects,70 assessing whether distributed water remains affordable for households living below the
extreme poverty line.71 Different scenarios have been simulated with capital costs per user from US$50–
80, depending on scheme size, type of assets, and variable hydrogeology conditions. The analysis assumes
that assets will be fully recovered within the lifespan of the equipment/infrastructure, and therefore full
depreciation costs have been simulated in the water tariff, along with other expense elements, that is,
running costs, taxes, profit, NRW losses, logistics, maintenance, and management. Simulations show that
breakeven tariffs range between CDF 115 and CDF 135 for a 25 liter jerry can (US$0.055–0.064), to be
rounded up to CDF 150 per jerry can (US$0.072).72 A price of CDF 150 per jerry can is a relatively well
accepted tariff by consumers, including the poorest.73 As current water usage remains low at around 10–
25 liters per person per day, this represents a daily water expense of 3.3 percent of the extreme poverty
line, within the benchmark of 5 percent.

 VI.     KEY RISKS

111. The overall project risk rating is Substantial because of the weak governance and slow
decentralization, the substantial fiduciary risks, the multifaceted project design involving multiple entities,
institutional sustainability challenges, substantial environmental and social risks, and potential constraints
in engaging private sector in an FCV context. Key risks and mitigation measures are discussed below.

112. Political and Governance: Substantial. The overall country environment is characterized by
volatile political context, lack of transparency, corruption, and the slowly advancing decentralization
process. The project provinces have different socioeconomic and governance challenges, with the Kasais
having specific FCV challenges. Some areas still have active conflict and insecurity, other parts are
recovering from conflict and chronic underdevelopment. To mitigate these risks, the project ensures
security risk assessments and tailors the interventions to the local context, informed by a social
assessment. Decentralization challenges are addressed by provincial capacity-building programs and the
engagement of civil servants in implementation and through PBC#2 that requires adequate staffing of
Regies Provinciales.

113. Macroeconomic: Substantial. Government revenue remains low and there are major external
vulnerabilities threatening to impair ongoing macroeconomic recovery efforts because of the volatility in
energy and commodity prices and global trade tensions. This affects prices of construction materials, costs
of transportation, and other inflationary pressures. Risks at the macroeconomic level are mitigated by the
projected strong demand for the DRC’s export commodities, which are critical for the global transition
toward lower carbon mobility. Project-level mitigation measures include maximizing the use of locally
available materials, including price contingencies in the project costing and no co-financing by

The World Bank
DRC Water Supply and Sanitation Acess Program (PASEA) Project (P178389)



government counterpart funds.

114. Sector Strategies and Policies: Substantial. While certain building blocks for the sector are in
place, such as the Water Law, a reform strategy for REGIDESO, and a national water and sanitation
program, there remain critical gaps: delays in passing bylaws for the Water Law and in operationalizing
OCE and ARSPE, the unclear institutional mandates for sanitation and rural water supply. These risks are
mitigated through several measures: (a) ensuring a long-term approach through the MPA for complex
reforms, (b) several PBCs to focus on policy improvement and clarification of mandates, (c) capacity
development for provincial support roles for rural water, (d) development of sanitation legislation at the
provincial level, and (e) technical assistance to operationalize ARSPE and OCE and framework for
economic regulation and WRM (complementary to the AGREE project).

115. Technical Design of Project: Substantial. Technical challenges in the project design exist, such as
related to durable use of toilets, maintenance challenges for solar pumped systems, and the limited
experience with market-based sanitation and the absence of any experience with FSM. To mitigate these
technical design risks, the project is: (a) introducing adaptations and innovations building on existing
approaches and global good practices; (b) introducing a strong learning agenda and project monitoring to
validate the technical design and adapt it based on implementation lessons; and (c) limiting investments
in FSM to small-scale pilots, following a learning-by-doing approach.

116. Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability: Substantial. The decentralized
PPIUs are new to the World Bank portfolio in the DRC. Institutional capacity to manage, coordinate, and
technically lead the delivery of the project activities and manage the associated risks is limited. Identified
sustainability risks can jeopardize investments due to poor maintenance of water systems and WASH
facilities. Risk mitigation measures are the following: (a) CEP-O, as the PCU, leads project management
and coordination during implementation, supported by a national coordination team; (b) the PPIUs will
be staffed with consultants; (c) Provincial Subsidiary Agreements will define roles and mitigate political
interference; (d) an MoU will define responsibilities of all technical entities; (e) technical support will be
extended to service providers; and (g) a young professional program will enhance PPIU capacity.

117. Fiduciary: Substantial. In the DRC, fiduciary risks are high and ubiquitous, notwithstanding
government commitment to combat corruption. The residual risk is discussed in section IV.B for both FM
and procurement aspects. While the PCU has ample experience and a satisfactory track record in fiduciary
management, newly established PPIUs at the provincial level will require significant risk mitigation and
capacity building. The project will introduce innovative procurement aspects (DBO contracts) as well as a
voucher scheme, catalytic and results-based grant schemes, and PBCs. Technical assistance will be
provided to mitigate risks, as well as other measures detailed in Annex 1.

118. Environment and Social: Substantial. This is based on the project’s anticipated environmental
and social risks and impacts which are discussed in section IV.D. Framework instruments to manage these
risks (ESMF, RPF, IPPF, SEP, and LMP) have been prepared. Risks related to implementation capacity will
be managed by ensuring that CEP-O, as an experienced PCU, holds the overall responsibility for
implementation of environmental and social measures. Due to the large geographical coverage of the
project, environmental and social experts (with GBV experience) will be placed within the PPIUs to ensure
adequate monitoring and support. Sufficient budget is included to implement the agreed actions in
accordance with the ESCP.

   ANNEX 1: Implementation Arrangements, Implementation Support Plan, and Fiduciary Assessment


 Implementation Arrangements
 1.      Under the strategic guidance of an NPSC, chaired by MRHE, the PCU, CEP-O, will coordinate and
 manage overall project implementation, supported by a national coordination team and by the
 provinces. Key national entities will assign staff to form the national coordination team (MRHE, MDR-
 ONHR, MP-CNAEHA, REGIDESO, MEDD-DAS, MSPHP-DHSP/DESP, and MEPST-DNAC/DEVC) responsible to
 support the project coordination, technical supervision, and lead the technical execution at the national
 level, while CEP-O retains the mandate for fiduciary and ESF compliance. Civil servants, eligible for
 reasonable transport and communication allowance, will be selected for the national coordination team
 based on a merit-based process with ToRs, as further detailed in the PIM. In addition, there are other
 collaborating entities that will be involved in specific activities under the project. Figure 1.1 illustrates the
 implementation arrangements.

 2.       At the provincial level, under the strategic guidance of the PSC, the PPIUs, created under the
 Governor’s office, are tasked with the execution of province-level activities, supported by provincial
 coordination teams. At the provincial level, civil servants from the procurement and FM divisions will be
 selected to be embedded within the PPIU based on a merit-based process with ToRs, as further detailed
 in the PIM, and eligible for reasonable transport and communication allowance. Staff from provincial
 entities will be selected for provincial coordination teams, amongst others Régies Provinciales de Service
 Public de l’Eau, provincial ONHR, REGIDESO directorate, DAS, DHSP, DESP, DNAC, and DEVC. Civil servants
 in the provincial coordination teams, selected through a merit-based process with ToRs, as further
 detailed in the PIM, will be eligible for reasonable transport and communication allowance. At the
 provincial level, the selected ETDs (communes, secteurs, chefferies, and city/chef lieu) will be closely
 involved in implementation. The PPIUs will—at first—have responsibilities for limited procurement
 activities with complex and high-value contracts to be procured by CEP-O. A performance review at MTR
 will indicate whether fiduciary responsibilities of the PPIUs can be enlarged.

 3.      The contracted staffing plan for the PPIUs for Phase 1 is proposed that includes per PPIU a
 provincial project coordinator/operations engineer (water), operations engineer (sanitation),
 procurement specialist, accountant, cashier, M&E specialist, environment specialist (shared with another
 province), and social/GBV specialist (shared with another province). Staffing needs will be reviewed during
 implementation.

 4.      As detailed in the Financing Agreement, all entities involved in the implementation will sign an
 MoU and the recipient will sign Provincial Subsidiary Agreements with participating provinces.
 Provincial governments execute their responsibilities through their respective PPIUs, as they will be
 responsible for the sub-accounts and for procurement activities at the provincial level (see figure 1.2. for
 the flow of funds). The Provincial Subsidiary Agreements will detail the roles and responsibilities of the
 provinces in the implementation of the activities of the project (fiduciary, environmental and social,
 technical implementation, and M&E).

 organize the ODF celebration, and execute the ODF incentive payments. The ODF celebration will include
 a community event, cultural activities, and small items for the community to enhance hygienic behaviors.

 Financial Management

 12.    The overall project management, including FM, will be carried out by the existing PCU, CEP-O,
 under MRHE. At the provincial level, the PPIUs will be set up under the Governor’s office in four provinces.

 13.     Country public financial management (PFM) situation and use of country systems. The DRC 2020
 PEFA assessment conducted highlighted weak PFM performance in budget reliability and predictability
 and management of assets and liabilities. Also noted was weak performance in other pillars which include
 budget transparency and comprehensiveness, budget cycle, predictability and control, accounting and
 reporting, and external and internal audit. Important provisions in the 2011 Public Finances Law have not
 yet been implemented. These include the adoption of program budgeting and the Treasury Single
 Account. The directorate of treasury (Direction General de Trésor et Comptabilité Public) which includes
 the national network of public accountants has been established on March 2, 2023. In the absence of the
 government treasury (before March 2023), there have been no external reviews of government
 expenditure financial reports by the Supreme Audit Institution. Other main weaknesses are related to
 redundant and lengthy steps in budget execution processes, the excessive and unnecessary use of
 exceptional or emergency procedures in the expenditure payment process, and the centralization of
 budget execution authority. Various World Bank-funded projects support the DRC’s PFM reforms.

 Disbursement

 21.     Funds flow and disbursement arrangements. Disbursements for the IPF will be made in
 accordance with the World Bank Disbursement Guidelines for Projects, dated February 1, 2017. The
 project disbursements of advances will be made into DAs based on the unaudited IFRs. Two DAs in US
 dollars will be opened at an acceptable commercial bank on terms and conditions acceptable to IDA. In
 addition, one sub-account per DA will be opened for each of the four PPIUs to fund activities as the
 provincial level (see figure 1.2). The DA-A will be used for payment of expenditures related to the PBC
 after verification conditions and eligible expenses by an IVA. The DA-B will be for all other IPF expenditures
 (input-based, not-PBC linked) of the project. The initial advance into the DAs will represent six months of
 forecasted expenditures (variable ceiling) in the IFRs. The replenishment of the DAs will also be through
 withdrawal applications supported by quarterly unaudited IFRs. The PIM will describe the details on the
 management of the DAs. The other disbursement methods available to the recipient are reimbursements,
 direct payments, and special commitments. Detailed information on the disbursement methods will be
 provided in the Disbursement and Financial Information Letter.

 22.      Disbursements against all PBCs. A certain amount of the proceeds will be allocated to each PBC
 which is the amount that the recipient can claim as disbursements against eligible expenditures if that
 PBC has been achieved and verified. Before achieving most of the PBCs, the project would have disbursed
 against some transactions. Such expenditures will be considered eligible only when the PBCs are fully
 achieved. In case of a partial achievement of a scalable PBC, expenditures incurred will be eligible up to
 the concerned PBC achievement rate. The PIM will include procedures related to the PBCs and provide
 details on the entire process from the identification of PBCs up to the payment (the PBC Manual annexed
 to the PIM). Based on ToRs acceptable to the World Bank, an IVA will be hired to verify achievement of
 conditions and eligibility of related expenses for all PBCs.

 28.     Procurement plan. A procurement plan for the first 18 months of implementation has been

 procurement activities, establish benchmarks, monitor delays, and measure procurement performance.

 29.     The procurement activities will be carried out at the national level by CEP-O, established in the
 MRHE, and by four PPIUs that will be established in each participating province. A procurement capacity

 such as procurement legislation, procurement organization and staffing, procurement strategies,
 procurement operations, procurement performance monitoring and measurement, and procurement
 control and oversight mechanisms.

 30.      Procurement Risk Assessment and Management System. The assessment revealed that CEP-O
 has acquired extensive experience through preparation and implementation of more than four
 infrastructure projects financed by donors including the World Bank and thus has acquired good
 knowledge of World Bank procurement procedures. CEP-O has a Procurement Unit led by the Head of the
 Procurement Unit and includes two procurement specialists (dedicated to Kin Elenda Project and AGREE
 Project) and one procurement assistant. Given the volume of procurement activities planned under
 PASEA, a dedicated procurement specialist will be recruited. At the provincial level, the assessment of the
 Procurement and Project Management Division (Cellule de Gestion de Projects et Passation de Marché)
 revealed weak capacities and no experience in World Bank-financed projects. Therefore, a dedicated PPIU
 will be established in each province with well-qualified staff recruited competitively, including a
 procurement specialist. In addition, for the four provinces, an international procurement technical
 assistant will be recruited. The procurement risk is rated High. The residual risk is Substantial considering
 the implementation of mitigation measures identified in Table 1.5. The PPSD includes the full list of risks
 and mitigation measures.
