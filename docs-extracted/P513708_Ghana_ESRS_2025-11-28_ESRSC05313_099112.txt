                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     Ghana has a road network of about 94,203 km, of which 27 percent paved and 73% unpaved. More than 50 percent of
                     the network, including many feeder and secondary roads, is in fair to impassable condition. Road density and coverage
                     are unevenly distributed: the north of the country remains poorly served, while the southern half has greater road
                     density supporting agricultural and livestock production, but often at a poor service level that increases travel distances
                     and costs to markets and limits opportunities. In fact, the population in the north is better connected to neighbor’s
                     countries and has some comparative productivity agricultural advantages. To address these challenges, the Government
                     of Ghana recently launched the Big Push Initiative 2025. The Program is aimed at reducing the investment gap in key

                     sectors and revitalizing aging, poorly maintained infrastructure. Among its priorities are feeder roads, which play a
                     crucial role in linking rural areas to main transport corridors. Investing in feeder roads is essential to connect farms and
                     rural communities with markets, reduce transport costs and post-harvest losses, and improve access to services.
                     Strengthening these networks will boost agricultural productivity, competitiveness, and inclusive growth, while better
                     connecting isolated regions of the country. The proposed operation will support (i) the rehabilitation of selected feeder
                     roads linked to key agricultural and industrial value chains, (ii) the road fund reform for improved maintenance and (iii)
                     private sector mobilization through technical assistance. The proposed operation will be structured around 3
                     components: Component 1: Feeder road rehabilitation Component 2: Road Maintenance and sustainability Component
                     3: Enhancing private sector mobilization and project management Component 4: CERC


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     The Ghana Market Access and Connectivity Project aim to improve rural connectivity, agricultural productivity, and job
                     creation in selected regions. Anticipated activities include rehabilitation of feeder roads, development of small-scale
                     market infrastructure, construction of storage facilities, road maintenance, and potential enhancements to digital and
                     energy connectivity. Exact locations and scopes are yet to be determined; works will primarily target existing rural
                     feeder roads.
                     Rural Ghana spans savannah and high forest ecosystems with significant biodiversity and natural resources. While
                     interventions will remain within existing road footprints, dispersion of sites along these ecosystems and habitats,
                     wetlands, streams, and farmland require careful planning and screening to avoid or minimize impacts.
                     Improved rural roads can reduce travel time, enhance safety, and facilitate market access, benefiting women and



Public Disclosure
                     minority groups and reduce post-harvest losses. Social gains include increased income, better access to services, and
                     potential upward mobility. Risks include seasonal isolation where roads are inadequate, disruption of traditional land
                     use and local dynamics, potential displacement, and tensions. Inclusive planning and community participation especially
                     in markets where women are predominant users, are essential to ensure culturally appropriate, equitable outcomes.
                     Construction-related risks (noise, dust, waste, traffic safety), resource use, community interactions, and potential
                     impacts on land, natural habitats, wetlands, water bodies, cultural heritage, and vulnerable groups. Given geographic
                     dispersion, cumulative impacts may arise without robust coordination.
                     A framework approach will be used. Subprojects will be selected based on technical/economic feasibility and ability to
                     avoid/mitigate E&S risks. Subprojects with massive displacement, significant livelihood impacts, or risks to cultural
                     heritage will be excluded.


                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Ministry of Roads and Highways (MRH), under the Chief Director, will lead implementation and oversight,
                     supported by a Steering Committee, a full-time Project Coordinator, and Agency Implementation Teams (AITs) in the
                     Department of Feeder Roads (DFR) and the Ministry of Food and Agriculture (MoFA). Each AIT will be led by a full-time
                     Manager with dedicated staff or consultants.
                     DFR will implement Component 1.2 and manage its activities under Component 3; MRH will lead Component 2 and
                     Subcomponent 3.1 with technical inputs from the Ghana Highway Authority, the Department of Urban Roads, and DFR;
                     and MoFA will deliver the agricultural subcomponent through the FSRP PIU. Both MRH and MoFA have World Bank
                     project experience, MoFA under the ESF and MRH under Safeguards.

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                         Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                  Substantial
                     The project’s environmental risk rating is classified as Substantial under the World Bank Environmental and Social
                     Framework (ESF), primarily due to the nature and scale of civil works (rehabilitation of feeder roads, small agriculture
                     market infrastructure, storage facilities, road maintenance works) planned across multiple sectors and some regions
                     in Ghana. This rating reflects the potential for significant environmental impacts, and may be revised as more detailed
                     information on specific interventions and selected locations becomes available. Key considerations informing the



Public Disclosure
                     ratings include: construction impacts, activities may generate dust, noise, vibration, soil erosion, vegetation loss, and
                     pollution from waste and construction runoff, all of which require careful management, probably biodiversity risks
                     which includes potential for habitat disturbance, and fragmentation, resource demand as the project will require
                     materials, water, and energy, underscoring the importance of sustainable sourcing and efficient resource use.
                     Potential OHS risk, previous transport projects in Ghana have recorded high fatalities due to work interfaces with live
                     traffic and heavy equipment, which elevated occupational health and safety (OHS) risks. Other risks include
                     cumulative impacts from multiple infrastructure upgrades that may result in cumulative environmental impacts that
                     must be addressed holistically and MRH’s capacity to effectively implement mitigation measures.
                                                                                                                                    Substantial
                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The social risk rating is assessed as Substantial at concept stage. The rating is based on project characteristics
                     including typology, scale and complexity, MRH’s capacity and commitment and contextual factors such as legacy
                     issues. Key social risks and impacts anticipated include; a) land acquisition or restrictions on land use, with potential
                     for physical or economic displacement especially for squatters and encroachers on feeder roads and market
                     rehabilitation sites; b) impacts on livelihoods, particularly for communities trading along the road and dependent on
                     right of way corridos; c) minor labor influx and associated risks including potential to upset local dynamics and the for
                     sexual exploitation and abuse; d) risks to tangible cultural heritage, should project activities intersect with sites of
                     historical or cultural significance; e) disproportionate adverse impacts on vulnerable groups requiring targeted
                     identification and mitigation measures to ensure equitable project benefits and avoid disproportionate harm
                     especially for interventions related to road fund reform and private sector mobilization. To facilitate the activation of
                     the proposed zero Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC), a CERC manual will be prepared to detail the

                     B. Relevance of Standards and Policies at Concept Stage

                     B.1 Relevance of Environmental and Social Standards

                      ESS1 - Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts                                   Relevant

                      The MRH and MoFA will conduct a detailed E&S risk assessment proportionate to the nature and scale of the
                      proposed sub-projects. They will prepare an ESCP, summarizing material measures and actions to address identified
                      E&S risks and impacts and update the Transport Sector Improvement Project (TSIP) ESMF that will include
                      components on Biodiversity, Waste and Traffic Management, OHS and an annex on CERC manual. MRH and MoFA
                      will also conduct site screening and site-specific ESMPs prior to the commencement of civil works. An RPF will be
                      prepared to guide the management of displacement impacts and if the utilization of IDA to finance displacement
                      impacts is confirmed, include estimate for compensation to accompany the RVP memo. MRH and MoFA will
                      additionally ensure E&S ToRs are included in the TAs for feasibility studies, PPP package and Road Fund reform (type
                      1&2 TAs under OESRC Advisory Note- TA and the ESF, May 21, 2019.) in a manner consistent with ESF.

                      ESS10 - Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure                                                        Relevant

                      Primary stakeholders include the public, who will benefit from improved feeder roads, and markets, and the private

                      meeting statutory mandates. Engagement will start at concept and continue throughout implementation, with
                      attention to women and vulnerable groups, ensuring adequate engagement and timely disclosure. A SEP will be

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                              Relevant

                      The project is expected to engage; a) Direct Workers that include staff directly employed by the AITs to manage and
                      oversee the project ; b) Contracted Workers who include third-party contractors likely be engaged for civil works c)
                      Primary Supply Workers: Workers providing essential materials and goods for the project’s core functions. Should the
                      project include community-driven components or labor contributions from local communities, community workers
                      may also be engaged (this will be confirmed during project appraisal) and; d) Where government civil servants are
                      involved in project management their terms will be governed by national law but OHS and GRM requirements under
                      ESS2 apply. An LMP will be developed to set out procedures for labor management and workers GRM.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                               Relevant

                      During construction, earthworks and material handling may cause dust and air pollution, while runoff, spills, and
                      higher water demand can threaten water resources. Construction will also generate multiple waste streams that

                      require proper management. The sourcing and use of sand, aggregates, timber, and water can create environmental
                      and social impacts on nearby communities if not well controlled, and poorly sited sanitary facilities (e.g., latrines,
                      septic systems) could contaminate groundwater. The project may also produce GHG emissions from diesel use in

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                               Relevant

                      Key risks include traffic and road safety hazards (especially for vulnerable users), higher risk of accidents from
                      construction activities, and minor labor influx leading to risks of sexual exploitation and abuse (SEA/SH), and
                      communicable diseases. To mitigate these risks, the project will develop and enforce Traffic Management Plans
                      (TMPs) and conduct Road Safety Audits to address traffic and pedestrian safety and implement community
                      awareness campaigns on road safety. The MRH will also apply robust labor management procedures, including codes
                      of conduct and accessible grievance mechanisms, to prevent and respond to GBV/SEA/SH. A SEA-SH Action Plan will
                      be prepared and annexed to the ESMF.

                      ESS5 - Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement                                   Relevant

                      Activities related to feeder road rehabilitation and small agriculture facilities may result in impacts related to land
                      acquisition or restrictions on land use with potential for physical or economic displacement for communities trading
