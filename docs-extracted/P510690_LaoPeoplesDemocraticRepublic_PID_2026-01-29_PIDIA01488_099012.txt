                           B. Introduction and Context
                          Country Context

                           1. The Lao People’s Democratic Republic (PDR) is a lower middle-income, landlocked country in Southeast Asia,
                              characterized by a natural resource-based economy and a tropical climate with pronounced monsoon seasons.
                              Despite rapid economic growth driven by foreign investment in mining, hydropower, and transport, poverty
                              reduction has lagged behind, constrained by limited job creation and modest tax revenues. Laos faces persistent

                                                                                                                                             Page 2

                               macroeconomic instability, with sharp currency depreciation, rising inflation, and mounting debt distress further
                               limiting fiscal space for development. The country’s vulnerability is heightened by recurrent natural disasters—
                               primarily floods and storms—which have impacted millions and caused annual losses of up to 4.1 percent of GDP.
                               Climate change is intensifying these risks, with projections of higher temperatures and more extreme rainfall
                               events, increasing the frequency and severity of flooding and landslides, especially in urban and riverine areas.

                           2. Rapid urbanization has further exposed Laos’ population and infrastructure to disaster risks, as unregulated
                              growth and weak spatial planning have led to settlements in flood-prone areas and the loss of natural flood
                              buffers. The government has responded with multi-pronged strategies to enhance resilience, including the
                              Southeast Asia Disaster Risk Management (SEADRM) Project, which has delivered flood protection infrastructure,
                              modernized hydrometeorological networks, and provided disaster insurance payouts. Building on these efforts,
                              the proposed SEADRM II Project aims to strengthen disaster preparedness and resilience by prioritizing
                              investments in vulnerable urban areas, improving early warning systems, and sustaining financial protection
                              mechanisms. These initiatives are essential for safeguarding communities and critical infrastructure, and for
                              enabling the government to respond effectively to the recurring threat of natural disasters.

                           Sectoral and Institutional Context




_For Official Use Only_
                          3. The Government of Laos (GoL) has made significant progress in integrating disaster risk management (DRM) and
                             climate resilience into national and sectoral strategies, as reflected in the National Socio-Economic Development
                             Plans (NSEDPs), the Green Growth Strategy 2030, and the National Strategy on Climate Change. Legislative
                             frameworks such as the Disaster Risk Management Law (2019) and the Climate Change Decree (2019) provide the
                             legal foundation for DRM and climate adaptation, while the National Strategy for Disaster Risk Reduction (NSDRR)
                             2021–2030 underpins emergency preparedness and response. These policies prioritize the development of climate-
                             and disaster-resilient infrastructure, improved hydrometeorological and early warning systems, and enhanced
                             institutional and financial readiness for disaster response.

                          4. The institutional landscape for climate and disaster risk management in Laos is complex, with responsibilities
                             distributed across multiple ministries. The Ministry of Finance (MOF) is central to funding disaster management and
                             ensuring that risk reduction measures are embedded in government-wide planning. The Ministry of Agriculture and
                             Environment (MAE), through its Department of Meteorology and Hydrology (DMH), manages hydromet monitoring
                             and flood forecasting, while the Ministry of Public Works and Transport (MPWT) oversees urban planning,
                             infrastructure rehabilitation, and flood control. The Department of Waterways (DOW) and the Department of
                             Housing and Urban Planning (DHUP) play key roles in riverbank protection and urban regulation. Despite these
                             efforts, investment needs remain substantial, and budget constraints have limited the realization of planned
                             interventions, prompting reliance on development partners such as the World Bank.

                          5. While DMH has upgraded its hydromet observation network, operational gaps persist due to limited maintenance,
                             technical capacity, and financial resources. Only a fraction of monitoring stations are fully functional, and the lack of
                             effective end-to-end early warning systems hampers disaster preparedness. The Department of Social Welfare
                             (DSW) coordinates early warning dissemination, but significant improvements are needed to provide timely and
                             accurate early warning service to at-risk communities. Financially, the GoL faces severe exposure to disaster impacts,
                             with contingent liabilities far exceeding available funds, leading to heavy reliance on international assistance. Recent
                             initiatives, such as the National Financial Protection Strategy and participation in the Southeast Asia Disaster Risk

                              Insurance Facility (SEADRIF), have begun to address these gaps, but stronger coordination and sustainable financing
                              mechanisms are needed to ensure effective disaster response and resilience.

                          6. The proposed project aims to continue advancing the integrated approach to disaster risk management adopted by
                             the SEADRM project and is designed to strengthen and expand interventions of the ongoing project in three focus
                             areas: urban resilience, early warning service delivery, and financial protection. This will be achieved through (i)
                             targeted investments in flood protection infrastructure in key urban areas; (ii) support for effective operations of the
                             hydromet monitoring network and timely issuance of early warnings; and (iii) assistance for securing SEADRIF or
                             equivalent disaster risk insurance product through premium financing. In the short to medium term, the project will
                             reduce the exposure of communities and assets in target urban areas to flooding and strengthen the capacities of
                             national and local authorities to manage flood risks, enhancing urban resilience. It will provide support for optimal
                             functioning of the hydromet monitoring network and improved early warning dissemination. It will also expand
                             access to disaster risk insurance and strengthen mechanisms for the timely triggering and disbursement of financial
                             resources, enhancing financial resilience to disasters. Over time, these changes will contribute to the long-term
                             development outcomes of reducing impacts of flooding in target areas, enhancing the Government’s capacity to
                             provide hydrometeorological services and disaster response and manage the financial impacts from disasters.

                           D. Project Description
                          7. The SEADRM II project is designed to build on the achievements and lessons learned from its predecessor,
                              addressing critical gaps in disaster risk management and climate resilience in Laos. With a total budget of US$60
                              million financed by the International Development Association (IDA), SEADRM II aims to strengthen flood protection,
                              enhance hydrometeorological monitoring and early warning systems, and improve financial planning for disaster
                              resilience. The project is structured around five components: (1) Integrated Urban Flood Risk Management (US$46
                              million), (2) Strengthening Hydromet Monitoring and Early Warning Systems (US$2 million), (3) Financial Planning
                              for Disaster Resilience (US$9 million), (4) Project Management and Coordination (US$3 million), and (5) Contingency
                              Emergency Response Component (CERC; US$0 allocation) for rapid response to eligible emergencies.

                          8. Component 1 focuses on investments to reduce flood risks in key urban areas, including flood protection
                             infrastructure in Muang Xay, Luang Prabang, and Paksan, as well as technical assistance for Vientiane and strategic

                              planning for Luang Namtha district. Nature-based solutions and participatory planning are emphasized, with support
                              for feasibility studies, technical designs, and compensation for affected households. Component 2 strengthens the
                              hydromet network managed by the Department of Meteorology and Hydrology (DMH), ensuring optimal functioning
                              of monitoring stations and piloting technology-driven early warning dissemination. Component 3 enables the
                              Government of Laos to access market-based disaster risk financing instruments, such as insurance, and supports
                              institutional reforms for efficient post-disaster resource management and adaptive social protection mechanisms.
                              Component 4 provides project management and coordination, housed at the Ministry of Finance, supporting
                              monitoring, evaluation, procurement, and financial management for the project’s activities. It also includes technical
                              support for mainstreaming disaster risk management in national development planning. Component 5, the CERC,
                              allows for the rapid reallocation of uncommitted project funds in response to major crises or disasters. The project’s
                              design incorporates lessons from previous implementation, prioritizes stakeholder engagement, and aligns with
                              national strategies for disaster risk reduction and climate resilience, ensuring a comprehensive and adaptive
                              approach to safeguarding communities and infrastructure in Laos.

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts

                          9. The overall Environmental and Social Risk of SEADRM II is classified as "Substantial" considering that the project will
                             finance civil works for flood protection infrastructure such as riverbank protection embankments, canal
                             improvements, water gates with pumping stations, river-side parks and tree-planting across target provinces.
                             Environmental risk is mainly associated with construction activities and includes Occupational Health and Safety
                             (OHS) of project personnel; traffic accidents due to the transportation of construction materials and operation of
                             project’s vehicles, and machines; physical and health impact on public spaces and local communities caused by
                             pollution generated from construction activities; sedimentation and erosion due to site clearance, operation, and
                             management of quarries and borrow pits; other hazards due to the operation of worker’s camps and maintenance
                             of project equipment and machinery; and health and safety of local communities who live and work near the
                             construction sites.

                          10. Social risk is primarily related to potential resettlement; land acquisition and economic displacement, particularly in
                              villages located along riverbanks where the works will be undertaken; OHS and CHS risks. During the construction,
                              CHS concerns include the risk of children drowning incidents, road safety risk, livelihood and business disruptions
                              and reduced access to rivers and water sources used for daily livelihood activities (e.g., washing, bathing, fishing).
                              Labor influx may elevate risks of SEA/SH, and potential risk of communicable diseases (air, wastewater born, sexually
                              transmitted diseases). SS-ESMP will include measures to manage the risks due to community exposure to
                              construction and labor influx related risks, such as signage, guards or flagmen to prevent unauthorized persons
                              especially children from entering to the construction area traffic management plans, public awareness raising
                              campaign, heath care protocols, grievance mechanism and emergency response procedures. Site layout and

                           E. Implementation

                           Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
                          12. The project will be implemented through existing GoL structures of line ministries, similar to the ongoing SEADRM
                              project's implementation arrangements. However, in view of the merger of the Ministry of Planning and Investment
                              (MPI) with MOF and based on implementation experience from the SEADRM project, a more streamlined
                              implementation structure with two Project Management Units (PMUs) led by MPWT and MOF has been designed.
                              The PMU at DOW-MPWT will fully oversee the implementation of Component 1, which will be undertaken by
                              Project Implementation Units (PIUs) at the DPWTs of BKX, LPB and ODX. It will also facilitate technical coordination
                              with DHUP and the Urban Planning Departments of Vientiane Capital and Luang Namtha district on activities carried
                              out in these cities. This PMU’s fiduciary management will be coordinated with the Department of Planning and
                              Finance (DPF), MPWT and environmental and social (E&S) safeguards compliance and monitoring with the Public
                              Works and Transport Institute (PTI), MPWT to utilize their specialized skills in the respective areas.

                          13. The second PMU at DOP, MOF will oversee overall project management and coordination, fiduciary management,
                              auditing, and E&S safeguards compliance of Components 2, 3, 4 and 5 and implementation of activities financed
                              under Component 4. A PIU at DMH, MAE will be responsible for implementing Component 2 and a PIU at FRD for
                              implementing Component 3.
