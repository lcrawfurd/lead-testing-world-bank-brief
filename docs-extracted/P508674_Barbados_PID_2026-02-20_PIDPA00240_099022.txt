 B. Introduction and Context
  Country Context


1. Barbados is a Small Island Developing State (SIDS) with a population of approximately 269,000 and a highly service-
oriented economy strongly dependent on tourism for income generation, export earnings, and employment. Tourism
supports approximately one-third of total employment and represents a major share of GDP and exports. Agriculture and
fisheries contribute modestly—around one percent of GDP and about three percent of employment—yet their economic
and social importance far exceeds their monetary value, being essential for food security and rural livelihoods. As a small
island economy with geographic isolation and reliance on a narrow set of service-oriented activities, Barbados remains
structurally vulnerable to external shocks. Economic performance and labor market outcomes are closely tied to tourism,
related transport and accommodation services, utilities, and agri-food supply chains. Disruptions to air and sea
connectivity, water and energy provision, coastal and marine conditions, or visitor perceptions can rapidly affect visitor
arrivals, firm revenues, food availability and prices, and fiscal stability, translating into job losses, reduced working hours,
and income volatility across the economy.


  Sectoral and Institutional Context
2. Climate-driven threats to water security pose a systemic risk to Barbados’ economic stability, jobs, and long-term
resilience. As one of the most water-scarce countries in the world, Barbados relies on groundwater for approximately 86
percent of its potable supply, with withdrawals already approaching safe yield limits. Climate change is intensifying drought
frequency, rainfall variability, saline intrusion, rising temperatures, and coastal flooding, further reducing aquifer recharge
and threatening supply reliability. Recent drought episodes have recorded rainfall deficits of up to 35–40 percent below
long-term averages, and modeling suggests groundwater yields could decline significantly by mid-century under certain
scenarios. Given the island’s limited surface storage capacity and heavy dependence on groundwater, climate variability
rapidly translates into supply stress. Coastal infrastructure—including hotels, utilities, and wastewater assets—is also
exposed to storm surge and sea level rise, compounding economic vulnerability in tourism-dependent areas. Water
insecurity has direct macroeconomic implications: approximately one-quarter of GDP and 44 percent of employment are
concentrated in water-dependent sectors, particularly tourism, which accounts for over 20 percent of GDP. Disruptions to

water supply, marine ecosystem degradation, and exposure of coastal infrastructure to storm surge and sea level rise
therefore directly affect productivity, fiscal stability, and livelihoods.

3. Beyond climate pressures, structural and institutional constraints further undermine Barbados’ water security. The
Barbados Water Authority (BWA), the sole provider of water supply and sanitation services, faces longstanding challenges
related to aging infrastructure, high levels of non-revenue water (NRW, estimated at approximately 55 percent), limited
asset management systems, and capacity constraints. Although BWA has established a dedicated NRW unit and initiated
district metered areas, water losses remain high and maintenance practices largely reactive. Metering coverage is
extensive, yet automation, data integration, and digital systems are not fully optimized, affecting operational efficiency,
billing accuracy, and customer service performance. Rising energy costs associated with groundwater pumping and
desalination further strain finances, while limited internal financing capacity constrains the scale of capital investments
required to modernize infrastructure, reduce losses, expand wastewater services, and strengthen climate resilience.
Institutional limitations also affect water resources management. While BWA’s Water Resources and Environmental
Management Unit (WREMU) holds a central mandate for groundwater management and well licensing, monitoring
networks, data systems, and inter-institutional coordination remain insufficient to support fully evidence-based resource
planning. Gaps in standardized data protocols and limited oversight of private abstractions constrain the effectiveness of
emerging tools, including groundwater modeling. Strengthening water resources governance, improving data systems, and
enhancing coordination across sectors—particularly agriculture, energy, tourism, coastal management, and climate
resilience—are critical in a context of extreme water scarcity. Addressing these operational and institutional constraints
requires modernization of organizational structures, digital systems, and performance management frameworks.
Strengthening technical capacity, improving data transparency, and embedding accountability mechanisms will be essential
to transform BWA into a more efficient, financially sustainable, and customer-focused utility capable of delivering reliable
services under increasing climate stress.

4. Inadequate wastewater management in Barbados threatens critical groundwater and coastal resources. Only 15
percent of the population is sewered with remaining households reliant on septic tanks or suck wells often serviced
informally with limited oversight of effluent conveyance and disposal. BWA is currently upgrading two wastewater
treatment plants to tertiary level to facilitate treated water reuse, one of the few utilities in the region to take such steps.
However, the need to expand sewerage coverage persists (including exploring lower-cost technologies) and to ensure that
septic tanks are properly managed with collected waste conveyed for treatment at designated sewage treatment plants
(STPs). Poor wastewater and septage management are major sources of groundwater and coastal contamination in
Barbados, as approximately 80 percent of the island is highly vulnerable to aquifer pollution, particularly from percolation
of nitrogen and phosphates into groundwater. These nutrients reach coastal waters through subsurface flow, thereby
negatively affecting the health of marine ecosystems.

5. Recognizing the severe constraints posed by Barbados’ water scarcity context and the need to enhance the reliability
and quality of water and sanitation services, the Government of Barbados (GoB) has elevated water security as a national
priority. It has acknowledged the need for substantial institutional strengthening within BWA to ensure that service delivery
keeps pace with climate pressures and expected service quality. To advance this objective, the GoB has established a high-
level BWA Transformation Committee tasked with preparing and guiding a comprehensive utility turnaround. The
subsequently developed BWA 2025-2030 Strategic Plan (SP) aims to modernize BWA’s systems, processes, and
organizational culture, and strengthen its strategic and operational capabilities.

    PforR Program Scope
6. The Barbados Water Security and Sector Performance Program-for-Results (PforR) supports BWA’s Strategic Plan
(SP, 2025-2030), which seeks to position the BWA as a fit for purpose, 21st century water utility that is able to deliver
equitable and reliable access to water for citizens. The Program constitutes Phase I of the Caribbean Water Security Multi-
Phased Approach (MPA). The Program comprises three results areas (RAs).

7. Results Area 1 – Building BWA capacity for well-performing service delivery. This RA, aligned with MPA’s Pillar 2 –
Strengthening Utility Performance and Service Delivery, will support the modernization of BWA’s operations and its
comprehensive capacity building towards more efficient service delivery. The efforts include utility-wide digital integration
with modernization of systems, processes, and procedures. Digital integration, including cross-cutting financial and
commercial systems, NRW and service delivery management, improved customer engagement, optimization of metering
management, professionalization of technical operations, and asset management. The bulk of the results are to
be achieved by BWA joining efforts with an experienced WSS operator (could be in association with a specialized
consultancy firm) through a Modernization Partner (MP) contract that will have clear targets on performance and service
delivery outcomes. The DLIs/DLRs for this RA include:

    • DLI 1: Improved BWA services continuity capacity and reduced water losses: This DLI promotes a shift toward a
    modern, data-driven operations, towards improved operational performance through: (i) the development and
    approval of a robust disaster recovery and business continuity framework for service reliability during climate and
    operational shocks (DLR 1.1); (ii) the operationalization of 24 priority District-Metered Areas (DMAs) (DLR
    1.2), encompassing about 18,000 water connections; (iii) the modernization of 80,000 customer water meters to smart
    meters, fully integrated with BWA’s central data systems (DLR 1.3); and (iv) the achievement of 30 percent or less NRW
    (physical and commercial losses) in the target DMAs (DLR 1.4).

    • DLI 2: Improved BWA customer responsiveness and financial performance: This DLI will incentivize the strengthening
    of BWA’s commercial and financial performance through three complementary DLRs. DLR 2.1 will incentivize the
    establishment and full operationalization of a Digital Customer Portal with an updated customer database and system
    generation of commercial reports, integrated with a Commercial Management System. DLR 2.2 will incentivize customer
    service by rewarding incremental improvements in BWA’s responsiveness to customer complaints per regulatory
    standards. DLR 2.3 will drive long-term financial sustainability and creditworthiness improvement by incentivizing
    improvements in operating revenues.

    • DLI 3: BWA’s organizational reform, systems digitalization, and staff enhancement program are implemented: This
    DLI incentivizes a comprehensive strengthening of BWA’s institutional capacity, performance management, and
    digital modernization. DLR 3.1 will drive improved service performance oversight through the signing of a Performance
    and Service Level Agreement with the government and the annual publication of a performance report. DLR 3.2 will
    incentivize BWA’s digital transformation by supporting the operationalization of three priority modules
    (Technical, Financial and WRM1) of the integrated utility-wide Management Information System (MIS). DLR 3.3 will
    incentivize organizational reform and capacity building through approval and roll out of a new BWA organizational
    structure clarifying roles, responsibilities, accountability across the utility.

8. Results Area 2 – Pollution management through country-wide safely managed sanitation services. This RA, aligned
with MPA Pillar 3 – Pollution management through safely managed sanitation, will support the reduction of pollution
loads reaching the ocean and the aquifer by incentivizing improvements in safely managed sanitation services. This RA will
also support establishing water reuse and reforming septage management regulations. The DLI/DLRs for this RA include:

  • DLI 4: Reduced unsafe disposal of untreated wastewater and septage: This DLI will incentivize regulatory reforms
  (DLR 4.1), improved sewerage coverage using lower-cost technologies (DLR 4.2), and strengthened septage
  management (DLR 4.3).


9. Results Area 3 – Foundations for long term water security. This RA, aligned with MPA Pillar 4 – Building foundations
for long term water security, resilience, and disaster response, will support improvements in the GoB’s capacity to manage
its water resources sustainably and adapt to climate variability. This RA will support the establishment of a high-level
coordination mechanism across relevant sectors, operationalization of the WWMC and integrate water information
systems with utility modernization efforts45. The DLI/DLRs for this RA include:

  • DLI 5: Implemented institutional, legal and technical mechanisms for water security management and reporting: This
  DLI will incentivize critical institutional and regulatory reforms as well as adoption of digital systems for improved
  national water governance and groundwater resource management. DLR 5.1 will incentivize BWA for the establishment
  of a high-level coordination mechanism for water security as well as for strengthened regulations for the Water and
  Wells committee’s functioning, including the operationalization of provisions to support actions associated with drought
  management; update of the national wells monitoring and inventory linked to the Water Resources Information
  Management System. DLR 5.2 incentivizes the publication of an annual water resources status report

 C. Proposed Program Development Objective(s)

 10. To enhance water security, manage pollution, and improve the performance of water services in Barbados.
 PDO-level results indicators are as follows:
  • Improved operational performance of BWA for water service delivery
  • People provided with safely managed sanitation services
  • People benefiting from climate-resilient planning, preparation, surveillance and/or response


 D. Environmental and Social Effects

11. The overall environmental and social (E&S) risk of the Program is moderate. None of the activities fall under the
policy exclusion definition in Paragraph 14 of the World Bank's guidance on Environmental and Social Systems Assessments
(ESSA). The Program design and screening processes exclude actions and activities that would have significant adverse
impacts that are sensitive, diverse, or unprecedented on the environment and/or affected people, and the Program will
not finance: (i) works that require land acquisition; (ii) works within or adjacent to protected areas, marine reserves, coastal
buffer zones, or designated ecologically sensitive sites; (iii) works in zones prone to coastal erosion, landslides, or flooding;
or (iv) works involving the use of prohibited materials or technologies, such as asbestos-containing materials, lead-based
paints, or hazardous chemicals prohibited under international conventions.

12. The Program is anticipated to deliver significant positive outcomes for both the people of Barbados and the
environment. It will enhance service reliability and improve environmental management through reduced groundwater
extraction and upgraded wastewater management, which will help protect water resources, decrease pollution, and
promote public health. By improving water conservation and reducing energy consumption, Barbados’s water system will
be more robust and efficient, while the Program will also build the capacity of institutions and staff, ensuring better
management, engagement with consumers, and oversight of water and sanitation services.


13. The main social risks are related to interactions with the community during small civil works as well as engagement
with broader society and BWA staff. They include temporary access disturbances, minor and short-term business
disruptions, traffic and mobility impacts, the possibility of chance finds in excavations works, occupational and community
health and safety, digital exclusion of vulnerable users, termination of informal or unregistered connections, gaps in
communication and stakeholder engagement, sexual exploitation and abuse/harassment (SEA/SH), and potential concern
amongst BWA staff due to organizational changes. From an environmental standpoint, although activities are confined to
small-scale works within established rights-of-way, risks include dust, noise, minor soil disturbance, waste generation,
temporary community inconveniences, and occupational hazards related to excavation, traffic, and heat exposure.


14. The Bank has developed an Environmental Social System Assessment (ESSA) for the Program. The ESSA finds that
Barbados’ national systems are well-aligned with World Bank PforR core principles, especially in areas
like environmental protection, worker safety, and grievance mechanism. However, there are gaps in BWA’s institutional
capacity and operational procedures, particularly in staffing, standardized Environmental Codes of Practice (ECoPs),
standard operating procedures (SOPs), and monitoring systems. The program includes the following E&S actions agreed
with the BWA: (i) strengthen the E&S capacity of the BWA by engaging professionals to focus on E&S risk management
during the implementation and build E&S capacity of BWA staff; (ii) develop and agree with the Bank a screening checklist
to identify environmental risks as well as E&S requirements to be included in the Programs' bidding and contracting
documents; (iii) develop standardized ECoPs and SOPs for small-scale water and sanitation works; and (iv) standardize
consultation and disclosure procedures for all water and sanitation small works. The consultation process of the
ESSA included engagements with BWA and other relevant agencies to gather information and discuss findings, including
sessions in November and on December 9, 2025 and January 20, 2026. The draft ESSA has been disclosed on January 30,
2026 (prior to appraisal). Feedback received through different channels, including public consultation will be integrated
into the final ESSA report and recommended actions. The ESSA will be finalized prior to Program negotiations and
publicly disclosed before World Bank Board approval.
15. Grievance Redress. Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected as a result of a Bank
supported PforR operation, as defined by the applicable policy and procedures, may submit complaints to the
existing Program grievance mechanism or the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints
received are promptly reviewed in order to address pertinent concerns. Program affected communities and individuals
may submit their complaint to the Bank’s independent Accountability Mechanism (AM). The AM houses the Inspection
Panel, which determines whether harm occurred, or could occur, as a result of Bank non-compliance with its policies
and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution Service, which provides communities and borrowers with the opportunity to
address complaints through dispute resolution. Complaints may be submitted at any time after concerns have been
brought directly to the Bank's attention, and Bank Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For information
on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS), please visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS.
For information on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s Accountability Mechanism, please
visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.
