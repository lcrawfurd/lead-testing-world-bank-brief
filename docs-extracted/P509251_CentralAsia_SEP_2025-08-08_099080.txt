1.              INTRODUCTION
1.1             Project Description
The Kyrgyz Republic is a landlocked, lower-middle-income country with rich natural endowments, including minerals,
forests, arable land and pastures, and significant potential for expansion of agriculture, hydroelectricity production, and
tourism.
In October 2021, the Cabinet of Ministers of Kyrgyz Republic (the Government) launched a medium-term development
program for 2021 to 2026 with the goal of “leaving no one behind.” The priority areas of the program include anti-crisis
measures; management reform; establishment of an enabling environment for development; development of key
sectors of the economy, including energy; social development; foreign policy and national security; and special
priorities. Energy is presented as a key sector to be reformed, including through gradual tariff adjustments toward cost
recovery, exploiting the country’s renewable energy potential, modernization of sector assets, and transition to the
wholesale electricity market.
In the recently prepared Energy White Paper that lays out the pillars of reform direction, the Government has set
ambitious targets for renewable energy deployment. Under the third pillar of the white paper, the Government seeks
to procure 700 megawatts of solar capacity and as much as 100 megawatts of small hydropower plant capacity by the
end of 2030, and it has accelerated the preparation of large HPPs such as Kambarata-1, which would take a decade to
develop and construct, to support the ambitious transformation of the energy sector.
The proposed Kambarata-1 Hydro Power Plant Sustainable and Transformational Energy Program (K1-STEP) supports
the Government and Central Asia countries to sustain strong economic growth, enhance energy security and advance
the clean energy transition. The K1-STEP could play a major role in meeting the country’s renewable energy targets,
facilitating renewable energy integration, and mitigating climate change impacts.
The Kambarata-1 Hydropower Plant (K1-HPP) is a transformational greenfield clean energy project with substantial
domestic and regional benefits aimed at bolstering energy security, accelerating the clean energy transition, improving
water management and strengthening the regional cooperation in Central Asia. The proposed plant’s installed capacity
of 1,880 megawatts could reach an average annual generation of 5,961 gigawatt hours, which is equivalent to 1,073
megawatts of coal-/gas-fired power plants operating at a 60 percent capacity factor. It is estimated to avoid emission
of about 4.8 million tons of CO2 per year from coal plants or about 2.5 million tons of CO2 from gas power plants.
The K1-STEP could also address the country’s energy security challenge, meeting export obligations and realizing export
revenues, while improving downstream HPP operations and water management. The development of renewables will
greatly reduce the country’s reliance on imported fossil fuels in winter while providing the country with additional
export revenue.
The K1-STEP is being prepared under the World Bank’s Environment and Social Framework (ESF).

1.2             Project Components
The K1-STEP Program is designed in three phases with indicative total funding envelope of US$1,500 million, including
the Phase 1 – US$500 million, from the World Bank to the Kyrgyz Republic, the Republic of Uzbekistan, and the Republic
of Kazakhstan.
Phase 1 of the K1-STEP Program (the Project) will consist of the following components.
      1.   Component 1. Construction works. This component will finance the early works such as diversion tunnels, dam,
           powerhouse and substation pre-excavation, site installation. It will also finance main civil works, including the
           mobilization of main civil works contractor, plus the equipment contract, including basic design, four turbines
           plus additional downpayment to book manufacturing facilities for main equipment.
      2.   Component 2. Project institutional setup, design, management and supervision. This component will finance
           the Project Management Consultancy (PMC), project implementation support, and the operationalization of
           the Kambarata-1 Regional JV.

1.3             Project Environmental and Social Risks/Impacts
Potential environmental risks, related to the K1-HPP, include permanent inundation of the reservoir area and changes
in landscapes; alteration of flow in some stretches of the Naryn River, its water quality, and hydromorphology, including
sediment transport; and changes in terrestrial and aquatic biodiversity in ecosystems dependent on it.
The works and inundation of the reservoir area are expected to have potential adverse risks and impacts on both
terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems and biodiversity, including potential cumulative impacts and transboundary impacts.
The variety of impacts of identified project activities, which include irreversible impacts as well as those that would alter
the natural flows and terrain significantly, augmented further by the need for more robust management due to the
complexities associated with project impacts and implementation, even with the current familiarity and experience of
the Borrower with ESF requirements, have led to environmental risk being rated as high.
The envisioned social risks of the project refer to limited stakeholder engagement, project information disclosure, and
the need for an effective grievance mechanism for handling complaints. Labor influx is expected to be significant for
around 8.5 years, with a peak of 7,000 workers, creating challenges for this low-density area.
Also, even if the initial social screening has shown that there are only a few permanent human settlements in the dam
site and impounded area, every year, shepherd families move with their herds from villages to the pastures located in
the project area.
Because of it, the project would likely cause the displacement of some structures used for seasonal animal husbandry
and temporary residence, limit the use and access to some seasonal pastures, potential economic displacement due to
more limited of access to pasturelands for communities living around the project area impacting local livelihood. For
the above reasons, the social risks have been classified as substantial.

1.4               Purpose and Objectives of the Stakeholder Engagement Plan
The overall objective of this SEP is to:
      •    Define a program for stakeholder engagement, including public information disclosure, grievance redress
           mechanism and consultation, throughout the implementation and operation of the proposed Project.
      •    Outline the ways in which K1-PMO and contractors will communicate with stakeholders and include a
           mechanism by which people can raise concerns, provide feedback, or make complaints about the project,
           contractors, and the Project itself.
The consultation and engagement with the local population is essential to the success of the Project in order to ensure
smooth collaboration between project staff and local communities and to minimize and mitigate environmental and
social risks related to the proposed Project.
The scope of the SEP is to assess the level of stakeholder interest and support for the Project, enable stakeholder views
to be taken into account in project design and environmental and social performance, promote and provide means for
inclusive engagement throughout the project life-cycle. This will ensure that appropriate project information is disclosed
to stakeholders in a timely, understandable, accessible, and appropriate manner and format, provide citizens with
accessible and inclusive means to raise issues and grievances and enable the project implementing entity to respond to
and manage such grievances

1.5               Beneficiaries
Beneficiaries of the K1-STEP are the electricity consumers, including industrial, commercial, and residential customers.
The newly constructed hydropower plant will help increase power supply in the Kyrgyz Republic and improve the quality
of power in the country. In addition, the local population is expected to benefit to a certain extent from employment
opportunities during the construction and operation phases.
The implementing agencies will also benefit significantly from the capacity building and technical assistance they will
receive, which will improve their capacity to plan, develop, manage and maintain existing hydropower systems and
services. Consequently, they will be able to deliver better, more reliable, and cost-effective services to customers.

1.6               Non-Beneficiary Stakeholders
The project must also engage with non-beneficiary stakeholders during the course of implementation of programs. Non
beneficiary stakeholders include non-governmental organizations (NGOs), academics institutions, Local institutes
including vocational training institutes and focal points initiatives by multilateral agencies, youth and women groups,
cooperatives and media. These stakeholders can bring their expertise regarding protection of biodiversity, human rights,
labor rights, cultural heritage etc. Women and youth groups can also play a key role in advocating for community
interests, participating in project-related training and employment opportunities, and driving initiatives for social and
environmental awareness in the project area.

2.              REGULATIONS AND REQUIREMENTS
2.1             Kyrgyz Republic Requirements
This SEP considers the following existing institutional and regulatory framework of the Kyrgyz Republic.
Constitution of the Kyrgyz Republic (May 5, 2021): The Constitution guarantees the right of citizens to participate in
discussions and decisions of national and local importance, allowing citizens to participate in legislative and political
processes. The Constitution protects the rights of people with disabilities, the elderly and other vulnerable groups and
ensures the functioning of a social protection system that promotes the full and equal exercise of human and civil rights.
It aims to enable social integration without discrimination and the creation of an accessible environment for people
with disabilities
Each citizen has the right to freely seek, receive, store, use information and distribute it orally, in writing or by other
means. Citizens have also the right to familiarize themselves with information about themselves in government bodies,
local government bodies, institutions and organizations. Everyone has the right to receive information about the
activities of state bodies, local government bodies and their officials, legal entities with the participation of state bodies
and local government bodies, as well as organizations financed from the republican and local budgets. Every citizen is
guaranteed access to information held by state bodies, local government bodies and their officials. The procedure for
providing information is determined by law.
The Law of the Kyrgyz Republic "On the Procedure for Considering Citizens’ Appeals" (May 4, 2007, No. 67, ). In the
Kyrgyz Republic, every citizen has the right to apply personally or through a representative to state bodies, local
government bodies and their officials, who are obliged to give a reasoned response within the time period established
by law.
This law obliges citizens' appeals to be registered, considered and resolved fairly, promptly and responsibly. It requires
that all appeals be officially registered and reviewed fairly, promptly, and transparently (Articles 2 and 4). Every citizen
has the right to appeal in person or through his/her representative to state authorities, local self-government bodies
and their officials, who are required to provide a reasoned response within the time frame established by law.
Citizens may submit appeals either personally or through a representative to state authorities, local government bodies,
or officials, who must provide a reasoned response within the legally defined timeframe (Article 4). Normally,
complaints must be addressed within 14 working days, but in exceptional cases, the review period may be extended to
a maximum of 30 days (Article 8). According to Article 7, officials are required to hold personal receptions of citizens at
least once a week.
Law of the Kyrgyz Republic “On the Fundamentals of Administrative Activities and Administrative Procedures” (July
31, 2015, No. 210). This law regulates the procedure for appealing against refusals to grant citizens the right to access
information. It ensures transparency in the conduct of administrative actions, systematic consideration of complaints,
includes requirements for administrative procedures and documents, various procedural deadlines for both
administrative bodies and applicants, describes the procedure for appealing decisions and actions of administrative
bodies and other procedures.
The Law contains an important provision regarding the application of the related Law "On the Procedure for Considering
Citizens' Appeals", which citizens continue to actively use, but which does not relate to administrative procedures. The
delineation of the spheres of application of the two laws is established by Part 4 of Article 96 of this Law, which provides
that the Law "On the Procedure for Considering Citizens' Appeals" applies only to the consideration of citizens' appeals
not related to the implementation of administrative procedures.
If a citizen disagrees with the decision/action of the Ministry of Energy, they can appeal to the central body of the
Ministry by submitting a complaint to the Ministry's leadership. If the citizen is dissatisfied with the Ministry's decision,
they have the right to go to court, if they believe their rights have been violated and administrative measures were not
taken in accordance with the law.
According to Article 115 of the Administrative Procedure Code of the Kyrgyz Republic, the court may return the claim if
the plaintiff has not followed the legally established pre-trial dispute resolution procedure, and the possibility of using
this procedure has not been lost. This means that before going to court, the citizen must go through all the prescribed
stages of pre-trial resolution, including appealing to the state authorities (in this case, the Ministry of Energy) and
receiving an official response.

Law of the Kyrgyz Republic “On the right of access to information” of the Kyrgyz Republic of (December 29, 2023
No.217). The law contains provisions aimed at implementing and protecting the right of everyone to access information
held by information holders, including government agencies and local governments. It is aimed at achieving maximum
information openness, transparency and accountability in their activities, including through the form of open data
(access to information via the Internet, Open Government). The law provides for the disclosure of information on the
activities of government agencies, current decisions and official events, ensuring access to documents, etc.
This law regulates the procedure for appealing against refusals of citizens’ rights to access information. It ensures that
administrative actions are conducted transparently and that grievances are addressed systematically and includes
requirements for administrative procedures and documents, various procedural deadlines for both administrative
bodies and applicants, describes the procedure for appealing decisions and actions of administrative bodies, and other
procedures. Furthermore, the Law contains an important provision regarding the application of the related Law “On the
Procedure for Considering Citizens’ Appeals,” which does not pertain to administrative procedures. The delineation of
the scope of application of the two laws is established by part 4 of article 96 of this Law, which stipulates that the Law
“On the Procedure for Considering Citizens’ Appeals” applies only to the consideration of citizens’ appeals that are not
related to the implementation of administrative procedures.
Law "On Regulatory Legal Acts" (July 20, 2009, No. 24). This law provides a framework for public discussion of draft
normative legal acts affecting the interests of citizens and legal entities by posting them on the official website of the
rule-making body. Draft normative legal acts of local government bodies may be posted in special places determined
by the representative body of local government.
According to Article 22, draft regulatory legal acts that directly affect the interests of citizens and legal entities, as well
as regulate entrepreneurial activity, are subject to public discussion by posting on the official website of the rule-making
body. Draft regulatory legal acts of representative bodies of local self-government may be posted in special places
(stands, stands) determined by the representative body of local self-government.
The regulatory body discloses contact information (address, including e-mail address, telephone numbers for receiving
fax messages), as well as the last name, first name, patronymic and contact information of the person responsible for
receiving proposals and comments, together with the draft regulatory act.
Regulations on local state administration and local government bodies (October 20, 2021, No. 123). According to
Article 7, Local self-government is carried out in the form of representative and direct participation of the local
community in local self-government. One of the forms of participation of the local community in the implementation of
local self-government is the discussion of all important issues of public and local significance at meetings (gatherings)
of members of the local community.
Citizens' meetings (gatherings) are a direct form of population participation in resolving issues of local importance by
discussing them at meetings of citizens living on the territory of one street, one block or village, with the adoption of
recommendations or decisions on them in accordance with the charter of the local community.
In order to take into account, the opinion of members of the local community on important issues, meetings (gatherings)
and public hearings are held with the participation of members of the local community with the adoption of
recommendations on them. The recommendations of meetings (gatherings) are considered with the participation of
representatives (delegates) of the relevant meetings (gatherings) (Article 63).
Citizens have the right to contact local government bodies and their officials, as well as to receive information about
the activities of local government bodies in accordance with the law. The local government administration of the district
is obliged to ensure interaction with civil society and the media.
Law of the Kyrgyz Republic "On Personal Information" dated April 14, 2008, No. 58. The law is aimed at legal regulation
of personal data on the basis of generally accepted international principles and norms, the Constitution of the Kyrgyz
Republic and in order to ensure the protection of human rights and freedoms associated with the collection, processing
and use of personal data.
Law "On Environmental Expertise" (June 16, 1999, No. 54): This law requires that public opinion be taken into account
when conducting environmental assessments. The purpose of public consultations is to inform the public about planned
activities, identify alternatives and determine all aspects of possible environmental impacts in order to obtain the most
objective information.
According to Article 4 of the Law on Environmental Expertise, one of the principles is taking into account public opinion.

2.2             International Bodies and Conventions
Both the Kyrgyz Republic and the Republic of Kazakhstan are Parties to two key international environmental
agreements: the Convention on Environmental Impact Assessment in a Transboundary Context (Espoo Convention,
1991) and the Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-Making and Access to Justice in
Environmental Matters (Aarhus Convention, 1998). Uzbekistan and Tajikistan are also parties to the Aarhus convention
but not to the Espoo convention.

The Espoo Convention requires that countries assess the environmental impacts of certain activities at an early stage of
planning and notify and consult with any Party which it considers may be an affected Party when a proposed activity is
likely to have a significant adverse transboundary environmental impact. This includes provisions for:
      •   Early notification to potentially affected Parties;
      •   Public participation in both the originating and affected partiess;
      •   Environmental impact assessment procedures aligned with international standards;
      •   Consultation mechanisms between states to avoid or mitigate transboundary harm.
This framework is particularly relevant in projects involving shared water resources, energy infrastructure, or activities
near international borders such as the K1-HPP, ensuring that neighbouring countries are informed and engaged in
advance of critical project decisions.
The Aarhus Convention complements the Espoo Convention by operationalizing three key pillars:
      •   Access to Environmental Information – the public must be able to obtain timely and relevant environmental
          information held by public authorities;
      •   Public Participation in Environmental Decision-Making – authorities must provide opportunities for public input
          during key stages of decision-making processes, particularly those with environmental implications;
      •   Access to Justice – the public must have access to judicial or administrative procedures to challenge public
          decisions that have been made without proper access to information or participation.
These conventions not only establish legal obligations but also serve as normative benchmarks for best practices in
stakeholder engagement, particularly in ESIA processes. By adhering to the principles embedded in these conventions,
the project aims to ensure transparency, build trust with affected communities, and proactively manage cross-border
environmental and social risks.
As of August 2025, the Kyrgyz Republic is in the process of preparing a formal transboundary notification to the
Government of Kazakhstan in accordance with the Espoo Convention. The Ministry of Energy, in coordination with the
Ministry of Natural Resources, Ecology and Technical Supervision, and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, is finalizing the
necessary documentation.
In line with the Aarhus Convention, the K1-PMO is taking initial steps to ensure transparency and inclusive access to
information. Draft environmental and social documents, including the ESIA and SEP, will be made publicly available on
the Ministry’s official website. Stakeholders from Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Tajikistan will be invited to participate in
riparian consultations planned for September 2025 to ensure that transboundary concerns and suggestions are properly
considered in the finalization of environmental and social instruments.

2.3             World Bank requirements for stakeholder engagement
The World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF)’s Environmental and Social Standard (ESS) 10,
“Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure”, recognizes “the importance of open and transparent
engagement between the Borrower and project stakeholders as an essential element of good international practice”
ESS10 emphasizes that effective stakeholder engagement can significantly improve the environmental and social
sustainability of projects, enhance project acceptance, and make a significant contribution to successful project design
and implementation. As a requirement of this ESS10 Standard this Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) has to be
prepared and will be implemented for the whole K1-STEP Project.
Scope of application. ESS10 applies to all projects supported by the Bank through Investment Project Financing. For the
purpose of this ESS, “stakeholder” refers to individuals or groups who: (a) are affected or likely to be affected by the
project (project-affected parties); and (b) may have an interest in the project (other interested parties).
The Bank standard on Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure requires that the project implementing
agency engages with stakeholders throughout the project life cycle, commencing such engagement as early as possible
in the project development process and in a timeframe that enables meaningful consultations with stakeholders on
project design. The nature, scope and frequency of stakeholder engagement will be proportionate to the nature and
scale of the project and its potential risks and impacts.

3.3             Next Steps of ESIA Consultations
3.3.1           National Level Consultations
The next steps regarding the national level consultation will be as follows:
      1.   Information disclosure
Providing timely and necessary background information of the Project to participants are paramount. It will make sure
the participant receives easy-to-understand information, and asked them to be prepared to provide feedback during
the consultation. Sharing information and receiving feedback graciously is an integral part of communications between
policy- or decision makers and their constituents.
After the consultation, it was always good practice to share the information back with the participants. This information
sharing will lead to inform discussions which will help to improve project and strengthen relationships between
policymakers and people. The Project will repeat and continue sharing of information throughout the project
implementation phase and keep participants engaged, and build trust that can later help to mobilize participation for
other consultation needs.
      2.   Close the feedback loop
Closing the feedback loop by informing participants of what was documented, and what will happen next, is of extreme
importance if national level policymakers anticipate future follow-up discussions. Continuous information sharing until
the project is completed builds trust and signals to participants that their investment in the participatory process was
not a waste of their time.
Through consultations, policymakers showed that citizens are equal partners in the governance process, and this should
not be taken lightly. Recognizing the importance of engaging with people by inviting them to participate should be done
with the right intention, and for the right purpose. Therefore, the Project ensures that Public participation should not

4.              STAKEHOLDER IDENTIFICATION AND ANALYSIS
4.1             Categories of Stakeholders
ESS10 identifies two broad categories of stakeholders: "Project Affected Parties" and "Other Interested Parties". ESS10
also indicates that the Borrower will identify those project-affected parties (individuals or groups) who, because of their
particular circumstances, may be disadvantaged or vulnerable.
Project affected parties (PAPs) are individuals, groups and other parties in the project area who are directly or indirectly,
positively or negatively (actually or potentially) affected by the project and/or who have been identified as the most
susceptible to project-related changes and who should be actively involved in identifying impacts and their significance,
as well as in deciding on mitigation and management measures.
Other interested parties are individuals/groups/organizations who believe or feel that their interests are affected by
the project and/or who have the potential to affect the project outcomes and its implementation process in some way.
Vulnerable groups are individuals who may be disproportionately affected or further disadvantaged by the project(s)
compared to any other groups due to their vulnerable status, and for whom special measures may be required to ensure
their equal representation in consultation and decision-making processes related to the project(s).

4.2             Stakeholders Identification and Prioritization
Stakeholder identification aims to identify and prioritize K1-HPP stakeholders for consultation. Stakeholder
identification is an ongoing process, and thus key stakeholders will continue to be identified during different stages of
the K1-HPP. Stakeholders have also been classified as per their interest and influence on the Project. Interest/influence
has been classified into 3 categories: i) high; ii) medium; and iii) low. This categorization aims to assess the power
relationship, impact, support, and attitude of the various identified stakeholders. The stakeholders are described in
details in the Table 4-1 below.

4.3             Vulnerable groups
Disadvantaged/vulnerable individuals and groups may be affected disproportionately, either adversely or positively, by
the project. Because of low socio-economic profile, their voices may not be heard in the formal decision-making process,
or included in consultation process but their feedback are not solicited in a manner that offers sufficient understanding
of how they are affected, how they would respond, and what support they may need to avoid or mitigate such impact
from their own perspective.
Identification of vulnerable individuals and groups, is an important step to effectively engaging them in the consultation
process throughout the project cycle. This enables project implementing agency (K1-PMO) to work out measures to
avoid adverse impacts, or minimize or mitigate such impacts to vulnerable groups, if avoidance is not possible. Inclusion
of disadvantaged/vulnerable individuals and groups, at the early stage of project planning, can also assist in project
design that provide them opportunities to restore and improve their livelihoods.
Vulnerable groups under the project may include the following categories, among others:
      1) Low-income households and individuals;
      2) The elderly, people of pre-retirement age;
      3) People with disabilities; and
      4) Women headed low-income households.
It is noted that the above categories have been identified based on the current stage of the project. These categories
of vulnerable households will be reconfirmed during the socio-economic survey (SES) conducted as part of the
Resettlement Plans (RPs) preparation, and the list will be updated accordingly as the project progresses.

5.1             Planned Stakeholder Engagement Activities
Stakeholder engagement activities need to provide specific stakeholder groups with relevant information and
opportunities to voice their views on topics that matter to them. The Table 55-1 below presents the stakeholder
engagement activities envisaged under the project. The activity types and their frequency are adapted to the three main
project stages (preparation, implementation and project design; construction; post-construction and operation phase).
The Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) outlines the engagement process, methods, including sequencing, topics of
consultations and target stakeholders. The World Bank and the Borrower do not tolerate reprisals and retaliation
against project stakeholders who share their views about Bank-financed projects.
The project will mostly employ interviews, focus group discussions, open meetings, and workshops as a means of
carrying out consultations. This will be done right in the 10 affected villages to enable easy participation of vulnerable
and disadvantaged groups of people. The strategy will also be different depending on the Project stages as presented
below.
      1.   Project preparation stage (before project appraisal). While some technical details of the K1-HPP will be
           unknown at this stage, the consultation will focus on a broader range of stakeholders to understand their
           general interests and concerns on the project and its potential positive and negative impacts to inform the
           project’s environmental and social frameworks. This is also the stage where potential
           vulnerable/disadvantaged groups relevant to the project will be identified.
      2.   Pre-construction, dam construction stage. At this stage, the technical details of the K1-HPP will be determined
           and specific project-affected parties (PAPs) identified. The consultation will focus on PAPs to address their
           concerns through the implementation of subproject-specific environmental and social plans. The project will
           ensure that the GRM will remain effective during the civil works to promptly address any grievances from the
           PAPs. The K1-PMO will pay particular attention to vulnerable groups to ensure inclusion and non-discrimination
           of such groups.
      3.   Reservoir impoundment. At this stage, the consultation will focus on the PAPs to address their concerns
           regarding the livelihood restoration program implementation and the restoration of access and the dam safety
           and community health and safety. The project will ensure that the GRM will remain effective during the civil
           works to promptly address any grievances from the PAPs. The K1-PMO will pay particular attention to involve
           vulnerable groups in the income restoration program and in the community health and safety.
      4.   Post construction/ maintenance stage. At this stage, the consultation will focus on the feedback from PAPs on
           the performance of environmental and social mitigations conducted during the construction and reservoir
           impoundment stages and in particular on the livelihood restoration program. The consultation on employment
           opportunities for O&M works will be also conducted. Areas for improvement and lessons learned should be
           identified for the following subprojects. Dissemination and reporting back to general stakeholders on the
           outcome of the monitoring and evaluation of the project will be also arranged

5.2             Methods to Be Used
5.2.1           Public consultations / hearings
At commencement of the Project implementation the K1-PMO will organize Project launch meetings at national level,
in each Aiyl Aimak and in the most affected villages. Upon these meetings the E&S K1-PMO specialists will help
organizing public meetings during the entire life cycle of the Project.
During construction and reservoir impoundment, the K1-PMO will organize bi-annual public consultations and quarterly
focus group discussions with project affected parties regarding key Project-related activities to ensure recurring
opportunities of all citizens, including vulnerable groups who will be specifically invited to participate in the focus group
discussions.
Meetings will be open house events where K1-PMO will present information and people will be invited to make
comments and express any concerns. A range of consultations with different stakeholders will be conducted and
documented, and community level meetings will also be conducted with men and women (including vulnerable
households) to better understand needs, expectations, and concerns of these population groups in relation to the
project. The feedback received at these meetings will be documented along with measures that the project will take to
address the feedback received.

5.2.2           Training, workshops
Training on a variety of social issues will be provided to K1-PMO, and possibly other relevant government or non-
government service providers. Issues covered will include sensitization to inclusion/exclusion, labor issues, gender-
based violence risks.

5.2.3           Grievance redress mechanism
 In compliance with the World Bank’s ESS10 and the Kyrgyz practices, a project-specific grievance mechanism will be set
up to handle complaints and issues, and this will be integrated into a country system on GRM that are available to
citizens. Dedicated communication materials (specifically, a GRM brochure or pamphlet) will be developed to help local
residents become familiar with the grievance redress channels and procedures. The K1-PMO will coordinate with
administrative authorities to maintain a grievance register in order to capture and track grievances from submission to
resolution and communication with complainants.
The initial effort to resolve grievances to the complainant’s satisfaction will be undertaken by administrative levels. The
mechanism is described in detail in Chapter 7. Grievances may be submitted anonymously, but in that case, it cannot
be known if the complainant is satisfied with the resolution. K1-PMO website, posters and leaflets will include clear
information on how feedback, questions, comments, concerns and grievances can be submitted by any stakeholder. It
will also provide information on the way grievances will be handled, both in terms of process and deadlines.

5.2.4           Mass/social media communication
K1-PMO staff will be engaged in the K1-HPP in order to post information on the dedicated MoE or Project website and
in social media of the Project, as well as to communicate with the local population via social media campaigns
throughout the Project’s lifecycle. Social media channels will be used as much as possible to disseminate information as
rates of social media (especially, Facebook, Instagram, Telegram, and WhatsApp) use appear to be high across users of
different age and background in project affected communities.

5.2.5           Information Desks
Information desks in each of the affected villages will provide local communities with information on stakeholder
engagement activities, construction updates (status and types of works, dates of reservoir impoundment), contact
details of the Project local representative and etc. Information boards will be posted at villages levels where they will
be easily PAPs, grievance redress mechanism. Brochures and fliers on various related social and environmental issues
will be made available at these information desks.

5.3              Strategy to incorporate views of vulnerable groups
Vulnerable groups will be consulted with using special methods, if necessary. Methods of interaction that will be used
in the Project are described in the sections below.
The website and other social media of the Ministry of Energy will be used for disclosing project documentation, including
SEP/ESIA/RPs, project updates and information, as well as the Project GM in Russian/Kyrgyz language. Where Internet
use is not feasible, traditional methods such as face-to-face meetings, focus groups discussions, information stands and
distribution of booklets will be used to inform and engage vulnerable groups. Further consultations to determine the
final list of vulnerable groups as part of the social impact assessment will be conducted in the course of the Project
implementation.
As indicated in the SEP, the Project will take special measures to ensure that vulnerable social groups have equal
opportunity to be involved in project activities, access information and benefits of the project, provide feedback, or
submit grievances. The following mechanisms will be used for disclosure of information to vulnerable groups:
    (i)    Social networks and traditional media for any announcements related to the project. These include the official

   (iii)   In the case of public meetings, including workshops or meetings, these meetings should be held at a suitable
           time and place for women, and should also take into account the needs of persons with disabilities (if any), or
           the barriers faced by vulnerable population groups. These meetings are often more conveniently held in public
           spaces or in women's civil society organizations (if available).

clearance and subsequent disclosure on the World Bank.

consultation.

7.              GRIEVANCE REDRESS MECHANISM
7.1             General Provisions
This Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) is created as a Project mechanism at the local and national levels for the
receipt, registration, consideration, assessment, resolution of grievances and decision-making on them arising in the
process of implementing the K1-HPP Project, in accordance with the national legislation of the Kyrgyz Republic and the
World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF).
This GRM is intended only for external stakeholders and not for the Project employees and contractors involved in the
implementation of the Project. A separate internal GRM for the Project workers’ grievances will be created for use by
Project employees and contractors.
Appeals in the form of obtaining information, recommendations, requests from citizens for assistance in the
implementation and restoration of their constitutional rights and freedoms or constitutional rights and freedoms of
other persons, or a message about the violation of laws and other regulatory legal acts, as well as about shortcomings
in the activities of state bodies and local government bodies and their officials not related to the implementation of
administrative procedures, are considered taking into account the Law of the Kyrgyz Republic “On the procedure for
considering citizens' appeals” dated May 4, 2007 No. 67 and the WB ESS10 “Stakeholder Engagement and Information
Disclosure” requirements.
Grievances directed at the actions or inactions of state and municipal bodies that have resulted in legal and /or actual
consequences for individuals or legal entities as a result of the implementation of the Project shall be considered taking
into account the Law of the Kyrgyz Republic “On the Fundamentals of Administrative Activity and Administrative
Procedure” (dated July 31, 2015, No. 210) and the WB ESS10 “Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure”
requirements.
A detailed GRM will be developed and approved by the Ministry of Energy of the Kyrgyz Republic (MoE) after approval
of the K 1- HPP project and must fully comply with the provisions specified in this GRM.
Grievances related to land acquisition, land use restrictions and involuntary resettlement, livelihood impacts, unfair
compensation, inadequacy of rights, inaccuracies in detailed measurements, or problems with the donation process,
etc., will be addressed taking into account the GRM details given in the LRRF.

7.2             Principles
This GRM is based on following fundamental principles for handling grievances and guarantees:
      •   Availability: GRM should be easily accessible to all external interested parties, taking into account
          confidentiality, legal obligations and vulnerability separate persons.
      •   Efficiency and effectiveness: necessary handle grievances effectively, promptly, discretely, objectively,
          sensitively, responsibly and transparent.
      •   Communication: Provide applicants with timely and continuous information on the progress of the review
          grievances, including reasons for any delays.
      •   Impartiality: Those responsible for handling grievances should not have no basis for favouring one solution
          over another or one party over another, and no outsiders should be allowed to interfere with the complaint
          resolution process.
      •   None costs: grievances should be resolved free of charge to the party that initiated the problem or concern.
      •   No restrictions on civil or legal rights: the mandatory consideration of anonymous grievances, participation in
          the MRI should not hinder or complicate the victim's right to other judicial, administrative, civil or other means
          of legal protection not prohibited by the legislation of the Kyrgyz Republic.
      •   Confidentiality: All grievances must be treated confidentially throughout the process and no information must
          be given to anyone not involved in the complaint.

The GRM shall include the following:
      •   Various ways for applicants to file grievances, including filing grievances in person or through a representative,

      •   Maintaining a record of grievances, which are recorded in writing and maintained as a database (Grievance

          acknowledgement, response and resolution of their grievances;
      •   An appeals process (including the national court system) to which unresolved grievances may be referred if
          resolution of the complaint has not been reached.

7.3.1           GRM Levels
The grievance redress mechanism is an integral part of the institutional arrangement in relation to the environmental
and social risk management. A two-stage process will be established for the Project as described below. Table 7-1and
Figure 7-1 summarize this process.
The complainant will be provided with assistance in relation to the grievances handling process at all levels of
consideration. Complainants have the right to ask any questions, express concerns or complain to local government
officials, state authorities or Project staff.
First level, Local. At the village level (Aiyl Okmotu) local contact persons receive/register grievances from applicants.
Complaint Registration Log Form) and inform the K1-PMO. After consideration of grievances with involvement of the
K1-PMO relevant staff and local self-government authorities, local contact persons provide a response to the applicant
within 14 working days. If the Applicant is not satisfied and/or the issue cannot be resolved at the first level of the GRM,
the complaint is escalated to the second (central) level.
Second level, Central. At the second level, the K1-PMO responsible staff receives and registers the complaint and
forwards it to the K1PMO director. The K1-PMO director assigns the PMO staff responsible for resolution of the
complaint, further together with other relevant staff, they will review the compliant and make a decision. A response
will be provided to the applicant in writing within 14 working days. Complaints requiring a collegial decision are
reviewed by a GRM committee established by the MoE.
For grievances in the form of obtaining information, recommendations, requests from citizens not related to the
implementation of administrative procedures, a decision is made within 14 working days, and for grievances directed
at the actions or inactions of state and municipal bodies that have entailed legal and /or factual consequences for
individuals or legal entities, a decision is made within 30 days.
If special checks, additional materials or other measures are required to resolve a complaint, the resolution period may
be extended: it can be prolonged exceptionally for no longer than 30 calendar days for the complaints not related to
the implementation of administrative procedures and no longer 60 calendar days for the complaints directed at the
actions or inactions of state and municipal bodies that have entailed legal and /or factual consequences for individuals
or legal entities.
The GRM should not interfere with or hinder the applicant’s right to other legal remedies. The applicant may terminate
participation in the project GRM process at any time and seek resolution through the courts in accordance with the
legislation of the Kyrgyz Republic.

7.4             Handling SEA/SH grievances
All World Bank-financed projects must comply with the WB ESF requirements on the prevention of Sexual Exploitation
and Abuse/Sexual Harassment (SEA/SH) and implement measures to raise awareness and mitigate the risks of SEA/SH.
The Project will develop a clear and transparent GRM process for receiving, investigating, and resolving SEA/SH
grievances. The GRM will ensure access and confidentiality for the consideration of special grievances, allowing
complainants to voice their concerns without fear of retaliation. These grievances will be investigated promptly, and all
responsible parties will be held accountable.
The K1-PMO will appoint SEA/SH focal points within the PMO and among contractors to oversee the implementation of
SEA/SH prevention measures and handle grievances.
The Project will use additional mitigation measures proportionate to the risk:
      •   Establish a secure and confidential reporting system for SEA/SH grievances, accessible to all community
          members.
      •   Conduct regular training sessions for project staff, contractors, and community members on SEA/SH
          prevention, reporting procedures, and the importance of confidentiality.
      •   Disseminate information about SEA/SH risks, prevention measures, and available support services through
          community meetings, posters, and leaflets.
      •   Ensure that all data related to SEA/SH grievances are securely stored and only accessible to authorized
          personnel.
      •   Establish a process for the prompt and impartial investigation of SEA/SH grievances.
      •   Establish partnerships with local organizations and service providers to offer comprehensive support to
          survivors.
      •   Regularly monitor the implementation of SEA/SH prevention measures and the effectiveness of the GRM.

7.5             Monitoring and reporting
The K1-PMO will establish a system to monitor the grievance management and satisfaction of complainants. This will
involve regular follow-ups with complainants to assess their satisfaction with the grievance resolution process and the

fairness of the grievance redress mechanism. The feedback collected will be analyzed to identify areas for improvement
and ensure that the grievance redress process is responsive to the needs and concerns of the community.

information and make appropriate notes in the unified GRM Log.

procedure will be assessed semi-annually by the project management.
