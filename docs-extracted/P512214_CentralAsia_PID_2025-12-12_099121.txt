                         B. Introduction & Context
                         Country Context
                         Kazakhstan is an upper-middle income country of nearly 20 million people with a vision to strengthen its economy
                         and invest in human capital and agrifood transformation. The country is currently transitioning from a hydrocarbon-
                         centric economy to one that prioritizes diversification and decarbonization. The development of the agrifood sector




Public Disclosure copy
                         and investment in human capital will be critical to enabling that transition. The country occupies a strategic position in
                         Central Asia. Kazakhstan plays a pivotal role in the trade dynamics of Central Asia due to its strategic location and
                         extensive transport infrastructure. The country serves as a natural transit corridor for goods moving between Central
                         Asia and other regions, including Europe and China. Kazakhstan's transport system, which includes 97,427 kilometers
                         of roads and 14,000 kilometers of railways, facilitates the movement of goods across borders. The livestock sector in
                         Kazakhstan holds substantial potential for trade within Central Asia and beyond. The country's vast pasture lands and
                         favorable conditions for livestock farming make it a promising area for growth. However, the sector faces challenges
                         such as inadequate disease surveillance and control measures, which hinder its competitiveness in international
                         markets. Effective disease surveillance is crucial for maintaining the health of livestock and ensuring the safety of
                         agricultural exports. Kazakhstan has made strides in improving its veterinary services, including the establishment of a
                         national reference laboratory and effective vaccination programs.
                         Uzbekistan, with a population exceeding 36 million, has achieved lower-middle-income status, propelled by its rich
                         natural resources and an expanding industrial base. Prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, the country experienced steady
                         economic growth, underpinned by ambitious reforms and a greater openness to trade and investment. Trade has played
                         a pivotal role in Uzbekistan’s development, facilitating access to new markets and driving modernization across key
                         sectors. Nevertheless, the economy remains vulnerable to external shocks, largely due to its reliance on a narrow set
                         of export commodities, including gold, natural gas, and cotton. This underscores the urgent need for broader economic
                         diversification and structural transformation to mitigate risks associated with global market volatility. In this context,
                         Uzbekistan’s substantial livestock sector presents significant untapped potential. Livestock production not only
                         supports rural livelihoods and food security but also offers opportunities for value-added exports and regional trade
                         integration. Harnessing the full potential of the livestock industry – through improved animal health, enhanced
                         productivity, and better market access – can contribute to more resilient and inclusive economic growth.

                         Sectoral and Institutional Context
                         KAZAKHSTAN
                         Kazakhstan’s PPR capacity is improving but faces persistent gaps. Despite advances in infectious disease control and
                         health coverage, Kazakhstan’s pandemic preparedness is limited by fragmented data management, insufficient early
                         warning systems, and incomplete implementation of response plans. Laboratory networks have expanded, but

                         shortages in equipment, infrastructure, and trained personnel—especially in remote areas—continue to hinder
                         effective emergency response.
                         AMR poses a major health challenge in Kazakhstan. Antimicrobial resistance is a significant threat, with high
                         mortality rates and a top global ranking for multi-drug resistant tuberculosis. Key drivers include limited veterinary
                         capacity, regulatory gaps in antimicrobial use, and low awareness in the agrifood sector. Overuse of antibiotics—30%
                         of prescriptions and 27% without medical oversight—accelerates resistance, while ongoing education for
                         veterinarians remains insufficient.
                         Food safety systems are improving but remain uneven. Food safety is affected by weak surveillance and
                         management systems, with limited data on antibiotic use and residues in food products. While veterinary services and
                         vaccination programs have expanded, access is still uneven for smaller farmers. Outbreaks of zoonotic diseases in
                         animals and migratory populations highlight the need for robust monitoring and coordination.
                         UZBEKISTAN
                         Uzbekistan’s PPR efforts are hampered by surveillance gaps. Public health risks from zoonotic and foodborne
                         diseases are exacerbated by incomplete surveillance and reporting. Laboratory capacity supports AMR monitoring,
                         but limited data on human zoonoses complicates risk assessment. Strengthening surveillance and laboratory networks
                         is essential for timely detection and response.




Public Disclosure copy
                         AMR is a growing concern, with infrastructure in place but gaps remaining. Uzbekistan’s laboratories can monitor
                         key AMR pathogens, but risks persist due to animal imports and potential cross-border transmission. Existing
                         measures—vaccination campaigns and training—are in place, yet surveillance, sampling, and traceability need further
                         improvement to control endemic and emerging diseases.
                         Food safety is challenged by animal health risks and environmental change. Livestock production is vital but exposed
                         to disease outbreaks and risks from imports. While prevention measures exist, gaps in traceability and laboratory
                         networking remain. Climate change and land degradation increase interactions between livestock, wildlife, and
                         humans, amplifying food safety and public health challenges.

                         Relationship to CPF
                         The One Health for Pandemic Prevention, Food Systems Resilience, and Ecosystem Health in Kazakhstan and
                         Uzbekistan fully consistent with the Country Partnership Frameworks (CPFs) of both countries.
                         Kazakhstan’s Country Partnership Framework for 2020–2025. The proposed project contributes to strengthening the
                         environment for private sector development, promoting market-led agriculture transformation, and enhancing
                         connectivity infrastructure and regional services—all under the focus area of promoting inclusive growth. The program
                         also advances objectives related to education, skills development, and access to quality health services and social
                         protection, supporting the country’s human capital agenda. The new CPF for 2025–2030 is currently under
                         development.
                         Uzbekistan’s CPF for 2022–2026. The proposed project contributes to the economic pathways outlined under HLO 1
                         “Increase Inclusive Private Sector Employment,” which explicitly recognizes One Health as a necessary approach and
                         HLO 3 “Improve Livelihoods and Resilience through Greener Growth.

                         D. Preliminary Description
                         Activities/Components
                         Under the MPA ‘One Health for Pandemic Prevention, Food Systems Resilience, and Ecosystem Health in Central Asia’
                         (P512827), the PF grant aims to help Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan strengthen their institutional and technical
                         foundations for adopting a regional One Health approach. The grant will support activities that address gaps in the




Public Disclosure copy
                         countries’ core capacities, focusing on establishing effective governance mechanisms and procuring targeted
                         equipment and consumables.
                         In Kazakhstan, the grant will support the implementation of activities under the Central Asia One Health MPA
                         Components 1, 2, 3, and 4. In Uzbekistan, the World Bank will finance activities under Components 1, 3, and 4, while
                         Component 2 (Knowledge and Workforce Capacity Development) will be implemented through the portion of the
                         multi-country PF grant managed by the FAO and WHO.
                         Further information on the Components under the Central Asia One Health MPA isprovided below:
                         Program Component 1: Strengthening One Health Governance. This component supports establishing and
                         strengthening regional, national, and sub-national institutions, structures, and procedures to effectively plan,
                         coordinate, monitor, and evaluate One Health interventions.
                             • Subcomponent 1.1: Multisectoral Planning and Coordination. This subcomponent aims to enable the
                                 coordination needed at all levels to implement a regional One Health approach.
                             • Subcomponent 1.2: Communication and Stakeholder Engagement. This subcomponent enables information
                                 sharing, a foundation of cross-sectoral and cross-country cooperation.
                         Program Component 2: Enhancing One Health Knowledge and Workforce Capacity. This component curates and
                         generates One Health knowledge and strengthens relevant workforce capacity.
                             • Subcomponent 2.1: Knowledge Management and Development. This subcomponent aims to curate existing
                                 One Health knowledge and generate new One Health evidence for dissemination.
                             • Subcomponent 2.2: Education and Training. This subcomponent aims to institutionalize the One Health
                                 approach and its implementation by strengthening the relevant capacities of agricultural, veterinary,
                                 environmental, and public health professionals.

                         Program Component 3: Improving One Health Prevention, Early Detection, and Response Systems. This component
                         invests in surveillance and response systems for managing priority zoonotic, foodborne diseases, and AMR.
                             • Subcomponent 3.1: Laboratory Networks. This subcomponent strengthens laboratory systems to inform risk-
                                  based prevention, monitoring, and detection, as well as response measures, at the national and regional
                                  levels.

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                          The environmental and social risks and impacts associated with the proposed project are considered Moderate. No civil
                          works are planned, and the project activities primarily involve institutional strengthening, procurement of equipment,
                          and capacity building under the One Health framework. The activities under Components 1 and 2 are primarily focused
                          on governance and capacity building, with minimal direct environmental risks. Under Component 3, environmental
                          risks relate to the generation and management of hazardous, biomedical, and electronic waste from laboratory
                          operations and veterinary service delivery, as well as air emissions and residual waste from the use of an incinerator
                          for safe disposal of animal carcasses. In addition, project interventions envisage monitoring and study of wild fauna,
                          particularly the saiga antelope (listed as Near Threatened on the IUCN Red List) research by the National Center for
                          Ecological and Epizootic Monitoring of Saiga. These efforts align closely with biodiversity conservation objectives. Any
                          potential impacts on disturbance of habitats or species will be managed in accordance with ESS6. The project faces
                          moderate social risks, mainly due to possible gaps in stakeholder engagement that could leave out vulnerable groups
                          such as the elderly, disabled, uninsured, migrants, single-parent families, and those in remote areas. There is also a risk
                          of spreading zoonotic diseases from improper waste management, affecting local communities, lab workers, waste




Public Disclosure copy
                          pickers, and border staff. These risks are site-specific, predictable, and manageable with appropriate mitigation
                          measures to be incorporated into the project Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP), Stakeholder
                          Engagement Plan (SEP) and Labor Management Procedures (LMP). According to the framework provided in the project
                          ESMP, site- and activity-specific ESMPs will be developed and implemented including waste management plans,
                          protocols ensuring safe operation of incinerator, biosafety protocols for laboratories and field sampling, sample
                          transportation and disposal; wildlife sensitive protocols providing specific biodiversity management measures to avoid
                          any disturbances to the saiga and its habitat, OHS regulations and outline of regular training on OHS for safe handling
                          and disposal of materials and wastes. Requirements pertaining environmental and social performance of the project
                          will be reflected in an Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP) which will be prepared by the Recipient prior
                          to the Appraisal. The project implementation will be aligned with relevant Environmental and Social Standards (ESS1,
                          ESS2, ESS3, ESS4, ESS6 and ESS10).
