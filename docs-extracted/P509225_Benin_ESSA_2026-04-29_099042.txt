     EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Context
1. The ALAFIA I - Nourishing Benin's Future Program-for-Results (PforR) operates in a context where
Benin's economic growth has not yet translated into equivalent progress in human capital. Chronic
malnutrition, anemia, preventable diseases and learning difficulties continue to limit the potential of
children, nearly 36.5% of whom suffer from stunted growth. These vulnerabilities, concentrated in the
first 1,000 days of life, are compounded by unequal access to essential services, climate shocks,
growing food insecurity and constraints on households, particularly women and young children. In
response to these challenges, the government has made nutrition, maternal and child health and child
development national priorities, reflected in the National Food and Nutrition Policy (PNAN 2024-2033),
the National Food and Nutrition Strategy (SNAN 2026-2030), the National Health Development Plan
(PNDS 2024-2030) and the "1,000 days+" initiative.
2. Within this framework, ALAFIA I is the first phase of a ten-year MPA supported by the World Bank
Group and the GFF, aimed at improving utilization of integrated nutrition, health and child development
services in targeted areas. The Program is built around two results areas: increasing the use of quality
integrated nutrition, health and child development services, and strengthening community resilience and
access to nutritious food, by mobilizing public, private and community actors under the leadership of
Agence Nationale de l'Alimentation et de la Nutrition (ANAN). By combining institutional strengthening,
local innovations and multi-sector partnerships, ALAFIA I aims to sustainably reduce malnutrition,
improve the well-being of women and children, and contribute to the transformation of human capital in
Benin.
Objective of the ESSA

3. The objective of the Environmental and Social System Assessment (ESSA) is to analyze Benin's
environmental and social management system, applicable to the Benin ALAFIA I Program, in order to
assess its compliance with the provisions of the World Bank's Policy on Program-Lending for Results.
The aim is to (i) ensure that the Program does not entail any significant environmental and social risks,
and that the systems in place enable the borrower to identify and manage any E&S risks likely to affect
the achievement of the Program's development objectives, and (ii) assess the capacity of the ANAN
and the agencies responsible for its implementation, to manage these risks.
4. Two (2) main result areas were identified:
    -    Increase the use of integrated, quality nutrition, health and child development services;
    -    Strengthen community resilience and access to a nutritious, quality food supply through local
         engagement and partnerships with the private sector.
Institutions Involved

5. The ALAFIA MPA Program will be implemented through a government-led multi-sectoral institutional
set-up, anchored at the central level and progressively strengthened at the decentralized level, to reflect
the cross-cutting nature of nutrition and early childhood development and to build on lessons learned
from previous World Bank-supported programs. The strategic and operational coordination of the
Program will be ensured by ANAN, placed under the authority of the Presidency of the Republic and
acting as the Program Coordination Unit (PCU), while strategic supervision will be the responsibility of
a Steering Committee chaired by the Presidency and made up of the main sectoral ministries concerned,
as well as structures competent in health and social affairs. This Committee will guide and validate
strategic choices, annual work plans and implementation reports, ensure the Program's alignment with

Methodology

programs.

Main environmental and social risks and benefits

8. The ALAFIA I Program mainly benefits pregnant and post-partum women, children under five,
especially during the first 1,000 days of life, and women and youth in vulnerable households and
communities in targeted areas, through improved access to integrated nutrition, health and early
childhood development services. It also supports schools, notably through school gardens and school-
based nutrition and health initiatives, and supports agri-food micro-businesses run by women and young
people through capacity-building activities. Finally, the Program indirectly benefits front-line players
(community health workers, community relays) through training and skills enhancement to ensure
sustainable implementation of interventions.
9. The main environmental and social benefits are
    -    Improved nutrition, maternal and child health and early childhood development, contributing to
         the reduction of malnutrition and anemia.
    -    Strengthening household and community resilience through improved food security and access
         to nutritious food.
    -    Promotion of sustainable agricultural and food practices (family and school gardens,
         agroecology), fostering climate adaptation and resilience.
    -    Economic and social empowerment of women and young people through support for agri-food
         micro-businesses and income-generating activities.
    -    Strengthening social cohesion, community capacities and local governance through participatory
         and multi-sectoral approaches.
10. The main environmental and social risks are
    -    Risks of mismanagement of plastic and biomedical waste linked to health and nutrition activities.
    -    Potential environmental nuisances (organic waste, water use, hygiene) associated with gardens,
         small-scale livestock farming and processing units if good practices are not followed.
    -    Risks of exclusion of certain vulnerable groups or community tensions linked to the targeting of
         beneficiaries and access to support.
    -    Risks of gender-based violence, abuse or harassment in community interactions and
         implementation chains.

Conclusions and recommandations

12. The ESSA concludes that the country has a generally sound national environmental and social
management framework that is compatible with the World Bank's principles for results-based financing,
enabling it to be used to implement the ALAFIA I Program under certain conditions.It underlines the

need to strengthen this system through the implementation of a formalized ESMS for ANAN, the

therefore MODERATE, and the GBV/SEA/SH risk is substantial.

SECTION I.                      PROGRAM DESCRIPTION
I.1. Background
14. The ALAFIA I - Nourishing Benin's Future (PforR) comes at a pivotal time for human development
in Benin. Despite sustained economic growth in recent years, social progress remains hampered by
persistent deficits in nutrition, maternal and infant health, and early childhood development. A child born
in Benin today is likely to reach only around 42% of his or her productive potential, as a direct result of
chronic malnutrition, anemia, preventable diseases and learning difficulties (World Bank Human Capital
Index, 2024). These fragilities, concentrated in the first 1,000 days of life, compromise the formation of
human capital and limit the country's ability to transform its growth into inclusive prosperity.
15. In light of this, the government has made child nutrition and development a national priority,
supported by the PNAN 2024-2033, SNAN 2026-2030, PNDS 2024-2030 and the "1,000 days+"
initiative. These guidelines reflect a clear determination to build an integrated, multi-sectoral and resilient
system capable of offering every woman, child and community the conditions they need to grow, learn
and contribute fully to the country's development.
16. This is the framework for ALAFIA I, the first phase of a ten-year program supported by the World
Bank through a USD 150 million PforR, supplemented by a USD 20 million grant from the GFF. The
program adopts a systemic and progressive approach, designed to sustainably strengthen national
capacities, improve the quality of essential services and create an enabling environment for nutritious
and accessible food.
17. The PforR result areas reflect a coherent vision: to increase the quality of essential services, while
strengthening the resilience of households, schools and communities to economic, climatic and
nutritional shocks. The program mobilizes all stakeholders - the State, local authorities, the private sector
and community organizations - under the leadership of the ANAN, the institution in charge of
coordination and implementation.
18. By combining systems strengthening, community innovation, public-private partnerships and
targeted investments in basic services, ALAFIA I aims to sustainably reduce the prevalence of stunting,
improve the health and well-being of women and children, and contribute to the national agenda of
human capital transformation and resilience.
19. To inform the preparation of the ALAFIA I Program, the World Bank Group has carried out the
present ESSA in order to: (a) analyze the potential environmental and social impacts of the Program
including direct, indirect, induced and cumulative impacts; (b) assess the borrower's capacity, with
respect to its national legal and institutional framework, to manage these impacts; and (c) evaluate the
likelihood that the proposed operation will achieve its environmental and social objectives. The ESSA
was carried out to ensure that the Program's E&S management system complies with the six
Environmental and Social Core Principles set out in the Bank's Program Financing for Results Policy,
published on November 17, 2017, as well as in the Bank's Guidelines for the Assessment of
Environmental and Social Systems of PforRs, published on September 18, 2020.

I.2. Government program
20. The Government of Benin has placed nutrition, maternal and child health, and child development at
the heart of its national human capital transformation agenda. This priority is supported by a coherent
set of national policies and strategies, including the PNAN 2024-2033, SNAN 2026-2030, the PNDS
2024-2030, the National Health Policy (PNS 2018-2030), as well as the government's "1,000 Days+"
initiative. These frameworks reflect a multi-sectoral, long-term vision that aims to sustainably improve

nutrition, strengthen primary health services, promote early childhood development, and increase
household resilience to economic, climatic and health shocks. The government has also launched the
Projet de Supplémentation Nutritionnelle (PSN) to improve nutrition outcomes among women and
children and strengthened the Programme National d'Alimentation Scolaire Intégré (PNASI) to improve
the quality of school meals and support student retention.
21. This government program is part of a broader ambition for structural transformation, as defined in
Benin Vision 2060 and the Government Action Program (PAG 2021-2026). It aims to correct persistent
deficits in human capital, reduce the prevalence of stunted growth, improve equitable access to essential
services, and strengthen community resilience, particularly in rural and northern areas of the country.
The government also intends to modernize governance, financing and multi-sector coordination
systems, relying on the ANAN as a central steering body. All these efforts form the basis of the national
program that the PforR ALAFIA I supports, by strengthening systems, improving the quality of services
and mobilizing public, private and community players to accelerate progress in nutrition and human
capital.

I.3. ALAFIA I program
I.3.1.   Description of the Program's scope and thematic limits
22. Phase I of the Program is based on the SNAN 2026-2030 (the "Program"), which extends the PNAN
2024-2033 and translates its strategic vision into concrete, costed interventions in the fields of health,
agriculture, education and social protection. Together, these two documents provide an integrated and
accountable roadmap for achieving national nutrition goals, combining nutrition-specific and nutrition-
sensitive actions with strong governance and coordination, transparent resource tracking, rigorous
monitoring and evaluation, capacity building, stakeholder mobilization and alignment with national
development priorities. The Program supports the four strategic priorities of the SNAN: (i) the
Programme d'appui institutionnel pour l'amélioration des environnements alimentaires; (ii) the
Programme de nutrition pour les femmes enceintes et le développement du jeune enfant (DJE); (iii) the
PNASI; and (iv) the Programme de lutte contre la malnutrition et l'insécurité alimentaire (PMIA). These
priorities will be implemented through two result areas: (i) improved utilization of integrated, quality

III.1. Environmental and social context
50. The Republic of Benin, a coastal West African country of some 23 million inhabitants, presents an
environmental and social context marked by profound structural challenges, which are continually
complicated by climate, health, security and social dynamics.
51. In environmental terms, Benin is one of the sub-region's most vulnerable countries to climate
change. While strong growth over the past decade has helped reduce poverty, development gains are
now threatened by the impact of climatic shocks. Recurrent flooding accelerated coastal erosion in the
Cotonou conurbation and drought in the north are the most significant risks. In response, the amount
allocated to climate-related activities in 2026 represents more than two-thirds of public investment, up
21.44% on 2025, and the government's strategy prioritizes flooding, coastal erosion and the energy
transition. At local level, the LoCAL project, launched in October 2024, aims to strengthen local climate
governance and integrate adaptation responses into communal planning processes.
52. These climatic pressures are being exerted on already fragile ecosystems. In 2024, forest cover is
estimated at around 51.15% of the national territory, or 5.9 million hectares, compared with 70.4% in
2007 - a loss of 22% in less than two decades, with an annual deforestation rate of around 2.2%, or
around 70,000 hectares per year. To halt this trend, in October 2025 the Council of Ministers adopted
the official incorporation of the Séri forest zone into the Pendjari National Park, whose area had shrunk
from 1,250 to 553 km² between 1996 and 2019, with the aim of restoring the site's ecological continuity
and protecting a biodiversity that includes elephants, lions and damalisques. In addition, the southern
wetlands - Lake Nokoué, the Porto-Novo lagoon and the Togbin mangroves - are recognized as being
of international importance under the Ramsar Convention, and are the focus of community restoration
initiatives.
53. Pressure on natural resources is closely linked to the land issue, which remains a sensitive one. In
the rural communes of the north, local people see the requisitioning of arable land for luxury tourism in
the WAP complex as a form of spoliation, while the ban on transhumance and the extension of the
agricultural front are fuelling recurrent conflicts between farmers and herders in the Alibori and Atacora
departments.
54. In terms of urban hygiene, solid waste management has undergone significant modernization. The
Société de Gestion des Déchets et de la Salubrité (SGDS SA), created in November 2018 by the
Beninese state, is tasked with ensuring the sustainable management of solid and liquid waste as well
as public health in the country's main cities. The Grand Nokoué territory is organized into 39 pre-
collection zones managed by local SMEs equipped with motorized tricycles, with recovery initiatives
including the production of ecological bricks from plastics in partnership with UNICEF. Since January 1,
2025, household waste collection in the towns of Grand Nokoué has been subject to the payment of
fees, after four years of free service. The management of biomedical waste and waste from care
activities involving infectious risks (DASRI) remains poorly structured on a national scale. Public health
facilities are faced with shortcomings in the sorting, collection and disposal of infectious waste, due to a
lack of compliant incinerators and organized channels. Occasional initiatives supported by WHO,
UNICEF and certain technical partners are accompanying pilot establishments, but the
institutionalization of a national DASRI management network is still in the process of being structured.

55. Child health is another major challenge. The infant mortality rate stands at 45 deaths per 1,000 live
births, and the neonatal mortality rate at 23 per 1,000, according to the WHO Benin Annual Report 2024.
Benin remains among the West African countries with the slowest decline in infant and neonatal

56. On the social front, GBV is a persistent concern. From 2020 to the end of October 2024, 58,469
cases of GBV were recorded in Benin, with an upward trend from 13,465 cases in 2020 to 19,421 cases
at the end of October 2024, According to the Observatoire des Femmes et de l'Équité (OFFE), GBV
mainly affects people aged 18 to 59 (69.83%) and adolescents aged 15 to 17 (18.73%), with a high
concentration in the departments of Zou, Atlantique, Collines, Ouémé and Couffo. At institutional level,
Benin has a repressive legal arsenal, a National Observatory (OFFE) and listening and care structures
throughout the country. Formal complaint management mechanisms are also deployed as part of
projects financed by international donors.
57. Finally, the security situation is now a structuring risk factor, particularly in the north of the country.
The first signs of the terrorist phenomenon date back to May 1, 2019, with the kidnapping of two French
tourists and the murder of their guide by the Katiba de Macina; since then, the departments of Alibori,
Atacora, Borgou and Donga have been the most severely affected. According to ACLED, the
northeastern border zone with Niger and Nigeria emerged in 2025 as a major new jihadist hotbed,
recording an 86% increase in attacks and 262% rise in the number of deaths in one year, with JNIM and
the Islamic State consolidating in the Alibori and Borgou departments. Timbuktu-institute In response,
the government has invested $130 million to reinforce security, deploying over 3,000 soldiers in border
communities and creating a special counter-terrorism unit in 2023. These troubles have direct
humanitarian repercussions: in February 2024, some 12,500 internally displaced people were registered
in northern Benin, a figure that has continued to rise with the continuation of violent incidents against
civilians. This instability undermines development gains and increases the vulnerability of populations
in an area already marked by poverty, land conflicts and the effects of climate change.

III.3.1. Main risks and negative environmental impacts of the PforR
61. Overall, the activities of the ALAFIA I program generate few direct environmental impacts, as they
are based mainly on strengthening existing services, training, community coordination and the
distribution of nutritional products. However, several indirect environmental risks need to be anticipated.
The increased distribution of SQ-LNS nutritional supplements may lead to an increase in plastic waste at
household and health center level if packaging is not properly collected or disposed of. Similarly,
increased attendance at postnatal services and training activities may generate more biomedical waste
(gloves, syringes, consumables), whose inadequate management could pose risks of contamination or
local pollution.
62. Activities linked to climate-smart gardens and small-scale livestock farming also present specific
environmental risks. Poor management of organic waste, uncontrolled use of water and lack of
biosecurity measures can lead to nuisance, zoonotic risks or increased pressure on local natural
resources. In some communes, the limited availability of water or land can accentuate conflicts of use
or lead to overexploitation of resources. Last but not least, the rise of agri-food micro-businesses, if
unsupported, may generate additional organic or plastic waste, pollution linked to processing practices,
or increased energy and water consumption.
63. These risks can be mitigated by simple but structuring measures: systematic integration of the One
Health approach in CCLS-TP, training community players in waste management (plastic, organic,
biomedical), promotion of clean technologies in micro-businesses, implementation of agroecological
practices in gardens, and reinforcement of biosafety measures for small livestock. The inclusion of
environmental components in communal action plans and regular supervision is also an essential lever
for preventing negative impacts.

III.3.2. Main risks and negative social impacts of the PforR
65. Although the activities of the ALAFIA I program are aimed at improving nutrition, maternal and child
health, child development and community resilience, they also entail a number of social risks that need
to be anticipated and managed proactively. These risks stem mainly from community dynamics, local
governance, interactions between agents and beneficiaries, mechanisms for distributing nutritional
inputs, and the implementation of activities involving women, young people and vulnerable groups.
66. A first major risk concerns equity and social inclusion. The functionalization of CCLS-TPs, the
distribution of SQ-LNS nutritional supplements, access to postnatal care or even the selection of
microenterprises and beneficiary households can lead to disparities if criteria are not clearly defined or
if certain communities - notably isolated rural populations, very poor households, minorities or people
living with disabilities - are not reached. The lack of inclusive representation on CCLS-TPs can also lead
to the marginalization of vulnerable groups and low participation by women, compromising the
transparency and effectiveness of local governance. Similarly, the turnover of trained staff, overworked
community agents and geographical disparities in access to training can accentuate inequalities
between communes.
67. A second major social risk concerns gender-based violence (GBV), including sexual exploitation
and abuse (SEA) and sexual harassment (HS). These risks can manifest themselves in a number of
contexts: interactions between community agents and beneficiaries during home visits, distribution of
SQ-LNS, women's participation in postnatal consultations, involvement of women and young people in
agri-food micro-businesses or school and household gardens, or within CCLS-TPs where power
relationships can create situations of vulnerability. The absence of safe, accessible and confidential
complaints mechanisms, or low community awareness of these risks, can increase the vulnerability of
women, adolescent girls and people with disabilities.
68. The program also entails risks linked to perceptions of inequity and community tensions. The
distribution of nutritional inputs, the selection of microenterprises or the allocation of climate-smart
gardens may give rise to frustration if the criteria are not transparent or if the beneficiaries perceived as
legitimate are not reached. In schools, garden management can generate conflicts between teachers,
parents and pupils if responsibilities are not clearly defined. Similarly, agri-food micro-businesses can
face tensions linked to local competition or the capture of opportunities by influential groups.
69. Security risks in the program's areas of intervention, especially in the north of the country, with
insecurity, terrorist attacks and refugee movements/displacement of people from conflict zones in
neighboring countries.
70. Finally, social risks are associated with the accessibility and cultural acceptability of services.
Women living in remote areas, households without means of transport, or communities with specific
cultural norms, may encounter obstacles in accessing postnatal consultations or nutrition services.
Awareness-raising messages, if not adapted to local languages or socio-cultural realities, can lead to
low uptake or inadvertent exclusion. The overwork of health and community workers, if not accompanied
by support measures, can also affect the quality of services and the motivation of those involved.
71. Overall, these social risks call for a proactive approach based on inclusion, transparency, community
participation, prevention of GBV/ASA/HS, cultural adaptation of interventions and strengthening of
complaints and accountability mechanisms. Rigorous management of these risks is essential to ensure
that program benefits reach all target populations equitably, and to ensure the sustainability of results.

III.4. Negative impacts and cumulative E&S risks
82. In addition to the negative environmental and social impacts of the Program's activities, this
assessment takes into account the cumulative negative impacts and E&S risks arising from ongoing
and planned projects and programs (MPAs) in the Program's area of intervention. Indeed, while most of
the activities to be carried out may have insignificant negative impacts and E&S risks taken individually,
the combination of several negative impacts and risks (even within the same Program) on the physical,
biological and socio-economic environment can, in the long term, have high levels of significance.
Cumulative negative impacts and E&S risks are changes to the environment resulting from combined
action with other past, present and future human actions. There are two (2) possible scenarios: (i) the
multiplication of activities similar (identical) to those of the Program, carried out at the same time or
successively and having the same negative impacts and E&S risks of minor or moderate importance on
a given area, but whose cumulative effect may prove harmful to the environment; (ii) the carrying out of
different activities within the framework of the same Program, generating individual negative impacts or
E&S risks of minor or moderate importance, but whose cumulative effect may prove harmful to the
environment. The main threats to Benin's valued environmental components, namely rural forests and
savannahs and their biodiversity, are agricultural pressure, loss of natural habitats, overexploitation of
natural environments, water and soil pollution, bush fires and transhumance (lack of grazing areas).
83. Specifically, when combined, the activities of the ALAFIA I program generate negative impacts and
cumulative environmental and social risks that require integrated management. On the environmental
front, the simultaneous accumulation of plastic waste from SQ-LNS supplements, biomedical waste

84. On the social front, the risks of exclusion and territorial inequity are mutually reinforcing, particularly
for isolated households, women far from postnatal services, landless households or vulnerable groups
poorly represented in the CCLS-TP. These risks can be reduced by transparent targeting mechanisms,
advanced strategies for reaching remote populations, linguistic and cultural adaptation of messages,
and the requirement for inclusive representation on local bodies. The risks of GBV, sexual exploitation
and abuse, and sexual harassment, present in several contexts of interaction (input distribution, home
visits, postnatal consultations, community and economic activities), constitute a high cumulative risk.
Mitigating these risks requires the establishment of safe and confidential complaint mechanisms,
training of staff in the prevention of GBV/ASA/HS, the adoption of mandatory codes of conduct and
community awareness-raising. Finally, the cumulative overload of health staff, ASCQs and community
relays, combined with the risks of local capture, favoritism and tensions over access to resources or
economic opportunities, can weaken social cohesion. Reinforced staffing levels, formative supervision,
transparency in decision-making processes and the establishment of participative management
committees help to mitigate these risks. Taken together, these measures make it possible to control the
cumulative risks and guarantee equitable, safe and sustainable implementation of the Program.

IV.3. Environmental and social management capacity
IV.3.1. Analysis of the capacity of the institutions involved in implementing the Program
ANAN
85. The Agence Nationale de l'Alimentation et de la Nutrition (ANAN) coordinates the implementation of
the Programme de Supplémentation Nutritionnelle (PSN) for the first 1,000 days. Created in 2023, it
centralizes the management of this project targeting pregnant women and young children, relying on
community involvement and the mobilization of local relays. ANAN is the main agency dedicated to
coordinating nutritional health projects, overseeing the NSP. It builds local capacity by engaging nutrition
support groups and community relays to ensure the program's lasting impact.
86. It collaborates with other agencies such as the Agence Nationale des Soins de Santé Primaire
(ANSSP) and the Direction Générale des Affaires Sociales (DGAS) to distribute supplements and raise
awareness.
87. In addition to the NSP, ANAN has taken on the implementation and coordination of the National
Integrated School Feeding Program (PNASI), which already provides a daily meal to 1.4 million children

88. In terms of environmental and social aspects (E&S), its management capacity is based on
progressive institutional and capacity building, and multi-sectoral coordination.
Ministry of the Environment and Transport
89. The Ministère du Cadre de Vie et des Transports, in charge of sustainable development, occupies
a central position in Benin's environmental and social governance, defining public policy and
coordinating the sector as a whole. Its main strength lies in the clarity of its mandate and its alignment
with international commitments, particularly in terms of climate and sustainable development. It also has
considerable strategic capacity to integrate environmental issues into key sectors such as urban
planning and transport. However, this centrality is not without its limitations, particularly in terms of cross-
sectoral coordination, which is sometimes inadequate given the cross-cutting nature of environmental
and social issues. Moreover, the integration of the social dimension into environmental policies remains
partial, and monitoring and evaluation mechanisms lack robustness and up-to-date data. With this in
mind, it would be appropriate to strengthen formal coordination mechanisms between ministries,
develop a digitalized national system for monitoring environmental and social performance, and
increase dedicated budgetary resources, notably through climate finance instruments.
EBA
90. The ABE is the operational pillar of the environmental and social management system, in particular
through the implementation of environmental and social impact assessments. It enjoys solid institutional
recognition and a relatively well-established regulatory framework, making it a credible interlocutor for
technical and financial partners. Its experience in the analysis of structuring projects is also a major
asset. Nevertheless, the agency faces major constraints, particularly in terms of technical and human
capacities, which can lead to delays in processing applications. In addition, monitoring of the
implementation of environmental and social management plans is still inadequate, which limits the
effectiveness of mitigation measures in the field. The question of its functional independence may also
arise in certain strategic project contexts. To reinforce its effectiveness, it would appear necessary to
increase its qualified human resources, digitalize its procedures, develop mechanisms for real-time
monitoring of environmental and social commitments, and introduce regular independent audits.
National Environment Observatory
91. The Observatoire National de l'Environnement plays an important role in the production and
centralization of environmental data, providing a strategic tool for planning and decision-making. Its
existence testifies to a desire for transparency and structuring of environmental information on a national
scale. However, its potential remains under-exploited due to the limited availability of up-to-date data
and insufficient interconnection with information systems in other sectors. What's more, the accessibility
of data for non-state actors and its analytical valorization remain weak. To improve its impact, it would
be advisable to develop an interactive open data platform, strengthen the integration of sectoral data,
and regularly produce forward-looking analyses to anticipate environmental and social risks.
Ministry of Social Affairs and Microfinance
92. The Ministry of Social Affairs and Microfinance is primarily involved in the social dimension of
development, in particular as regards the protection of vulnerable populations and social inclusion. Its
territorial presence and experience in providing social support are important assets, particularly in
projects involving population displacement or impacts on livelihoods. However, the company's
involvement in environmental and social assessment processes is still limited, which makes it difficult to

Ministry of Health
93. The Ministry of Health plays a crucial role in addressing environmental health impacts, particularly
in terms of public health and risk management. Its network of health facilities and expertise in emergency
management are major assets. However, the links between health and the environment are still
insufficiently integrated into policies and project assessments. Data on diseases linked to pollution or
environmental degradation are also poorly exploited. To improve this situation, we need to
systematically integrate health impact assessments into high-risk projects, develop environmental
disease surveillance systems, and strengthen collaboration with environmental institutions.
Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock and Fisheries
94. The Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock and Fisheries occupies a strategic position in natural resource
management and climate resilience, particularly in rural areas. It has a strong capacity for intervention
through its agricultural development programs. However, pressures on natural resources, such as
deforestation and soil degradation, reflect shortcomings in the application of environmental measures.
Coordination with environmental policies also remains limited. In this context, it is recommended to
promote sustainable agricultural practices, strengthen environmental control mechanisms, and
systematically integrate environmental and social requirements into agricultural projects.

V.4. Conclusions on the risk level of the PforR
98. This Assessment of Benin's Environmental and Social System (ESES) was carried out with a view
to examining the convergence between the requirements of the World Bank's principles applicable to
the Results-Based Financing mode and the provisions of national environmental and social
management systems within the framework of the implementation of the components and sub-projects
of the ALAFIA I Program.

99. A fairly comprehensive national environmental and social management system exists, which can be
used to implement the Program in compliance with the World Bank's basic principles. It is imperative to:
    -    Set up a formalized ESMS for ALAFIA I to establish a single operational framework for
         systematically identifying, managing, monitoring and reporting the environmental and social risks
         and impacts of the Program in line with PforR principles.
    -    Finalize the recruitment and operationalization of E&S specialists to strengthen internal technical
         capacities, ensure E&S screening, monitoring and reporting, and effectively support sectoral and
         local stakeholders in the implementation of mitigation measures.
    -    Set up an E&S screening system for activities to assess environmental and social risks
         upstream, exclude ineligible activities and ensure a preventive and proportionate approach to
         risk management.
    -    Strengthen the existing MGP by formalizing the management of sensitive complaints and an
         internal MGP at ANAN;
    -    Mandatorily integrate E&S requirements into work plans (PTAs) to ensure that the human,
         financial and logistical resources required for E&S management are effectively planned and
         implemented each year.
    -    Strengthen waste management (plastic, biomedical and organic waste) by setting up appropriate
         sorting, collection, treatment and recovery systems to reduce the health and environmental risks
         associated with program activities.
    -    Strengthening local E&S governance by supporting communes and community structures to
         improve social inclusion, transparency, accountability and participatory management of
         environmental and social risks.
100. ANAN must strengthen its institutional capacity by finalizing the recruitment of specialists dedicated
to environmental and social management, by integrating operational recommendations into the
Program's Action Plan, by training its staff and updating its procedures, and by making the complaints
management mechanism and the code of good conduct fully operational, accompanied by awareness-
raising actions aimed at all stakeholders and beneficiaries.
101. The ALAFIA I Program will thus contribute to filling the main institutional, technical and operational
gaps in the national environmental and social management system, particularly in terms of human
capacity, formalized procedures, E&S risk monitoring, complaints management, prevention of gender-
based violence, and multi-sectoral coordination, to ensure more effective, inclusive implementation in
line with the environmental and social principles applicable to Programs for Results.
102. In substance, the environmental and social risks and impacts associated with the implementation
of the ALAFIA I Program are moderate, with substantial GBV/EAS/HS risks, and remain generally
manageable through appropriate E&S measures indicated in the Program Action Plan, including: (i)
Setting up and operationalizing an environmental and social management system (ESMS), (ii)
Strengthening the institutional capacities of key players, (iii) Improving governance, social inclusion and
social risk management, (iv) Strengthening operational environmental management, and (v) Setting up
a monitoring, reporting and accountability system. Particular attention will be paid to security risks to
ensure efficient implementation.
