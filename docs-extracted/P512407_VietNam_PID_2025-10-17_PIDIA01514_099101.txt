                           B. Introduction and Context

                           Country Context
                              1. Viet Nam’s 3,260-kilometer coastline and extensive exclusive economic zone are the foundation of the country’s
                          blue economy, which underpins growth, jobs, and food security. Coastal provinces account for nearly 60 percent of GDP
                          and more than half of the total employment. Viet Nam’s blue economy depends on the productivity and health of its
                          marine resources. Coastal ecosystems—from mangroves and coral reefs to seagrasses—support biodiversity, protect
                          communities from storms, and store carbon, reinforcing both economic and ecological stability. Yet the country’s natural
                          blue capital faces mounting pressures: overfishing, pollution, and climate change—including sea-level rise, salinity
                          intrusion, and warming oceans—that threaten productivity and the resilience of coastal communities, underscoring the
                          need for strategic, sustainable management of Viet Nam’s blue economy.
                              2. The country has set ambitious goals to grow its marine economy to 10 percent of GDP by 2030, with a focus on
                          tourism, aquaculture, and other emerging blue industries. Following on expanded periods of overexploitation, the

                          protection and sustainability of these resources is a core priority reflected in the Strategy for the Sustainable Development
                          of Viet Nam’s Marine Economy by 2030 and Vision for 2045 (Marine Strategy).1 The Marine Strategy prioritizes prosperity,
                          safety, and resilience of coastal communities, where over half of the country’s population resides. Improved fisheries
                          sector governance, recovery of Viet Nam’s fish stock, strengthened international and domestic market reputation, and
                          higher fish food quality are also key priorities. However, the recent World Bank Public Expenditure and Investment review
                          highlights persistent gaps in resources and governance towards the achievement of these goals. Coastal investment
                          declined from 27.2 to 22.6 percent between 2016 and 2022, while approximately 80 percent of marine-related spending
                          remains focused on transport infrastructure. There are clear needs to strengthen the capacity, funding and governance to
                          improve fisheries management, address major infrastructure gaps, advance shrimp aquaculture technologies, upgrade
                          the national vessel database, and reduce plastic waste in fisheries and aquaculture.2

                           Sectoral and Institutional Context
                              3. Viet Nam’s fisheries sector is a cornerstone of the national economy, sustained by its rich coastal and marine
                          ecosystems and supporting millions of livelihoods. Contributing around 5-6 percent of GDP, the sector provides direct and
                          indirect employment to nearly 10 percent of the population. Its geographic diversity and abundant marine life underpin
                          both capture fisheries and aquaculture, positioning Viet Nam as a global seafood leader. Over the past decade,
                          aquaculture has grown to surpass wild capture, now accounting for over 54 percent of total seafood production and 75
                          percent of sectoral value. This growth has been driven by technological innovation, improved management, rising global




_For Official Use Only_
                          demand for sustainable seafood, and strategic government support for export-oriented aquaculture industries.
                              4. Despite their central role within Viet Nam’s blue economy, capture fisheries are under mounting pressure. Past
                          policies prioritized maximizing catch volumes over long-term sustainability, leading to the depletion of key stocks. National
                          assessments show a 41.7 percent decline in benthic species since the 2000s, while the Food and Agriculture Organization
                          (FAO) classifies most commercial species as fully exploited or overexploited. Structural weaknesses— including fleet aging
                          and overcapacity, lacking port and landing infrastructure, limited product traceability, and fragmented monitoring
                          systems—further constrain sustainable management. Left unaddressed, declining capture fisheries threaten both national
                          output and the resilience of coastal communities that depend on them for livelihoods and food security.
                              5. Climate change magnifies the pressures on Viet Nam’s fisheries and aquaculture. Rising sea levels and saline
                          intrusion threaten the Mekong Delta, which accounts for 65 percent of aquaculture production and 60 percent of fish
                          exports, placing farms, infrastructure, and livelihoods at risk of inundation and disruption. More frequent and intense
                          storms endanger fishers and expose coastal communities to severe losses, underscoring the urgent need for resilient
                          infrastructure and safety systems. Warming waters and ocean acidification are already affecting species growth rates,
                          distribution, and health, thus further undermining sector productivity. Without adaptive measures—ranging from safer
                          ports and storm shelters to diversified, climate-smart aquaculture—these climate impacts could erode the economic
                          viability of the seafood sector and deepen social vulnerabilities in coastal areas.
                               6. Sustainable sector growth will depend on a strategic sector transformation “from capture to culture” and “from
                          volume to value.” This transition requires not only improved management practices but also climate-ready infrastructure,
                          diversified farming systems, and value addition throughout the seafood supply chain. The Government of Viet Nam (GOV)
                          is prioritizing modernization initiatives, green technologies, and market-oriented reforms designed to regenerate aquatic
                          resources, protect coastal livelihoods, and promote environmentally responsible aquaculture. By aligning production with
                          sustainability objectives, these efforts aim to secure both economic and ecological returns over the long term.

                              7. National strategies provide a clear framework for this transformation. The Marine Economy Strategy 2030 (vision
                          to 2045), the Fisheries Development Strategy 2030 (vision to 2050), and the Sustainable Capture Fisheries Program to
                          2030 establish concrete targets for stabilizing seafood production, enhancing exports, and generating employment.
                          Together, they emphasize high-value aquaculture, resilient offshore fishing, and the conservation of blue natural capital.
                          These strategies explicitly link economic growth to environmental stewardship, recognizing that the long-term viability of
                          the sector depends on the health of Viet Nam’s marine ecosystems.
                               8. The SFDP is supporting the GOV’s strategies’ transformation into practical action. Through modernizing ports and
                          aquaculture infrastructure, enhancing governance and digital traceability, and promoting climate-smart, high-value
                          production, SFDP will help shift the sector from “capture to culture” and “volume to value.” The project will support
                          integrated rice-shrimp and polyculture systems, advanced shrimp and mariculture techniques, and Good Aquaculture
                          Practices (GAP), while building skills and opportunities for local people including women and youth. By linking climate-
                          resilient infrastructure, governance, and sustainable production, SFDP will safeguard Viet Nam’s marine resources,
                          strengthen livelihoods, and demonstrate how targeted investments can drive both economic growth and environmental
                          stewardship, setting the stage for the project’s detailed interventions.

                           D. Project Description
                          26. The SFDP is designed as Investment Project Financing (IPF), providing financing for essential infrastructure
                          investments and complementing capacity activities to enhance the management of Viet Nam’s fisheries sector (both
                          capture fisheries and aquaculture) towards sustainability and climate resilience. The project also seeks to increase the
                          sector’s value addition and improve compliance with the country’s fisheries regulations and international requirements.
                          The project will be implemented by the Ministry of Agriculture and Environment (MAE) at the national level, and by the
                          provinces of An Giang, Thanh Hoa, Ca Mau, and Can Tho City, at the sub-national level.

                          27. The MAE is mandated by the GOV as the lead national government agency overseeing fisheries and aquaculture
                          development in the country, while the four participating provinces and city are coastal provinces/city in which fisheries are
                          considered the priority sector that drives the province’s sustainable development and economic growth agenda, as well as

                          key contributors to the national export targets for aquatic and aquaculture products. These provinces are also among the
                          ones most affected by climate change and rising sea water, frequently hit by typhoons, storm surges, and saline intrusion
                          that negatively impact vulnerable communities and livelihoods along the coastline. Therefore, investments in adaptation
                          of the sector to climate change have been prioritized.

                          28. The SFDP is structured in five components, each to be implemented by the respective recipient agencies in parallel.
                          Each component will finance (i) essential smart infrastructures to address key fisheries sector’s priorities (covering both
                          capture fisheries and aquaculture), (ii) technical assistance to enhance management and capacities as well as promote
                          sustainable, climate-resilient practices, and (iii) support for project management and M&E, as follows:

                          Component 1. MAE: Sustainable Fisheries Development (IBRD: US$ 76 million; Counterpart funds: US$ 28 million) will
                          support MAE to enhance the management of the fisheries sector and increase the value of target fisheries in the country.
                          To this end, it will invest in storm shelter and fishing port infrastructure of national and regional significance under the
                          state management of MAE in 5 coastal provinces (Hai Phong, Thanh Hoa, Gia Lai, Khanh Hoa, and An Giang), as well as
                          provide technical assistance and strengthen institutional capacities at national level. In particular, project financing will be
                          provided for access and harbor basin canal dredging and related embankment works, construction, rehabilitation, and/or
                          upgrading of docking berths, wharves and wharf yard revetments, as well as construction and/or renovation of port
                          facilities, yards and access roads, and port utilities (such as water supply and drainage, sanitation and waste water




_For Official Use Only_
                          treatment, power supply, fire protection, and communications). Project support will be further extended for capacity
                          building activities and technical assistance aimed at strengthening technical and managerial capacities for fisheries
                          sector’s state agencies as well as equipping farmers and fishers with advanced aquaculture practices and skills for applying
                          modern aquaculture technologies.

                          Component 2. An Giang Province: Sustainable Fisheries Development (IBRD: US$ 19.9 million; Counterpart funds: US$ 7.2
                          million) aims to support aquaculture adaptation in An Giang province, enhancing the sector’s quality and productivity and
                          unlocking value-addition opportunities. The component will finance activities to (i) develop infrastructure systems (at farm
                          and community levels) to enable sustainable aquaculture production practices that are resilient to the impacts of climate
                          change; (ii) build capacity for farmers and local communities for climate-resilient and diversified livelihoods; (iii) enhance
                          linkage mechanism between farms and enterprises to improve value chains; and (iv) build a brand of safe, ecological and
                          natural aquaculture products. In this context, project financing will be provided for sluice gate rehabilitation and
                          construction, and improved market access support for sustainable aquaculture production, including access roads and
                          electricity connections. Training and technical assistance will be further extended for local farmers, farmers’ groups and
                          cooperatives, and local communities to adopt climate-resilient practices and diversify livelihoods (polyculture and other
                          aquaculture) in An Giang province.

                          Component 3. Can Tho City: Sustainable Fisheries Development (IBRD: US$ 30.8 million; Counterpart funds: US$ 12 million)
                          will contribute to Can Tho City’s efforts to adapt to climate change and modernize infrastructures to enhance the efficiency
                          of the capture fishery, improve aquaculture and management capacity, and increase livelihood opportunities for the rural
                          coastal communities which include ethnic minorities. More specifically, project financing will provide essential
                          infrastructure for enhanced climate resilience and sustainable aquaculture development (namely, storm shelters, water
                          management infrastructure, rural connectivity systems, etc.) in the coastal areas of Can Tho City. These investments will
                          improve water management for aquaculture production (i.e., supply and discharge systems) and enhance environmental
                          management of brackish-water shrimp farming areas. By investing in rural connectivity systems, the sub-component will
                          improve mobility and market access, thereby reducing post-harvest loss, allowing for fresh, higher-quality and higher-

                          Component 5. Ca Mau Province: Sustainable Fisheries Development (IBRD: US$ 16.8 million; Counterpart funds: US$ 6.4
                          million) aims to contribute to Ca Mau’s efforts to modernize infrastructure, enhance the efficiency and sustainability of
                          its capture fishery, improve climate-resilient aquaculture and management capacity, and increase livelihood opportunities
                          for rural coastal communities. The component will finance essential infrastructure for enhanced climate resilience and
                          sustainable fisheries development, including fishing ports, enabling resilient aquaculture (i.e. through dredging of canals,
                          upgrading of embankments, and rural access road upgrades), etc. in the Ca Mau coastal areas. These investments will
                          improve the aquaculture production systems adapt to increasing salinization by helping to create better water quality and

                          resilient shrimp farming areas. By investing in rural connectivity system, the component will improve mobility and market

                          capacity-building programs for relevant provincial government agencies as well as for local farmers, including for
                          technology adoption for sustainable fish harvesting and post-harvest loss reduction, strengthening domestic IUU fishing
                          management capacity, application of advanced technologies for fish product traceability, and the development of fish
                          farming certified bio-safety areas, value chain improvement, and brand development.

                               9. The SFDP’s main environmental risks and impacts during pre-construction and construction include: (i) safety risks
                          related to unexploded ordnance (UXO); (ii) pollution from dust, waste, and wastewater; (iii) water and soil pollution and
                          impacts on aquatic biodiversity due to improper management and disposal of dredged materials; (iv) risks of erosion and
                          subsidence during the construction of embankments and wharfs; (v) disruption of waterway traffic and disturbances from
                          transporting leveling and excavated materials; and (vi) health and safety risks to the community and workers. These
                          impacts are short-term, moderate to substantial in scale, and reversible. Key environmental risks during operation include:
                          (i) potential soil and water pollution from oil leakage from vessels and improper management of solid waste and
                          wastewater at ports; (ii) contamination of aquatic systems and health risks from overuse of chemicals/medicine in shrimp
                          cultivation; (iii) risk of land salinization and water pollution from saline shrimp farms; and (iv) occupational and community
                          health and safety risks during port operation. These impacts are long-term, reversible, and can be mitigated through
                          appropriate design and management practices. The implementing agencies have limited experience with the
                          Environmental and Social Framework (ESF). Given the project's type, location, sensitivity, scale, and the implementing
                          agencies' capacity to manage environmental risks, the environmental risk is assessed as ‘Substantial.’

                               10. The SFDP’s key social risks include those related to land acquisition and resettlement (LAR), impacts on the
                          livelihoods of ethnic minorities (EMs), and risks related to labor conditions and community health and safety.
                          Infrastructure upgrades will require land acquisition, physical relocation, and restricted access to livelihood opportunities.
                          More specifically, social risks and impacts include: (i) loss of land, assets, livelihoods, and properties due to permanent
                          and temporary land acquisition and relocation; (ii) disruption of livelihood activities for farmers and fishers due to




_For Official Use Only_
                          construction and operation of fishery infrastructure; (iii) labor influx and related community safety risks; (iv) resistance to
                          adapting new livelihood models; (v) potential adverse impacts on welfare from failed livelihood models; (vi) uneven access
                          to project benefits among vulnerable groups; (vii) relocation of graves; and (viii) health risks from overuse of
                          antibiotics/chemicals in production activities. The government and implementing agencies have limited capacity in LAR
                          processes, budgeting, and complaint resolution. These potential impacts require appropriate mitigation plans to be
                          developed and effectively implemented during project preparation and implementation. While the implementing agencies
                          have experience managing World Bank safeguards, they have limited experience with labor conditions and community
                          health and safety under the ESF. Impacts on EMs could be more acute, but the numbers affected are not expected to
                          exceed the implementing agencies' capacity to mitigate. Considering land acquisition issues, potential household
                          relocation, a large labor force, and the challenges with GRM/GRS and PCAPs cases related to land acquisition/livelihood
                          programs, which highlight institutional weaknesses in E&S management, the SFDP social risk is assessed as ‘Substantial.’

                               11. The World Bank Policy concerning Projects on International Waterways (OP 7.50) is applicable to the SFDP because
                          the Project will finance activities that may use or risk polluting the waters of the Mekong River and the Ma River, which
                          are considered international waterways. The exceptions to the riparian notification requirement according to paragraphs
                          7(a) and (c) of the Policy apply. Activities are limited to rehabilitation, upgrading and expansion of existing schemes, which
                          will not adversely change the quantity and quality of water flows to other riparian countries. Moreover, works supported
                          under the Project, that use, or risk polluting water of the international waterways are limited to water from tributaries
                          and on-farm canals connecting to tributaries of the Mekong River and Ma River that are exclusively located in Viet Nam,
                          which is the lowest downstream riparian country. The exception to the notification requirement is under review for
                          approval prior to issuance of invitation to negotiate to the Government.

                           Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
                            12. The SFDP will be implemented by MAE and the provinces/city of An Giang, Can Tho, Thanh Hoa, and Ca Mau. The
                          MAE and the participating provinces/city will each establish a Project Management Unit (PMU), which will be responsible

                          for management and implementation of their respective project activities. For the MAE-led project activities, the PMU
                          will be established as a Central Project Management Unit (CPMU) under MAE’s Agricultural Project Management Board
                          (APMB) with the task of management and implementation of the activities under Component 1 and with the overall
                          coordination of, and reporting on the SFDP project. Key CPMU responsibilities include: (i) preparation of work plan and
                          budget plan; (ii) handling procurement activities; (iii) management of financial and accounting systems; (iv) E&S
                          compliance; (v) M&E, including preparation of progress reports; and (vi) coordination with the provinces.

                          Management Units (PPMUs/CPMU) will be established under the respective provincial Department of Agriculture and
                          Environment (DAE) or ODA Management Board, as applicable, using newly established or existing implementation

                          financial plans; (ii) handling procurement activities; (iii) financial and accounting management; (iv) monitoring project
                          implementation; (v) E&S compliance; and (f) coordinating with the MAE and with district- and commune-level
                          stakeholders, as appropriate. The P/CPMUs will receive support from and report to the P/CPCs and DAEs, with provincial
                          departments of finance and relevant technical departments also involved in various implementation steps, as per Viet
                          Nam’s regulations.
