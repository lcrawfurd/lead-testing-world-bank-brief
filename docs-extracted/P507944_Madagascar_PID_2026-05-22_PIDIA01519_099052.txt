                          B. Introduction and Context

                          Country Context

                          1.     Madagascar faces persistent development challenges characterized by low and volatile economic growth, high
                          poverty levels, and recurrent political instability. Real GDP per capita has declined over the long term and remains
                          among the lowest globally, with approximately two-thirds of the population living below the international poverty line.
                          While urbanization is accelerating—at an estimated rate of 4.4 percent annually—it has not translated into sufficient job
                          creation or improved living standards. Urban poverty has increased sharply, driven by rapid and largely unplanned urban
                          expansion that has outpaced the provision of resilient infrastructure, serviced land, and formal housing.
                          2.     Madagascar is also among the countries most exposed and vulnerable to climate change and extreme weather
                          events. Floods, cyclones, and droughts generate recurrent economic and social losses estimated at over US$100 million
                          annually. Climate shocks disproportionately affect cities, where dense populations, informal settlements, inadequate
                          drainage, and weak solid waste management systems amplify flood risks. In early 2026, successive tropical cyclones

                          caused severe damage, particularly in Greater Toamasina, highlighting the urgent need to reduce urban vulnerability and
                          strengthen disaster resilience.
                          3.       Greater Antananarivo and Greater Toamasina are central to Madagascar’s economic prospects, accounting for
                          a large share of national GDP and employment. Reducing exposure to climate shocks in these urban areas is therefore
                          critical to safeguarding livelihoods, attracting private investment, and enabling sustainable job creation.

                          Sectoral and Institutional Context

                          4.      Urban flooding in Madagascar reflects systemic urban risk arising from the interaction of outdated drainage
                          systems, insufficient solid waste management, informal land occupation, and weak land administration. In Greater
                          Antananarivo, drainage performance is undermined by clogged canals, poor maintenance, and widespread dumping of
                          solid waste. In both Antananarivo and Toamasina, informal settlements have expanded into flood-prone areas, increasing
                          exposure of households and economic assets.
                          5.      Weak land administration further constrains infrastructure delivery and private investment. Land tenure
                          insecurity, incomplete cadastral information, missing public rights-of-way, and slow transaction processes delay
                          infrastructure works, raise investment risks, and limit the formal development of housing and urban services. Municipal
                          governance and fiscal capacity remain constrained, with limited ability to mobilize own-source revenues for operations
                          and maintenance.




_For Official Use Only_
                          6.      Despite these challenges, Madagascar has built significant experience through prior urban resilience
                          interventions, notably the Integrated Urban Development and Resilience Project for Greater Antananarivo (PRODUIR).
                          This experience has demonstrated that integrated investments—combining flood protection, land regularization, solid
                          waste management, and strong community engagement—can effectively address systemic urban risk. Building on these
                          lessons, the proposed project scales up and deepens this integrated model, extending it to Greater Toamasina while
                          incorporating housing recovery and strengthening institutional systems within a longer-term programmatic engagement.

                          C. Proposed Development Objective(s)


                          Development Objective(s) (From PAD)
                          The Project Development Objective (PDO) is to strengthen climate resilience of urban communities, improve land
                          administration, and support jobs in selected cities .

                           Key Results
                          7.    Key results include:
                             • Increased resilience to climate risks for approximately 1.5 million people
                             • Reconstruction of 20,000 resilient housing units in Greater Toamasina
                             • Decrease in time to conduct a land transfer in Greater Antananarivo
                             • Creation of 15,000 jobs through infrastructure investments, recovery activities, monetized avoided productivity
                                losses from flood damage and inadequate solid waste management, and economy-wide effects from increased
                                land registration.

                          C. Project Description

                           8.       The project is part of a long-term programmatic engagement supporting integrated urban resilience and job
                           creation in Madagascar. It builds on the results of PRODUIR and extends interventions to Greater Toamasina.
                           9.       The project is structured around four components:
                          Component 1 – Foundational Urban Infrastructure and Climate Resilience (US$202.5 million)
                          Subcomponent 1.1 – Public Infrastructure for Flood Reduction in Greater Antananarivo (GA): This subcomponent finances
                          the rehabilitation and upgrading of primary and secondary drainage canals, retention basins, and pumping stations; the
                          reinforcement of river dikes along the Ikopa, Mamba, and Sisaony rivers; urban upgrading in selected flood-prone
                          neighborhoods; and the optimization of solid waste management (SWM) infrastructure and operations along drainage
                          corridors to reduce canal clogging and improve flood conveyance capacity.
                          Subcomponent 1.2 – Resilient Housing Recovery and Public Infrastructure in Greater Toamasina (GT): In response to the
                          damages caused by Cyclone Gezani in early 2026, this subcomponent finances: (a) a housing reconstruction program to
                          help approximately 20,000 households rebuild to resilient standards; (b) the establishment of Housing Reconstruction
                          Centers providing technical assistance, materials, and support to beneficiary households; and (c) the rehabilitation and
                          reconstruction of resilient public infrastructure and building as well as infrastructure roads, drainage, and community
                          facilities.
                          Subcomponent 1.3 – Strengthening Disaster Preparedness: This subcomponent finances the design and construction of
                          multipurpose emergency shelters and the modernization of Early Warning Systems (EWS) to improve institutional




_For Official Use Only_
                          readiness for future climate shocks.

                          Component 2 – Improving Land Administration and Urban Governance for Jobs Creation (US$16.5 million)
                          Subcomponent 2.1 – Land Administration Capacity: This subcomponent advances land governance reforms, supports
                          systematic land regularization in targeted urban areas, and finances the establishment of a national Land Information
                          System (LIS) to modernize cadaster and land registry functions.
                          Subcomponent 2.2 – Property Tax, Urban Planning, and Municipal Governance: This subcomponent supports updates to
                          land-use and urban master plans, improvement of municipal asset management, and advancement of property tax reform
                          (IFPB) to strengthen the fiscal base of municipalities and ensure long-term sustainability of infrastructure operations and
                          maintenance.

                          Component 3 – Project Management and Coordination (US$6 million)
                          This component finances the operational costs of the project, including fiduciary management (procurement and financial
                          management), environmental and social (E&S) standards compliance, monitoring and evaluation (M&E), and project
                          coordination across implementing agencies.

                          Component 4 – Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC) (US$0 million)
                          This zero-dollar component provides a pre-agreed mechanism allowing the rapid reallocation of uncommitted project
                          funds to finance eligible emergency response activities in the event of a natural disaster or other crisis declared by the
                          Government of Madagascar. It is activated upon formal request by the Borrower and approval by the World Bank.

                          Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts


                          10.     In accordance with the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), the environmental and social
                          risk classification (ESRC) of the project is rated as High, due to High social risk. Environmental risk is substantial, and
                          SEAH risk High. All ESSs are relevant, except ESS7 or ESS9.
                          11.     Key potential environmental risks and impact include: generation of construction and domestic waste, potential
                          for hazardous waste from SWM activities, noise and dust pollution from civil works, water and soil contamination,
                          increased traffic and associated community safety disruptions, and resource efficiency concerns related to water and
                          energy consumption, water pollution; odors and air pollution from decomposing organic matter in dredged materials,
                          health and occupational risks; temporary traffic disruptions, and soil and groundwater contamination from leachate if
                          not properly managed.




_For Official Use Only_
                          12.     Key social risks and impacts include: (i) involuntary resettlement risks, including physical and economic
                          displacement, resulting from the land acquisition expected to be carried out for the project’s infrastructure works, and
                          business interruption associated to a difficulty on the achievement of compensation process (due to complex procedures,
                          limited institutional commitment) ; significant adverse impacts on informal livelihoods, including of those dependent on
                          informal recycling (waste pickers, which may include highly vulnerable individuals in extreme poverty), as a result of the
                          expected changes to the existing landfill operations, as well as of those involved in informal commerce in areas targeted
                          for infrastructure development. To date around 2600 parcels, 4000 households could be permanently or temporary
                          impacted by land acquisition as well as by physical or economical displacement, many of which are socioeconomically
                          vulnerable; (ii) risk of local tensions, particularly as part of the land tenure certification process, but also for the provision
                          of housing reconstruction support to affected households in urban and rural areas of Grand Toamasina, potential
                          exclusion of vulnerable groups, and elite capture; (iii) community health and safety risks, including those associated with
                          the expected interactions between contractors’ workers and the local population, construction accidents for the
                          population in areas surrounding civil works, traffic accident increases, and potentially increased levels of Sexual
                          Exploitation and Abuse (SEA) and Sexual Harassment (SH), and transmission of communicable diseases as well; (iv)
                          downstream social impacts of the TA activities, especially the new landfill whose studies will be supported by the Project;
                          (v) and potential impacts on cultural heritage.
                          13.     Civic and citizen engagement activities. During the preparation the project has already conducted stakeholder
                          consultation both in Antananarivo and Toamasina, to ensure project ownership and to improve the design of the project.
                          In accordance with the ESS 10 and the Civic and Citizen Engagement (CCE) Guidelines in IPF projects, the project has one
                          civic citizen engagement indicator (Project-affected people who report being adequately informed and consulted on
                          project activities (Percentage), disaggregated by gender) which should be captured through an annual feedback
                          perception surveys to monitor stakeholder engagement and satisfaction with project activities. In addition, the GM
                          should be able to manage all complaints received by the project. Therefore, the project will develop and implement a
                          tailored GM for all project activities beyond environmental and social considerations.
                          14.     Mitigation hierarchy will be applied, prioritizing avoidance of E&S impacts where possible, followed by
                          minimization, mitigation, and as a last resort, compensation or offsetting.

                          15.     As the detailed technical designs are not yet available at the preparation phase, and the exact locations of some
                          activities (such as quarry sites and worker camps) under Component 1.1 and Component 1.2 are not yet fully defined and
                          key information required to finalize the technical studies is still being developed, the project has prepared and disclosed
                          on May 18, 2026 (i) a draft Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) for assessing and managing
                          environmental and social risks and impacts of sub-projects, including procedures for screening, assessment, mitigation,
                          and monitoring. This will include specific guidance for managing potential impacts related to drainage infrastructure,
                          solid waste management facilities, and land regularization activities (ii) draft Resettlement Framework (RF) covering the
                          entire project. Given the potential for land acquisition and physical or economic displacement associated with
                          infrastructure development (e.g., dikes, drainage canals, waste facilities) and land regularization efforts, the RF set out
                          the principles and procedures for addressing involuntary resettlement, including compensation, livelihood restoration,
                          and grievance redress. (iii) draft stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP), including mechanisms for information disclosure,
                          consultation, and a robust Grievance Mechanism (GM); (iv) a SEA/SH Action Plan for addressing potential SEA/SH risks
                          (as an annex of the ESMF), (v) draft Labor Management Procedures (LMP). These Documents must be finalized no later
                          than three months after effective date.
                          16.     During the implementation of the project, and in parallel with the detailed technical designs site-specific ESMPs,
                          ESIAs, and Resettlement Plan (RPs) and/or Livelihood Restoration Plans (LRPs) will be prepared for the rehabilitation and
                          upgrade drainage canals in GA-GT, retention basins, pumping stations, and river dikes along the Ikopa, Mamba, and
                          Sisaony rivers in Antananarivo, and reconstruction of affected households, rehabilitation and reconstruction of public

                          E. Implementation

                          Institutional and Implementation Arrangements

                          19.     MATSF will act as the lead implementing agency, ensuring overall coordination, strategic oversight, and alignment
                          with national policies. Implementation arrangements build on the existing PRODUIR setup, combining two Project
                          Management Units (PMU) in GT and GA, a delegated implementing agency (AGETIPA) and structured coordination
                          mechanisms. The PMU in GA will retain responsibility for core fiduciary, environmental and social (E&S), monitoring and
                          reporting functions, and will implement non-infrastructure and institutional activities (Subcomponents 1.1 non-
                          infrastructure, 1.3, and Component 2). A dedicated Toamasina Management Unit (TMU) will be established to manage
                          reconstruction activities under Subcomponent 1.2. AGETIPA will deliver infrastructure works in Greater Antananarivo,

                          addressing cross-cutting areas such as E&S, digital innovation, and citizen engagement.
