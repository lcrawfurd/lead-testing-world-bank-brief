    I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT
1.      This Project is the first phase of the Sierra Leone Water Security and Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH) Access
Improvement Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) Program and marks the World Bank’s re-engagement in the
water sector in Sierra Leone, after decades of absence.

    A. Country Context
2.       Sierra Leone is highly vulnerable to economic and climate shocks, which have disrupted macroeconomic
stability and hindered sustained growth. Over the past decade, Sierra Leone faced several crises that have disrupted
growth. With a Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita of US$542, 24.5 percent of the population lives below the
international poverty line 1. The country aims to achieve middle-income status by 2039, requiring significant reforms in
macroeconomic stability, access to services, human capital development, disaster resilience, and governance.
3.       Rising economic pressures in Sierra Leone have led to tough policy measures to prevent a crisis. Monetary policy
was tightened by 250bps during 2024 to bring down inflation from its 54.6 percent peak in October 2023 to 13.8 percent
by December 2024. Since then, inflationary pressure decelerated continuously to reach 6.5 percent in July 2025. Inflation
decline is driven by global energy and food prices, monetary policy tightening and a stable rate exchange throughout 2024,
compared to a 58 percent combined depreciation between 2022 and 2023. Domestic revenue collection, despite reaching
10.2 percent of GDP in 2024 compared to 7.4 percent of GDP in2023, remains among the world’s lowest. Expenditure
pressures persist with recurrent overruns which increased the debt distress risk and the debt service cost, at the expense
of social spending. Employment remains primarily in low-productivity and climate-vulnerable subsistence agriculture.
4.      Climate change impacts are severe in Sierra Leone. The country underwent over 300 disasters in the past 25
years. The Notre Dame Global Adaption Index ranks Sierra Leone’s vulnerability to climate change 179 out of 187 countries
and territories. 2 It is considered the ninth most climate vulnerable country in the world with increasing exposure to
recurrent and more extreme flooding, landslides, drought, and wildfire events, particularly in the Western Area. Urban
expansion, hillside deforestation, poor drainage, and settlement in floodplains and coastal areas exacerbate flood impacts.
Droughts are more frequent because of shifting rainfall patterns, less predictable rainy seasons, and rising temperatures
leading to higher evaporation. The World Bank Climate Change Knowledge Portal highlights that the growing number of
climate change-exacerbated floods and droughts affects water resources and related infrastructure and hinders growth.
5.      Water security 3 and reliable access to Water Supply, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) are fundamental to
human development and to reducing vulnerability to climate-sensitive public health risks. 4 Sierra Leone’s Human
Development Index score of 0.467 falls below the Sub-Saharan Africa average of 0.568, ranking 185th out of 193 countries. 5
Poor WASH cause waterborne diseases such as diarrhea and cholera, thus adversely affecting health, cognitive
development, and economic growth. Limited WASH access reduces living standards and hampers economic growth. To
meet Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) 6, which calls for universal access to safely managed water supply 6 and
sanitation by 2030, Sierra Leone must expand basic WASH access 7 and invest in service efficiency improvement. These

 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
6.       Sierra Leone has abundant water resources yet faces an economic water scarcity challenge. The country receives
an average of 2,526 millimeters of rain annually. This is the highest in West Africa. The actual withdrawal rate is only 0.1
percent of approximately 160 billion cubic meters of annual renewable water. While it is enough to meet basic domestic
needs in absolute terms, these resources are not equitably distributed in space and time and are insufficient to cover
agricultural, commercial and industrial needs. This reveals a systemic economic water scarcity problem, which is primarily
due to limited water infrastructure, inefficient services, and poor water governance, compounded by climate change.
7.      Access to WASH remains stubbornly low hindering human development and exacerbating poverty. Sierra
Leone's population doubled between 2000 and 2022, but access to WASH only recorded marginal increases. Access to
water supply increased on par with population growth but not sanitation and hygiene. While 63 percent of Sierra Leoneans
have access to basic water, only 23 percent have access to improved drinking water sources within premises. Only 32
percent of people have access to either safely managed or basic sanitation and 12 percent to basic hygiene as illustrated
by Figure 1. Sierra Leone's open defecation rate is 25 percent. Human feces and untreated wastewater pollute human
settlements and ground- and surface water. A 2022 study found that all tested standpipes and wells in Freetown were
contaminated with E. coli. 8 Earlier studies reported that 30 to 60 percent of water sources surveyed across the country
showed bacteriological contamination. 9 Joined UNICEF/WHO/World Bank health indicator monitoring reports that child
mortality in the country is 45 percent higher than Sub-Saharan Africa’s average and over 26 percent of children under 5
are stunted. These statistics highlight the need for improved water management and development strategies to harness
the underutilized resources, increase water security for all purposes and improve access to WASH in Sierra Leone.

8.      Binding constraints to WASH service delivery. The WASH Poverty Diagnostic 2024 10 highlights the binding
constraints to WASH sector development and service delivery. In addition to poor water resources management, low
operational efficiency, rapid and unplanned urbanization, and the impacts of climate change, other aspects that hinder
the sector and restrict the operator’s capacity to achieve sustainability and expand access to WASH include:
        •    The sanitation sector is the least developed: Sanitation is almost inexistent. When people have access to a toilet
             facility, it is usually not connected to a proper on-site treatment system. There are a few vacuum truck operators,
             but no Fecal Sludge Treatment Plant (FSTP) able to treat the collected sludge. Freetown has a few kilometers of
             sewerage network conveying sewage from the central business district and discharging it untreated into the sea.
        •    Inadequate sector financing and investment: To meet SDG 6 targets, Sierra Leone needs to invest about US$557
             per capita until 2030, US$84 million per annum to achieve universal basic access, and US$361 million for safely
             managed access to WASH. In comparison, the budgetary allocation to the water sector was US$19 million in FY22,
             representing 9 percent of the national budget, of which only 27 percent materialized. The sector depends heavily
             on external funding and operators are not creditworthy, which prevents them accessing private capital.
        •    Poor sector coordination: WASH interventions in Sierra Leone are hindered by a lack of coordination among
             stakeholders. There is insufficient collaboration between the government and its development partners, as well
             as within government entities. The absence of long-term vision and planning, along with weak implementation
             and policy enforcement, creates a constant need for quick fixes and an over-dependence on short-term projects.
        •    Sector inefficiencies and capacity gaps: Key water utilities in the sector could be more efficient. The Guma Valley
             Water Company (GVWC) reports 60 percent Non-Revenue Water (NRW) and 73 percent collection efficiency. This
             means that only 29 percent of the water injected into the network generates revenue. The Sierra Leone Water
             Company (SALWACO) reports 30 percent NRW. Institutional and human resource capacities are limited, with
             insufficient focus on systems strengthening, human resource development, and institutional development to plan,
             deliver, and manage WASH services effectively. Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) systems and WASH data
             platforms exist, but routine data collection and management need improvement. In addition, WASH emergency
             preparedness is essential, learning from the Ebola and COVID 19 outbreaks.
        •    Institutional weaknesses. The sanitation mandate was transferred from the Ministry of Health to the Ministry of
             Water Resources (MWRS) in July 2023. This transfer needs to be operationalized, and the institutional capacity of
             the MWRS has to be built. The National Water Resources Management Agency (NWRMA), which started its
             operations in 2019, also requires extensive capacity building to carry out its mandate effectively. Local councils,
             which have a sanitation mandate, are inadequately staffed and have insufficient budget to deliver on it.
9.      Recognizing the need for a comprehensive programmatic approach to support the WASH sector, the
Government of Sierra Leone (GoSL) prepared a National WASH Sector Development Plan 2025-2035 (NWASHSDP). The
NWASHSDP builds on previous efforts and aligns with national, regional, and international commitments, such as the
Medium-Term National Development Plan (MTNDP) 2024-2030, SDG 6 and the African Union's Agenda 2063. It presents
GoSL’s vision to develop WASH and a roadmap to achieve its goal of universal access to WASH. The vision of the
NWASHSDP is “a water-secure and sanitation-safe Sierra Leone through efficient and resilient water institutions by 2035”.
By aligning sector interventions with the roadmap, GoSL will strengthen its WASH sector and generalize access to WASH.
10.      The MWRS has the overall governance mandate for WASH. This entails sector policy and governance, sector
oversight, monitoring, evaluation, and collaboration with water supply service providers, local governments responsible
for sanitation, regulators, and parallel ministries to promote the development and improvement of the WASH sector.

 C. Relevance to Higher Level Objectives

11.      This program aligns with Sierra Leone’s MTNDP 2024-2030, the World Bank’s Country Partnership Framework
(CPF) for FY21–FY26 11, and the NWASHSDP (2025-2035). The MTNDP stresses the need for human capital development
with emphasis on scaling-up investment in water, environmental sanitation, waste management and hygiene. Likewise,
the CPF, particularly under its Human Capital Acceleration for Inclusive Growth focus area, advocates for improvements
in water infrastructure and waste management. This dual emphasis highlights the relevance that resilient water security
will play in advancing human capital development and fostering inclusive growth for Sierra Leone.
12.     Further, the recently completed Performance and Learning Review of the CPF (May 2024) 12 reinforces
enhancing access to water services. The Government has expressed interest in reinvigorating the World Bank’s
engagement in the water sector. In this context, the proposed MPA, of which this proposed Project is Phase 1, is being
designed to support these higher-level objectives by:
    (a) Enhancing Water Service Delivery and Access to Sanitation: Directly contributing to the expansion and reliability
        of WASH services, the Project will improve service delivery, ensuring that communities have reliable access to safe
        and climate-resilient WASH - a key determinant of public health and human and economic development.
    (b) Supporting Human Capital Development: By improving WASH services, the Project will help reduce water-borne
        diseases, create a healthier environment, and improve educational outcomes and economic productivity.
    (c) Leveraging National and Sectoral Strategies: By supporting the implementation of the NWASHSDP, which
        provides a comprehensive and dedicated framework for sustainable WASH improvements. It combines long-term
        capacity building and institutional strengthening with immediate service improvements.

13.      Gender and Social Inclusion. Women play a critical role in households and communities for water management
and use, especially for the efficient use of water. However, preliminary data collected through the Equal Aqua survey
suggests that women are under-represented in both managerial and technical roles within Sierra Leone's water sector.
Across GVWC, 13 percent of jobs are held by women, including 6 percent in technical positions and 22 percent in senior,
managerial or executive positions. A detailed gender gap analysis is included in Annex B to inform necessary actions that
will allow reducing or closing the gender gap and propose relevant indicators to monitor progress during implementation.
14.     Maximizing Finance for Development (MFD) and Private Capital Enabling (PCE). The Project is aligned with the
World Bank MFD approach. The Project has been tagged as MFD-e as Component 1 will support the review of the 2010
WASH Policy and other regulations and will assess Public-Private Partnerships (PPP) options to leverage private sector’s
value addition to improve WASH service delivery and access, including for the construction and operation of FSTPs or
climate-resilient toilet facilities (Component 4); and Component 3 will fund feasibility studies for the construction of dams
to mobilize water resources, and related treatment and distribution infrastructure, exploring PPP modalities, especially
where power generation potential exists. The Project has also been tagged as PCE as Component 3 will finance
performance-based contracts to generalize metering and reduce NRW. This is captured under a non-monetary
intermediate results indicator linked to component 1 in the Results Framework, namely: “PPP contractual arrangements
for NRW reduction prepared or at procurement stage (Number)”. The volume of private investment expected from the
Project will be tracked based on contracted amounts of private sector investment and will count towards PCE. In all MPA
phases, the World Bank will seek to collaborate with the International Finance Corporation (IFC) and Multilateral
Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA) to help deliver PPPs. The Project will establish a water sector donor coordination
and financing platform involving the GoSL, IFC, MIGA and development partners. It will examine co-financing opportunities
to finance sector priorities and ensure efficiency and sustainability of WASH service provision in later phases of the MPA.

 D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach

Description of the MPA
15.      Rationale for using an MPA: Given the sector challenges, the MPA is the most appropriate modality for reengaging
the World Bank in the WASH sector in Sierra Leone. By providing assurance to the GoSL that the World Bank will support
the sector over the next decade, the MPA allows putting a strong emphasis in the first phase on foundational Policies,
Institutions and Regulations (PIR) and on strengthening sector institutions and staff capacity. It focuses investments on
critical performance improvement needs in Greater Freetown and more broadly in the Western Area, where water
resources only allow about eight hours of water supply per week on average and where GVWC reports losing 60 percent
of the water in the network. The MPA permits studying in a phase investments and PPPs to be implemented in subsequent
phases. A programmatic approach also provides sufficient time to structure delivery mechanisms and management
models, in particular PPPs, for greater results and impacts. These are significant advantages over single projects.
16.      The phased approach builds in incentives across phases. The MPA is structured in three phases which are building
upon one another to deliver both immediate service improvements and long‑term water security gains. The MPA
establishes a logical progression across phases and includes incentives to deliver on PIR activities within and across phases.
In particular, the preparation of the next phase of the MPA will be conditioned on progress related to foundational PIR
and absorption capacity aspects, as well as minimum commitment and disbursement levels. The phased approach is
designed to improve WASH services while strengthening institutional capacity, ultimately contributing to improving
human capital and human development outcomes in Sierra Leone. Incremental testing and scaling of innovative
technological and financial solutions will be a key feature. Structuring the program into successive phases allows the World
Bank to work with the GoSL and other partners to promote donor coordination and collaboration, mobilize further
resources and prepare leveraging private sector’s value addition and capital through PPPs, e.g. to improve service delivery
performance, mobilize additional water resources and increase access to WASH. The phased approach may create an

concepts and gather critical insights that will shape the approach for horizontal scaling in later phases. Each phase of the
MPA will learn from previous one(s) and apply key lessons to government, sector institutions and local governments.

 B. Project Components
Component 1: Institutional Strengthening and Capacity Building (US$8.55 million equivalent).
32.      Subcomponent 1.1: Strengthening PIR (US$1.35 million) will: (i) review and complete existing sector policies and
regulations (e.g. 2010 Water Policy and draft 2021 WASH Policy) to ensure that they contribute to delivering on WASH
priorities and include provisions to address climate-related risks such as floods and landslides; (ii) support MWRS in
operationalizing its sanitation mandate by developing its organizational structure, responsibility-sharing arrangements
and essential management systems and tools to be able to deliver on its mandates; (iii) strengthen the role of the MWRS
in coordinating WASH sector interventions across national and local governments; (iv) support logistics related to regular
donor coordination group meetings involving all development partners and Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs)
involved in the sector to structure it as a platform to share information, promote coordinated approaches, ensure
alignment with GoSL’s priorities, crowd in more funding for the sector and promote private sector participation; (v) review
policies and regulations and advise on PPP options to leverage private sector value addition to improve the quality-of-
service delivery and to extend access to WASH services in Sierra Leone; and (vi) develop sanitation and fecal sludge
management guidelines and design standards for climate-smart WASH systems with specific measures to meet ECOWAS’
energy-efficiency standards; and (vii) strengthen the national WASH M&E platform.
33.      Subcomponent 1.2: Capacity Building and Knowledge Development (US$2.00 million) will: (i) assess the
institutional and staff capacity building needs of MWRS, NWRMA, GVWC, SALWACO, SLEWRC, FCC and WARDC; (ii) build
on the assessment to develop a comprehensive institutional and staff capacity building plan and design adapted delivery
mechanisms for each institution involved; (iii) develop training material and curriculum for entry level staff, vocational
and on-the-job trainings for existing technical and managerial staff, and evidence-based decision-making course for
executives and senior officials. Specific training materials, processes and incentives targeted to women will be devised to
ensure that women’s perspectives are considered during curriculum development and delivery, and that training formats

are adapted to encourage women's participation, including through safe learning environments and consideration of
practical constraints; and (iv) equip one existing training center. Parts of the capacity building activities may be delivered

34.     Subcomponent 1.3: Construct a WASH Administrative Center (US$5.20 million) will procure and construct a WASH

learning site. It will also provide laboratory equipment to allow regular water quality measurements to detect industrial
or domestic pollutants and will structure a revenue stream to ensure the sustainable supply of consumables.
35.     Component 2: Water Resources Management Improvement (US$3.00 million equivalent).
36.      Subcomponent 2.1: National Water Resources Master Plan and Sector Knowledge Enhancement (US$2.60 million).
Activities under this subcomponent will develop a national Water Resources Master Plan, including updating the existing
national inventory of ground- and surface water abstraction points in Sierra Leone, informing the hydrological and
hydrogeological characteristics, determining the water balance in the country’s various River Basins, estimating the water
resources and groundwater potential of each River basin, modelling climate-induced impacts on water resources, and
mapping and documenting land and water use patterns of major River Basins. The masterplan will also examine measures
to enforce the implementation of existing WRM Regulations, in particular the annual ‘licensing to operate’ for all drilling
companies operating in Sierra Leone, and the need to obtain a permit and to register any new borehole into the national
inventory. This is a prerequisite to securing water resources outside of the Western Area in subsequent MPA phases.
37.      Subcomponent 2.2: Raising awareness, catchment protection and rejuvenation need (US$0.40 million). This
subcomponent will include three main activities. First, it will fund erecting billboards to demarcate the greenbelt boundary
in areas requiring raising awareness of the limit beyond which human activity means encroaching and increase monitoring
and surveillance. Second, it will build protective barriers to protect the catchment of selected water supply weirs either in
the buffer zone outside the greenbelt or within it and which are under increasing encroachment pressure. Third, it will
prepare a map of priority areas suggesting catchment restorative nature-based solutions (e.g., afforestation, soil
conservation) to be implemented to restore the greenbelt forest cover in places where it has been degraded and monitor
soil moisture and carbon sequestration to demonstrate that preserving forest cover increases climate resilience. To
implement these activities, NWRMA will collaborate with MWRS, GVWC, Ministry of Environment and Climate Change
(MoECC), the National Protected Area Authority and NGOs active in this field, such as the Catholic Relief Services.
38.     By enhancing water resources planning, monitoring, and protection, Component 2 will support long-term water
security, improve resilience and reduce the potential impact of floods (as water will be less contaminated) and droughts,
as enough clean water will become available when and where needed, and water service disruptions will be reduced.
Component 3: Priority Investments in Urban Water Supply (US$16.25 million equivalent).
39.     Subcomponent 3.1: Strengthening GVWC management tools and improving service efficiency (US$7.40 million).

(iv) update customer database and GIS mapping in the process to maximize revenue generation, which is essential for
better demand management as well as increasing cost recovery and profitability; (v) carry out a full safety inspection of
the Guma Dam and study the feasibility of a design and build contract to increase the height of the Guma Dam; (vi)

coordination platform to discuss financing options, possibly through parallel financing of MPA Phase 2 and/or 3; and (viii)
fund a construction supervision consultant to review technical soundness and value for money of contracted activities.
41.     With these strategic investments - ranging from commercial process improvements and metering to infrastructure
upgrades and feasibility studies - Component 3 will reduce water losses, increase revenue, and improve service reliability,
notably in low-income areas, thus making communities more resilient to climate change–aggravated droughts. Improved
water supply infrastructure is more resistant to damage from extreme rainfall or flooding.

Component 4: Urban Sanitation Service Delivery Improvement (US$9.20 million).
42.     Subcomponent 4.1: Improving Sanitation in the FCC Area (US$4.80 million). This subcomponent will be

points of Freetown, through the construction of up to 50 improved public toilet facilities with handwashing and adapted
menstrual health and hygiene solutions in selected schools, hospitals/health centers and possibly market places, bus
stations and parks or recreational areas where people gather in their free time.
43.       Subcomponent 4.2: Improving Sanitation in the WARDC Area (US$3.70 million). This subcomponent will be
implemented by WARDC, in partnership with the Sanitation Directorate of the MWRS. It will: (i) build at least one FSTP in
the WARDC area, using a climate-efficient technology such as biogas digestion combined with biochar generation, which
can be used for cooking instead of wood or charcoal, hence decreasing pressure on the WAPNP forest; (ii) increase access
to improved sanitation in critical points of the WARDC area, through the construction of about 45 improved public toilet
facilities with handwashing and adapted menstrual health and hygiene solutions in selected schools, hospitals/health
centers, market places, bus stations and public institutions.
44.      Subcomponent 4.3: Preparing Nationwide Sanitation Development Campaign (US$0.70 million). This

Inclusive Sanitation (CWIS) plan to structure the sludge management value chain in the Western Area for the safe
collection, transport and treatment of fecal sludge from households’ domestic and public toilet facilities.

 C. Project Beneficiaries
48.     The Project is designed to directly benefit at least 1,700,000 people in the Western Area mainly in Freetown and
parts of WARDC (about 400,000 for water supply and 1,300,000 for sanitation and hygiene). MPA phases two and three
are also expected to directly benefit an increasing number of people (see Table 1). The number of beneficiaries is likely
higher but is conservatively estimated until there is more visibility on the implementation timeframe of the National Water
Resources Masterplan and when the national campaign to make Sierra Leone ODF can yield results. The Project-funded
technical assistance and capacity-building activities will also benefit all seven institutions responsible for water resource
management and service delivery to enhance their efficiency and financial sustainability (MWRS, GVWC, SALWACO,
SLEWRC, NWRMA, FCC, and WARDC). Other city and local councils will also benefit from similar activities in later phases.
The Project will build about 95 WASH facilities in selected schools, health centers, and public spaces. Additionally, by
enforcing gender sensitive infrastructure and developing an integrated approach to promote adapted menstrual health
and hygiene solutions, the Project will emphasize gender and social inclusion, recognizing the disproportionate impact of
inadequate WASH services on women persons living with disability and marginalized groups. Overall, about 1,400 jobs are
expected to be generated in the first phase.

 D. Rationale for World Bank Involvement and Role of Partners
49.     Rationale for World Bank involvement. The World Bank is the largest development partner in the WASH sector
worldwide and has extensive experience in financing and implementing water supply and sanitation projects across Sub-
Saharan Africa. The World Bank is well-positioned to support Sierra Leone’s efforts to address its WASH sector challenges
and to support a platform to attract more funding to the sector. The proposed Project draws on international experience
and long-term engagements in WASH across the region. The Global Water Security and Sanitation Partnership (GWSP),
also funded foundational analytical work through the recent WASH Poverty Diagnostic and the ongoing NWASHSDP. By
leveraging its analytical work, technical expertise, global best practices, and long-standing engagements in the sector, the
World Bank is uniquely positioned to contribute to improving WASH access, ultimately driving economic growth and
human development in Sierra Leone. The World Bank is also uniquely placed to convene convergence across sector
stakeholders, be it local institutions, operators, or technical and financial partners. It also brings the IFC and MIGA into a
one World Bank Group approach to enable private sector participation.

 E. Lessons Learned and Progress on Learning Agenda
51.      The Project will benefit from a robust review of past experiences and coordinated sector initiatives, in Sierra
Leone and other countries, which have generated several key lessons and will inform the ongoing learning agenda.
These include the importance of strengthening institutional and staff capacity, fostering effective collaboration,
maintaining a clear geographical focus, and validating the management model as preconditions for successful project
scaling. The Project design integrates a continuous learning agenda that monitors these key areas through tailored
performance indicators, and Project management practices. This approach will not only support ongoing improvements
during implementation but also inform subsequent phases of the program and future operations in the WASH sector of
Sierra Leone. By institutionalizing these lessons, the Project will contribute to a broader knowledge base that can guide
similar initiatives and strengthen overall sector performance.
52.     Enhancing capacity building for greater absorption capacity: Experience from similar in-country operations, such
as AfDB’s ongoing Project, highlighted the critical need to build the capacity of existing sector institutions to enhance their
performance and potential to absorb more funding in the future. The lead agency of the AfDB project is the GVWC and
not the MWRS, but it is implemented by a dedicated PIU with support from consultants, and not directly by GVWC. Early
lessons include a slower than anticipated knowledge transfer to GVWC. The proposed Project opted to have MWRS
oversee Project implementation in close partnership and collaboration with sector institutions concerned for the design,
procurement and implementation monitoring of the activities. This allows the benefits of the Project to include greater
capacity and knowledge building, aligning incentives to perform, equipping each institution to be able to take on more
projects in the future, thereby enhancing absorption capacity, both in absolute terms and on a per-institution basis.
53.      Fostering collaboration: The WASH Poverty Diagnostic 2024 identified the lack of collaboration among key sector
stakeholders (government and development partners) as a binding constraint. The Project design and preparation process
has worked to foster collaboration and coalitions for change. The World Bank will foster collaboration externally by
working together with sector institutions, ministries, local governments and development partners but also internally by
modeling collaboration among sectors and Global Practices (GPs). Internally, the team is working with the Education,
Health, Urban, Environment, Social Development and Governance GPs, to leverage ongoing World Bank-funded
operations and policy dialog to promote a collegial and inter-ministerial approach for WASH and get Government and
public institutions to work together in discussing Project activity design, phasing and implementation monitoring, thereby

forming a greater momentum for WASH. Externally, maintaining a donor coordination group under the leadership of the
MWRS will be instrumental for greater impact and to ensure that foreign funding is aligned with the government’s
priorities. This dual-level collaboration approach promotes a new way of working through coalition for positive change
and relying on comparative advantages, creating a comprehensive support system for the WASH sector.
54.     Strategic geographical focus: Lessons from previous MPAs and engagements in Sierra Leone stressed the
importance of focusing on a limited scope and geographic area to ensure timely and quality delivery to unlock next phases.
The focus on the Western Area, which combines the administrative areas of the FCC and WARDC, is a critical strategic
decision. By concentrating initial efforts in Freetown and the Western Area, which has the highest potential for
demonstrable impact, the Project can create a solid foundation of success, which can then be leveraged through Phase 2
and 3 of the MPA. In addition, early wins will build the Recipient’s and stakeholders’ confidence and will also provide a
model for scaling up the components related to efficiency, security, and access to other cities and rural areas over time.
55.     Upfront agreement on Operation and Maintenance (O&M) management models: Early lessons from the Sierra
Leone Free Education Project (P167897) showed that setting up professional O&M management models prior to
construction is critical to ensure sustainability and expected impacts. Recent attempts to develop infrastructure relying
on community involvement alone for asset management led to abandoned or dysfunctional assets. Phase 1 of the MPA
will ensure that O&M management models, such as PPPs, with clear roles and responsibilities for the management and
financing of infrastructure such as public toilet facilities and FSTPs be developed prior to their construction. Phase 1 will
be testing such models and will involve dedicated support and feedback loops to adjust the models as needed.

 IV. PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY
 A. Technical, Economic and Financial Analysis
67.     Technical analysis: The technical aspects of the sector entities involved in the Project were assessed to be strong.
Each agency has demonstrated significant sector experience and strong institutional memory, which will feed into the
bidding document for each activity. The technical aspects of each activity under the Project were discussed in detail and
a preliminary scope of works has been agreed upon. GVWC’s masterplan provided pre-feasibility studies for some of the
proposed investments under the Project, which will ease the preparation of bidding documents. The World Bank team
provided advice to FCC and WARDC about possible designs for FSTPs and public toilets so they can transform the sludge
into biogas and biochar and in ways in which O&M arrangements could be structured. This process will be further
supported by the technical consultants which MWRS plans to hire to support the Project on specific technical aspects.
68.     The Project’s viability analysis shows strong financial and economic prospects with positive outcomes in Net
Present Value (NPV), Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) and Economic Internal Rate of Return (EIRR). The Project is estimated to
generate total discounted benefits of US$72.7 million, at a 10 percent discount rate, demonstrating its strong economic
return. An estimated NPV of US$32.7 million indicates that the Project benefits outweigh its costs, making it a worthwhile
investment. A BCR of 1.8 further demonstrates the Project’s viability, as every dollar invested generates a US$1.8 return
on direct economic and social benefits. Additionally, the EIRR of 19 percent exceeds conventional discount rates,
reinforcing the Project’s attractiveness. The EIRR means that the Project not only delivers strong financial returns but also
contributes to economic growth by improving public health and productivity.
69.       Project benefits stem mostly from reduced healthcare expenditures, increased work productivity, lower
waterborne disease prevalence, and improved operational efficiency of water service providers. These combined effects
contribute to a more resilient and self-sustaining WASH sector that support long-term economic stability in Sierra Leone,
boost utility efficiency, leading to annual savings and increased revenue and improve sanitation in the Western Area.
Community engagement initiatives will raise awareness of residents on hygiene and the importance of maintaining WASH
facilities, fostering responsibility and accountability. By investing in human capital alongside infrastructure, the Project
ensures that the benefits of improved water and sanitation services are sustained beyond the Project’s lifecycle.

D. Environmental and Social
84.      The Project’s E&S risk rating is substantial. Potential risks emanate from Project-funded activities in water
infrastructure, pollution, disease transmission from fecal sludge handling, and weak institutional capacity to manage these
risks. MWRS and partners will establish E&S risk management as per the E&S Framework (ESF) and the negotiated
Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP). 17 The Project does not involve dam construction, but will finance
technical and feasibility studies, detailed designs, and preparation of tender documents for dams and related water
infrastructure. It will build FSTPs. There are risks associated with fecal sludge handling, civil works, biodiversity impact,
and building protective barriers in not encroached water catchment areas of the WAPNP. Construction may cause noise,
air, water pollution, and health and safety risks.
85.     Institutions involved have some E&S risk management experience from previous projects but require
systematic ESF institutional capacity assessment, including on gender aspects, and proportionate capacity building
plans (human resource, soft skills training, and logistics). Environment Protection Agency Sierra Leone (EPASL) has a
rigorous risk assessment process. Stakeholder consultation and Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) will be prioritized
both during Project preparation and implementation.
86.     Considering the Project component and activities, potential social risks include social exclusion, involuntary
resettlement, and ensuring clean water access in poor neighborhoods within FCC and WARDC. Building new FSTPs may
require land acquisition and disadvantaged groups must be included in consultations. Civil works could induce moderate
labor influx. The Project may cause temporary and permanent land acquisition and displacement due to civil works and
measures to protect areas such as the WAPNP. MWRS’ weak E&S capacity requires strategic capacity building and
technical assistance for design and feasibility studies. An Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) and
a Resettlement Policy Framework (RPF) were prepared and publicly disclosed prior to Project Appraisal.
87.     The risk of SEA/SH is substantial, with 62 percent of women in Sierra Leone experiencing violence. The Project

90.      Gender is a key driver to achieving sustainable water security and WASH improvements in Sierra Leone. To
promote inclusive participation from the outset, the Project carried out a comprehensive gender gap analysis to identify
gender-specific gaps in increasing access to WASH services and participation in the water and sanitation sector. This
analysis supported the development of a tailored action plan setting clear targets for female participation and
representation, to decrease the gender gap in technical or managerial employment within the MWRS and the GVWC.
Beyond data collected through the Equal Aqua (EA) survey at GVWC, gender-disaggregated data is not produced on a
regular basis for stakeholders in the WASH sector (MWRS, NWRMA, SLEWRC, GVWC, SALWACO). As with other technical
sectors, the main drivers contributing to women’s underrepresentation include social norms, with traditional gender roles
often discouraging women from pursuing technical careers. Additionally, the absence of dedicated gender-sensitive
employment policies impacts not only the recruitment but also the promotion of female staff into managerial positions.
The lack of flexible working arrangements or schedules and childcare support as well as pathways for career advancement
such as structured training and mentorship programs are key challenges. The pipeline of female technical and/or
managerial roles is constrained by the fact that technical graduates from tertiary education are overwhelmingly men.
Based on results of the EA survey, the Project will design and implement actions aiming at closing this gap, e.g. establishing
dedicated training and effective mentorship and internship programs that will complement GoSL’s efforts to enhance
career development opportunities for women. The water sector will thereby reap the productivity gains of tapping into
women talents including through promoting a more diversified workforce. The institutional and staff capacity assessment
will seek to identify any gender-specific obstacles, constraints or biases and propose measures to mitigate them. The
development of training material and master’s program curriculum will involve women to add their perception and
sensitivity to the process. The mentorship and the internship programs will set entry requirements, and the Project will
communicate extensively to reach out to potential mentors and mentees as well as candidates for internships. They will
aim at responding to constraints identified during ongoing diagnostic work, including challenges related to workplace
culture, mobility, and balancing personal responsibilities. Both mentorship and internship programs’ effectiveness will be

 V. GRIEVANCE REDRESS SERVICES


 93.     Grievance Redress. A Project GRM will be established to allow affected stakeholders to submit grievances and
 seek redress when they perceive that a negative impact has arisen from the Project interventions. It will be designed in
 consultation with relevant government and non-government stakeholders. It will establish accessible processes to
 submit complaints and clear procedures from investigation to resolution and feedback. The GRM will include the
 provision for appeal if aggrieved parties are dissatisfied with the outcome. A communication campaign will be
 implemented aiming at raising awareness, informing stakeholders on how to use the GRM and explaining the
 investigation and resolution sequential process, timeline, and procedures.
 94.     Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by a Project supported by the World
 Bank may submit complaints to existing Project-level grievance mechanisms or the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service
 (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed in order to address Project-related concerns.
 Project affected communities and individuals may submit their complaint to the Bank’s independent accountability
 mechanism. The accountability mechanism houses the Inspection Panel, which determines whether harm occurred, or
 could occur, as a result of Bank non-compliance with its policies and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution Service,
 which provides communities and borrowers with the opportunity to address complaints through dispute resolution.
 Complaints may be submitted to the accountability mechanism at any time after concerns have been brought directly
 to the attention of Bank Management and after Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For
 information on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS), visit
 http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s Accountability
 Mechanism, visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.


 VI. KEY RISKS

95.      The overall Project risk to achieving the PDO is assessed as Substantial. The Project-specific risks and explanation
are as follows:
96.     Political and Governance risk is High, given fragile institutions, widespread poverty and un(der)employment,
which, in the context of high inflation, can result in conditions conducive to social unrest. The 2023 elections resulted in a
phase of political uncertainty, which was subsequently mediated. However, political tensions and threats to social stability
continued, aggravated by economic challenges and strong divide along party lines. World Bank-supported initiatives in

                       Official Use Only                                                                               Page 23
       The World Bank
       Sierra Leone Water Security and WASH Access Improvement Project (P507588)


skills development, cash transfers, public works, food security, agriculture help improve living conditions, alleviate youth
pressures, thereby helping mitigate these risks. The Project was prepared in close collaboration with all IAs including
opposition-led FCC and WARDC. Implementation arrangements provide IAs with the highest incentive to perform. The
Project will implement the SEP and ensure community engagement, including through the WFF.
97.     Macroeconomic risk is High. Reversals on domestic revenue mobilization or significant expenditure overruns
would undermine fiscal consolidation. Larger deficits would in turn threaten fiscal and debt sustainability, possibly
spurring an inflation-depreciation spiral. External risks are also substantial, from global commodity prices, global demand,
and imported inflation. These risks are partly mitigated by GoSL’s commitment to enhance fiscal sustainability and restore
macroeconomic stability, including through the implementation of Performance and Policy Actions of the World Bank’s
ongoing first Macro Stability and Resilience Development Policy Operation (P503960), and a 2024 IMF-supported
Extended Credit Facility program. The Project will also adopt stringent financial controls, transparent procurement,
contracts awarded in US$, direct payment and transfer from US$ to SLL account at the time of payment to limit the impact
of macroeconomic shocks such as inflation and currency depreciation. Mitigations measures also include strengthened
Public Financial Management (PFM) and procurement systems, adopting best practices in governance and relying on
multi-stakeholder platforms such as the PSC, the development partner’s coordination platform and community
engagement to complement MWRS’ oversight.
98.     Sector strategy and Policies is Substantial: GoSL partnered with the World Bank and other donors on foundational
analytical work and sector policies which informed Project preparation, including a WASH Poverty Diagnostic (2024), a
NWASHSDP (2025 - 2035), a National Water and Sanitation Policy (2021). However, the capacity to implement strategies
and enforce policies justifies the Substantial rating. Component 1 on institutional strengthening and capacity building is
expected to mitigate this risk by reviewing policies and regulations, turning them into action and creating the conditions
for meaningful policies and regulations enforcement. Gathering MWRS, NWRMA and SLEWRC in one place will also help.
99.      Technical Design of Program and Project risks are Substantial. Capacity building activities, consultants support
and technical assistance, combined with the team’s close supervision and review of technical designs, will reduce this risk.
It is, however, maintained as Substantial until local availability of high-quality consultants and capacity to attract foreign
firms to perform critical studies or the design and bidding document of feasibility studies of dam construction is confirmed.
100. Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability risk is High. Institutional capacity is low. Recently
created MWRS and NRWMA are understaffed, under-resourced and struggle to carry out their mandates. The MWRS
recently acquired the sanitation mandate from the Ministry of Health and needs to reorganize and increase its capacity to
deliver. MWRS’s building burnt in the summer of 2023 and it struggled to secure a suitable location since. The Project will
mitigate this risk by building a Water Administrative Center, strengthening PIR and capacity, having clear implementation
arrangements agreed by all IAs, supporting IAs to deliver on their mandate and creating incentives to perform. GVWC,
NWRMA, FCC and WARDC will build on their experience of implementing World Bank-funded operations. MWRS
demonstrated its capacity to gather and coordinate all IAs in an open and constructive way throughout preparation and
will remain in this coordinating role. Mitigation measures also include mobilizing consultants to be embedded within IAs
with the mission to provide both support and transfer Project implementation knowledge and skills to IA staff. The World
Bank will provide HEIS for procurement, FM, and E&S through the first two years of implementation. The World Bank
Governance team is working to strengthen mitigation measures and support implementation. It also works with IFC and
MIGA to establish key prerequisites for PPPs that incentivize efficiency improvement, capacity building and quality O&M.
101. Fiduciary risks are Substantial. The fiduciary risks are closely linked to the governance and political risks. GoSL is
advancing PFM reforms but faces challenges such as budget deviations, procedural overrides, inefficient investment
management, and unplanned political spending. Importantly, limited independence from the executive branch of the
auditor general and the Audit Service Sierra Leone (ASSL) poses a high FM risk. However, the fiduciary arrangements under
the proposed Project are designed to mitigate these risks, including: (i) a requirement for a foreign exchange Dedicated
Account to receive the grant; (ii) retaining the right to audit the Dedicated Account; (iii) the selection and training of
                       Official Use Only                                                                               Page 24
       The World Bank
       Sierra Leone Water Security and WASH Access Improvement Project (P507588)


procurement and FM staff of the WASHIT of the MWRS and of all IAs no later than 90 days after Project effectiveness. The
World Bank carried out a comprehensive review of the processes and systems used by the MRWS and in the country and
agreed to an action plan to mitigate the risks. The Project design also incorporated lessons from ongoing and past World
Bank projects. At the request of the GoSL, the World Bank will mobilize a HEIS consultant to support capacity building on
fiduciary aspects at least during the first year of implementation, during which most procurement will take place, and FM
and reporting practices will be tested. With these mitigation measures, the residual fiduciary risks remain Substantial.
102. Environment and Social risks are Substantial: There is currently weak capacity to manage the potential risks
emanating from Project activities in infrastructure, pollution and disease transmission from fecal sludge handling, civil
works, biodiversity impact, potential conflicts over barriers in water catchment areas, and studies of Dam construction.
Risks include social exclusion, involuntary resettlement, and ensuring clean water access in poor and remote areas. The
Project may cause temporary and permanent land acquisition and displacement. Civil works could induce labor influx and
GBV risks. MWRS’s weak E&S capacity will be addressed through strategic capacity building and technical assistance
support for design and feasibility studies and to ensure required coordination and consultation of affected communities
with special attention to involve women and poor and/or vulnerable groups. MWRS requested a HEIS to strengthen E&S
instruments preparation, establish E&S risk management and mitigation measures as per the ESF and build staff capacity
in the process. The World Bank assessed the country system and proposed practical measures to address E&S risks,
building on EPASL’s rigorous risk assessment process. Some IAs have project E&S risk management experience but require
ESF training. Stakeholders’ consultations confirmed support to the Project. A GRM will be in place in all Project areas.
Gender disaggregated toilets in schools and other places will seek to mitigate GBV risks.
103. The Project procurement risk is Substantial: identified procurement risks are: (a) procurement delays due to poor
coordination among WASHIT and IAs; (b) weak procurement and contract management capacity of WASHIT and IAs due
to limited knowledge of World Bank’s Procurement Regulations and of STEP; (c) insufficient competition due to limited
capacity of local contractors and lack of interest by international bidders; (d) weak complaint redress mechanism; and (e)
time and cost overruns due to poor planning. The proposed measures to mitigate these risks include: (i) developing a
detailed PIM by effectiveness which outlines procurement and contract management responsibilities, procedures and
complaint handling mechanism; (ii) recruiting an experienced procurement specialist within WASHIT; (iii) train WASHIT,
IA staff and procurement committees members on Procurement Regulations and contract management; (iv) establishing
a robust contract management structure; (v) using consultants to prepare procurement and supervise construction, where
capacity is lacking, (vi) Providing HEIS to WASHIT; and (vii) promoting advance procurement to accelerate Project kick-off.

                                                        ANNEX

                                  A: Implementation Arrangements and Support Plan

Introduction

1.      The proposed Project implementation arrangements are structured at three levels: coordination and strategic
direction, implementation oversight, and technical execution. At the top, the Ministry of Water Resources and Sanitation
(MWRS) provides overall leadership and strategic direction. The PSC plays a central role in governance, ensuring inter-
ministerial coordination with key governmental bodies and multi-sectoral approaches, crucial for effective Water,
Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) interventions.

2.       Below the PSC, the WASHIT is responsible for day-to-day implementation. It manages critical project functions
such as procurement, FM, E&S safeguards, communication, M&E and reporting to the World Bank. Technical execution is
handled by the responsible implementing agency including NWRMA, SLEWRC, GVWC, FCC, WARD-C, and MWRS. The
WASHIT oversees the technical execution of Project-funded activities. Each IA designates key staff to work with the
WASHIT to ensure coordination, technical collaboration, and relay implementation support needs as required. Key agency
staff will benefit from knowledge transfer and capacity strengthening from the WASHIT subject matter specialists (Figure
A.1).

facilitate MWRS’ intervention with other authorities in case of administrative obstacles.

                                                                      B: Gender Gap Analysis
1.      Rationale: Why a focus on gender in water and sanitation matters? Reliable household water supply, improved
sanitation, and well-maintained public water points reduce water poverty and improve hygiene. This can open income
opportunities for both women and men. Infrastructure in social institutions addresses basic needs and helps close gender
gaps in education and health. Women often handle unpaid water collection and sanitation tasks, so they can benefit from
earning income in construction, maintenance, management, and production of hygiene products. Involving women in
technical and managerial roles and decision-making in water and sanitation agencies is crucial. Decentralized technologies
like boreholes and rainwater harvesting support small businesses by freeing up time for income-generating activities.
Economic opportunities and social dynamics determine the most effective gender gap interventions.
2.     Context: Sierra Leone ranks among the 10 most unequal countries in the world in terms of gender, according to
the Gender Inequality Index published by UNDP (rank 184 out of 193 in 2024). 19
3.      Poverty and wealth20: In Sierra Leone, 25.1 percent of households are female-headed, with a higher percentage
in urban areas (28 percent) and a lower in rural areas (23.2 percent). The poverty rate is similar for both genders, with 52
percent of males and 53 percent of females below the international poverty line. Both genders also make up 40 percent
of the bottom 40 percent income distribution. However, 47.5 percent of female-headed households (FHHs) are below the
poverty line compared to 43.8 percent of male-headed households (MHHs). 21 Nationwide, wealth quintiles show little
variation by sex of household head. 19.6 percent of MHHs and 21.3 percent of FHHs are in the poorest quintile. In rural
areas, 31 percent of MHHs and 36 percent of FHHs fall into the poorest quintile. In urban areas, 51 percent of MHHs and
40 percent of FHHs are in the richest quintile. Adding up both top quintiles, 84 percent of urban MHHs and 77 percent of
urban FHHs are in the wealthiest categories.
4.      Labor and Income 22: International Labour Organization (ILO) data shows minimal gender gaps in labor force
participation rates, with 56.3 percent of females and 57.6 percent of males participating. However, women are more likely
to be in part-time employment (29.9 percent vs. 24.4 percent), informal sector jobs (94.1 percent vs. 89.6 percent), and
unpaid work. Global data indicates underrepresentation of women in technical and non-technical roles within the water
sector, which is male-dominated. In Sierra Leone, trends suggest lower percentages 23, but exact figures require a baseline
assessment. The UNDP’s Human Development Report shows that the estimated female GNI per capita is US$1,238 (81
percent of male GNI per capita at US$1,525), in 2011 PPP US$. Men earn significantly more than women: three times
more in wage employment, 2.5 times more in self-employment, and nearly double in agriculture. 24
5.       Water access: In Sierra Leone, women and girls collect water, which can take several hours daily, particularly in
rural areas. This task limits their educational opportunities, earning potential, and health. Social norms dictate that water
collection is women’s responsibility. Climate variability and inadequate water infrastructure mean long water-fetching
journeys, which pose safety risks including harassment and physical injury. Girls often miss school due to water collection
duties, leading to high dropout rates. Limited access to credit hampers FHHs' ability to invest in water solutions.
Inadequate sanitation access disproportionately impacts women and girls, increasing health and safety risks related to
reproductive health, menstrual hygiene, and maternal care. Women frequently serve as primary caregivers responsible
for hygiene and sanitation. Socio-economic consequences include higher maternal mortality rates, long-term health issues
from poor menstrual hygiene, and increased school dropout rates. Cultural stigmas surrounding menstruation exacerbate
these challenges. Urban informal settlements suffer from overcrowding and lack of gender-segregated facilities,

worsening sanitation problems. The absence of sanitation infrastructure in rural areas heightens the risk of sexual

6.        Productivity and access to productive resources: Men own a higher share of agricultural land, according to Sierra
Leone’s Integrated Household Survey, 75 percent of plots were controlled by men, with larger plots. 58 percent of male-
headed households (MHHs) own land compared to 49 percent of female-headed households (FHHs). MHHs also make up
75 percent of all households, resulting in a higher total number of male landowners. Both MHHs and FHHs have similar
ownership rates of livestock, but the absolute number of MHHs is three times higher. 25 Customary law and legal framework
allow sex discrimination in “adoption, marriage, divorce, burial, devolution of property on death or other interests of
personal law”. Widows rarely inherit family or community property. According to the World Bank systematic country
diagnostics, only 12.7 percent of widows inherited the majority of their spouses’ assets in 2008. This affects women's
economic opportunities, especially in agriculture, which is crucial for poorer households, and there is strong evidence of
a link between land tenure security and productive investments in land. 26 Limited asset ownership impacts access to
utilities like water and energy. Identifying and overcoming these barriers is essential for improving women's access to
basic services.
7.       Financial Inclusion 27: Global FINDEX data shows a disparity favoring males in financial inclusion indicators like
owning an account (24.8 percent of males vs. 15.4 percent of females), borrowing money in the past year (45.3 percent
vs. 53.1 percent), saving money in the past year (57.0 percent vs. 51.7 percent), and making/receiving digital payments in
the past year (16.6 percent vs. 10.1 percent). Analyzing the sources of loans, most of the gap arises from informal sources,
like family and friends. Males are more likely to have saved during the last year to “start, operate, or expand a farm or
business”, while females are more likely to have borrowed for the same. Similarly, access to ﬁnancing will be crucial in how
women beneﬁt from access to water and sanitation and whether they will be able to undertake income-generating
activities. Enhancing women's financial inclusion can boost their economic opportunities through productive uses.
8.       Entrepreneurship: The Enterprise Survey collects data from the manufacturing and service industries around the
world. In 2017, it showed that, in Sierra Leone, only 18.8 percent of firms had female ownership, lower than Sub-Saharan
Africa's average (29.6 percent) and the global average (35.7 percent). The share of firms with a female top manager was
15.9 percent, close to the regional average (15.4 percent) and global average (18.1 percent).
9.      Maternal health: Sierra Leone has made significant progress on maternal and child mortality rates. The World
Health Organization indicators 28 show that the maternal mortality ratio in 2023 is 354 deaths per 100,000 live births, which
is lower than the Africa average of 442 and slightly above that of low-income counties’ figure of 346.
10.       Healthcare quality and access are priority areas identified by the World Bank, especially maternal and child health
services. Poor maternal health and inadequate family planning limit women's economic participation. In Sierra Leone,
women typically have their first child at 19 and average five children. High maternal mortality combined with high fertility
means 1 in 20 women die from childbirth complications. Outcomes vary among women. Women in the poorest quintile
have twice the fertility rate of those in the least-poor quintile. Rural areas have more limited health services than urban
areas. 29

11.      Education30: While the gender gap is closing in primary education, disparities remain at secondary and tertiary
levels. Girls' enrollment is higher at primary level but drops significantly from age 13, widening the gap in higher education.
Financial constraints are the main reason for dropping out, along with cultural issues like early marriage. The 2014 Labor
Force Survey showed that women were more likely to be barred from school by their families or undervalue education.
Women make up only one-third of tertiary enrollments, with limited employment opportunities. Promoting women's
employment in the WASH sector could create opportunities for skilled graduates and increase participation in relevant
education. The Project aims to address key gender gaps:
     a. Poorer FHH in urban areas have less access to WASH services.
     b. Women, due to lower asset ownership and access to finance, start fewer income-generating activities than men.
     c. Women are under-represented in the sector's talent pool.
     d. Women's basic health and education needs lag behind men's, partly due to inadequate access to WASH services.
12.      Entry Points and Potential Actions: The Project can address gender gaps by:
      a. Reducing disparity in access to water and sanitation services for poorer urban households, especially FHHs.
      b. Assessing barriers to asset ownership, financial inclusion, and entrepreneurship, and designing targeted
          programs, potentially involving private sector and MSME support, to improve women's livelihoods and
          participation in income-generating activities.
      c. Creating opportunities for women to join the water sector workforce, which may also strengthen staff capacity
          at key sector institutions.
      d. Improving women’s health and education outcomes by targeting WASH services for social infrastructure that
          addresses their needs.
13.     The Project could improve women's outcomes by providing access to poorer households, especially by assessing
and considering factors for their connection during implementation. Tailored interventions in both technical and non-
technical roles in the water sector could ensure balance and enhance the talent pool for future benefits.
14.      Project Component 1, Subcomponent 1.2: Capacity Building and Knowledge Development, could lift obstacles to
women's careers in managerial, technical and senior positions within MWRS and sector institutions. Activities planned to
develop a water management curriculum at the university of Sierra Leone could improve access of women and girls to
technical studies in water and facilitate work transition to the WASH sector at all levels of qualiﬁcation. Good practice in
similar countries provides a toolbox of possible actions which could be adapted to the national context, including next to
campus housing, raising awareness to reduce harassment during studies, mentoring, access to training, internship, etc.
15.      The Project Subcomponent 3.1 could deﬁne quotas for female participants in training activities, e.g. on upgraded
EDAMS for GVWC staﬀ. It could ensure that the practical organization of training activities are compatible with the female’s
speciﬁc constraints, e.g. ensuring a “safe space” for women in training. Within the same subcomponent, the Project could
specifically train teams of female technicians to install and maintain water meters. The added value for utilities and
communities of having such tasks performed by women has been documented, taking benefit from the positive perception
by consumers of female operators. Also, in the fight against illegal connection, women arguments has been shown to be
more effective in changing perceptions and social norms. The Project will empower women in water institutions and in
local communities to ensure that water tankers funded under the Project supply water kiosks in low-income communities
and avoid directing trucks to service wealthy people not connected to the water network.
16.      Recognizing the evolving nature of gender capacity and gaps, the Project will refine its diagnostic work post-
effectiveness through the EA platform and participatory planning using the post-effectiveness roadmap tool for deeper
institutional assessments, co-design of responsive interventions, and iterative adjustment of activities during
implementation.
