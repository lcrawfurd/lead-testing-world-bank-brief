                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The proposed Project extends the Bank’s continued support to the provision of potable water, sanitation and transport
                     connectivity services in the Pacific region of Colombia. It will complete and operationalize the infrastructure originally
                     envisioned under the Water Supply and Basic Sanitation Infrastructure and Service Delivery Project (P156239) and the
                     Enhancing Waterway Connectivity and Water Service Provision in Colombia’s Plan PAZcífico Project (P156880). By
                     carrying forward and finalizing these investments, the proposed Project aims to convert partial progress into sustained
                     service delivery outcomes for water, sanitation and transport connectivity in one of Colombia’s most underserved
                     regions, while addressing the bottlenecks that have slowed execution to date. Project implementation will build upon

                     the experience and lessons learned from the FTSP during the execution of the original water and connectivity
                     operations. The MIE, as implementing agency, has established a PIU for project management, fiduciary tasks, reporting,
                     and environmental and social management. Project components include: Component 1: Improving Water Supply and
                     Sanitation Supports expanding and upgrading potable water and sanitation services in urban areas with low coverage.
                     Investments include water intake, treatment, distribution, metering, and sanitation infrastructure to reduce health and
                     environmental risks. Also addresses solid waste management and upgrades WASH facilities in schools and health
                     centers for greater health, inclusivity, and disease resilience. Component 2: Improving Waterway Transport Finances the
                     design, construction, and maintenance of resilient, accessible river and maritime docks, including floating docks.
                     Supports climate-resilient infrastructure, low-emission electric boats, and navigation aids to improve safety and
                     connectivity. Component 3: Capacity Building and Institutional Strengthening Enhances the capacity of water and
                     transport service providers and local institutions through training, performance-based management, citizen
                     engagement, and support for internships and community micro-enterprises. Focuses on gender inclusion, participatory
                     governance, and prevention of gender-based violence in public transport. Component 4: Project management and
                     environmental and social management provides support for project implementation, including financial management,
                     procurement, disbursement, and the design and execution of environmental and social instruments, as well as
                     operating costs and audits. Component 5: Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC) establishes a mechanism
                     for rapid access to financing in response to eligible crises or emergencies, enabling the reallocation of funds to address
                     major adverse impacts from natural or man-made disasters.


                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings



Public Disclosure
                     The Colombian Pacific region comprises 178 municipalities spread across four departments: Cauca, Chocó, Nariño and
                     Valle del Cauca. Its location on the Pacific coast makes it one of the country's most biologically diverse areas. Around 1.5
                     million people live in this region; ethnics groups are 80% Afro-Colombian, 12% indigenous peoples and 8% mestizos
                     (Truth Commission, n/d). The region has 158 community councils and 273 indigenous reserves, which contribute to its
                     great social and cultural diversity. Nevertheless, it is a region characterized by significant social disparities, evident
                     through issues such as: i) multidimensional poverty, which was expected to affect 11.8% of the population by 2024
                     (Dane, 2024); ii) limited access to safe or quality drinking water in 122 of its municipalities, which has an impact on the
                     incidence of acute diarrheal disease, one of the leading causes of child morbidity in the region; iii) dispersion of health
                     and education services, among others, meaning that in most municipalities these services can only be accessed via
                     informal, costly and insecure river and maritime infrastructure (RAP Pacífico, 2021). The humanitarian situation in the
                     Pacific region has deteriorated over the past two years due to natural disasters such as flooding and armed conflict.
                     These events have resulted in mass displacement and confinement, restricting the population's mobility and access to
                     essential goods and services. This low and uneven territorial development has worsened by history of internal conflict,
                     lack of trust between the region and the Government of Colombia (GoC), underinvestment in basic infrastructure, weak
                     local governance, illegal activities that drive the economy in some areas, and inadequate rural-urban linkages
                     exacerbating socioeconomic gaps. On the other hand, Colombia’s Pacific region also stands out for its rich Afro-
                     Colombian and Indigenous cultures and rich natural assets.

                     II. SCREENING OF POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL RISKS AND IMPACTS

                     A. Environmental and Social Risk Classification (ESRC)                                                         Substantial


                     A.1 Environmental Risk Rating                                                                                  Substantial



Public Disclosure
                     The environmental risk of the project is rated as Substantial, given the nature and scale of the proposed
                     interventions. The project is expected to deliver positive environmental benefits, including improved drinking water
                     quality, sanitation services, and connectivity. A key benefit is the anticipated reduction in pollution of natural water
                     bodies through the construction of wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) in selected municipalities. The project
                     scope includes: i) completion of the water supply infrastructure of the current project of the Todos Somos Pazcífico
                     Plan; ii) construction of new water supply systems with existing designs and Environmental and Social Management
                     Plans (ESMPs) previously not objected to by the World Bank; and iii) implementation of new sanitation infrastructure,
                     including WWTPs, for which designs and environmental instruments are yet to be developed. However, the
                     construction phase may generate adverse impacts in urban areas. Activities include trenching along roads, installing
                     water and sewer pipelines, transporting and disposing of materials and excavation waste, upgrading existing water
                     treatment plants, and building new WWTPs. Potential risks include: i) traffic disruptions; ii) increased accident risks
                     due to diversions; iii) disturbances from noise, vibration, and dust; iv) occupational health and safety risks; v) runoff
                     potentially affecting water bodies; and vi) possible alteration of riverbanks during dock construction. An
                     Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) will be prepared to assess risks and define mitigation
                     measures aligned with the Environmental and Social Standards (ESS). It will also include provisions to complement
                     existing ESMPs developed under the Bank’s Safeguard Policies and develop new ESMPs for new projects, ensuring full
                     compliance with the Environmental and Social Framework (ESF).
                                                                                                                                    Substantial
                     A.2 Social Risk Rating
                     The recommended social risk rating at concept stage is substantial. Despite the benefits that improved health, trade
                     and access to scattered services derived from water, sanitation and connectivity works, social risks and impacts have


                    Oct 22, 2025                                                                                                       Page 4 of 9
                               The World Bank

                     been identified at this stage: (i) low community trust stemming from past failures by national/departmental entities
                     (poor contractor performance, prolonged delays) in the execution of activities under current operations which has led
                     to prolonged negative impacts to citizens, particularly to businesses and youth; (ii) designs that overlook user needs,
                     expectations, or cultural characteristics; (iii) the inadequate use of improved infrastructure due to a lack of awareness
                     and a reluctance to pay for the necessary resources to ensure its provision and sustainability; (iv) works that may
                     generate land acquisition or temporary relocations during scheduled activities and (v) mobility restrictions and work
                     disruptions caused by contextual insecurity related to armed conflict, confinement, or displacement from illegal
                     armed groups. Regarding security risks, the project area is characterized by the active presence of illegal armed
                     groups which pose direct threats to the safety of consultants, contractors, and local communities. These risks are
                     exacerbated by logistical challenges in accessing remote regions, increasing vulnerability for all personnel involved
                     and delays in works completion. Insufficient consultations risk overlooking community concerns, exacerbating project
                     impacts, and undermining trust. Without broad and inclusive engagement—especially with groups facing specific
                     harms—misunderstandings, conflict and reputational damage may increase. Strengthened, continuous citizen
                     engagement is needed to surface risks early, co-design mitigations and support monitoring and empowerment,
                     reducing the likelihood of social tension, implementation delays and inequitable outcomes.

                      ESS10 - Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure                                                       Relevant

                      A robust Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) will focus on a highly participatory approach to create the conditions
                      necessary for stakeholders to take ownership of project activities and build trust, leveraging the participation plans

                      The current grievance mechanism (GM) will be strengthened to meet ESF requirements and ensure accessibility,
                      confidentiality (especially for SEA/SH-related grievances) and timely responses based on lessons from prior
                      implementation. Additionally, project activities and initiatives are being evaluated to enhance and integrate social
                      inclusion measures aimed at strengthening community empowerment and monitoring.

                      ESS2 - Labor and Working Conditions                                                                          Relevant

                      According to the WB’s Overview Assessment, Colombian labor and occupational health and safety (OHS) regulations
                      are materially consistent with ESS2, although implementation challenges persist; measures complementary to the
                      legislation will be set –to be determined whether through a Labor Management Plan (LMP) or specific measures in
                      ESMP, POM, code of conduct, etc.– to ensure ESF compliance. Areas to be strengthened and embedded in contracts
                      are: clarity and written communication of employment terms; specific GM for workers with anonymous reporting and
                      confidentiality options, specific SEA/SH channel, and culturally appropriate measures for indigenous and Afro-
                      Colombian workers; enhancing contractor oversight. The PIU will have teams in Bogotá and the Pacific region
                      responsible for monitoring labor management by contractors, suppliers and consultants. Health and safety measures
                      and training will be implemented throughout the project, in line with ESS2 and World Bank EHS Guidelines.

                      ESS3 - Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention and Management                                           Relevant

                      The project will implement civil works for water supply, sanitation (including WWTPs), WASH improvements, solid
                      waste management, and dock construction, mainly in urban areas. Construction may cause air, water, and soil
                      pollution, noise, GHG emissions, and natural resource exploitation. The project will apply the mitigation hierarchy to
                      manage these risks. Existing ESMPs will be reviewed and complemented to align with ESS3, considering local
                      regulations, cumulative impacts, and resource use. Timber must be sourced from licensed suppliers using good
                      practices, especially in forest reserve zones. WWTPs must meet discharge standards and avoid risks to nearby
                      populations; the ESMPs will include sludge and solid waste management plans. All works must have ESMPs consistent
                      with ESS3 and not objected to by the Bank. The ESCP will include this requirement and mandate submission of TORs

                      for ESMP consultancies to ensure alignment with the ESF and relevant standards.

                      ESS4 - Community Health and Safety                                                                           Relevant

                      Communities and workers are exposed to construction hazards, increased traffic and navigation risks, hazardous
                      materials management, disease transmission and contextual insecurity. These risks will be assessed and mitigated
                      with measures in the ESMF, ESMPs and LMP/labor management measures. SEA/SH and labour influx-related risks
                      (including GBV) will be subject to further assessment during preparation; mitigation measures will include a Code of
                      Conduct, specialized GM-channel for SEA/SH cases and capacity building. Contextual insecurity may limit the access
                      of contractors into the territory, or restrict community members from participating in meetings and training sessions,
                      which could exacerbate other risks. Mitigation measures include hiring a security specialist for the PIU, enhanced
                      security plan and protocols, ongoing risk assessments, contingency planning and close coordination with government
                      and international partners to adapt to evolving threats.

                     C. Overview of Required Environmental and Social Risk Management Activities

                     C.1 What Borrower environmental and social analyses, instruments, plans and/or frameworks are planned or
                     required by Appraisal?
                     The following environmental and social (E&S) instruments are required prior to or by Appraisal:

                     1. Draft Environmental and Social Commitment Plan (ESCP): it will establish specific actions and timelines to ensure
                     compliance with Colombian regulations, the World Bank’s Environmental, Health, and Safety (EHS) Guidelines, and the



Public Disclosure
                     Project’s E&S instruments.

                     2. An Environmental and Social assessment, which identifies impacts and risks in a manner consistent with the ESF. It
                     will serve as the basis to develop the Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF), which will be finalized
                     after Effectiveness – exact timeline will be defined prior to Appraisal based on the confirmation of project activities –
                     and will include: i) the applicable regulatory framework and its requirements; ii) a description of the works to be
                     developed, their physical implementation status and schedule; iii) environmental and social characteristics of the areas
                     where the works will be carried out and their areas of influence; iv) the status of environmental and social management
                     instruments of the works and an evaluation of existing instruments to define complementary measures to comply with
                     the ESF; v) Measures to manage E&S risks in line with the mitigation hierarchy, legal requirements, and ESF
                     commitments in the ESCP; vi) specific management instruments for the new activities based on the findings of the
                     assessment; vii) Institutional organization for E&S management within the PIU and requirements for contractors, and
                     supervisors, and capacity building; and viii) E&S requirements for contractors, subcontractors and suppliers. The ESMF
                     will be reviewed and approved by the Bank prior to final disclosure and implementation.

                     3. An advanced draft Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP) will be prepared and consulted among interested parties prior
                     to Appraisal, based on ESS10. It will include the description of the project’s grievance mechanism (GM).
