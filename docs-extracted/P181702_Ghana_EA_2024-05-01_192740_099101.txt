1.0       INTRODUCTION


The terrestrial flora and fauna survey, as part of the EIA requirements for Asafo Simplified Sewage System
Priority Work Project (ASSSPWP), was conducted with the following objectives:
      •   Identify and describe the main plant communities, habitats of ecological interest (forests, wetlands,
          less disturbed habitats, habitats that may contain special-status species, etc.) and plants species
          found;
      •   (Near Threatened (NT), Vulnerable (VU), Endangered (EN) and Critically Endangered (CR));
      •   Determine the presence of invasive species;
      •   Identify ecosystem and cultural services provided by habitats found inside the project area and
          provide a list of plant species with their associated uses by local communities; and
      •   Survey of terrestrial fauna- mammals, birds, reptiles and amphibians in the community.


2.0       METHODS


2.1       Flora Survey


The flora survey was in two parts:


1. Literature Review – relevant literature on the vegetation and ecological characteristics of the project
affected area was carried out. The literature consulted included Hawthorne and Musah (1995), Hall and
Swaine (1981), Hutchinson and Dalziel (1954-1972) and Taylor (1960), with the objective of obtaining a
general overview of the vegetation and environment of the project location.


2. Field survey – A reconnaissance survey was conducted in the project site and its external boundaries to

obtain an overview of the extent, topography and complexity of the vegetation. Transect walks (100 m)

were conducted along various sections of the project site. A total of 9 sample locations were assessed in the

proposed project site. All plant species, encountered within 10 m on either side of the transect, were

identified and recorded. The sample location coordinates and profiles are presented in Table 1.

3.0     RESULTS


3.1     Flora


3.1.1   General Vegetation of the Project Area
The Greater Kumasi Metropolitan Area lies within the Moist Semi-deciduous Northwest subtype of the
forest zone of Ghana (Hall and Swaine, 1981). The vegetation of the area had earlier been placed within
the Celtis-Triplochiton Association (Taylor, 1960). The characteristic species (Hall and Swaine, 1981) are
Turraeanthus africanus, Daniellia ogea, Khaya ivorensis, Illigera pentaphylla, Pteris togoensis,
Chytranthus macrobotyrs and Cola nitida.

A total of 9 vegetation samples were taken across the three project sites (Asafo, Kumasi Zoo and Kaase),
in the Greater Kumasi Metropolitan Area, during the survey. The total number of species encountered in
the survey was determined and used in determining the floristic composition of the project site. Floristic
analysis (Appendix 2) was carried out to determine the dominant families, life form composition and the
ecological guilds of the species. The analysis also included the composition of species in terms of their Star
Rating and IUCN threat status. The species list was inspected for invasive alien species.



3.1.2   Existing Vegetation of the Project Site
The project area is generally built-up with undulating topography and drained by Subin River and its
tributaries. The Subin River has been diverted into a large drain along most of its course through the project
area. There is no significant forest vegetation in the project area. A small patch of gallery forest occurs
upstream of the river in the Kumasi Zoo enclave, while patches of aquatic vegetation, dominated by Thalia
geniculata, occur wetlands along the banks of the Gee stream and Subin River. A mosaic of farmlands and
farm re-growths/secondary thickets occur in well drained areas within the project site.

The farm re-growths/thickets have species such as Ficus exasperata, Alchornea cordifolia, Solanum
verbascifolium, Mimosa pudica, Ipomoea involucrata, Sporobolus pyramidalis,Carica papaya,
Broussonetia papyrifera and Chromolaena odorata. Plates 1 to 10 illustrate the vegetation types within the
project area. (Plates 1 to 10 show the vegetation encountered in the project site).

3.1.3    Floristic Analysis
The species lists for the 9 samples analysed are presented in Appendix 1 while the floristic composition and
analyses of the GKMA project sites are presented in Appendix 2. Appendix 2 shows that the survey
recorded a total of 77 species in 71 genera belonging to 36 families. The families with high species
representation were the Fabaceae (10), Poaceae (10) and Euphorbiaceae (5). These 3 families (out of total
of 36) accounted for about 32.5% of the species recorded. The rest of the families were poorly represented
with less than 5 species.

The Life form composition of the flora (Table 5) showed a dominance of the Tree Life form (about 37.7%)
followed by the Herb, Shrub and Climber life forms with 28.6%, 24.7% and 9.1% respectively of the flora.
Disturbance due to land clearance and regeneration in the farm regrowth and thickets could explain the
observed diversity of life forms.

Inspection of Appendix 2 revealed two of the species recorded were Invasive Alien Species viz.,

3.1.4     Conclusion on Flora Survey
The flora survey revealed that no significant vegetation or natural habitat exists in the project sites and its
area of influence, with the exception of a small patch of riverine forest within the Kumasi Zoo enclave.
Urbanization, and its associated infrastructural development, is largely responsible for the paucity of natural
vegetation or habitat within the project area.

    3.2.3    General species richness
Animals contribute to the functioning of ecosystems in diverse ways. Their diversity and abundance provide

    insight into environmental health. The presence, as well as changes in the abundance and diversity of

    mammals and herpetofauna, therefore presents the opportunity for monitoring the impact of changes in

    the environment.

This study recorded 13 species of animals consisting of (2) species of amphibians, four (4) species of reptiles,

    four (4) species of birds, and three (3) species of mammals.

The trends in the species diversity of animals in the area can be attributed to the level of infrastructural

    development in the area. The habitats have completely been built-up but had pockets of vegetation in the

    form of trees associated with riverbanks, hedges, and shade tree that appear to provide habitats for the birds.

    Additionally, the species of birds encountered were mostly generalists that inhabit a wide range of habitats

    ranging including urban areas. Some of the species such as Pied crows, Cattle egrets, and laughing doves

    are also cosmopolitan.

In contrast, regular disposal of sewage and household waste into the drains and streams adversely affects the

    survival of amphibian populations (Gbogbo and Attuquayefio, (2008) and this could account for their low

    species diversity. The species of mammals inhabiting the area consist exclusively of rodents. Apart from

    the Grasscutter which was reported to be last seen about five years ago, the other two species of rodent

    reported to inhabit the area are commonly associated with human settlements (Garshong et al. 2013).

Clearly, the species inhabiting the area are either associated with degraded habitats, and urban areas, or are

    cosmopolitan. No sensitive species were seen or reported to inhabit the area.
3.2.4   Conservation Significance
The study area is not home to any endangered, vulnerable or threatened species. It supports a number of

vertebrate species listed by IUCN as species of Least Concern. Based on the Ghana Wildlife Convention

Regulation, the Nile Monitor and the Nile crocodile were the only completely protected species reported to

inhabit the area. It was however clear from the interview that both species are rare in the area. These,

therefore, indicate that development projects in the area would not be of any adverse consequence to faunal

conservation in Ghana.

   1. INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND


Global and national increases in human populations have led to increases in sewage and
wastewater available for treatment. This results in ecological and socioeconomic problems for
society. Sometimes, sewage and wastewater are not treated but discharged directly into
receiving waters. However, in recent times, various levels of sewage treatment is affordable
to many societies such as septic tanks. Sewage treatment plants are installed primarily to
remove biological oxygen demand (BOD), reduce quantity of solids (dissolved and suspended)
in the waste, and removal of nutrients (mostly nitrates and phosphates). Secondly, waste
treatment plants help to reduce bad odour and appearance; as well as control disease causing
organisms, e.g., coliforms.
Quantities, frequency and timing of discharge, treatment methods and efficiency, and point of
release of sewage determine the level of impact on ecosystems and humans. Often, sewage is
discharged and channeled into treatment plants in pulses – daily, seasonally, following human
migratory patterns, and the response times of ecosystems of release. After treatment, the liquid
waste is either re-used or discharged into large bodies of water such as streams, bays, rivers,
lakes, estuaries, and the oceans for dilution; while the solid sludge may be dislodged and used
to fertilise crops on lands. The characteristics of receiving waters such as currents and mixing
are also very important in determining impact of sewage and wastewater on ecosystems.
Choice of treatment methods is determined by local priorities and constraints; including legal,
socioeconomic, and ecological implications. Many developing countries have inadequate
funds and logistics to spare and therefore utilize low cost technologies such as Waste
Stabilisation Ponds (WSPs). WSPs utilize shallow basins for wastewater treatment through
natural disinfection mechanisms by integrating the activity of phototrophic, autotrophic and
heterotrophic microorganisms (Rittmann & McCarty. 2001). Effectively treating wastewater
effluents can efficiently contribute to water conservation, expansion of irrigated agriculture,
environmental and public health protection (Papadopoulos & Savvides. 2003).
Waste stabilization systems are used globally in many countries. They are typically large
basins, earthen embarkments, usually part of a series of basins, low construction and
maintenance cost, low intensity, high pathogen removal, reliable in operation, complex
ecosystems, and low understanding of processes. The disadvantages of WSPs include large
land requirements, sensitivity to climate, proliferation of mosquitoes and other insects, and
dependency on topography.        Commonly, WSPs comprise a single series of anaerobic,
facultative and maturation ponds, or several such series in parallel (Fig.1).




Figure1: Schematic Diagram of the Waste Stabilization Ponds


Biological assessments of the efficiency of sewage and wastewater treatment is achieved
through determination of biological diversity, abundance, and sensitivity of organisms to
pollution stress. Biological assessment has the advantage of being direct in measurement of
impact and the ability to integrate over time and effects.




   2. OBJECTIVE
This aquatic biological survey seeks to collect baseline information on the local dynamics of
the flora, fauna (biological assessment) and water quality in the project area. This information,
albeit unidirectional, provides a rapid appraisal of the status and ecological health of the aquatic
ecosystem in order to inform additional monitoring or corrective actions for the purpose of
ensuring the sound administration of project activities. The report is presented with the
intention of contributing to establishing a baseline for the project and managing environmental
concerns associated with development activities in order to ensure that national ecosystem
conservation and preservation objectives are met.
The research included both desk-based, field, and laboratory assessments. As part of the
desktop research, all pertinent and accessible literature was consulted in order to provide
baseline environmental information and to enlighten the analysis of the study's findings.
Observation, sampling, and data collection on the field, including experimental fishing and
    interviews with locals, were followed by laboratory analysis and report writing. Field data
    collection was undertaken on 28th August, 2023.

4.0 FIELD SAMPLING AND METHODOLOGY
4.1 Water Quality
All measurements were obtained using standardised procedures. Using potable water quality
metre, temperature, conductivity, total dissolved solids, and pH were measured in situ.
Temperature, conductivity, total dissolved solids, and pH were measured with a HORIBA U-
50 series multimeter. At each location, replicate measurements were collected, and the mean
result was calculated (Table 1).


4.2 Flora and fauna survey
For the purpose of evaluating the overall biological integrity of the aquatic ecosystems of ponds
and other locations within the project area, a quantitative approach was used to capture all data.
At each location, aquatic fauna was sampled, identified, and its abundance estimated using
standard procedures. All categories of fauna were sampled and documented in duplicate. All
species were identified utilising standard identification guides and expert knowledge. Other
pertinent observations were made, and results were analysed to aid in the prediction and
mitigation of major impacts. The aquatic macrophytes, including flora, floating and
overhanging flowering plants, were evaluated in terms of plant life. Where feasible, a
fisherman was engaged to conduct experimental fishing at the sampling locations, and all
catches were analysed and quantified.
4.3 Aquatic Macro-invertebrates
The sampling procedures used in this study were based on the approach outlined by Gordon
(1994). Pond nets were used for the purpose of gathering aquatic organisms that was closely
linked with flora that either thrived or extended into the aquatic environment. A total of ten
duplicates of sweep net sampling were conducted at each location. The captured specimens
from the sweep net were transferred into a receptacle and preserved using a 5% formalin
solution in order to facilitate further enumeration and taxonomic identification inside the
controlled environment of the laboratory. Pebbles of small size, capable of being manually
extracted from the aquatic environment, were carefully placed into receptacles and afterwards
subjected to a filtration process using a sieve with a mesh size of 200 microns. At each sample
site, when feasible, a quantitative estimate of abundance was obtained by extracting three
benthic cores. These cores had a length ranging from 10 to 20 cm and a diameter of 5 cm. The
core samples underwent a process of filtration using a sieve with a mesh size of 200 microns,
resulting in the collection of sediment-associated organisms into a designated sample container.
The collected samples were preserved in the field using a 5% formalin solution and then
transferred to a laboratory setting, where they were maintained in 70% ethanol for the purpose
of identification and quantification.
4.4 Fish sampling
Experimental fishing and interviews were conducted at Maturation Pond 1 (TPS1) to give
quantitative estimates of the fishery resources. A fisherman was engaged to collect samples,
with due consideration to catch per unit effort. Hook and line gear was used. The length-weight
relationship of each fish species collected was noted. The fish was examined individually,
measured for standard and total lengths, and weighed.
4.5 Zooplankton
Due to the shallowness of the sampling sites, plankton nets could not be towed in the pond and
Subin river. As such twenty litres (20 L) of raw water samples were collected and filtered
through a 60 𝜇m mesh size plankton net. The collected samples were carefully backwashed
and transferred into a sampling receptacle and fixed with four per cent (4%) buffered formalin
to which Rose Bengal at 1 mg/l had been added. The samples were sorted by eye and by
compound low microscope and all the organisms found identified and counted.
4.6    Phytoplankton
Samples of phytoplankton were collected for species diversity and abundance assessment,
respectively. Standard protocols were followed during the sample collection and preservation
for the laboratory investigations. At each sampling location, a total of 20 litres of raw water
samples from an area undisturbed by previous sampling was collected and filtered through a
60 𝜇m mesh size plankton net. The collected samples were carefully backwashed and
transferred into a sampling receptacle and fixed with four per cent (4%) buffered formalin for
laboratory analysis. In the laboratory, the collected samples were further concentrated into an
aliquot of between 20- and 80- ml volumes. For the species identification and abundance
estimation, 1 ml of the concentrated phytoplankton samples was transferred into 1𝜇L x 1 mL
Sedge Rafter Chamber. The introduced aliquot was covered using a cover slide and the whole
set-up was mounted on the mechanical stage of the Leica light microscope and observed under
100 magnification. Phytoplankton cells were identified with the aid of identification guides and
counted within 25% of the total unit of the chamber, respectively. Species abundance was
estimated using simple ratio proportions and presented as a number of cells per unit volume
for each sampling location.
Phytoplankton cell enumeration was computed by simple proportion based on the number of
individual plankton species counted within a subset of the counting chamber times the total
cells (1000) as shown in equation 1. On the other hand, species abundance per volume was
computed by summation of individual species and divided by the total water sample filtered
and presented as the number of organisms per litre [cell/L] (Perry, 2010).

5.2 Macroinvertebrate community
Freshwater invertebrate fauna plays key ecological roles in the maintenance of aquatic
ecosystem services, mainly in secondary production and energy flow dynamics. Among these
organisms, aquatic insects are the most important because they are the major proportion of
functional feeding groups helping in ecosystem dynamics, but nonetheless, they are still the
most threatened.


There were similarities between the aquatic insect (macroinvertebrate) species found at diverse
locations. A total of 1,031 species have been identified. The maximum species diversity was
recorded in maturation pond, followed by the Gee River. The relative abundance of species in
the maturation ponds indicated improved environmental condition. On the other hand, the
limited species diversity observed in the Anaerobic and Subin Rivers indicated a high level of
pollution. This was typical of a pond of water body lacking dissolved oxygen. The greatest
number of chironomids was also recorded in the anaerobic reservoir. Because these insects
have a high tolerance for low oxygen conditions in the sediments of freshwater ecosystems,
they are regarded as excellent bioindicators of poor water quality (Ansa-Asare et al., 2009).

6.0      POTENTIAL IMPACTS AND MITIGATION

6.1      Potential Impacts
Waste Stabilization Ponds (WSPs) effluents can be impacted by nutrient enrichment. Elevated
levels of nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus, when discharged into aquatic ecosystems,
can lead to nutrient enrichment (eutrophication). This can result in algal blooms, oxygen
depletion, and changes in the composition of aquatic flora and fauna.


The decomposition of organic matter in WSPs can consume oxygen, potentially leading to
reduced dissolved oxygen levels in the receiving water body, which can harm fish and other
aquatic organisms. If WSPs do not effectively and efficiently treat sewage, they can release
pathogens into the receiving water, posing a risk to human health and aquatic life.


The settling of solids and algal growth within WSPs can lead to the accumulation of sludge
and sediment, which, when discharged into aquatic ecosystems, can smother benthic habitats,
and increase water turbidity.


Discharge of toxic compounds when sewage plants are compromised by stormwater or
industrial effluent then there could be impact on aquatic flora and fauna. Some wastewaters
may contain toxic substances, such as heavy metals or industrial chemicals, which can be
released into aquatic ecosystems if as expected they are not properly treated in WSPs.

6.2.     Proposed Mitigation Measures

When it comes to mitigating the potential negative impacts of WSPs on aquatic ecology,
several measures can be implemented to protect and enhance the environment. Here are some
mitigation measures for waste stabilization ponds in terms of aquatic ecological study:



⚫      Regular Monitoring and Assessment:
Establish a comprehensive monitoring programme to assess the ecological health of the
receiving water body and the WSPs themselves. Monitor key ecological indicators, such as
water quality parameters (e.g., dissolved oxygen, pH, nutrient levels), biological diversity, and
the presence of indicator species.
⚫     Vegetative Buffer Zones
Establish and maintain vegetative buffer zones around WSPs to help filter pollutants and
provide habitat for wildlife. Native vegetation can be planted to create buffer zones that can
trap sediment, absorb nutrients, and provide a natural habitat.


⚫     Proper Sizing and Design
Ensure that WSPs are properly sized and designed to handle the wastewater load and
hydrologic requirements to avoid overloading and to promote effective treatment.


⚫     Effluent Quality Standards
Establish and enforce effluent quality standards to limit the discharge of harmful pollutants into
the receiving water body.        Regular monitoring, as afore stated, is essential to ensure
compliance.


⚫     Sediment management
Regularly maintain and clean the WSPs to prevent the accumulation of excess sludge and
sediment, which can negatively impact water quality and aquatic life.


⚫     Wildlife Habitat Enhancement
Design and manage WSPs to enhance wildlife habitat by creating diverse aquatic and riparian
ecosystems. This can include nesting and foraging sites for birds and other wildlife.




7.0        CONCLUSION

This study has shown that the water quality and aquatic life in the ponds are largely typical of such
WSPs. There is low biological diversity, presence of some toxic organisms, and poor quality of
receiving water possibly due to low volumes. The findings indicate a system with a heavy organic load.
The ponds can be dislodged to improve its effectiveness. In addition, terrestrial plants and human
activity around the ponds and receiving waters should be controlled to achieve improved environmental
quality.
